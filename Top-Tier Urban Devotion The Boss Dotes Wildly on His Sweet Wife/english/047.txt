Chapter 47 — Light Without End
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626249.html

The story should have ended here. But Su Qingyuan said it hadn't. She said light doesn't end. It only changes direction and keeps on shining.
The last time I went to Nancheng was in autumn. The osmanthus trees were in full bloom, their fragrance so sweet it was almost cloying. Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan still lived in that courtyard. The old osmanthus tree had grown taller, and the ones they'd planted later had long since spread into full, leafy canopies. Beneath the trees, Lu Shiyan had set out a stone table and two rattan chairs, and on the table there was always a pot of tea—sometimes Longjing, sometimes Pu'er. Every morning they drank tea together, then went their separate ways to their own work—she to her studio, he to the company. In the evening they came home together, sat out in the courtyard, and waited for Lu Xi to video-call with the children.
Lu Xi was in London now, already the design director of a renowned jewelry brand. James had opened his own architecture firm and cycled to work and back every day. Yinuo and Yiyan had planted an osmanthus tree in their garden—not in Nancheng, but in a garden on the outskirts of London. Lu Xi said that once the tree grew up, its fragrance would drift across the whole yard, just like the one at Grandma's house.
Su Qingyuan showed me the photo: two little children crouched beside the planting hole, hands covered in mud, eyes crinkled into crescent moons from laughing. She said, "See? Light passes itself down on its own." I said, "Like your work." She thought for a moment. "Will my work get passed down too?" I said, "It will. Because sincere things never go out of style."
Zhou Wanyin had handed her jewelry studio over to the younger generation and moved to the suburbs to focus on painting. She held an exhibition once a year—small, but every time someone made the trip just to see it. She said it didn't matter whether the paintings were good or not; what mattered was that there were still things she wanted to paint. Qu Ya was still taking photographs. She'd shot her "Roots" series for thirty years, from Nancheng to all corners of the world. She said that after thirty years she'd come to realize that roots aren't a place—they're relationships. The connection between you and a person, a tree, a city.
Chen Xiaoman's restaurant had opened a branch in the city center of Melbourne, and business was good. Every year she sent Su Qingyuan a photo, and in it was the osmanthus tree outside the restaurant's door—a sapling she'd brought over from Nancheng, planted ten years ago, and which had finally bloomed. Su Qingyuan pinned that photo to the wall of her studio, alongside all the others. She said, "See? Light finds its own place to take root."
News of Su Yurou grew scarcer and scarcer. Her social media had gone quiet since last spring, the final photo a beach in Melbourne, captioned with just two words: "Clear skies." Su Qingyuan tapped a like. She didn't comment. Some relationships need neither closeness nor severance. They simply exist, like a tree that has stopped growing taller—not flourishing, but still alive.
The day I left, Su Qingyuan walked me to the gate. She broke off a small sprig of osmanthus from the tree, wrapped it in white paper, and handed it to me. "Take it home. Set it on your table—it'll stay fragrant a long while."
I took it and bent my head to breathe in the scent. "Su Qingyuan, do you still paint these days?"
"I do. Every single day."
"Paint what?"
"Light. Same as when I was a child."
"The light from your childhood, and the light now—how are they different?"
She thought about it, then said, "The light when I was a child was what I wanted to become. The light now is what I already am."
She didn't see me all the way to the front gate; she stopped beneath the osmanthus tree. She said she was used to seeing people off here. Whenever Lu Shiyan went out, she walked him to this spot; whenever Lu Xi left home, she walked her here too. This was the place where she watched all her light travel off into the distance.
I turned and walked a few steps, then looked back. She was still standing there, in that old sweater, cradling that cup of unfinished tea in her hands. Osmanthus blossoms fell on her shoulder, and she didn't brush them away. The sunlight fell over her, and she didn't shy from it.
In that moment I suddenly understood the "light" she spoke of. It wasn't the aurora in the distance, nor the sun overhead—it was her, standing right there. Quietly, surely, becoming her own light.
I wrote this story for a long time. By the end, I could no longer tell which parts were real and which I had imagined. But one thing I am certain of—Su Qingyuan is real. Not real as a character in a novel, but real as "a possibility." She tells you: a person can climb up out of the mud, can choose not to forgive yet still let go, can shine by her own strength, can love and be loved. These aren't fairy tales—they're the road she walked.
If you are in the dark right now, I hope this story can give you a little light. Not the kind that's dazzling, blinding, that makes you feel ashamed of yourself, but the kind that's gentle and quiet, the kind that tells you, "You can, too."
Turn off the light—the day will always break. This isn't consolation. It's a fact.
