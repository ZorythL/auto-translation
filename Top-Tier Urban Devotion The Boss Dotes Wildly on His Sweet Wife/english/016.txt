On the third day after Su Yurou got out, Su Qingyuan received an anonymous text.
"You think stepping on me means you've won? Sister Qingyuan, in all the years we've grown up together, when have you ever truly beaten me?"
There was no signature, but that form of address—in all the years they'd grown up together, only one person had ever called her that.
Su Qingyuan read it, deleted the message with a blank expression, and went back to the final stage of polishing "Daybreak."
She knew Su Yurou wouldn't let it go. But she knew something else even better: the woman she was now was no longer the girl who could be frightened by a single anonymous text.
"Who's messaging you?" Lu Shiyan sat on the sofa in the lounge area, a document in his hand, not even lifting his head.
"Spam," Su Qingyuan said lightly.
Lu Shiyan raised his eyes to her, his gaze lingering on her face for a second. He didn't press, just lowered his head back to the document.
But Su Qingyuan noticed that he turned the pages a little slower than before—he was thinking about something.
The polishing of "Daybreak" had entered its most critical phase. The three-layered halo of the moonstone had already taken shape, but that innermost touch of warm gold was never quite right—either too faint, faint to the point of being nearly invisible, or too thick, thick enough to smother the blue and white of the outer layers.
Su Qingyuan had already ruined five moonstones, each one worth a small fortune. It pained her to waste the material, and it pained her even more to waste the time.
"Where are you stuck?" Lu Shiyan had come to stand behind her at some point she hadn't noticed.
Su Qingyuan didn't turn around. She pointed at the moonstone under the magnifying glass. "The inner halo won't come out. The effect I want is 'the gradient of the sky at the very instant of daybreak, shifting from dark to light,' but right now it's either too dark or too bright."
Lu Shiyan watched for a few seconds, then suddenly said, "Have you tried changing the angle at which the blade enters? Instead of cutting from the front, cut in from the side, against the grain."
Su Qingyuan froze for a moment, then turned to look at him. "You know gem-cutting?"
"No." Lu Shiyan's expression was perfectly candid. "But I've read some material on it. The way you're cutting now follows the direction of the crystal structure, so the halo it produces is linear. If you go against the structure just a little, the light might refract out from within in different layers."
Su Qingyuan stared at him for three seconds, then turned, picked up a fresh moonstone, secured it, and adjusted the angle of the blade.
The first cut went in, and the change in the halo was visible to the naked eye.
It wasn't a linear gradient—it was a ring upon ring of light spreading outward from the core, like ripples.
That innermost touch of warm gold had finally come out.
Su Qingyuan's hands trembled faintly—not from nerves, but from exhilaration.
"Lu Shiyan, are you sure you don't know gem-cutting?"
"I'm sure."
"Then that suggestion you just made…"
"Logical reasoning." Lu Shiyan slid both hands into his pockets, his tone flat. "Cutting is essentially about controlling the path light refracts along inside the stone. Your problem was that the refraction path was singular—so you change the path. It's the same logic as making a business decision."
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath, bent back down, and focused on driving the rest of the cut forward.
Lu Shiyan didn't say anything more. He went quietly back to the sofa and returned to his document.
The only sounds in the studio were the faint friction of tools against stone and the occasional birdsong drifting in from outside the window.
This state—each absorbed in their own work, undisturbed yet keeping each other company—was more reassuring than any sweet words could ever be.
Two hours later, Su Qingyuan straightened up, lifted "Daybreak" off the worktable, and cradled it in her palm.
Under the natural light, the moonstone revealed a perfect three-layered halo—the outer layer the deep blue of the hour before dawn, the middle layer the silver-white of daybreak itself, the inner layer the warm gold of the first ray of sunlight piercing through the clouds.
She turned it at an angle, and the three layers of halo seemed to come alive, slowly flowing, merging, separating—as though the entire passage of the sky from night to sunrise had been compressed into one small stone.
"It's done." There was a slight tremor in Su Qingyuan's voice.
Lu Shiyan walked over, lowered his head to look at the "Daybreak" in her palm, and was silent for a long time.
"Su Qingyuan." His voice was very low.
"Mm?"
"This piece is going to win an award."
Su Qingyuan looked up at him. The halo of the moonstone was reflected in his eyes, and those three layers of light seemed to settle into the depths of them too.
She suddenly felt that this moment was worth remembering more than any award.
The night "Daybreak" was finished, Su Qingyuan sent the photo to Zhou Wanyin.
Zhou Wanyin's reply was a voice message a full fifty-nine seconds long. Su Qingyuan opened it: the first fifteen seconds were silence, only the sound of rapid breathing, and then came Zhou Wanyin's voice, struggling to suppress her excitement:
"Qingyuan, I'm driving right now—when I saw your message I pulled over to the side of the road. This piece isn't 'going to win an award,' it's 'if it doesn't win, I'll flip the judging committee's table.' Send me the photo and the detailed materials. I'll submit the Biennale recommendation package tonight. Also, I want to arrange for a professional jewelry photographer to shoot the finished images of 'Daybreak.' When are you free?"
Su Qingyuan replied: [Anytime. Tomorrow works.]
Zhou Wanyin: [Then I'll have the photographer at your studio at ten tomorrow morning.]
At ten the next morning, the photographer arrived right on time.
He was a man in his early thirties, surname Lin, one of the most expensive jewelry photographers in the industry. For Zhou Wanyin to get him to come, she'd clearly spared no expense.
When Photographer Lin saw "Daybreak," the hand holding his camera paused.
"This halo…" He leaned in and studied it for a long while, then straightened up and said to Su Qingyuan, "Miss Su, I've been shooting jewelry for over a decade and I've never seen a cutting technique like this. Is it your own original work?"
"Yes."
"Does it have a name?"
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment. "Call it the 'Ripple Cut.'"
Because the halo spread outward from the core in ring after ring, just like ripples.
Photographer Lin nodded, asked nothing more, and began setting up the lighting to shoot.
The shoot went on for four hours. Photographer Lin cycled through seven different light sources and more than a dozen angles before he finally captured the most perfect rendering of "Daybreak's" halo.
He exported one raw frame on the spot for Su Qingyuan to see.
Su Qingyuan looked at the moonstone on the screen, glowing as if lit from within, was silent for a few seconds, then picked up her phone and sent the photo to Lu Shiyan.
The caption was just two words: [It's done.]
Lu Shiyan's reply was a screenshot—he'd set that very photo as his phone wallpaper.
Su Qingyuan stared at the screenshot, her ears turning red.
[System: Even though the affection level is already maxed out, the System still has to say this—this kind of quiet, sneaky sweetness is a hundred times more lethal than just saying "I love you."]
Su Qingyuan: …Didn't you say you were retiring to the background?
[System: For a scene this iconic, the System absolutely has to be here.]

At the same time Zhou Wanyin submitted the photo of "Daybreak" to the Biennale judging committee, she also posted it on her own social media.
The caption was extremely brief: "The latest work by my student Su Qingyuan, 'Daybreak.' Technique: original 'Ripple Cut.' I won't say much—see for yourselves."
Within an hour of that post going up, the shares broke ten thousand and the comments broke thirty thousand.
The industry blew up.
"Ripple Cut? Never heard of a cutting method like this."
"I zoomed in and looked for ten minutes. This kind of layered halo isn't something ordinary cutting can achieve."
"Zhou Wanyin's student? No wonder."
"It's not 'no wonder'—it's that Zhou Wanyin got lucky and stumbled onto a gem."
"Su Qingyuan—isn't that the one who went public with Lu Shiyan? I thought she got into the industry through connections, but turns out she's actually got real talent."
"Can connections get you an original cutting technique? Cut one and show me, then."
The tide in the comments section turned from doubt to recognition in less than half a day.
And those who had once followed the marketing accounts in calling Su Qingyuan a "plagiarist" quietly deleted their comments and pretended nothing had ever happened.
Su Qingyuan didn't bother scrolling through those comments. She sat in her studio, and before her, all seven pieces of the now-completed "First Light" series were lined up in a row across the worktable.
From "Breaking the Cocoon" to "Daybreak"—seven pieces, seven stories, strung together they traced her entire journey climbing from the abyss to the summit.
She took a group photo and posted to Weibo:
"The 'First Light' series, seven pieces, all complete. From conception to finished work, it took twenty-three days in total. Over these twenty-three days, I lost some things, and gained even more. Thank you to everyone who walked through it with me. The date for the series launch will be announced shortly."
The attached image was a family portrait of all seven pieces.
Within ten minutes of that Weibo post going up, the likes broke half a million.
The comments were a uniform chorus demanding the launch, demanding the presale, demanding more work.
Su Qingyuan read through them one by one, the curve of her lips growing wider and wider.
She set down her phone, walked to the window, and pushed it open.
The scent of osmanthus had already faded—at this time of year, the osmanthus was nearly done blooming.
But the sunlight was still there.
It fell on her face, warm.
Her phone buzzed.
Lu Shiyan: [For the launch venue, I had someone prepare a few options. Pick one.]
Su Qingyuan: [When did you secretly start preparing the launch too?]
Lu Shiyan: [From the day you started making "Daybreak." I knew you'd make it the best it could be, so I prepared a stage worthy of it in advance.]
Su Qingyuan looked at those words and drew a deep breath.
This man—he always had everything arranged before she'd even opened her mouth.
She chose one of the options—the Chengnan Arts Center, the place she'd passed by countless times, looked up at countless times, yet had never once stepped inside.
Su Qingyuan: [Decided. The Chengnan Arts Center.]
Lu Shiyan: [Good. Leave the rest to me.]
That phrase again.
"Leave the rest to me."
From that rainy night until now, she'd long since lost count of how many times he'd said it.
But every time she heard it, she felt that—however vast this world was, however many storms it held, she was never alone.

The launch was set for two weeks later.
Over those two weeks, Su Qingyuan was so busy her feet barely touched the ground. The launch run-of-show, the media invitations, the work-display layout, the on-site security, the guest list… every single thing required her review and confirmation.
Lu Shiyan reassigned an entire team from Lu Group's PR division to help, but Su Qingyuan insisted on personally overseeing every detail.
"This is my first series launch," she told Lu Shiyan. "I have to watch over it myself."
Lu Shiyan didn't object. He simply showed up at the studio door at ten o'clock sharp every night to take her home.
One day she worked until three in the morning, and when she came out of the studio, she found his car still there.
She walked over and knocked on the window. It rolled down. His eyes were threaded with red, but when he saw her, he still smiled.
"Why didn't you just go home first?" Su Qingyuan felt a pang of tenderness.
"I said I'd pick you up."
"You could've just texted to say it was too late and I'd take a cab home myself."
Lu Shiyan glanced at her, his voice very soft. "Su Qingyuan, every word I've ever said counts."
Su Qingyuan got into the passenger seat, buckled her seatbelt, didn't look at him, but her voice was a little muffled. "You're going to spoil me like this."
Lu Shiyan started the car, the corner of his mouth lifting slightly. "Spoiling you is exactly the point. If no one else can stand you, then you'll have no choice but to stay by my side."
Su Qingyuan turned to glare at him. He kept his eyes on the road ahead, his expression perfectly solemn.
But that little curve at the corner of his mouth gave him away.
[System: This man—saying "spoiling you is exactly the point" with his mouth, while in reality wishing the whole world would dote on her. Saying one thing and meaning another—he learned that from the female lead.]
Su Qingyuan: System, can you not over-analyze everything?
[System: The System's reading-comprehension ability is a perfect score. There's no need to "over-analyze."]

The day of the launch.
The Chengnan Arts Center, the main exhibition hall.
Su Qingyuan stood backstage, peering through a gap in the curtain at the audience below.
Not a seat was empty.
Those who'd come weren't just media and industry insiders—there were also plenty of ordinary visitors who'd bought tickets on their own just to see the exhibition. The launch tickets had sold out within ten minutes of going on sale; when Su Qingyuan heard the news, she'd thought she'd misheard.
In the front row below, Zhou Wanyin sat dead center, flanked by several heavyweights from the jewelry world—people Su Qingyuan had only ever seen in magazine photos.
In the second row sat reporters from more than a dozen mainstream and specialist jewelry outlets, their lenses long and short all trained on the stage.
And at the very back of the hall, in an inconspicuous corner, stood a man in a black suit.
Lu Shiyan.
He hadn't taken a seat in the front row. He hadn't claimed a prominent spot by the title of "Su Qingyuan's boyfriend." He hadn't even told anyone he'd be coming.
He simply stood there, like a silent mountain.
Through the gap in the curtain, Su Qingyuan saw him.
She drew a deep breath, turned, and said to the staff, "I'm ready."
The lights dimmed.
A single spotlight fell on the center of the stage.
Su Qingyuan walked out from behind the curtain, wearing a simple black dress, her hair pinned up to reveal the slender line of her neck. She wore no jewelry at all—because today the star wasn't her; it was her work.
She walked to the center of the stage, picked up the microphone, and the room fell silent.
"Thank you all for coming today to the launch of the 'First Light' series."
Her voice wasn't loud, but it was steady, carrying through the sound system to every corner of the hall.
"The name of this series comes from a very important turning point in my life. Before it, I was always in the dark. After it, I began to see light."
She paused, her gaze drifting unconsciously toward that corner at the back of the hall.
Lu Shiyan stood there, hands in his pockets, looking at her across an entire sea of people.
"This series has seven pieces in total, each corresponding to one of the seven stages of my journey from darkness into light. They weren't conjured out of thin air—they are my autobiography."
The hall was utterly silent.
Su Qingyuan pressed the remote in her hand, and the large screen behind her lit up. The high-definition photo of the first piece, "Breaking the Cocoon," appeared on the display.
"The first piece, 'Breaking the Cocoon.'" Her voice was calm and powerful. "The inspiration came from the moment I made up my mind to leave the place that had trapped me for more than a decade. The dark tones of the opal represent the abyss; the diamond wings around it represent the strength to break free."
She introduced them one by one—the source of inspiration, the creative process, the technical difficulties of each piece, all laid out clearly.
When she reached the last piece, she paused.
The photo of "Daybreak" appeared on the large screen.
The moonstone's three-layered halo shifted under the lens, like a true dawn.
"This piece is called 'Daybreak,'" Su Qingyuan's voice softened, "the final piece of the 'First Light' series, and the one I treasure most. Its core technique is an entirely new way of cutting moonstone, which I call the 'Ripple Cut.' This cutting technique was completed with the inspiration of… one particular person."
In the audience, Zhou Wanyin arched an eyebrow and smiled knowingly.
Su Qingyuan didn't look toward that corner, but her voice was a little softer than before, a little gentler too:
"The core logic of the 'Ripple Cut' is to change the refraction path of light. I used to always follow a single direction, walking straight into a dead end without ever knowing to turn. It was someone who reminded me—maybe if I changed direction, the light would shine in from a different angle."
She raised her head and finally looked toward the back of the hall.
Lu Shiyan stood there, perfectly still.
Across that great distance, Su Qingyuan couldn't make out his expression, but she could see the hand hanging at his side, the fingertips curling slightly.
"So, 'Daybreak' isn't only my daybreak," Su Qingyuan's voice grew a little hoarse, but she held it steady, "it's also the daybreak we lit together."
The audience was silent for an instant, and then thunderous applause erupted.
Su Qingyuan bowed deeply. When she straightened up, there were tears glistening at the corners of her eyes, but she didn't wipe them away.
The lights came back up.
Staff wheeled the seven pieces out from backstage and arranged them in glass display cases at the center of the hall.
The audience surged forward, crowding around the cases, camera flashes going off one after another, gasps of wonder, the buzz of discussion, the click of shutters all merging into one.
Su Qingyuan stood at the edge of the stage, watching it all, and was suddenly struck by an overwhelming sense of unreality.
Twenty-three days ago, she'd still been sitting on a hotel floor, staring blankly at an utterly empty bank account.
Twenty-three days later, she stood in Nancheng's most prestigious arts center, presenting her work to the whole world.
And the one person she most wanted to thank was, at this very moment, making his way toward her from the back of the hall, through the crowd.
The crowd parted to make a path of its own accord.
Not because they'd recognized him as Lu Shiyan—though some genuinely had—but because the aura around him was too powerful, so powerful that no one dared stand in his way.
He walked up to Su Qingyuan and stopped.
The two of them stood face to face, surrounded by the clamoring crowd, the flashing lights, the rise and fall of conversation.
But in his world there was only her.
"You spoke beautifully," he said, his voice low enough for only her to hear.
Su Qingyuan looked up at him, tears glistening in her eyes, and a smile too. "You were standing in the back the whole time? Why didn't you sit in the front row?"
"The front row is reserved for your career," Lu Shiyan said, lowering his head to look at her, undisguised affection in the depths of his eyes. "The back row is reserved for your person."
Su Qingyuan finally couldn't hold back, and the tears fell.
Lu Shiyan didn't say "don't cry," nor did he wipe her tears for her.
He simply took a step forward, came to stand at her side, his shoulder lightly brushing hers.
Then he turned his head, his voice low as if meant for her ears alone:
"Su Qingyuan, you're beautiful today. Not because of how well you're dressed, but because of the way you're standing here—you earned this yourself."
Su Qingyuan sniffled, wiped the tears away with the pad of her finger, and turned her head to glance at him.
"Lu Shiyan, could you not always say things like this at moments like this? My makeup's going to run."
Lu Shiyan glanced at her, the corner of his mouth lifting slightly. "You're not wearing any makeup."
Su Qingyuan: "…"
She really wasn't wearing makeup. Today had been too busy—there'd been no time at all.
Lu Shiyan let out a soft laugh and said nothing more.
He turned to face the crowd and stood at her side—not "standing in front of her to protect her," not "standing behind her to silently support her," but—shoulder to shoulder.
Like two trees growing side by side, roots entwined underground, branches and leaves touching in the wind, each independent, yet supporting the other.
[System: The affection level is already maxed out, so the System won't be broadcasting anymore. But the System wants to say one thing: this is the best ending. No—it's not an ending. It's a beginning.]
In her heart, Su Qingyuan silently said to the System: Thank you.
[System: You're welcome. The System will always be here. Even though it won't broadcast the affection level anymore, it'll always be watching over the two of you. All right, enough sentimentality. Today's launch was a great success. Congratulations, Designer Su.]
Su Qingyuan curved her lips into a smile.
The launch lasted three hours.
When it ended, Su Qingyuan received three things:
A formal shortlist notification from the Biennale organizing committee—"Daybreak" had passed the preliminary selection and advanced to the final round of evaluation.
A letter of intent for collaboration from a certain international jewelry brand, hoping she'd become the brand's first Chinese partner designer.
And one photo.
It was taken by Lu Shiyan, on his phone, of the launch in progress—she stood at the center of the stage, the spotlight falling on her, microphone in hand, her eyes full of light.
Below the photo was a single line:
"My designer."
Su Qingyuan stared at that line for a long time, then saved the photo and set it as her own phone wallpaper.
She sent a screenshot of the wallpaper to Lu Shiyan.
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly:
[Stealing my photo?]
Su Qingyuan: [What do you mean, stealing? You shot me—the copyright belongs to me.]
Lu Shiyan: [Fair point. Then I'll buy the copyright. Name your price.]
Su Qingyuan: [You can't afford it.]
Lu Shiyan: [Want to bet?]
Su Qingyuan: [What I want isn't money.]
Lu Shiyan: [Then what do you want?]
Su Qingyuan looked at that question, thought for a long while, typed out a line, then deleted it character by character, and in the end only replied with two words:
[Guess.]
Lu Shiyan didn't volley "Guess" back at her to play along with the game.
He replied with three words:
[I know.]
Su Qingyuan stared at those words—"I know"—and her heartbeat suddenly quickened.
He said he knew.
Knew what?
She'd said "Guess," and he'd said "I know."
Which meant—he didn't need to guess. He'd known all along.
Su Qingyuan set her phone face-down on the table and buried her face in both hands.
She was done for.
This man—he could even hear the words she'd never spoken aloud.
Outside the window, the night in Nancheng had already grown deep.
But Su Qingyuan felt that the sky had already been bright for a very long time.
