Chapter 39 — The Light of Everyday Life
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626241.html

Su Qingyuan had been putting together a new collection lately. She hadn't settled on a name yet, but the theme was already crystal clear—the everyday. Those overlooked, trivial, hardly-worth-mentioning moments: the back of him making coffee in the morning light, the profile of his face as he waited under the lamp for her to finish work, the angle at which he tilted the umbrella on rainy days, the way he tucked her cold hands into his own pocket in winter. These moments were so small—too small to deserve a place in any story. But Su Qingyuan wanted to make them into jewelry. To let that tenderness, the kind so easily forgotten, be worn over the heart, hung at the ear, held in the palm of a hand.
She sketched many drafts. The first was a brooch shaped like one hand holding another—not the tight grip of interlaced fingers, but a loose, natural clasp, like the casual linking of hands on a stroll. She used two different metals, platinum and rose gold, woven together, like two people with different skin tones and different warmth who fit together just right when their hands met. The second was a pair of stud earrings, inspired by a streetlamp. She made a tiny, glowing lamp, set with a yellow sapphire inside its shade, the light rendered as fine engraved lines radiating outward from the wick. On every late night home, the streetlamp was waiting for her. The third was a necklace, its pendant a half-open door, warm yellow light spilling through the crack. Not a door flung wide, nor one shut tight—a half-open one, waiting for you to come back. This was her own favorite, because every time Lu Shiyan came to her studio, he never knocked. He had a key; he'd push the door open and walk in, a thermal bag in his hand. "The door's unlocked," he always said. Only later did she understand—he hadn't forgotten to knock. He wanted her to know: this door would always be open for him.
When Su Qingyuan finished the third sketch, she looked up out the window. The new leaves on the osmanthus tree had grown a little larger, the tender green deepening to emerald, shimmering faintly in the afternoon sun. She closed her sketchbook, took out her phone, and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Come home early today. I'll cook for you." Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "You sure?"
Su Qingyuan: "Sure."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. I'll come home early."
Su Qingyuan's lips curved up. She put the sketchbook back in the drawer, grabbed her keys, and went out. She made a trip to the supermarket and bought pork ribs, greens, tofu, and a fish. The ribs she'd do sweet-and-sour, the greens stir-fried, the tofu in a soup, and the fish… she thought it over and decided on steaming it, simple. Back home, she changed into an apron, washed her hands, and started cooking. First she blanched the ribs, then mixed the sweet-and-sour sauce by the recipe from the video—one spoon of cooking wine, two of light soy, three of sugar, four of vinegar, five of water. She mixed and tasted as she went, adding sugar when it was too sour, vinegar when it was too sweet, and the flavor she ended up with wasn't bad at all. She cleaned the fish, stuffed it with ginger slices and scallion, drizzled steamed-fish soy sauce over it, and put it in to steam. She washed the greens and crushed the garlic, planning to stir-fry them once the ribs were done. She cut the tofu into cubes and simmered it with tomato into a soup, adding a pinch of salt and some chopped scallion.
When Lu Shiyan came home, she was stir-frying the greens. The range hood was humming, and she didn't hear the door open. Not until a pair of hands wrapped around her waist from behind did she jump, nearly dropping the spatula.
"You scared me half to death." She turned her head and glared at him.
Lu Shiyan didn't let go, his chin resting on her shoulder. "Cooking all this?"
"Mm. To celebrate."
"Celebrate what?"
"To celebrate that today you wanted to eat my cooking."
Lu Shiyan laughed, low, the vibration carrying through her back, ticklish. She shrank her neck. "Stop it, the food's going to burn." Only then did he let go and walk over to the table to help set out the bowls and chopsticks.
Four dishes and a soup: sweet-and-sour ribs, stir-fried seasonal greens, steamed sea bass, tomato and tofu soup. Su Qingyuan untied her apron, sat down across from him, and watched him a little nervously. "Try it."
Lu Shiyan picked up a piece of rib, took a bite, and chewed for a long time.
Su Qingyuan's heart climbed into her throat. "Not good?"
"It's good."
"Really?"
"Really. Much better than the first time. The first time was too sour."
Su Qingyuan paused. "The first time? When did you ever eat the first thing I cooked?"
Lu Shiyan looked at her, the corners of his lips lifting. "The first time you made ribs, you made them for yourself. That day you worked overtime until very late, came home and cooked ribs, and posted about it on your social feed. You said, 'Worked till the dead of night, treated myself to a plate of sweet-and-sour ribs—a little sour, but very satisfying.' Three years ago, December seventeenth, eleven forty-two at night."
Su Qingyuan froze. "You… you've seen my feed from three years ago?"
"Not seen. Scrolled through. From your very first post all the way to the last."
"You went through the whole thing?"
"Many times."
Tears slipped down Su Qingyuan's face. She lowered her head and poked at the rice in her bowl with her chopsticks, not letting him see her face. "Lu Shiyan, you're really such a freak."
"Mm."
"Who digs up someone's posts from years ago and reads them all?"
"I do."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. She lifted her head and wiped her eyes with her sleeve. "So what else did you see?"
"I saw that on your birthday three years ago, you posted, 'No one remembers today.' I saw that on New Year's Eve two years ago, you posted, 'Eating instant noodles alone in my rented room—happy new year.' I saw that last spring, you posted, 'The osmanthus is blooming, it smells so sweet.'"
Su Qingyuan's tears wouldn't stop. She covered her face, her shoulders trembling faintly. All those years, she'd thought no one remembered her birthday, no one cared how she spent New Year's Eve, no one noticed the curve of her smile when she caught the scent of osmanthus. It turned out it wasn't that no one cared—it was that he hadn't appeared yet.
"Lu Shiyan, why didn't you come find me back then?"
"Because back then you weren't ready yet." Lu Shiyan reached out and gently took hold of the hands covering her face. "I knew you'd be ready sooner or later. I waited for you."
Su Qingyuan looked at him through the gaps in her fingers, eyes blurred with tears. "How did you know I'd be ready?"
"Because you're you."
Those same three words again.
Su Qingyuan lowered her hands, sniffled, and picked up her chopsticks. "Eat. The food's getting cold."
"All right."
She picked up a piece of rib and put it in her mouth. A little sour, and a little sweet.
That night, Su Qingyuan didn't go back to her own apartment.
It wasn't the first time she'd stayed over, but tonight was a little different. Lu Shiyan had prepared a new toothbrush for her, a new towel, new pajamas—pale pink, pure cotton, the material she liked. After her shower, wearing the new pajamas, she stood at the bedroom's floor-to-ceiling window, looking out at the night view.
"Do you like it?" Lu Shiyan came out of the bathroom, hair still wet, wearing dark blue loungewear.
"Mm. The pajamas are very comfortable."
"I don't mean the pajamas. I mean here."
Su Qingyuan turned and looked at him. "Here is wonderful."
"Then move in?"
Su Qingyuan paused. "What?"
"Move in. Then you won't have to run back and forth anymore." Lu Shiyan walked over and stood in front of her. "Your studio is in the south of the city, and from here it's a twenty-minute drive to the creative park. Your apartment is too small; it can't hold your work. There's an empty room here that can be turned into your second studio. You can go back to your place whenever you don't want to stay—but at least you'll have a place that's yours."
Su Qingyuan looked at him for a long while. "Lu Shiyan, is this an invitation to move in together?"
"Yes."
"Last time you proposed, this time you want to live together. Is the next step supposed to be a wedding?"
Lu Shiyan laughed. "If you want, then yes."
Su Qingyuan turned her face away, looking out at the night view. "I didn't say I wanted to."
"Then you don't want to."
"I didn't say I didn't want to either."
Lu Shiyan looked at the reddened tips of her ears and laughed. "So are you moving in or not?"
Su Qingyuan was silent for a few seconds. "I'll move in."
"Tomorrow?"
"The day after. I have to pack up tomorrow."
"All right. The day after, I'll come pick you up."
Su Qingyuan nodded, then turned and walked to the bed, pulled back the covers, and slipped underneath. The blanket was soft, carrying the fresh scent of laundry detergent, the kind of smell she liked. She rolled over, her back to him. After a while, the other side of the bed sank down, and he turned off the light.
In the darkness, Su Qingyuan lay with her eyes open, watching the moonlight that filtered through the gap in the curtains. "Lu Shiyan, are you asleep?"
"No."
"Tell me, will we fight in the future?"
"We will."
"How do you know?"
"Because when two people are together, they always end up fighting. Even teeth and lips bicker—how much more two people."
Su Qingyuan rolled over to face him. In the dark she couldn't make out his face, only the silhouette of his outline. "Then what do we do when we fight?"
"Make up."
"How do we make up?"
"However you want to make up, that's how we'll make up. If you don't want to make up, I'll wait for you. Once your temper cools, I'll come find you again."
Su Qingyuan reached out, feeling through the darkness until she found his hand and held it. "Lu Shiyan, tell me, why are you so good to me?"
"Because I have you."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, holding his hand, and sank into a deep sleep.
On moving day, Lu Shiyan came to help her pack. Su Qingyuan didn't have much—clothes, books, sketchbooks, carving knives, a few half-finished pieces. The heaviest was that box of sketchbooks, from her university days until now, dozens of them, every one filled to the margins.
"All of these are coming?" Lu Shiyan looked at the box of sketchbooks.
"All of them."
"Then they're coming." He hoisted up the box. "Go take a look and see if anything's been missed."
Su Qingyuan made a round through the apartment. The pots and pans in the kitchen, the chamomile on the table, the moth orchid on the windowsill, the key rack in the entryway. Every single thing held a memory, but she couldn't carry all of those memories away. She took only a few—her favorite cup, the scarf Lu Shiyan had given her, the pot of moth orchid from the windowsill. The rest she left behind, for whoever moved in next.
"Let's go," she said.
Lu Shiyan carried the box, she held the moth orchid, and the two of them walked out the door. Su Qingyuan glanced back at the apartment she'd lived in for so long—empty now, sunlight pouring in through the windows and falling across the floor, bright enough to dazzle. She closed the door.
The car was parked downstairs at Lu Shiyan's building. Su Qingyuan pushed the door open and got out, looking at the familiar tower, and suddenly a strange feeling rose in her heart—not unfamiliarity, but at last.
"From now on this is your home too," Lu Shiyan said, standing beside her, still holding the box.
"Mm. My home."
It took them several trips to move everything in. Lu Shiyan had turned an empty room into her second studio; the workbench was newly bought, identical to the one in her south-city studio. The display cases, the tool racks, the lighting system—all of it arranged the way she liked.
Su Qingyuan stood in that little studio, looking at the familiar setup, warmth spreading through her chest.
"Do you like it?" Lu Shiyan stood in the doorway.
"I like it."
"Then from now on it's yours."
Su Qingyuan turned, walked over, rose onto her toes, and kissed him on the lips. "Thank you."
"You're welcome."
On the third day after moving into Lu Shiyan's home, Su Qingyuan received an email from Venice. The Venice Biennale jury committee was formally notifying her that her "Roots" series had been selected for the Biennale's "Thematic Exhibition" section. This wasn't a parallel exhibition, nor a fringe one—it was the Biennale's most central, most closely watched thematic show. The artists selected for the thematic exhibition were all among the world's very best. She was one of them.
After reading the email, Su Qingyuan set her phone face-down on the desk, slumped over the workbench in her studio, shoulders trembling faintly. Not crying—overcome.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "See it?"
Su Qingyuan: "How did you know?"
Lu Shiyan: "Cartier told me. They have a partner in Venice who saw the list."
Su Qingyuan: "Since when have you been so close with Cartier?"
Lu Shiyan: "Since the day you signed the contract. Anything to do with you, I want to know."
Su Qingyuan's lips curved up. She picked up her phone and wrote back: "Dinner's on me tonight. Really on me—the kind where I don't let you pay."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. You pick the place."
Su Qingyuan: "Hotpot. The usual spot."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. I'll come get you."
In the evening, Lu Shiyan came to pick her up. The car stopped outside that Hunan restaurant—the red lanterns still just as red, the "signature chopped-chili fish head" ad on the glass window still just as eye-catching. Su Qingyuan pushed the door open, got out, took his arm, and walked in.
Still the same private room, still the same table, still the same pot of red oil rolling at a boil. Su Qingyuan blanched a slice of tripe—seven dips up, eight dips down—and put it in her mouth, sucking in air at the spice but unwilling to spit it out. "Delicious."
Lu Shiyan looked at her lips, gone red from the spice, his gaze as soft as moonlight. "Su Qingyuan."
"Mm?"
"The Venice Biennale—I'll go with you."
"Don't you have to work?"
"Work can come with a laptop."
Su Qingyuan set down her chopsticks and looked at him. "Lu Shiyan, you didn't used to be like this. You used to put work first."
"I didn't have you before."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head and went on eating hotpot, her ears red.
The Venice Biennale was in June. Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan arrived in Venice three days early, staying at a small hotel beside an old canal. Open the room's window and there was the water; gondolas glided past below, the boatmen's songs drifting across the surface.
"Beautiful?" Lu Shiyan stood behind her.
"Beautiful." Su Qingyuan looked out at the canal. "But thinking about setting up the exhibition tomorrow makes me a little nervous."
"No need to be nervous. Your work belongs here."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "How is it you say that every single time?"
"Because every single time, that's how I feel."
Su Qingyuan laughed, rose onto her toes, and kissed him on the cheek. "Come on, let's go out and walk around. We're here, after all."
They strolled slowly along the canal. Venice's water lanes were narrow and deep, sunlight slipping down through the gaps between buildings to fall on the water and shatter into a sheet of golden light. Su Qingyuan walked very slowly, because she wanted to remember every little alley, every little bridge, every streetlamp. Maybe one day she'd turn them into jewelry.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Tell me, what kind of place will we live in someday?"
"What kind of place do you want to live in?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it. "One with a courtyard, with an osmanthus tree planted in it. In autumn the whole yard would be sweet. With a big studio, windows facing south, plenty of sun. And a kitchen—it has to be big, because you like to cook."
Lu Shiyan looked at her and smiled. "All right. We'll live in a place just like that."
Su Qingyuan's lips curved up, and she kept walking, the flagstone road beneath her feet worn smooth by the years, refracting the light of the setting sun. She suddenly felt that the future was far away, yet every step landed on solid ground.
The thematic exhibition hall of the Venice Biennale was in the Giardini park. Su Qingyuan's "Roots" series had been placed in a small standalone gallery—not large, but in an excellent spot, the second gallery from the entrance, so everyone passing through would see it. Setting up took two days; the placement angle of every piece, the direction of light from every lamp, she adjusted all of it herself. Lu Shiyan couldn't help with the work, so he sat on the bench outside the gallery waiting for her, his laptop open, handling emails now and then, glancing up at her now and then.
Once the last piece was in place, Su Qingyuan stood in the center of the gallery and turned a full circle. Five works glowed with a soft light under the lamps—every gem, every line of texture, every detail clearly visible.
"Done?" Lu Shiyan walked in.
"Done."
"Then it's all on tomorrow."
"Mm. On tomorrow."
On opening day, the crowd was large. Su Qingyuan stood in the gallery, watching the people come and go—some stopped to look at her work, some took photos, some discussed in low voices. She heard people speaking Italian, people speaking English, people speaking languages she couldn't understand. But she could read their expressions—some were nodding, some were smiling, some read the text on the placard, then looked up at the work again, as if searching for something.
A white-haired old lady stood before the "Veins" necklace for a long time, then turned and said something to Su Qingyuan in Italian. Su Qingyuan didn't understand, and the interpreter beside her said, "She says this piece reminds her of her grandmother. Her grandmother's hands had a lot of bulging veins too, like tree roots. She used to think they were unsightly; now she thinks they were the most beautiful."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot. She walked over and took the old lady's hand. "Thank you."
The old lady held her hand and patted it, then said a few more words in Italian. The interpreter smiled. "She says: young one, your work has a soul. Keep doing this, and do it well."
Su Qingyuan nodded.
After the Venice Biennale ended, the "Roots" series won the thematic exhibition's Special Mention award. Not the top prize, but for a jewelry designer attending the Biennale for the first time, it was already an extremely high honor. Su Qingyuan didn't go up to accept it; she had the curator accept it on her behalf. Because she felt the "Roots" series didn't need her standing on a stage—it would speak for itself.
By the time they got back to Nancheng, it was already the end of June. Summer had come; the leaves of the osmanthus tree were greener now, a green too thick to dissolve. Su Qingyuan stood at the studio window, looking at that osmanthus tree, and remembered last autumn, when she'd just moved into this studio and the tree was covered in blossoms, the fragrance sweet to the point of being cloying. Back then she and Lu Shiyan hadn't been together long; everything was just beginning, everything was unknown. Now, the osmanthus tree was still here, the studio was still here, and he was here too.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "What do you want to eat tonight?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it and typed two words: "Dumplings."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. I'll come get you. We'll make them together."
Su Qingyuan's lips curved up. She slipped her phone into her pocket, locked the door, and walked out of the studio. The sun was lovely, falling on her shoulders, warm. She drew in a deep breath and let it out. The summer air was hot, carrying the green, unripe scent of osmanthus leaves.
She walked out of the creative park and waited for the car at the intersection. In the distance, a black car was driving toward her.
She knew—that was her light.
