Roots and Light, the Day They Bloom
On the morning the joint exhibition opened, a thin snow fell over Nancheng.
Su Qingyuan stood at the floor-to-ceiling windows of the Lu Group lobby, watching the fine flakes drift down through the morning light. The snow was light, melting the instant it touched the ground, but that hushed, white-washed atmosphere made the whole city look like an ink-wash painting. She wore a black long-sleeved dress with that smoke-gray cashmere coat over it, her hair pinned up to reveal the pair of "First Snow" earrings. She had on no other jewelry, because today the star wasn't her—it was her work.
"Nervous?" Lu Shiyan's voice came from behind her. Su Qingyuan turned to see him standing at the lobby entrance in a black suit and a dark gray tie, looking even more nobly aloof than usual. But his hands were tucked into his trouser pockets, his fingertips curled slightly—he was nervous. Nervous for her.
"A little," Su Qingyuan admitted. "But more than that, I'm looking forward to it."
Lu Shiyan walked over and stood in front of her, bending his head to straighten the collar of her coat. "A lot of people will come today. Reporters, critics, collectors, people from the industry. Whatever they say, don't take it to heart. Your work—you know exactly what it's worth."
Su Qingyuan looked at his earnest expression and smiled. "Are you giving me a pep talk?"
"Yes."
"When did you learn how to do that?"
"From the day I met you. I learn a little more every day."
Su Qingyuan reached out, laughing, and adjusted his tie. "Your tie's crooked."
Lu Shiyan looked down at her hand, the corner of his mouth lifting. "Thank you."
At nine in the morning, the guests began to arrive.
The first was Zhou Wanyin. She wore a dark green gown and carried a bouquet of white chamomile, and the moment she spotted Su Qingyuan she hurried over and pressed the flowers into her arms. "These are for you. An opening gift. I didn't buy them—I grew them myself."
Su Qingyuan looked down at the bouquet, the tiny blossoms crowded together, clean and warm. "Sister Wanyin, when did you start growing flowers?"
"After you went to Paris. I had nothing to do, so I grew them for fun." Zhou Wanyin smiled. "Turns out they grew rather well. Just like you—give them a little sunlight and they bloom."
Su Qingyuan laughed, her eyes growing a little hot. She set the flowers down on the sign-in table and turned to link her arm through Zhou Wanyin's. "Come on, let me show you around."
In the exhibition hall, Qu Ya was already greeting the first batch of guests. Today she wore a white shirt and black wide-leg trousers, her hair cut short, looking crisp and capable. Seeing Zhou Wanyin, she came over with a smile. "Wanyin, long time no see."
"Sister Ya, you look wonderful today."
"None of that flattery." Qu Ya laughed and gave Zhou Wanyin a light pat, then turned to Su Qingyuan. "Qingyuan, Secretary-General Bai is here."
Su Qingyuan followed her gaze. Secretary-General Bai was standing before the first piece of the "Roots" series, bent at the waist, peering at the tree-ring brooch through a magnifying glass. His expression was intent, his brow slightly furrowed, as if he were puzzling over some profound question.
Su Qingyuan walked over. "Secretary-General Bai, welcome."
Secretary-General Bai straightened, lowered the magnifying glass, and looked at her. "Miss Su, the tree-ring section of this piece—you used six kinds of gemstones?"
"Yes. At the very center is a dark brown diamond, then moving outward, in order: deep green emerald, golden sapphire, pale yellow diamond, white sapphire, and the outermost layer is a clear diamond."
"Each kind of gem represents one ring of growth?"
"That's right. From origin to growth, from struggle to settling, from settling to protection. The story of a tree is also the story of a person."
Secretary-General Bai was silent for a few seconds, then nodded. "Miss Su, about the venue earlier—that was an oversight on my part. Having seen your work today, I take back what I said before. Your work deserves to be shown anywhere."
Warmth bloomed in Su Qingyuan's chest. "Secretary-General Bai, thank you."
Secretary-General Bai waved a hand and turned to go on to the next piece.
By ten in the morning, the hall was growing more and more crowded. Su Qingyuan stood in the "Roots and Light" intersection zone—a space designed specifically to stage a dialogue between her work and Qu Ya's. On the left was Qu Ya's photograph: the root base of a great tree, thick roots driven deep into the earth, like an old man's hands, like the veins of time. On the right was Su Qingyuan's fourth "Roots" series piece—a necklace called "Veins," woven from antiqued silver and bright gemstones, like roots spreading underground, like blood flowing through the body.
The photograph and the necklace placed side by side—one the roots of nature, one the "roots" within the human body—the same theme, expressed differently. But set together, they seemed to be in conversation.
A collector stood before this zone for a long while, then turned to ask Su Qingyuan, "Miss Su, is this necklace for sale?"
Su Qingyuan shook her head. "The 'Roots' series is not for sale. These are pieces I made for myself. They're not on the market."
The collector was taken aback for a moment, then smiled. "What a pity. But I understand—some pieces are kept to warm yourself by."
Su Qingyuan nodded. Yes—the "Roots" series was kept to warm herself by.
At eleven in the morning, the reporter Lin Xiaoxi appeared in the hall. She wore a red dress and held a recording pen, a photographer trailing behind her. The instant Su Qingyuan saw her, she remembered the sharp questions on the phone that day, and her chest tightened slightly—but nothing showed on her face.
"Miss Su, congratulations." Lin Xiaoxi came over with a professional smile. "The exhibition is a great success."
"Thank you."
"Could I interview you for a few minutes?"
"Of course."
They moved to a quiet corner of the hall. Lin Xiaoxi switched on her recording pen and asked a few standard questions—creative inspiration, the theme of the joint exhibition, how it felt to collaborate with Qu Ya. Su Qingyuan answered each one, neither haughty nor humble.
Then Lin Xiaoxi changed tack. "Miss Su, there's a tip going around online that you switched venues not because the art museum was requisitioned, but because the museum thought your work wasn't 'up to standard.' How do you respond to that?"
Su Qingyuan looked into Lin Xiaoxi's eyes. In those eyes there was curiosity, there was probing, and there was a faint, barely perceptible—excitement. Like a hunter who had finally cornered her prey.
Su Qingyuan didn't shy away from that gaze. She said calmly, "Reporter Lin, you say there's a tip going around online. Can you tell me who tipped you off? Is there any evidence?"
Lin Xiaoxi hesitated. "This… the source is anonymous."
"Then there's no evidence." Su Qingyuan's voice was soft but perfectly clear. "I won't respond to a tip with no evidence. But I can tell you one thing—among the guests here today are Mr. Jean-Pierre, creative director of Cartier headquarters; the editor-in-chief of Vogue's jewelry section; and Madame Isabelle Mollier, chair of the Biennale judging committee. They didn't come because of any connection to me—they came because of my work. If my work weren't 'up to standard,' they wouldn't be here."
Lin Xiaoxi's expression froze for a moment. She clearly hadn't expected Su Qingyuan to push back so directly, and to invoke names like Jean-Pierre and Isabelle—top-tier figures in the industry. The weight of those names was heavier than any defense.
"Reporter Lin, if you want to write a piece about the joint exhibition, you're welcome to view it carefully and write something objective, something with depth. But if you're just looking for a gimmick, digging for dirt, then you've come to the wrong person." Su Qingyuan paused. "My work doesn't need dirt to set it off. And your article doesn't need a gimmick to draw eyes."
Lin Xiaoxi's color changed. From red to white, from white to red. She opened her mouth as if to say something, but in the end nothing came out. She switched off her recording pen and nodded. "Miss Su, I understand. Thank you for the interview."
She turned and left. The photographer shouldered his equipment and followed, glancing back at Su Qingyuan once, his eyes holding both sympathy and admiration.
Su Qingyuan stood where she was, watching Lin Xiaoxi's back vanish into the crowd, feeling no anger—only a strange calm. Lin Xiaoxi wasn't a bad person; she was just a reporter trying to stand out in fierce competition, who had chosen the easiest route—digging up dirt. But Su Qingyuan wasn't her dirt. Su Qingyuan was her blind spot—a person with no dirt to dig up, who let her work speak for itself.
At noon, the guests moved to the adjacent lounge area for lunch. Su Qingyuan didn't go to eat; she held a glass of water and stood in a corner of the hall, watching the people come and go. They stood before her work—some nodding, some smiling, some frowning, some murmuring to their companions. She took in every expression.
"Not going to eat?" Qu Ya came over, holding a plate of sandwiches.
"Not hungry."
"Too nervous to eat?"
Su Qingyuan smiled. "Maybe."
Qu Ya handed her the sandwich. "Eat something. There are more important guests this afternoon."
"Who?"
"The director of the Municipal Bureau of Culture, and a few Nancheng officials." Qu Ya's tone was flat, as if she were mentioning something trivial. "They heard the joint exhibition was being held at Lu Group headquarters and wanted to come take a look."
Su Qingyuan was startled. The director of the Municipal Bureau of Culture? Nancheng officials? People you couldn't get to come even if you begged—and today they were asking to come of their own accord?
"Sister Ya, did you invite them?"
"No. Lu Shiyan did." Qu Ya watched Su Qingyuan's expression and smiled. "But not by pulling strings. It's because this exhibition is worth seeing. Lu Shiyan just put in a word; they looked at the materials and decided to come on their own."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head and took a bite of the sandwich. The bread was a little dry, the ham a little salty, but she chewed it carefully, because Qu Ya had handed it to her.
At two in the afternoon, the director of the Municipal Bureau of Culture and several officials arrived. Su Qingyuan received them personally, walking them through the entire hall and explaining in detail the creative concept and craftsmanship of every piece. The director was a woman in her fifties, short-haired, without makeup, with a capable air. She stood for a long time before the fifth piece of the "Roots" series—a necklace called "Source," whose main stone was an uncut, raw gemstone that kept its natural cracks and inclusions.
"Miss Su, this piece—why isn't it cut?"
"Because 'Source' means the beginning. At the beginning, none of us is perfect. This stone isn't perfect, but it's the most truthful. I wanted to preserve that truth."
The director was silent for a few seconds, then turned to look at her. "Miss Su, your work makes me think of one word—'sincerity.'"
The word seared Su Qingyuan.
"Sincerity. Not technique, not splendor—it's taking yourself out and laying yourself bare for others to see." The director paused. "Your work has that."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot, but she didn't cry. She nodded. "Thank you."
As the director left, she shook Su Qingyuan's hand and said, "Keep it up," then turned and went. Su Qingyuan stood at the lobby door, watching the black sedan drive out of the parking lot and disappear around the corner. She looked down at her hand—the one the director had just shaken—and the warmth of the other woman's palm still lingered on it.
At four in the afternoon, the guests began to leave one after another. The hall gradually grew quiet, with only a few staff left tidying up the materials. Su Qingyuan stood alone in the center of the hall, looking at the pieces, the lighting, the empty aisles, and was suddenly seized by a feeling of unreality.
"Is it over?" she murmured to herself.
"It's over." Qu Ya came up behind her, holding two glasses of champagne. "But it's also a beginning."
She handed one glass to Su Qingyuan. "Congratulations, Qingyuan. The joint exhibition was a success."
Su Qingyuan took the champagne and touched it to Qu Ya's glass. "Congratulations to us."
The two of them drank, the champagne bubbles rising in the glasses like countless tiny stars.
"Sister Ya, how do you think today went?"
"Very well." Qu Ya looked at the pieces in the hall. "But tomorrow will be better. And the day after, better still. Because people will keep coming to this exhibition."
Su Qingyuan nodded. Yes—today was only the beginning. Over the coming month, this hall would welcome more and more visitors. Their work would be seen, discussed, and remembered by more people.
"Qingyuan, I'm heading off. It's been a long day—I'm going home to soak in a bath." Qu Ya set down her glass and patted Su Qingyuan's shoulder. "You should head home and rest too. There's still another day tomorrow."
"Okay. Sister Ya, thank you for all your hard work today."
"You too."
Qu Ya left. Only Su Qingyuan and a few staff remained in the hall. She walked to the "Roots and Light" intersection zone and stood for a long time between the photograph of the great tree and the "Veins" necklace.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "Done?"
Su Qingyuan: "Done. Where are you?"
Lu Shiyan: "Eighty-eighth floor. Come up?"
Su Qingyuan: "Okay."
She took the elevator up to the eighty-eighth floor. The instant the doors opened, she saw Lu Shiyan standing at the end of the corridor, in front of that pair of dark wooden double doors. He had changed clothes—no longer in the formal black suit from this morning, but in a dark gray cashmere sweater and black trousers. He looked far softer.
"You've worked hard today," he said.
"It wasn't hard." Su Qingyuan walked over. "Your venue was very useful."
"You're welcome. It was nothing."
Su Qingyuan smiled and followed him into the office. Beyond the floor-to-ceiling windows, dusk was settling over Nancheng, an orange-red glow spilling across the entire sky. In the distance, the creative park, the Cartier building, the Hunan restaurant, the art museum, Qu Ya's studio, her apartment, his home—the whole city spread out beneath them, like a vast, living painting.
"Lu Shiyan, the sunset's beautiful today."
"Mm. Just like you."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. The sunset fell across his face, his profile especially gentle in the dusk. "When did you learn to say things like that?"
"From the day I met you. I learn a little more every day."
Su Qingyuan laughed and leaned against his shoulder, watching the sunset beyond the window. "Lu Shiyan, do you think we'll always be like this from now on?"
"Like what?"
"Watching sunsets together. Eating together. Doing lots of boring things together."
Lu Shiyan lowered his head, his lips pressed to the crown of her hair. "We will. Always."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, feeling his warmth. Beyond the window, the sun sank slowly, the sky shifting from orange-red to deep purple, from deep purple to inky blue. The city's lights came on one by one, joining into a warm sea of light.
She suddenly remembered a long time ago—a rainy night, when she'd stood at the roadside, not knowing where she was meant to go. Now she knew. The place she was meant to go was wherever he was.
On the third day, the joint exhibition welcomed its first weekend.
The crowds were even larger than on opening day. The hall was packed—jewelry enthusiasts who'd traveled in from out of town, photography students, young parents with their children, white-haired old couples. Su Qingyuan didn't make an appearance; she stood at the floor-to-ceiling windows on the eighty-eighth floor, watching the people queuing below to get in, and felt something very strange in her heart—not pride, but emotion.
These people were complete strangers to her. They were willing to spend their time, their money, their energy to come see her work. Not because she was famous, but because her work had moved them.
"Not going down to take a look?" Lu Shiyan stood beside her.
"No. Let them look in peace." Su Qingyuan turned to look at his office. "You're not working today?"
"It's the weekend."
"You work on weekends too."
"Not today."
Su Qingyuan looked at him and smiled. "Because I'm coming over?"
"Mm."
Su Qingyuan walked over and kissed him on the cheek. "Thank you."
Lu Shiyan touched the spot where she'd kissed him, the corner of his mouth lifting. "You're welcome."
They sat on the sofa, drinking coffee, looking out at the city. Below were the queuing crowds; above were two quiet people. One above, one below, one moving, one still—like two parallel worlds.
But Su Qingyuan knew these two worlds were connected. Because her work was the bridge that joined them.
In the second week of the exhibition, Su Qingyuan received a letter from Australia. The envelope bore an Australian stamp, the handwriting crooked and uneven—obviously written by Su Yurou.
She opened the letter; inside was only a photograph. It showed a beach, blue sky and emerald sea, with a line of footprints on the white sand winding forward, no end in sight. On the back of the photo was a single line: "A beach in Melbourne. No auroras, but the sunshine is lovely. I think you'd like it. —Yurou"
Su Qingyuan looked at that line for a long time. She put the photograph in a drawer, locked away with those old photos, the USB drive, the envelope. Then she picked up her phone and sent Su Yurou a message—not a reply, but a new conversation: "The Melbourne beach is lovely. The auroras are up north; you can't see them from where you are. If you want to see them, go to Norway. But the sunshine in the Southern Hemisphere is lovely too. Each has its own beauty."
After sending it, she put down her phone and went back to work. The sunlight beyond the window was lovely, falling on her hands, on the gemstone taking shape there, on the five completed pieces of the "Roots" series.
Everything was moving forward. So was she.
In the third week of the exhibition, Su Qingyuan got a call from Jean-Pierre.
"Miss Su, the global tour of the 'Light Traces' collection—the first stop in Paris had an excellent response. New York can hardly wait; they're asking whether we can move it up."
"Move it up to when?"
"March next year."
Su Qingyuan thought it over. "That works. No problem on my end."
"Then it's settled. March, New York." Jean-Pierre paused. "Miss Su, one more thing. Cartier wants to sign a long-term guest artist agreement with you—not one season a year, but three years. One collection each year, led entirely by you."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, her heart racing. "And the split?"
"Sixty for you, forty for Cartier. Same as before."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a few seconds. "Jean-Pierre, I accept. But on one condition."
"What condition?"
"The 'Roots' series—I want to add a stop to the tour. A China stop. Not Shanghai, not Beijing—Nancheng. Because the roots of the 'Roots' series are in Nancheng."
Jean-Pierre was silent for a moment, then laughed. "Miss Su, you're the first designer ever to make such a condition to Cartier. But I'll grant it. Because the 'Roots' series deserves to be shown on its own soil."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan stood at the window, looking at the osmanthus tree outside. Spring hadn't arrived yet; the branches were still bare. But she could see that the tiny, tender green buds had already, quietly, begun to swell.
Spring was coming.
On the last day of the exhibition, Su Qingyuan went back to the hall once more. Not to work—to say goodbye.
There weren't many people in the hall, since it was a weekday. She walked alone among the pieces, looking at her five works and Qu Ya's twenty photographs, with so much she wanted to say but no one to say it to. At last she stopped at the "Roots and Light" intersection zone, looking at the photograph of the great tree and the "Veins" necklace.
"Thank you," she said softly. "For walking this stretch of road with me."
The exhibition ran for a month, drew over fifty thousand visitors, and garnered more than three hundred media reports, with industry reviews almost entirely positive. One critic wrote a long piece titled "Roots and Light: Su Qingyuan and Her 'Nonfiction' Jewelry." The last line of the article read: "Su Qingyuan's work isn't telling a fictional, perfect story. She's telling her own story—imperfect, but true. And truth is the scarcest thing of this era."
After reading that article, Su Qingyuan saved it. Not on her phone—in her heart.
That evening, Lu Shiyan came to pick her up. The car didn't head toward her apartment; it headed somewhere else—the tallest building in Nancheng, his home.
"We're going to your place?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"Mm. There's something I want to show you."
The elevator rose; the doors opened. Lu Shiyan went in first, Su Qingyuan following, changing into that pair of pale pink fuzzy slippers. The living room lights were still that warm yellow, white chamomile still sat on the coffee table, and the pale beige throw still lay draped over the sofa. Nothing had changed—but Su Qingyuan noticed that there was now a small box on the coffee table. Black, velvet, the kind that held jewelry.
"What's this?"
"Open it and see."
Su Qingyuan picked up the box and opened it. Inside was a necklace—not one she had designed. The chain was fine white gold, and the pendant was a small moonstone, cut in the technique she knew best—the Ripple Cut. The halo radiated outward from the core ring by ring, three graded layers: deep blue on the outside, silver-white in the middle, warm gold within. Exactly like the moonstone in her "Daybreak."
Su Qingyuan's hands trembled. "This is…"
"I had it made. Using your technique." Lu Shiyan's voice was low. "I asked Director Chen of Zhenyi to help. She said it was the hardest necklace she'd ever made. Because the parameters of the Ripple Cut are yours alone—she didn't dare ask you for them, so she tried it herself. She tried for two months and ruined over a hundred moonstones before she made this one."
Su Qingyuan's tears fell.
"Su Qingyuan, this necklace is called 'Light.'" Lu Shiyan lifted the necklace from the box, stepped around behind her, and fastened it for her. His fingers brushed the skin at the back of her neck, cool, but her heart was burning.
"Why 'Light'?" she asked, her voice a little hoarse.
"Because you're my light." Lu Shiyan fastened the clasp, came back around in front of her, and looked down at her. "From the glass door until now, you always have been."
Su Qingyuan reached up to touch the moonstone at her throat, her vision blurred with tears. "Lu Shiyan, you really are insufferable."
"Mm."
"You pull this same trick every time."
"As long as it works."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. She rose onto her toes, wrapped both arms around his neck, and kissed him.
Beyond the window, Nancheng's night view spread out beneath them, ten thousand household lights joining into a warm sea of light. In the distance, somewhere she couldn't see, her studio was still there, the osmanthus tree was still there, the five pieces of the "Roots" series were still there.
But she knew that tomorrow she would carry new work, new stories, a new self, and keep moving forward.
Because the light was ahead. And he was in the light.
