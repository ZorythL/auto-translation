Chapter 23 — Before the First Snow, Before the Storm

The design work for Cartier's capsule collection turned out to be more complicated than Su Qingyuan had anticipated.
It wasn't the difficulty of the creation itself—her inspiration had never once run dry—but the friction between the brand's requirements and her own personal style. On the third day after the contract was signed, Anna sent over a detailed set of design guidelines, an exhaustive document running more than a dozen pages, covering everything from gemstone selection to formal language, from price range to target demographic, each dimension annotated with explicit "recommendations."
Su Qingyuan read through the guidelines and fell silent for a long while.
The guidelines themselves weren't wrong. Cartier was a hundred-year-old house, and every set of its design guidelines was the distilled product of countless market figures, consumer insights, and accumulated brand equity. Following them would minimize market risk to the greatest possible degree. But the problem was this—she wasn't a Cartier employee, she was a guest artist. And the value of a guest artist lay precisely in bringing the brand something it didn't already have, not in replicating what it already possessed.
She set the guidelines aside, picked up her sketchbook, and laid the tip of her pencil against the paper—but she didn't rush to draw. Instead she closed her eyes for a moment.
She was turning over a single question: had Cartier come to her because she could draw a rose in the Cartier style, or because she could draw a rose that only Su Qingyuan could draw?
The answer was obvious.
She opened her eyes and began to draw.
By the time the first sketch was finished, the sky outside the window had already gone dark. She looked at the lines on the paper—lines that resembled neither the traditional Cartier nor entirely her own "First Light" style—hesitated for a few seconds, then snapped a photo with her phone and sent it to Anna, along with a single line:
"This is my direction. It may not fully align with the guidelines. But I believe this is the reason Cartier came to me."
After sending it, she set the phone face-down on the table and kept drawing. She didn't like waiting for replies; in the time spent waiting, she chose to work.
An hour later, Anna's reply arrived. Not text—a voice message. Su Qingyuan tapped it open. There was an emotion in Anna's voice she had never heard from her before—not satisfaction, not dissatisfaction, but something stirred, something faintly taut.
"Ms. Su, this direction of yours—I don't understand it."
Su Qingyuan paused.
"I don't mean that in a bad way." Anna was speaking much more slowly than usual, as though weighing each word. "I've worked at Cartier for fifteen years. I've seen the portfolios of countless designers, and I've never seen anything like this. It doesn't look like Cartier—but it makes me feel that Cartier should become this. I need a little time to take it in. Give me two days."
Su Qingyuan listened, and the corner of her mouth curved.
"I don't understand it" might be an even higher compliment than "it's beautiful." Because "beautiful" was within expectation, while "I don't understand it" was beyond it. And what lay beyond expectation often meant new possibility.
She didn't wait two days. The next morning, Anna's call came.
"Headquarters has seen your sketch." Anna's tone was completely different from the day before, carrying an excitement she couldn't quite suppress. "The creative director said one thing—'Let her draw. Don't put any limits on her.' Ms. Su, you've won."
Su Qingyuan held the phone, listening to Anna's voice, and a scalding wave of emotion welled up in her chest.
It wasn't "won against Cartier." It was "won against doing things strictly by the book." She had proven that a designer's greatest value lay not in obedience, but in creation.
"Thank you, Anna. And thank headquarters for their trust."
"Thank me for what? This is your own talent." Anna paused, then laughed softly. "Oh, right—headquarters asked, does the capsule collection have a name? If not, they'd like to call it 'Light Traces'—your light, Cartier's traces. What do you think?"
Su Qingyuan repeated the name silently to herself. Light Traces. Light was her core language; trace was the byword for Cartier's century of craftsmanship. Two words, writing two names side by side.
"Perfect. Let's call it 'Light Traces.'"
Hanging up, Su Qingyuan stood at the window, gazing out at the bare osmanthus tree, and let out a long breath. The white mist of her breath condensed into a small cloud in the cold air, then scattered. She suddenly realized—winter in Nancheng was on its way.
Her phone buzzed again. This time it wasn't Anna.
Lu Shiyan: [Something's come up with the bid. I may not be able to pick you up tonight. The driver will take you home. Stay safe.]
Su Qingyuan looked at the message, her fingers tightening slightly. The trouble with the bid—she didn't need to ask to know who it was tied to: Gu Changfeng. She replied: [Okay. You handle things, I'll be fine. Tell me when it's over.]
Lu Shiyan replied with a single word: [Mm.]
That "Mm" left her faintly uneasy. Lu Shiyan was never a man who made her feel "uneasy." He was always certain, always composed, always making her feel everything was firmly under control. But today that "Mm" lacked the certainty, the warmth, he usually carried. Su Qingyuan set down her phone, paced two laps around the studio, then picked up her bag and decided to head home on her own. No need to trouble the driver.
By the time she stepped out of the creative park, the sky had gone completely black. The late-autumn night wind poured down her collar, cold enough to make her hunch her neck. The street outside the park was nearly empty of pedestrians, only a few streetlamps glowing in lonely isolation.
She was just about to head toward the intersection when a black van suddenly pulled to a stop in front of her.
The door slid open, and someone sat inside.
Su Yurou.
Su Qingyuan halted, looking at that gaunt, pale face in the car, the obsessive cold light at the bottom of those eyes. She didn't step back, and she didn't panic.
"Su Qingyuan, get in. Let's talk." Su Yurou's voice was very calm—calm to the point of being eerie.
"No." Su Qingyuan's tone was equally calm. "If you have something to say, say it. If not, I'm leaving."
Su Yurou looked at her, then suddenly smiled. That smile reminded Su Qingyuan of when they were children—it was the exact expression Su Yurou wore every time, right before she put on her pitiful act in front of the adults. First the smile, then the rims of her eyes reddening, then the tears falling, and then everyone deciding that Su Qingyuan had bullied her.
"You're still like this. You never give me a chance." Su Yurou's voice softened, carrying a note of self-mockery. "Ever since we were little, you've been better than me at everything. Better at drawing, better at design, even Mom and Dad—adoptive parents though they were—liked you more. Do you know how much I envied you?"
Su Qingyuan said nothing, looking at Su Yurou as though looking at a shadow from long ago, already blurred by time.
"I stole your designs not because I couldn't make my own, but because I wanted you to know—you're no genius either. The things you make, I can make them too." The rims of Su Yurou's eyes reddened, but no tears fell. "But every time, you'd climb back up. Every time I thought you were finished, you'd climb back up again. Su Qingyuan, you really are infuriating."
The night wind blew Su Yurou's hair into disarray. She sat in the car like a caged beast that had been trapped too long and had finally given up the struggle.
"Are you finished?" Su Qingyuan asked.
Su Yurou looked at her, her lips moving as if she wanted to say something, but in the end she only shook her head.
"I'm finished. I'm leaving."
The door slid slowly shut. The black van pulled away into the night, its taillights shrinking into two blurred red dots in the distance.
Su Qingyuan stood beneath the streetlamp, watching the car vanish around the corner, and in her heart there was no hatred, no sympathy—only a strange feeling, something like "turning a page." Su Yurou hadn't come to apologize, nor to provoke. She had come to say one thing—I'm still here. I won't disappear. Don't think that just because you've won, you can forget me.
Su Qingyuan drew back her gaze and turned to walk toward the intersection.
I won't forget you. But I won't let you affect me ever again.
The driver came after all. The one Lu Shiyan had sent—a black sedan, pulling up punctually at the park gate. Su Qingyuan climbed into the back seat, told the driver the address, then leaned back against the seat and sent Lu Shiyan a message.
[Su Yurou just came to see me. At the park gate, in a car. Said a few things, then left. I'm fine.]
This time the reply came fast. Not text—a call.
"What did she say?" There was a tightness in Lu Shiyan's voice.
Su Qingyuan recounted roughly what Su Yurou had said. When he'd finished listening, Lu Shiyan was silent for a few seconds.
"Her coming wasn't Gu Changfeng's idea. It was her own."
"I know." Su Qingyuan said softly. "She came to say goodbye—not to make peace with me, but to wrestle with herself. She just wanted me to know that she's still alive, still breathing, still hasn't admitted defeat."
The other end of the line fell silent again for a few seconds. Then Lu Shiyan said something that made Su Qingyuan's heart burn.
"The bid on my end—the results come tomorrow afternoon. No matter how it turns out, tomorrow evening, we'll have dinner together. Just the two of us."
"Okay."
Hanging up the call, Su Qingyuan gazed at the neon flowing past the car window, and the corner of her mouth slowly curved upward.
No matter how it turns out. What those words meant was—win, and we celebrate together; lose, and we shoulder it together. She liked the word "together."
The next afternoon, Su Qingyuan waited for word in her studio. She didn't ask Lu Shiyan, afraid of disturbing him. But her heart hung suspended the whole time, like a taut string, so tense she couldn't even hold her carving knife steady.
At 3:17, her phone buzzed.
Not Lu Shiyan—a message from Zhou Wanyin:
[Qingyuan! Look at the news! The results of the bid for that plot in the south of the city are out!]
Su Qingyuan tapped open the news link. The headline was in bold black type:
"Bidding for the South City Plot Settled: Lu Group Defeats Dingfeng and Other Rivals to Secure the Land!"
She stared at the headline, her heartbeat suddenly surging, then slowly, slowly settling back into place.
Lu Group had won. He had won.
A few seconds later, Lu Shiyan's message came, just four words:
[Won. Wait for me.]
Su Qingyuan looked at those four words and suddenly felt her eyes go hot. Not because he'd won, but because of "wait for me"—win or lose, he would have come to say "wait for me." Win, and it was to celebrate; lose, and it was to lean on. To him, her being there was the destination.
She replied with one word: [Okay.]
Then she stood up, tidied her worktable clean, changed her clothes, and waited by the window.
Waited for that beam of headlights to light up the studio door.
At dusk, Lu Shiyan's car pulled up punctually beneath the osmanthus tree.
When Su Qingyuan walked out, he was already standing beside the car, suit jacket draped over his arm, shirt cuffs rolled up to his forearms, looking far more relaxed than usual—not slack, but the perfectly measured ease of a man who'd just set down some heavy burden.
When he saw her, he didn't say "I won," didn't say "let's celebrate." He simply pulled open the car door and said one thing: "Get in. I'm taking you somewhere."
Su Qingyuan didn't ask where. She obediently got in.
The car drove out of the creative park, threaded through Nancheng's bustling streets, growing more and more remote, until at last it climbed a mountain road she had never traveled before. The sky had already darkened; the streetlamps along the mountain road were sparse and scattered, the headlights illuminating the road ahead like a single moving shaft of light.
"Where are we going?" Su Qingyuan finally couldn't help asking.
"The summit." Lu Shiyan held the wheel with one hand and reached the other over to clasp hers. "You'll know once we get there."
Twenty minutes later, the car stopped at a scenic overlook at the mountain's summit.
Su Qingyuan pushed open the door and stepped out, and in an instant the sight before her struck her speechless.
All of Nancheng unfurled at her feet, the lights of ten thousand homes merging into a single warm sea of light, the distant river surface reflecting the city's glow like a flowing silver galaxy. Along the skyline, the last trace of sunset had not yet fully faded; the violet-red glow and the city's lights shimmered against each other, beautiful to the point of unreality.
"This place…" Su Qingyuan's voice softened. "How did you find it?"
"Back when the pressure got heavy, I used to drive up here alone." Lu Shiyan stood at her side, his gaze resting on the distant city. "But before, I came alone, at night, looked for a while, then left. Today, I came with two."
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him. The night wind had tousled his hair a little, and the line of his profile looked especially gentle in the city's lights.
"Back when you watched alone, what were you thinking about?" she asked.
"I was thinking—in a city this vast, when would there ever be someone who'd make me want to bring her here." Lu Shiyan turned his head to look at her, the light at the bottom of his eyes brighter than the ten thousand lights below them. "Now there is."
Su Qingyuan's nose stung, but she didn't cry. She leaned against his shoulder, looking at the city beneath their feet, and said softly, "Lu Shiyan, do you think someday we'll become two of those lights too?"
"No." Lu Shiyan lowered his head, his lips pressed to the crown of her hair, his voice muffled. "We'll be two lamps. The kind nestled right against each other."
Su Qingyuan's mouth curved, and she said nothing more.
The wind at the summit was a little strong, but with Lu Shiyan's coat draped over her shoulders, she wasn't cold at all. In the distance, the city's lights grew brighter and brighter, like a field of stars sprouting up from the ground.
They stayed at the summit for a long time—long enough that Su Qingyuan's legs went a little numb—before returning to the car.
On the way down the mountain, Lu Shiyan drove very slowly. Not because the road was bad, but because he didn't want this moment to end too soon.
"The bid—what's the reaction been from Gu Changfeng's side?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"He lost. He won't let it rest." Lu Shiyan's tone returned to the steadiness of his working hours. "But every time he loses, his resources shrink a little more. Dingfeng isn't his alone; there are other shareholders on the board. He's suffered setbacks in both the jewelry sector and the real estate sector now, and the other shareholders have already begun to question his decisions."
"How will he strike back?"
"I don't know." Lu Shiyan's fingers tapped lightly twice against the steering wheel. "But no matter how he strikes back, I'm prepared. And besides—" he glanced sideways at her, "—I have you."
That single glance sent Su Qingyuan's heart racing.
"What good am I? I don't understand corporate warfare."
"You don't need to understand it." Lu Shiyan drew his gaze back, the corner of his mouth lifting slightly. "As long as you're here, I won't lose."
Su Qingyuan turned her face toward the car window, pretending to watch the night scenery outside. Her reflection on the window glass showed a face whose curving lips simply refused to be pressed back down.

The car stopped at the foot of Su Qingyuan's apartment building.
Su Qingyuan didn't get out right away. She sat in the passenger seat, looking at the streetlamps beyond the windshield, and suddenly said, "Lu Shiyan, do you think it'll snow tomorrow?"
Lu Shiyan checked the weather forecast and said, "Tomorrow's overcast, minus two to five degrees. Nancheng's first snow usually doesn't come until December."
"That's still a month away." Su Qingyuan sounded a little disappointed.
"Can't wait?"
"It's not that I can't wait…" She paused, her voice dropping lower. "I just want to watch the first snow with you."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze deep as the night.
He reached out, gently caught her chin, and turned her face toward him. Then he lowered his head and kissed her.
Not a fleeting, tentative kiss—a tender, lingering kiss that carried a promise. It lasted a long time, long enough that they both lost track of the hour.
When they parted, Su Qingyuan's lashes trembled faintly, her lips flushed red, her eyes bright.
"When the first snow comes," Lu Shiyan said, his forehead pressed to hers, his voice low and husky, "we'll watch it together."
"Okay."
Su Qingyuan pushed open the car door, walked two steps, then turned back.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Tonight's view from the summit—it was beautiful."
"Mm."
"But you're more beautiful than the view."
With that she spun around and ran, dashing through the building entrance and vanishing into the stairwell.
Lu Shiyan sat in the car, looking in the direction she'd disappeared, and slowly, slowly began to smile.
He picked up his phone and sent her a message:
[Su Qingyuan, what did you just say? I didn't catch it.]
Su Qingyuan's reply came fast—just one emoji: []
Looking at that emoji, Lu Shiyan laughed out loud. He started the car and drove out of the complex. Outside the window, Nancheng's night wind was chilly, but his heart was warm. From the inside out, from the past to the future, all of it was warm.
Inside the apartment, Su Qingyuan leaned against the door panel, both hands covering her face, her ears red enough to bleed.
What had she just said? You're more beautiful than the view.
God, how had she let that out of her mouth. So unbearably sappy. So unlike her.
But that last message Lu Shiyan had sent—she read it over and over, unable to bear deleting it.
That was the most understated "me too" she could think to give.
Outside the window, Nancheng's night was quiet and deep.
Tomorrow, there would be new challenges. But tonight, there was only a heart pounding away, and a scalding tenderness, caught and held steady.
