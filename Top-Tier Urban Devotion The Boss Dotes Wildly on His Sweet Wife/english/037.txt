Chapter 37 — The Aurora, and His Promise
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626239.html

Three days after the joint exhibition ended, Su Qingyuan was packing up at her studio. Not moving out—packing for a trip to the Nordics. Lu Shiyan had said the matter of the aurora was best done sooner rather than later, and he'd booked tickets for three days out. At the time she'd thought he was joking, right up until he forwarded her the flight details and she realized he was serious.
"You really have the time?" she asked him.
"I took leave."
"You take leave? Aren't you the man who works every day of the year?"
"I used to be. Now I have a reason to take some off."
Su Qingyuan looked at the message, the corners of her mouth curving up, slipped her phone into her pocket, and went back to folding clothes. She packed a lot—a thick down jacket, thermal underwear, wool socks, scarves, gloves, hats. Lu Shiyan messaged that it was minus twenty degrees up north and told her to bundle up. She wrapped herself into a ball to test it, decided it still wasn't enough, and stuffed in two more fleece-lined base layers.
The day they left, it was raining in Nancheng. Su Qingyuan sat in the car, watching the wipers sweep the rain off the windshield again and again, only for it to blur over again and again, and a sense of unreality settled in her chest. A few hours ago she'd still been polishing the samples for the "Roots" series in her studio; now she was off to the Nordics to see the aurora.
"Nervous?" Lu Shiyan asked, hands on the wheel, eyes forward.
"Not nervous," Su Qingyuan said. "It just feels too fast. The exhibition just ended, the Cartier contract was just signed, I haven't even caught my breath."
"Then go before you catch it. Once you do, you'll start thinking, 'I still have so much to do, I can't get away.'"
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him. "How do you know I'd think that?"
"Because that's the kind of person you are," Lu Shiyan said, glancing at her. "You always feel there's work you'll never finish. So I'm taking you away before you decide you 'can't get away.'"
Su Qingyuan said nothing. She turned to look out the window; the rain was coming down harder, the whole city wrapped in a gray curtain of it. But inside her was a patch of clear sky—because he was taking her away.
The plane flew from Nancheng to Helsinki, ten hours. Su Qingyuan leaned on Lu Shiyan's shoulder, watching through the porthole as day turned to night, as clouds gave way to stars. She couldn't sleep; too much was turning over in her head—what to do after the "Roots" series, how to arrange the New York stop of the "Light Traces" touring exhibition, what the theme of the first collection under the three-year Cartier agreement should be. These things churned for a long while, then a hand pressed them gently still. Not a real hand—his voice.
"Stop thinking. Close your eyes. Rest."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes. She thought she wouldn't be able to sleep, but before long she sank into a deep slumber.
They changed planes in Helsinki, bound for Ivalo. The plane got smaller, the noise got louder, and the scenery outside shifted from city to forest, from forest to snowfield, from snowfield to a vast expanse of white. Su Qingyuan pressed against the porthole, looking down at the boundless white below, like one enormous, quiet dream.
"Are we there?" she asked.
"We're here."
Ivalo Airport was small—small enough that there was only a single baggage carousel. Su Qingyuan stood beside the belt waiting for her luggage, watching the cluster of fellow tourists waiting too—they wore brightly colored down jackets, chattering excitedly in English, French, Japanese, every face written over with "here to see the aurora." All at once she felt she was one of them. Not Su Qingyuan the designer, not Su Qingyuan, Lu Shiyan's girlfriend—just an ordinary girl come to see the aurora.
The hotel Lu Shiyan had booked was on the outskirts of Ivalo, a glass-igloo resort. Every room was its own freestanding glass dome; lying in bed, you could see the sky. When Su Qingyuan walked into the room, she stood beneath the glass dome and gazed up for a long time. It wasn't dark yet; the sky was a grayish blue, a few birds flying past, leaving blurred shadows on the glass.
"Lie here tonight and you'll see the aurora," Lu Shiyan said, setting down the suitcase. "If we're lucky."
"And if we're not?"
"Then we look again tomorrow. The aurora's there every day—it's just hidden by clouds sometimes."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "How do you know everything?"
"I looked it up."
"How much did you look up?"
"Everything there is on auroras, starting the day I booked the tickets." Lu Shiyan's tone was casual, as if he were mentioning something trivial.
Su Qingyuan looked at him, and her heart went utterly, hopelessly soft.
That evening they had dinner in the hotel restaurant. Reindeer meat, salmon soup, lingonberry sauce—the flavors were unusual, and Su Qingyuan couldn't say whether they were good or not, but she ate carefully, because this was a meal he was sharing with her.
After dinner they went back to the room and changed into warm clothes. Su Qingyuan put on two layers of fleece base layers, a thick sweater, a down jacket, and a windbreaker over it all. Two layers of scarf, two hats, thick gloves, wool socks and snow boots on her feet. She stood in front of the mirror and looked at herself, and thought she resembled a round, roly-poly penguin.
"Do I look good?" she asked.
"You look good." Lu Shiyan stood behind her in a black down jacket, scarf slung carelessly around his neck, looking more than half as bundled as she was.
"You're not cold?"
"Not cold."
"Liar."
Lu Shiyan laughed, reached out and tugged her hat down to cover her ears. "Let's go."
They stepped out of the hotel into a vast, sweeping snowfield. The sky was completely dark now—no moon, no stars, only the faint glow reflected off the snow. The air was cold enough to cut like a knife; the white breath she exhaled gathered into a cloud of fog before her face and lingered, slow to fade. Su Qingyuan trod through the snow, one foot deep, one foot shallow, the snow over her ankles, crunching with every step.
"Where to?" she asked.
"That way. There's an observation deck—best view of all."
They walked about ten minutes and reached a wooden observation deck. No one else was there, only a few ground lamps casting a faint light. Su Qingyuan stood at the deck's edge, looking out at the horizon in the distance. The sky was very dark—dark as one enormous sheet of velvet, without a trace of light.
"No aurora." She felt a little let down.
"Wait."
They waited a long time. Su Qingyuan's feet went numb with cold and her hands stiffened, but she didn't want to go back—because she was afraid that the moment she did, the aurora would come out. Lu Shiyan pulled two hand warmers from his pocket, tore them open, and tucked them into her gloves. "Put your hands in."
Su Qingyuan slid her hands into the gloves; the warmth of the hand warmers seeped slowly into her palms, warming her from her hands to her heart.
"How do you have everything?"
"Saw it while reading up. They say you should bring hand warmers to see the aurora."
Su Qingyuan looked at him and laughed. "Did you read through the entire internet?"
"More or less."
She leaned on his shoulder, watching the sky. It was still very dark, no aurora, but a single cloud drifted slowly over, shifting shape as the wind moved it. Like a bird, like a fish, like a heart. Su Qingyuan looked at that heart-shaped cloud and the corners of her mouth curved up.
"Lu Shiyan, what if we don't see the aurora today?"
"We come again tomorrow."
"And if we don't see it tomorrow?"
"The day after."
"And if we never see it?"
Lu Shiyan looked down at her, the faint glow off the snow falling on her face; in her eyes there was expectation, and also unease. "Then we keep coming. For ten years, twenty, thirty. We'll see it eventually."
Su Qingyuan's nose stung. She lowered her head and buried her face in her scarf. "You—you're really insufferable."
"Mm."
"You always say things like that."
"Because every time, I mean it."
Su Qingyuan fell silent. She leaned on his shoulder and went on waiting.
They waited a long time. Long enough that she thought they wouldn't see it tonight. And then, at the edge of the sky, a band of green light appeared.
Not the overwhelming, sky-filling green of the photographs—a thin band, like a ribbon of green, stretching from one end of the horizon to the other, flowing slowly through the darkness. Su Qingyuan held her breath, afraid that one exhale and it would vanish. But the light didn't vanish; it grew brighter and brighter, from green into pink, from pink into purple, spreading from the horizon to overhead, like an immense, glowing length of silk drifting slowly across the night sky.
Su Qingyuan's tears fell.
Not tears of sorrow—the kind that comes from waiting a long time and finally, finally being rewarded—scalding tears.
"Lu Shiyan, look."
"Mm. I see it."
She turned to look at him, the aurora's light reflected in his eyes—green, pink, purple—as if the entire universe had been gathered into them.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"You once said that if you could see the aurora with your own eyes one day, you'd have no regrets left in this life."
Su Qingyuan nodded, tears still flowing.
"Now that you've seen it, do you still have any?"
Su Qingyuan wiped her tears, looked at the flowing light in the sky, and thought about it. "One."
"What?"
"You still haven't said you'll spend a lifetime with me."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, the aurora's light falling on his face, his expression tender and certain. He reached out and gently took her hand, fingers interlaced.
"Su Qingyuan, I'll spend a lifetime with you."
"A lifetime of what?"
"A lifetime—telling you good morning every day, telling you good night every day. Asking you every day what you feel like eating, picking you up from work every day. Bringing you to the Nordics once a year to see the aurora, to ride a sled, to stay in a glass igloo. Going to Paris once a year to eat croissants and see the Wall of Love. Making you a bowl of noodles every year on your birthday. They might not taste good, but I'll keep making them—until they do."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. "Is this a proposal?"
"No."
"Then what is it?"
"A promise. A proposal needs a ring. I didn't bring one."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a light punch. "Then bring one next time."
"Okay. Next time."
The aurora flowed slowly overhead, like a glowing river. Su Qingyuan leaned on his shoulder, watching that light, and felt this life had been worth it. Not because she'd seen the aurora—because the person watching it with her was him.
By the time they got back to the hotel, it was very late. Su Qingyuan peeled off the thick layers, took a hot shower, and lay down in bed. On the glass dome, the aurora was still there, fainter than before but still beautiful, like a painting that moved.
"Lu Shiyan, why do you think the aurora is green?"
"Because charged particles in the solar wind collide with oxygen atoms in the Earth's atmosphere."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "You looked that up too?"
"Mm."
"Could you maybe not be so all-knowing?"
Lu Shiyan laughed. "No."
Su Qingyuan laughed too. She rolled over to face him. The two of them lay beneath the glass dome, watching the aurora overhead, neither saying a word. It was quiet for a long time—long enough that Su Qingyuan thought he'd fallen asleep.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"You said before that a proposal needs a ring. When are you getting ready?"
"Guess."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a punch. "I won't guess. Tell me when you're ready."
"Okay."
She closed her eyes and listened to the sound of his breathing. Outside, the aurora was still there, faintly green, like a lamp that never went out. She suddenly remembered, long ago, a rainy night, when she'd sat on a hotel floor thinking her world was over. Back then she hadn't known that that rain was the end of a storm, and also the beginning of dawn. Now she knew.
The next day, they rode a sled. Huskies pulled the sled racing across the snowfield, fast enough that Su Qingyuan screamed out loud. Lu Shiyan sat behind her, both arms around her waist, saying in her ear, "Hold on tight." She gripped the reins, the wind howling past her ears, snow striking her face, biting cold—but she didn't want to stop.
The third day, they went to the Santa Claus Village. Su Qingyuan wrote a postcard at the post office and mailed it to herself. On the postcard she wrote: "I'm in the Arctic Circle, and I saw the aurora. And him. —Su Qingyuan." She didn't know whether the postcard would ever arrive, but she wanted to send it. Because it was a trace she'd left within the Arctic Circle.
The fourth day, they returned to Nancheng.
As the plane landed, Su Qingyuan looked out at the hazy gray sky and suddenly felt that Nancheng's winter was beautiful too. Not because there was no snow—because he was here.
Back at the studio, everything was as before. The cyclamen on the windowsill had withered and been replaced by a new pot—a white phalaenopsis orchid. Su Qingyuan watered the flower, opened her laptop, and started working through the accumulated tasks. The Cartier contract needed signing, the New York stop of the "Light Traces" tour needed its plan confirmed, the "Roots" series needed its portfolio compiled. There was a lot, but she wasn't anxious. Because she knew that, whatever lay ahead, he would be there.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "What do you feel like eating tonight?"
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment and typed two words: "Hot pot."
Lu Shiyan: "Okay. I'll come pick you up."
Su Qingyuan: "No need, I'll go myself."
Lu Shiyan: "Okay. See you at home."
Su Qingyuan's mouth curved up. She put away her phone and went back to work. Outside, Nancheng's sky was still a hazy gray, but she knew spring would come soon.
