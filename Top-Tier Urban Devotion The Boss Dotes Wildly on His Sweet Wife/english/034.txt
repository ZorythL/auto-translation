Chapter 34 — The Journey Home, the Journey Begins
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626236.html

On their last day in Paris, Su Qingyuan rose early. She walked alone into the hotel's little courtyard, sat down beneath that nameless tree, and cradled a cup of hot coffee in her hands. Morning light spilled through the gaps in the rooftops and fell across the bluestone paving, like gold shattered all over the ground. From somewhere far off came the toll of a church bell, deep and slow, one stroke after another, like some ancient promise.
These past few days in Paris, she had been to many places and done many things. The launch at the Cartier flagship, the Wall of Love in Montmartre, the three treasures of the Louvre, the bridges over the Seine, gazing up beneath the Eiffel Tower. But what stayed with her most weren't those landmark views—it was the small, trivial moments that weren't worth mentioning: the slight embarrassment on Lu Shiyan's face as he ordered in French at the croissant shop; the way he gripped her hand when he said "I love you" at the Wall of Love; the effortless ease with which he shielded her from the crush of the crowd at the Louvre; the light in his eyes when he said "standing beside you" by the Seine.
Those moments were worth keeping far more than any scenery.
"Up this early?" Lu Shiyan's voice came from behind her. Su Qingyuan turned and saw him standing in the courtyard doorway, wearing a dark grey cardigan, a cup of coffee in his hand, his hair not yet combed, a few strands falling over his forehead. He looked far softer than usual. She curved her lips. "It's our last day. I couldn't bear to sleep."
Lu Shiyan came over and sat down beside her. The two of them sat shoulder to shoulder beneath that nameless tree, drinking coffee, listening to the faint, distant bells. Neither spoke, but the silence was comfortable—like two rivers that had run their long courses and finally converged on a wide, open plain.
"Lu Shiyan, do you think we'll ever come back to Paris?"
"We will."
"When?"
"Whenever you want to."
Su Qingyuan tilted her head to look at him. The sunlight fell across the side of his face, throwing his profile into tender, clear relief. "Then it's settled. Once every year from now on."
"All right."
"Every year we'll go to Montmartre, eat croissants, see the Wall of Love."
"All right."
"Every year we'll go to the Louvre, fail to squeeze in to see the Mona Lisa, and just look at her from outside."
Lu Shiyan turned to look at her, the corner of his mouth lifting. "Su Qingyuan, are you setting up a once-a-year date with me?"
Su Qingyuan's face went red, but she didn't deny it. "Yes. Do you agree?"
Lu Shiyan looked at her for a few seconds, then reached out and gently ruffled her hair. "I agree. Once a year. Not one time less."
Su Qingyuan smiled, her eyes curving into crescent moons.
That afternoon they packed their luggage and checked out. Jean-Philippe drove them to the airport, and the whole way Su Qingyuan watched the Paris streets slide past the window, committing every corner, every tree, every streetlamp to memory. She knew she would come back, but the next time she did, she might already be someone different. Paris wouldn't change—but she would. She would return here carrying new work, new stories, a new self.
The airport. The departure lounge. Su Qingyuan sat by the floor-to-ceiling window, watching the planes out on the tarmac. Sunlight poured in through the vast glass curtain wall and settled over her, warm and gentle. Lu Shiyan had gone to check the bags; she sat there alone, holding two cups of coffee—an Americano, hers, and a latte, his. She looked down at the names written on the cups and suddenly remembered the first time she'd drunk the coffee he bought, back in Nancheng. Back then they weren't yet together. He'd invited her up to his office on the eighty-eighth floor; she'd taken a sip of an Americano and frowned at the bitterness, and he'd immediately had someone swap it for a latte. She hadn't said anything at the time, but she'd kept it in her heart—this man had even noticed how she liked her coffee.
Lu Shiyan came back and sat down beside her. Su Qingyuan handed him the latte. "Yours." He took it and drank a sip. "Thanks." The two of them sat side by side, watching the planes beyond the window. The sunlight grew brighter and brighter, turning the whole departure lounge into one enormous, transparent greenhouse.
"Su Qingyuan, once we're back, what do you most want to do?"
Su Qingyuan thought it over. "Go to the studio and look at the last two pieces of the 'Roots' series. Then find Qu Ya and finalize the last details of the joint exhibition. And then—"
"And then?"
"And then go see the aurora with you."
Lu Shiyan turned to look at her. "Weren't we going to wait until after the joint exhibition?"
"No more waiting," Su Qingyuan said, looking out the window, her voice very soft. "The things you want to do, do them early. The people you want to see, see them early. The places you want to go, go early."
Lu Shiyan fell silent for a few seconds, then reached out and took her hand. "All right. Once we're back, I'll arrange it."
When the plane took off, Su Qingyuan leaned against Lu Shiyan's shoulder, watching Paris grow smaller and smaller beyond the cabin window. Paris was like a painting, slowly shrinking in her field of vision, dwindling to a single point, until at last it vanished into the clouds. She closed her eyes and silently said to herself—Goodbye, Paris. Thank you for these days of light.
As the plane cut through the clouds, Su Qingyuan fell asleep again. She had another dream. In the dream she was still that little girl sitting on the attic floor, drawing. But this time, the sky outside the window wasn't a dull grey—it was blue, so very blue, like the sky over Paris. Downstairs there was no foster mother's voice nagging her to cook; there was only the rustle of wind through the leaves. She kept drawing, and what she drew was light—not the thin thread of gold seeping out from behind the clouds, but light that filled the whole sky, light like the aurora.
Nancheng. Touchdown.
Coming out through customs, Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath of Nancheng air. Cold, dry, carrying the biting chill peculiar to northern cities. So unlike the dampness of Paris—but this was her city. She had grown up here, been wounded here, picked herself back up here, met him here. Every street, every building, every streetlamp in this city held a memory of hers.
"We're back," she said.
"Mm. We're back." Lu Shiyan stood beside her, pushing two suitcases. One black, one white, leaning against each other, like a pair.
"Shall I take you to the studio first?" he asked.
Su Qingyuan thought about it. "Home first. I want to shower, change clothes, and then go to the studio."
"All right."
The car pulled out of the airport and merged into Nancheng's traffic. The streetscape outside rushed backward—familiar road signs, familiar billboards, familiar traffic lights. Su Qingyuan leaned back in her seat, looking out the window, and suddenly felt as though Nancheng had changed a little—not that the city had changed, but that the way she looked at it had. She used to think Nancheng was huge, so huge she couldn't find her own place in it. Now she felt Nancheng was small, small enough that his shadow lingered in every corner.
The car stopped below Su Qingyuan's apartment building. She pushed open the door, got out, and lifted her white suitcase from the trunk. Lu Shiyan got out too, standing across from her. The afternoon sunlight fell in the space between them, like a transparent wall.
"I'll pick you up tonight, for dinner," he said.
"All right."
"What do you want to eat?"
"What you cook."
Lu Shiyan glanced at her, the corner of his mouth lifting. "All right. I'll cook."
Su Qingyuan dragged her suitcase in through the building's entrance and looked back once. He was still standing by the car, the sunlight falling on his shoulders, gazing in her direction, like a lighthouse that would never move. She curved her lips, turned, and went up.
Home. She opened the door, switched on the lights. Everything was as before. The cyclamen on the windowsill was still blooming, its pink-white petals trembling faintly in the afternoon sun. She watered the flowers, opened her suitcase, and took things out one by one—clothes, scarves, sketchbook, carving knife. She opened the sketchbook; tucked inside was a postcard she'd bought at the Louvre, a print of the Mona Lisa. She looked at it for a few seconds, slipped it back between the pages, and closed the book.
She showered, changed into clean clothes, and went out. A taxi dropped her at the entrance of the creative park. She walked in, and from a distance saw her own studio. That door, that window, that bare osmanthus tree. She stood in the doorway, took the key from her bag, slid it into the lock, and turned. The instant the door swung open, the familiar smell washed over her—wood, metal, paper, and a faint lingering trace of osmanthus. She walked in, switched on the light, and sat down at the workbench.
Everything was still here. Her carving knives, her tools, the unfinished fourth sample of the 'Roots' series. She picked up a carving knife, turned it over in her hand, then set it down. No work today. Today she had simply come to tell the studio—I'm back.
She stood, walked to the window, and pushed it open. Cold air rushed in, dispersing the air that had sat stale in the room for over ten days. In the distance, along Nancheng's skyline, the clouds were thick and greyish-white—but the sun broke through the gaps in them, falling onto the ground, onto that bare osmanthus tree, onto her face.
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath, and let it out.
That evening, Lu Shiyan came to pick her up. The car stopped below his building, and Su Qingyuan looked up at it, remembering how, the last time she'd come, he'd said, "You're welcome here anytime." Today she'd come again.
The elevator rose; the doors opened. Lu Shiyan went in first, Su Qingyuan following behind, swapping her shoes for that same pair of pale grey plush slippers from last time. The living room lights were still that warm yellow, white chamomile still sat on the coffee table, that pale cream throw was still draped over the sofa. Everything was just as it had been last time—but Su Qingyuan noticed that the entryway now held an extra pair of slippers: pale pink, fluffy, in her size.
"New slippers?" she asked.
"Mm. The last pair were guest slippers. These are yours."
Su Qingyuan looked down at the pale pink slippers, and something inside her went soft. She changed into the new slippers, walked into the living room, and sat down on the sofa. Lu Shiyan had gone to the kitchen, and she heard the refrigerator door opening and closing, the sound of running water, the clatter of pots and bowls. She leaned back on the sofa, hugging that pale cream throw, listening to all those small sounds from the kitchen, and suddenly felt—"home" was this sound. Not the luxurious décor, not the expensive furniture, but the sound of someone cooking for you in the kitchen.
Su Qingyuan stood, walked to the kitchen doorway, and leaned against the frame, watching Lu Shiyan. He was chopping vegetables—not exactly skilled, but very earnest. One stroke after another, neither fast nor slow, the shredded potato coming out evenly cut.
"When did you learn to chop vegetables?"
"These past few days, while you were away. I watched a few videos and practiced."
"How many days did you practice?"
"Started the day you left."
Su Qingyuan fell silent. She'd been gone over ten days, and he'd been practicing chopping vegetables from the very day she left. Not for himself—so that when she came back, he could make her a proper meal.
"Lu Shiyan, if you keep this up, I'm going to cry."
"Then cry. It's not like I'll laugh at you."
Su Qingyuan didn't cry. She walked over, wrapped her arms around him from behind, and pressed her face to his back. Through the sweater she could feel the warmth of his body, warm, like a stove in winter.
Lu Shiyan's hands paused mid-chop, then went on. He didn't say anything, but she could feel his heartbeat had quickened.
Dinner was stir-fried shredded potato, scrambled eggs with tomato, and a bowl of seaweed and egg-drop soup. All the simplest of dishes, but Su Qingyuan ate with great care, chewing every bite slowly. Because these were the vegetables he'd chopped for her in the kitchen, the dishes he'd cooked for her, the soup he'd boiled for her.
"Is it good?" he asked.
"It's good."
"Really?"
"Really. A lot better than the congee last time."
Lu Shiyan smiled, his brows and eyes relaxing open, like the first crack splitting across an ice-bound lake in spring.
After dinner, Su Qingyuan offered to wash the dishes. Lu Shiyan stood in the kitchen doorway, leaning against the frame, watching her profile as she washed. The warm yellow light fell over her, her hair loose around her shoulders, sleeves rolled to her elbows, baring slender wrists.
"Su Qingyuan, you look lovely washing dishes."
Su Qingyuan's hands paused. "Have you never washed dishes before?"
"I have. But I've never watched anyone else do it."
Su Qingyuan, blushing, set the last bowl in the rack, wiped her hands, and turned to look at him. "Seen enough?"
"No. I'll never see enough."
Su Qingyuan walked over, rose on her toes, and pecked him lightly on the lips.
"Good night. I should head back."
"I'll walk you."
"No need, you wash the dishes."
Lu Shiyan smiled. "All right. Message me when you get home."
Su Qingyuan changed her shoes, walked to the door, and looked back once. He was standing in the kitchen doorway, apron still on, holding the dishcloth she'd just used, gazing in her direction. She curved her lips, pushed the door open, and went out.
The instant the elevator doors closed, she leaned against the elevator wall, looked at her flushed face in the mirror, and laughed. She took out her phone and sent him a message: "I just left and I already miss you."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "Then come back."
Su Qingyuan looked at those three words, smiled, and slipped the phone into her pocket. She didn't go back. Because tomorrow she'd come again, and the day after, and every day after that—she could come anytime. One night wouldn't make a difference.
Back at the apartment, she showered, lay down in bed, and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Home."
Lu Shiyan: "Mm. Shut the windows tight, don't turn the heating up too high."
Su Qingyuan: "Shut them. Set it to twenty degrees."
Lu Shiyan: "Good. See you tomorrow."
Su Qingyuan: "See you tomorrow."
She set the phone on the nightstand and turned off the light. In the darkness, she closed her eyes. So many things were turning in her mind—the sunlight of Paris, the words on the Wall of Love, the Venus of the Louvre, the figure chopping vegetables in the kitchen, that pair of pale pink slippers, his heartbeat when he said "Then come back." Those things turned for a long while, then slowly quieted down.
She rolled over, pulled the blanket up to her chin, and sank into a deep sleep.
The next morning, Su Qingyuan went to Qu Ya's studio. Qu Ya was in the darkroom developing photographs; hearing the doorbell, she came out with wet hands to open the door. "You're back? How was Paris?"
"Wonderful. The Cartier launch was a big success—the 'Light Traces' series was recognized."
"I knew it." Qu Ya smiled and pulled her inside. "Come, take a look at the final plan for the joint exhibition."
The long table in the studio was covered with design drawings, photo proofs, and booth layout plans. Qu Ya pointed at one of them. "The venue's confirmed—the Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art, main hall, five hundred square meters. Your work takes half, mine takes half, and the middle is a shared zone. The theme is called 'Convergence.'"
Su Qingyuan looked at the layout plan, her heart racing. "'Convergence'—the source of the light and the home of the roots, converging right there."
"Exactly. That's the idea." Qu Ya turned to another page. "The promotional materials are settled too. Posters, invitations, media kits—all one visual system. Your 'Roots' series and my 'Roots' series set side by side, one theme, two expressions."
Su Qingyuan turned through it page by page, every page the fruit of her and Qu Ya's labor. This joint exhibition, from the first spark of inspiration to the final plan now, had taken nearly two months. In those two months, she'd made five pieces, Qu Ya had shot twenty photographs, they'd held countless meetings and quarreled countless times—not for the sake of winning, but to make the exhibition better.
"Sister Ya, thank you."
"Thank me for what? I should be thanking you." Qu Ya closed the portfolio and looked at her. "Without your 'Roots,' there'd be no exhibition."
The two of them looked at each other, and both smiled.
As Su Qingyuan came out of Qu Ya's studio, she received a WeChat message. Not from Lu Shiyan, not from Zhou Wanyin, but from a name she hadn't heard from in a long time—Chen Xiaoman.
"Qingyuan, I've made it to Australia. I found a job at a restaurant, room and board included, the pay isn't much but it's enough to live on. The sunshine here is wonderful. I think my life can start over. Thank you for not pressing charges. I'll live well. I'm sorry, and thank you."
Su Qingyuan looked at the message and was silent for a long while.
She didn't reply. Not because she didn't want to, but because she didn't know what to say. "It's all right"? No—because Chen Xiaoman really had hurt her. "I forgive you"? No either—because Chen Xiaoman didn't need her forgiveness. In the end she typed four words: "Live a good life." Then she sent a photo she'd taken that day—Nancheng's sky, greyish-white, but with the sun breaking through from behind the clouds, falling onto the ground, gleaming gold.
After sending it, she put her phone away, walked to the parking lot, and got into the car. She leaned back in the rear seat, watching the streetscape rush backward beyond the window. Nancheng's winter was still going on, but she knew the coldest part was already behind her. Not because Gu Changfeng had lost, but because she herself had won—won the battle inside her own heart. She used to fear being doubted, feared being denied, feared having all her effort wiped away by a single line: "She got there through connections." Now she wasn't afraid anymore, because her work was her proof, and her name was her brand.
Her phone buzzed—a message from Lu Shiyan: "What do you want to eat tonight?"
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment and typed two words: "Hot pot."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. I'll come get you."
Su Qingyuan: "No need to pick me up, I'm already on the way. See you at the studio."
Lu Shiyan: "All right. See you at the studio."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips, put her phone away, and looked out the window. In Nancheng's evening, the sky slowly darkened, the streetlamps lighting up one by one, linking into a warm river of light. She suddenly remembered, a long time ago, one rainy night, standing at the roadside, not knowing where she was going.
Now she knew.
