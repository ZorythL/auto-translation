Chapter 33 — Shadows of the Louvre, Light on the Seine
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626235.html

On her third day in Paris, Su Qingyuan was woken by the sun.
Golden light spilled in through the gap in the curtains, falling across the white sheets like a thin river of light. She lay in bed staring at the ceiling, listening to the occasional whistle of pigeons outside the window and the blurred hum of distant traffic, her thoughts turning slow and lazy—today they were going to the Louvre, to see the Mona Lisa, the Winged Victory, the armless Venus.
She rolled over and picked up her phone. There was a message from Lu Shiyan on the screen, sent seven minutes ago: "Awake? Breakfast's downstairs."
The corners of Su Qingyuan's mouth curved up, and she replied with a single word: "Coming."
She washed and dressed at record speed. Today she wore a cream-white knit dress, with that smoke-grey cashmere coat over it, and the scarf Lu Shiyan had bought the night before at a little shop in Montmartre—deep blue, cashmere, soft as a cloud. She checked herself in the mirror, looped the scarf around again so it looked carelessly thrown on—though in truth she'd adjusted it several times.
The hotel restaurant was on the ground floor, small but warm. Floor-to-ceiling windows faced a little courtyard with a tree of some unknown kind, its bare branches still hung with last night's raindrops, glittering in the morning light. Lu Shiyan sat by the window. On the table before him were two cups of coffee, a basket of croissants, a small dish of butter, a small dish of jam. He'd changed too—a deep blue sweater, black trousers, sleeves rolled up to his forearms. The whole effect was far softer than usual, like an ordinary man simply waiting to have breakfast with his girlfriend.
Su Qingyuan walked over and sat down across from him. "Have you been here long?"
"Not long." Lu Shiyan pushed a coffee toward her. "Just came down."
Su Qingyuan glanced at his coffee cup—already half empty. "Liar."
The corner of Lu Shiyan's mouth lifted slightly; he didn't deny it. He picked up a croissant, sliced it open carefully with the butter knife, spread butter and jam over it, then set it on the plate in front of her. "Try this jam. The hotel makes it themselves—raspberry."
Su Qingyuan took a bite. The sweet-tart jam melted on the tip of her tongue, mingling with the rich scent of the butter, so good it made her narrow her eyes. "How did you know raspberry jam was good?"
"Looked it up yesterday." Lu Shiyan's tone was offhand, as if it were the most trivial thing.
Su Qingyuan watched the side of his face as he bent over the jam, and her heart went utterly soft. This man—he even looked up which breakfast jam to order in advance.
The Louvre wasn't far from the hotel, less than a twenty-minute walk. They strolled along the Seine, where sightseeing boats glided slowly past on the water, the people aboard holding up their phones to photograph the buildings on the banks, the tower in the distance, and—who knew if anyone had caught the two of them in a shot. Su Qingyuan held onto Lu Shiyan's arm, her steps unhurried, because she didn't want to walk too fast. Every corner of Paris deserved to be seen slowly, felt slowly.
"Lu Shiyan, have you been to the Louvre before?"
"Once. On a business trip, accompanying a client."
"And did you go in to look?"
"No. Spent two hours waiting for the client at the entrance."
Su Qingyuan looked up at him, picturing him standing alone at the Louvre's entrance in a suit, expressionless, waiting for a client to come out. She couldn't help laughing. "Then today you can have a proper look."
"Mm. Together with you."
The Louvre's glass pyramid sparkled in the sunlight like an enormous crystal sculpture. A long queue stretched at the entrance, but Lu Shiyan had booked tickets online ahead of time, and they went in through the fast-track lane with almost no waiting. Su Qingyuan stood beneath the pyramid, tilting her head back to gaze up at the glass structure overhead, the sunlight falling through it onto her face, refracting into rainbow-colored flecks.
"Beautiful?" Lu Shiyan stood behind her.
"Beautiful. I'd only ever seen it in photos. I never imagined that actually standing here, I'd feel so…" She thought for a moment, found the word, "moved."
Lu Shiyan said nothing, only reached out and took her hand.
They went first to the ancient Greek sculpture gallery. The armless Venus stood at the center of the hall, the white plaster glowing softly under the lights. Su Qingyuan stood before her and looked for a long time. She had seen countless pictures of this statue, but only when she truly stood before it did she understand why it was called "the model of beauty"—not because the proportions were perfect, but because it held a quiet power that transcended time. Over two thousand years, it had lost its arms, lost its color, lost its original context, yet it remained beautiful. This kind of beauty needed no wholeness, no explanation.
Su Qingyuan suddenly thought of her own "Roots" series—the piece made with flawed pearls. Imperfection wasn't a defect; it was a story. The ancient Greek craftsmen of two thousand years ago could never have imagined that the "imperfection" they left behind would find an echo in another person's work.
"What are you thinking?" Lu Shiyan asked.
"I'm thinking that beautiful things don't need to be perfect."
Lu Shiyan looked at the statue of Venus and was silent for a few seconds. "Just like you."
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him. "What about me isn't perfect?"
"You are perfect." Lu Shiyan's voice was very soft. "But your beauty isn't because of perfection—it's because it's real."
Su Qingyuan turned her face away, pretending to keep looking at the statue. Her ears had gone red.
The Winged Victory's gallery was up a flight of stairs. As Su Qingyuan climbed the steps, the first thing she saw was the pair of wings—spread wide, immense, facing into the wind. The statue had no head, no arms, only a body and wings, yet that posture of pressing "forward" carried more power than any intact statue. She stood before it and thought of her own "Breaking the Cocoon"—the necklace made of opal and diamonds. "Breaking the Cocoon" had no head either, only wings. Because in the moment of breaking free, the head had not yet emerged; the wings came out first.
She took out her phone and snapped a photo of the Winged Victory, sending it to Zhou Wanyin. The caption read: "Paris, the Winged Victory. It made me think of 'Breaking the Cocoon.'"
Zhou Wanyin's reply came quickly: "'Breaking the Cocoon' IS the Winged Victory's wings. You've been doing the same thing all along."
Su Qingyuan looked at the message and the corners of her mouth curved up.
The Mona Lisa's gallery was packed, layer upon layer of tourists all holding up their phones. Su Qingyuan stood at the edge of the crowd, looking from afar at that small painting—much smaller than she'd imagined, shielded behind thick glass, the lights striking it and throwing back so many bright flecks that she couldn't make out the details.
"I can't squeeze in." Su Qingyuan was a little disappointed.
"Seeing it is enough." Lu Shiyan stood behind her, blocking the people pressing in from behind. "Some things don't need to be seen up close."
Su Qingyuan tilted her head toward him. "Such as?"
"Such as—you don't need to be close to me for me to still see you."
Su Qingyuan's heart beat faster. She turned back around and kept looking at the blurred Mona Lisa in the distance, saying nothing. But her hand stayed held in his the whole time.
By the time they came out of the Louvre, it was already two in the afternoon. Su Qingyuan's legs ached a little, and she was hungry too. Lu Shiyan took her to a restaurant tucked away in a small alley near the Louvre, the storefront tiny, but pushing the door open revealed a whole other world inside—a glass atrium planted with greenery, sunlight pouring down from overhead, warm and golden.
"You looked this up too?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"Mm."
"How many things did you look up?"
"Not many."
"Tell me the truth."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, the corner of his mouth lifting. "From the day we settled on the Paris trip, I looked up every place you might want to go."
Su Qingyuan opened her mouth to say something, but her throat felt as if it were blocked by something. From the day they settled on the trip. How long ago was that? Half a month? Twenty days? Back then she'd still been racing to finish the "Light Traces" collection, still fretting over Gu Changfeng's smear pieces. And he had already been researching Paris guides for her—the croissant shop, the Wall of Love, the Louvre, this restaurant hidden in an alley.
Everything he did was small. But strung together, it was one whole map, densely marked, with her name written all over it.
"Lu Shiyan, from now on don't look things up anymore."
Lu Shiyan's brow furrowed slightly.
"From now on, wherever we want to go, we'll look it up together."
Lu Shiyan looked into her eyes for a few seconds, then smiled. "All right. We'll look it up together."
The restaurant's food was wonderful. The starter was foie gras with toasted bread, the main course a confit duck leg, the skin crisp, the meat so tender it fell apart at a flick of the fork. Su Qingyuan ate slowly, because she didn't want the meal to end too soon.
"Lu Shiyan, where do you think is the best place in Paris?"
Lu Shiyan thought for a moment and said, "Montmartre."
"Why?"
"Because that's where the Wall of Love is."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head, prodding at the leftover duck on her plate with her fork. "You never used to say things like that."
"Things like what?"
"Sweet nothings."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a second. "I never used to know how. But with you, I don't need to know how—when I want to say it, I just say it."
Su Qingyuan's face flushed. She picked up her wine glass and took a big sip of red wine, using the burn of it to suppress the heat rising in her cheeks. Across from her, Lu Shiyan watched her, the smile in his eyes impossible to hide.
After lunch, they strolled along the Seine. The afternoon sun spilled over the water, breaking into a sheet of golden light. Su Qingyuan leaned against the railing of the riverbank, gazing at the distant Eiffel Tower, and suddenly remembered something.
"Lu Shiyan, you said you'd take me to see the Northern Lights—when are we going?"
"When do you want to go?"
"I don't know. When things aren't busy?"
"When are you ever not busy?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it and laughed. "Seems like I'm always busy."
"Then we'll make time." Lu Shiyan stood beside her, looking at the tower in the distance. "Time is like water in a sponge—squeeze, and there's always some to be had."
"When did you get so full of feel-good wisdom?"
"Learned it from you."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a light punch. "I am not full of feel-good wisdom."
"Every piece you make is feel-good wisdom. 'Breaking the Cocoon' is feel-good wisdom, 'Daybreak' is feel-good wisdom, 'Roots' is feel-good wisdom too. But the feel-good wisdom you make tastes good."
Su Qingyuan didn't know whether to laugh or cry at that. She wanted to argue, but when she thought about it, she realized he wasn't really wrong. Her work really did all speak to a single theme—walking out of the darkness, finding the light, becoming the light. If that counted as feel-good wisdom, then it was a brew she'd simmered with her own hands, using her own bones and flesh.
"Then the Northern Lights, it's settled. After the 'Roots and Light' joint exhibition ends, we'll go."
"All right."
"To Northern Europe?"
"All right."
"See the aurora, ride a sled, stay in a glass igloo?"
"All right."
"Why do you keep saying all right to everything?"
"Because whatever you say is all right by me."
Su Qingyuan looked at his perfectly serious expression and laughed. She laughed until her eyes curved into crescent moons, laughed until the corners of her mouth tilted high, laughed until a passing old Frenchman glanced at her and started smiling too.
In the evening, they went to a bridge over the Seine—the Pont des Arts. The bridge was covered with love locks, dense and countless, some already rusted, some still brand new. Su Qingyuan walked across it, looking at the locks—some inscribed with names, some with dates, some with just a single heart.
"Should we lock one too?" Lu Shiyan asked.
Su Qingyuan thought about it and shook her head. "No. If we lock it, we'd have to come back to unlock it—too much trouble."
Lu Shiyan looked at her and smiled. "Then we won't lock one."
The two of them walked across the bridge, the setting sun sinking slowly behind them, dyeing the whole sky orange-red. Su Qingyuan glanced back, and that bridge hung with locks looked in the sunset like a golden necklace stretched across the Seine.
She suddenly felt that not locking one was just as good. Because a lock stood for "fear of losing." And she wasn't afraid of losing, because he wouldn't leave.
At night, they went to the Eiffel Tower. Not up it—they stood at its base, heads tilted back, looking up at it. The tower glowed with golden light in the night, and on the hour it would even sparkle, like countless stars lighting up all at once. Su Qingyuan looked up, her neck aching but unwilling to lower her head.
"Lu Shiyan, why do you think the Eiffel Tower is so tall?"
"So people can see."
"See what?"
"See all of Paris. See where they themselves are standing."
Su Qingyuan lowered her tilted head and looked at him. "Then where are you standing?"
Lu Shiyan looked down at her, his gaze deep as the night. "Standing beside you."
Su Qingyuan smiled. She rose onto her tiptoes and pressed a light peck to his lips. "Let's head back. We've got the exhibition to see tomorrow."
"All right."
They walked back to the hotel along the Seine. The night breeze was cool, but Su Qingyuan wasn't cold at all. Because she wore the cashmere coat he'd given her, wrapped in the handmade scarf he'd bought in Montmartre, her hand held warm in his palm. She looked down at their two clasped hands and suddenly felt that winter in Paris was warmer than spring in Nancheng.
Back at the hotel, Su Qingyuan stood at the door of her room and didn't go in right away.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Tomorrow's the last day in Paris."
"Mm."
"I'm a little reluctant to leave."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, reached out to unwind her scarf, folded it, and placed it in her hands. "Reluctant to leave Paris?"
"Reluctant to leave these few days of you showing me around Paris."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a second, then leaned in and dropped an exceedingly light, exceedingly gentle kiss on her forehead. "There'll be other chances. This isn't the last time."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, feeling the warmth of that kiss. Then she opened her eyes and smiled. "Goodnight."
"Goodnight."
She turned and walked into her room, closing the door. She leaned against the door panel for a long while, listening to the sound of his footsteps gradually fading down the corridor, then the door of the room next to hers opened, and closed again.
She pressed the scarf to her face and caught the clean, cool scent of him. Then she picked up her phone and sent him a message: "I forgot to give you back the scarf."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "It's a gift for you."
Su Qingyuan: "Didn't you say you just bought it on a whim in Montmartre? How did it become a gift now?"
Lu Shiyan: "I bought it on the side in Montmartre, but it was always meant for you. Not on the side—on purpose."
Su Qingyuan stared at those words, "on purpose," and the corners of her mouth curved to their widest arc. She folded the scarf, set it beside her pillow, and turned off the light.
In the darkness, she closed her eyes. The last night in Paris was quiet and tender. Outside the window came the occasional sound of a car driving past, and from somewhere far off the faint strains of music, as if someone were playing the violin, broken and intermittent, but lovely.
She rolled over, pulled the blanket up to her chin, and sank into a deep sleep.
Tomorrow, back to Nancheng.
After she returned, the "Roots and Light" joint exhibition would be waiting for her, the global tour of the "Light Traces" collection would be waiting for her, new designs, new challenges, new stories—all waiting for her. But she wasn't anxious, because she knew that whatever lay ahead, he would be there.
