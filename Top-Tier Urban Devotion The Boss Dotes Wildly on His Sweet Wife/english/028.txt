Chapter 28 — Eyes in the Shadows, Light in the Open
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626230.html

Su Yurou came three times.
The first time, she waited outside the studio door for forty minutes, and Su Qingyuan watched her leave from inside her car. The second time was at dusk; Su Qingyuan was on the phone with Qu Ya, hashing out the details of their collaboration, when through a gap in the curtains she saw that figure in the black down jacket standing in the gathering twilight for over half an hour, until the cold wind finally drove her off, shoulders hunched. The third time was early morning. Su Qingyuan had just arrived at the studio when she spotted Su Yurou from a distance, sitting on the steps by the door, a cup of long-cold coffee cradled in her hands, as if she had been sitting there a very long time.
This time, Su Qingyuan didn't walk around her.
She went over and stood in front of Su Yurou, looking down at her from above. The early light was thin, stretching their two shadows long and longer, overlapping and then parting again.
"You've come three times now." Su Qingyuan's voice was calm. "Whatever it is, say it now."
Su Yurou lifted her head. Her eyes were bloodshot, the skin beneath them a heavy bruised blue. She looked like she hadn't slept well in a long time, the whole of her like a flower battered by frost—wilted, drooping, yet with roots still clinging fiercely to the soil.
"Sister Qingyuan, could I come in and sit for a bit? It's cold out here."
Su Qingyuan looked at her for a few seconds, then took out her key and opened the door.
"Ten minutes."
Su Yurou stepped into the studio, her gaze sweeping every corner—the tools laid out neatly on the workbench, the finished pieces in the display cases, the butterfly orchid blooming just right on the windowsill, the pale beige throw blanket on the sofa in the lounge area. There was something complicated in her eyes: envy, jealousy, and a regret too tangled to name—something like, "If I hadn't taken the road I took back then, would a piece of this place have been mine too?"
She sat down in a chair, hands resting on her knees, like a little girl visiting someone else's home for the first time, awkward and ill at ease.
Su Qingyuan didn't pour her any water, and she didn't sit down either. She leaned against the window, arms crossed, watching Su Yurou.
"Go on."
Su Yurou drew a deep breath, as if steeling herself for something enormous.
"Sister Qingyuan, I want to apologize to you. Not the kind of 'I was wrong, so forgive me' apology—a real one. I know the things I did before can't be wiped away with a single 'I'm sorry.' Stealing your designs, taking your boyfriend, falsely accusing you of plagiarism—every one of them, I knew was wrong. But I did them anyway."
As she spoke, her eyes reddened, but no tears fell.
"Because I was jealous of you. From the time we were small, you were better than me at everything. Better at drawing, better at design, even Mom and Dad—even though they're your adoptive parents, they treated you better than they treated me. You never fought, never grabbed for anything, yet every good thing in the world ran straight to you on its own. I couldn't accept it."
Su Qingyuan didn't interrupt her. She listened in silence.
"I took your things not because I wanted them. It was because I wanted you to know—you can't have everything. I wanted you to taste 'loss' too." Su Yurou's voice dropped lower and lower. "But then I realized, no matter what you lost, you never cried, never made a scene. You just quietly accepted it, and then kept walking forward. The more you were like that, the more I hated you."
Su Qingyuan looked at her, silent for a long time.
"Are you finished?"
Su Yurou froze. Clearly she hadn't expected this reaction from Su Qingyuan—no anger, no being moved, not even a ripple.
"You… you're not angry?"
"No." Su Qingyuan's voice was faint. "Su Yurou, your apology is something you owe yourself, not compensation you owe me. You've said it, and it makes you feel better inside—that's your business. But to me, whether you say these things or not, it doesn't matter anymore."
She straightened up from the window, walked to the door, and pulled it open.
"Ten minutes are up. You should go."
Su Yurou stood. Her lips trembled; she wanted to say something, but when she saw Su Qingyuan's eyes, calm to the point of being almost without emotion, she swallowed all of it back. She walked to the door, paused, her back to Su Qingyuan, her voice very soft.
"Sister Qingyuan, I'm going to leave Nancheng."
Su Qingyuan said nothing.
"Gu Changfeng promised me a sum of money to go abroad. I'm planning to go to Australia, to start over." Su Yurou turned around and looked at Su Qingyuan, her eyes red, but her expression far more sincere than before. "I'm not here to say goodbye to you. Because I know you don't care for that. I just wanted you to know—I concede. Not because you won, but because I'm tired."
Su Qingyuan looked at her, silent for a few seconds.
"When you get to Australia, live well."
Just that one line. No "I forgive you," no "I hope you do well," no "we'll still be sisters from now on." Just one line—when you get to Australia, live well. Not forgiveness, but letting it go. Not a blessing, but standing aside. Not reconciliation, but simply no longer being entangled.
Su Yurou's tears finally fell. She wiped them away quickly, turned, and left. The click of her heels against the ground grew fainter and fainter, until at last it vanished into the morning light of the creative park.
Su Qingyuan closed the door, leaned against it, and shut her eyes.
She didn't feel any sense of release, nor did she feel sad. She just felt that—this matter could finally be turned to a new page. It wasn't that she had forgiven Su Yurou; it was that she no longer needed anything from Su Yurou. Not her apology, not her concession, not her departure. She had long since moved Su Yurou out of her life, moved her out cleanly and completely. Whether Su Yurou came or not, apologized or not, left or not—none of it could move her in the slightest.
This kind of "not caring" was more thorough than any revenge.
Su Qingyuan walked to the workbench, picked up her carving knife, and went on polishing the second sample of the "Roots" series. Outside the window the sunlight grew brighter and brighter, falling on her hands, on the carving knife, on the gemstone taking shape beneath them.
Everything went on as usual.
Qu Ya's studio sat in an art village on the western outskirts of Nancheng, converted from an old warehouse. When Su Qingyuan arrived, Qu Ya was in the darkroom developing photographs; hearing the doorbell, she came out to open the door with her hands still wet.
"You're here? Come in, sit wherever you like. I'll be done with this print in a minute."
Su Qingyuan stepped inside and wandered slowly through the studio. The walls were hung with Qu Ya's representative works from across the years—from her early "Homeland" series, to her mid-period "Migration" series, to her most recent "The Unseen Parts." Each photograph was like a window, and through them you could see two decades of Qu Ya's deepening exploration and understanding of the theme of "roots."
Su Qingyuan stopped in front of one photograph. It was a black-and-white image of an old man's hands. Those hands were rough, covered in calluses, with dirt that could never quite be scrubbed from beneath the nails. And yet it was these very hands that cradled a tender sprout just broken through the soil, the gesture as light as if they held the most precious thing in all the world.
The photograph's title was: "Inheritance."
Su Qingyuan looked at those hands and suddenly thought of her own pair. Her hands had once been rough too, calloused, scarred from the carving knife. Now her hands still bore calluses and scars, but those calluses and scars weren't marks of humiliation—they were marks of creation. She suddenly very much wanted to turn this feeling into a piece of work—not the hands themselves, but what the hands carried.
"Lost in it?" Qu Ya's voice came from behind her. She had already emerged from the darkroom, two cups of tea in her hands.
Su Qingyuan took the teacup. "This photograph made me think of myself."
Qu Ya followed her gaze to "Inheritance" on the wall and smiled. "Most people look at this photo and think of their elders. You're the first to say it made you think of yourself."
"Because my hands are like that too." Su Qingyuan held out her hand, palm up. There were a few faint scars on her fingers, and a thin layer of callus at the web of her thumb. "These scars used to be given to me by others. Now these calluses are ones I ground out myself."
Qu Ya lowered her head to look at Su Qingyuan's hand, fell silent for a few seconds, then raised her eyes to look at her, something hard to name now in her gaze. "Su Qingyuan, I'm beginning to understand why your work can carry that kind of warmth. Because your hands are hands with a story."
The two of them sat down on the sofa, tea in hand. Qu Ya's studio wasn't large, but every inch of it breathed its owner's character—understated, yet every detail with a provenance.
Su Qingyuan opened the tablet she'd brought and showed Qu Ya a photograph of the first completed brooch in the "Roots" series. Qu Ya took the tablet, zoomed in, and studied it for a long time.
"The growth-ring part—you used five different gemstones?" There was an undisguised astonishment in her voice.
"Six." Su Qingyuan pointed at the very center. "The innermost is a deep-brown diamond, very small, 0.3 carats. But I searched a long time to find a diamond in this color, because it had to be like a 'starting point'—not the dazzling kind, but the kind that says, 'everything begins from here.'"
Qu Ya handed the tablet back to her, leaned back against the sofa, gazed up at the ceiling, and let out a long breath.
"Su Qingyuan, I've made a decision."
"What decision?"
"Your 'Roots' series, together with my new work—we do a joint exhibition. Not 'I show mine, you show yours,' but a blended show—my photographs and your jewelry placed in the same space, echoing each other. The viewer's gaze moves from the photographs to the jewelry, then back to the photographs, and they see two expressions of the same theme."
Su Qingyuan's heartbeat quickened.
"The venue, the curation, the promotion—I'll handle it all." Qu Ya's tone was as certain as if she were stating something already settled. "You do your creating, I do mine. In half a year, we each bring out our very best work, and we hold a two-person joint exhibition."
"Teacher Qu Ya, I already have a name in mind for this exhibition." Su Qingyuan looked into her eyes. "Call it 'Roots and Light.'"
Qu Ya repeated the name once, and her eyes lit up. "Roots and Light. Your 'Roots' series, my 'Light'? No—your 'First Light' has light in it, and there's light in my photographs too."
"'Roots' is where we come from, 'Light' is where we go." Su Qingyuan said softly. "From roots to light—that's the story I've been telling all along."
Qu Ya looked at her and suddenly laughed.
"Su Qingyuan, how old are you this year?"
"Twenty-four."
"When I was twenty-four, I was still in the darkroom developing photos until dawn, with no idea whether my work would ever be seen by anyone. At twenty-four, you already know what story you want to tell." Qu Ya raised her teacup and tapped it against Su Qingyuan's. "I envy you, and I congratulate you."
Su Qingyuan was scalded a little by those words—"I envy you." Qu Ya—an internationally renowned photographic artist—saying she envied a twenty-four-year-old jewelry designer. Not as a courtesy, not as flattery, but from the heart.
"Teacher Qu Ya, thank you."
"Stop calling me 'Teacher.' Call me Sister Ya."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "Sister Ya."
When she walked out of Qu Ya's studio, the sky over Nancheng was awash with sunset—violet-red, orange-gold, pink-purple, layer upon layer, like an immense oil painting. Su Qingyuan stood at the door, tilted her head up to look at that sky, and drew a deep breath.
She thought of the line Lu Shiyan had once said—"You're standing now at a height where she can see you."
Yes. It wasn't that Qu Ya had suddenly discovered her; it was that she was already standing there. And there wasn't the mountaintop—it was halfway up. The summit was still far, but she was already on her way.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: 【Done talking? How did it go?】
Su Qingyuan: 【Done. The joint exhibition—in half a year, called "Roots and Light." Qu Ya said she envies me.】
Lu Shiyan: 【Envies you for what?】
Su Qingyuan: 【Envies that at twenty-four I already know what story I want to tell.】
Lu Shiyan paused, then sent another line: 【What she doesn't know is that before you turned twenty-four, you'd already been telling your story for twenty years. There was just no one listening back then.】
Su Qingyuan looked at that line of words, and her nose suddenly stung.
Twenty years of stories. Those stories had no audience, no applause, no one cheering her on. But she had always been telling them—in the attic of the old Su family house, in the corners of the Su family shop, through countless days and nights that went unseen. Those stories had later grown into "First Light," grown into "Light Traces," grown into "Roots." They hadn't disappeared; they had been waiting all along—waiting for her to stand somewhere high enough that the whole world would hear.
She sent Lu Shiyan two words: 【Understood.】 Then she put away her phone, walked into the sunset, and headed toward the parking lot. She knew Lu Shiyan's car would be waiting there. No matter how late, no matter the wind or rain, he would be there.
On the third day after Su Qingyuan came back from Qu Ya's studio, she received a call from Anna at Cartier.
"Miss Su, the production schedule for the 'Light Traces' collection is running 30% ahead of expectations. Headquarters would like to move the global launch up to mid-January. Is that a problem on your end?"
Su Qingyuan glanced at the calendar on the wall. Mid-January—less than two months away.
"No problem. Has the launch location been decided?"
"It has. Paris, the Cartier flagship store."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, her fingertips tightening slightly. The Cartier flagship store, 13 Rue de la Paix—that was the birthplace of Cartier, and a "holy land" in the hearts of jewelers the world over. Countless top-tier jewels had been launched there, but never once had the work of an independent Chinese designer had its global debut in that place.
"Anna, what do I need to prepare?"
"You only need to do one thing—make sure all five pieces of the 'Light Traces' collection are finished and shipped to Paris a week before departure. The rest, leave to us."
"The rest, leave to us" again. Su Qingyuan suddenly remembered that Lu Shiyan often said this line too—"The rest, leave to me." Different people, different matters, the same trust.
"All right. I will."
Su Qingyuan hung up and sat at the workbench for a long while. Then she picked up a pen and wrote a line in her sketchbook: January, Paris. The Cartier flagship store. The launch.
After writing it, she looked at it for a few seconds, then added another line in smaller characters underneath: Take Lu Shiyan to see the Eiffel Tower.
Once she'd written it, she felt it was a little childish and wanted to scratch it out, but her pen tip hovered above the paper and in the end she didn't do it. Never mind. He'd never see it anyway. And even if he did… he'd probably be very happy.
That evening, when Lu Shiyan came to pick her up, Su Qingyuan told him the Cartier launch had been moved up. After he'd heard it all, he didn't say "congratulations," didn't say "I'll go with you." Instead he asked one thing: "Mid-January, Paris is very cold. Do you have a thick coat?"
Su Qingyuan was taken aback, then laughed. "That's what you're worried about?"
"That's the most important thing." Lu Shiyan started the car, his tone as serious as if he were running a project assessment. "Paris in January averages three degrees, and the wind chill could be below zero. You feel the cold easily—you can't stand around in an evening gown for too long. I'll have someone prepare a coat for you, cashmere, light but warm."
Su Qingyuan looked at the dead-serious set of his face, and her heart went hopelessly soft. Another boyfriend hearing that his girlfriend was about to do a global launch in Paris would probably react first with "That's amazing" or "I'll come with you." His first reaction was whether she'd be cold. This man put her warmth and comfort ahead of a "global launch."
"Lu Shiyan, do you know that sometimes you really sound like my mom?"
Lu Shiyan turned his head to glance at her. "I'm not that old."
"I mean nagging."
"I don't nag."
"You're being naggy right now."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a second, then reached over and took her hand, saying nothing more. But the curve at the corner of his mouth—he couldn't press it down no matter what.
The following week, Su Qingyuan went into "seclusion" mode.
The second and third pieces of the "Roots" series had their concepts and first drafts completed within that week. The second was a bracelet, inspired by Qu Ya's "Inheritance"—the chain part used antiqued silver, looking like an old heirloom, but set every few links with a tiny brilliant gemstone, like light that had emerged from the dimness after being worn smooth by generation upon generation of hands. The third was a pair of stud earrings, with the theme of "wounds," using irregular pearls, each one flawed—pits, wrinkles, imperfect shapes—but she didn't hide those flaws. Instead she wrapped fine gold around them, like setting a gold rim around a wound.
A flaw isn't a defect; it's a story. This was a truth she only really understood after doing the "Roots" series. She used to chase perfection in her designs, chase flawlessness, chase having every gemstone present itself in its most perfect state. But now she knew that what truly moves people isn't perfection—it's authenticity. And those flaws were the authenticity.
At the same time, she was pushing forward the last two pieces of the "Light Traces" collection. Cartier was pressing hard, but she didn't lower her standards just to save time. Every piece, she had to personally polish the key parts herself—not because she didn't trust Zhenyi's craftsmen, but because she couldn't set her mind at ease. These works carried her name; she had to make sure every detail was above her standard.
Working over twelve hours a day, sometimes so busy she forgot to eat. Lu Shiyan didn't urge her, "Don't overwork yourself," because he knew it wouldn't work. He just showed up at the studio on time every day, bringing food, watching her finish it, then clearing away the lunch box. Not talking, not disturbing her, just being there.
This kind of "company" wasn't "I sacrificed my time for you," but "your time is my time." Sometimes Su Qingyuan would be drawing and drawing, and look up to see him sitting on the sofa handling documents, and she'd have a hazy sense that—they seemed to have been together a very long time already. Long enough that they didn't need to talk, didn't need to interact; as long as the other was there, there was peace of mind.
On Friday night, Su Qingyuan rarely knocked off early. Lu Shiyan took her to a newly opened restaurant for Sichuan food, her favorite. When the maoxuewang came out, its red chili oil bubbling and rolling, Su Qingyuan's eyes were brighter than the chili oil on the table.
"Eat slowly, no one's going to fight you for it." Lu Shiyan poured her a glass of iced water.
Su Qingyuan picked up a piece of tripe, sucked in air at the heat of it but couldn't bear to spit it out, and said in a muffled mumble, "It's good."
Lu Shiyan looked at her lips, reddened by the spice, his gaze as soft as moonlight.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Hm?"
"Once the Paris business is over, I'll take you to see the aurora."
Su Qingyuan's chopsticks paused mid-air, and she looked up at him. "Didn't you say we'd go once everything was over?"
"Everything will never be over." Lu Shiyan's voice was very soft. "Gu Changfeng won't disappear, Su Yurou won't disappear, trouble won't disappear. But we can choose, in the gaps between the trouble, to go and see the aurora."
Su Qingyuan set down her chopsticks and looked into his eyes. The restaurant's warm amber light fell on his face, his expression calm and certain, as if he were speaking of something bound to happen.
"All right. We'll go see the aurora." She picked up her iced water and tapped it against his glass.
The two of them clinked glasses, the iced water rippling faintly in them, reflecting the warm amber light overhead.
Outside the window, Nancheng's night was deepening.
The weather forecast said there would be snow tonight.
Nancheng's first snow was finally about to come.
When they walked out of the restaurant after dinner, Su Qingyuan tilted her head up to look at the sky and waited a long while, but no snow fell. A little disappointed, she pulled her scarf up to cover half her face.
"It didn't snow." Her voice was muffled in the scarf, sounding like a child who hadn't gotten the candy she was promised.
Lu Shiyan looked down at her, reached out to straighten her scarf, his fingertips brushing her cheek. "It might tomorrow. Or the day after. But it will snow eventually."
"How do you know?"
"The weather forecast said so."
"The weather forecast isn't accurate."
"Then I say it's accurate." Lu Shiyan took her hand, fingers interlaced with hers. "My forecast is more accurate than the weather forecast."
Su Qingyuan let him pull her along, and looking at their two clasped hands, she suddenly felt that whether it snowed or not no longer mattered. Because her hand was already held in the warmest place.
The car stopped at the foot of her apartment building. Su Qingyuan didn't get out right away; instead she sat in the passenger seat, watching through the windshield what was drifting down beneath the streetlamps—not snow, but sleet, fine and dense, melting the instant it struck the glass.
"Does this count as the first snow?" she asked.
Lu Shiyan looked out the window, thought for a moment, and said, "It counts. In meteorology, sleet counts as snow too."
Su Qingyuan laughed, her brows and eyes curving, like a child who'd finally gotten her candy.
"Then we saw it together."
"Mm. Together."
The two of them sat in the car, watching the fine, dense sleet beneath the streetlamps, neither of them speaking. The world outside the window was very quiet—quiet enough to hear each other's breathing.
Su Qingyuan suddenly leaned over and dropped a very, very light kiss on Lu Shiyan's lips.
"A first-snow wish."
"What wish?"
"I'm not telling you. If I say it, it won't come true."
Lu Shiyan looked at her bright, shining eyes and didn't press. He reached out and gently ruffled her hair, his voice low. "Then keep it. Tell me when it comes true."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips, pushed the door open, got out, and walked briskly into the building entrance. At the foot of the stairs, she stopped and looked down through the stairwell window. His car was still parked there, headlights off, but the window half-down, and he was gazing up at this building, though she couldn't tell which window he was looking at. She picked up her phone and sent him a message: 【I'm up. You can head back.】
Lu Shiyan's reply: 【Mm. Shut your windows tight.】
Su Qingyuan: 【Done.】
Lu Shiyan: 【Is the heating on?】
Su Qingyuan: 【On. Why are you just like my mom.】
Lu Shiyan: 【You don't like it?】
Su Qingyuan looked at those three words, the corner of her mouth curving to its widest arc, and typed: 【I like it.】
The other side was silent for a few seconds.
Lu Shiyan: 【Good, then. Goodnight.】
Su Qingyuan: 【Goodnight.】
She didn't draw the curtains; instead she stood at the window, watching his headlights come on, drive out of the complex, and disappear around the street corner.
The sleet outside the window was still falling, fine and dense, striking the glass and leaving trails of water. She stretched out a finger and drew a crooked little heart on the windowpane, then watched it slowly blur and vanish, covered over by new streaks of water.
She drew her hand back, walked to the workbench, picked up her sketchbook, and opened to a new page.
Her pen tip touched the paper, and she wrote a line:
"First snow, Nancheng, sleet. He's downstairs."
After writing it, she looked at it for a long time, then folded down a corner of the page, closed the sketchbook, and turned off the light.
In the dark, she lay in bed, listening to the fine, dense sound of the sleet outside the window, and shut her eyes. She dreamed many dreams—dreams with auroras in them, with the Eiffel Tower, with that bare osmanthus tree outside the studio window, with his face.
Every one of those dreams was bright.
