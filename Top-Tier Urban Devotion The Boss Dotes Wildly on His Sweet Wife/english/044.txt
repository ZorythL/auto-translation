Light and Xi
The year Lu Xi turned six, the final stop of Su Qingyuan's "Light Traces" world tour returned to Nancheng. Cartier set the exhibition in the ground-floor lobby of Lu Group — the very same place where the "Roots and Light" joint exhibition had been held all those years ago. Standing in that familiar hall, looking at the pieces arranged in their display cases, Su Qingyuan felt something strange well up inside her. Five years ago, she had held a joint exhibition here, so nervous she couldn't sleep. Five years later, she stood in the same spot, and her heart held nothing but calm.
"Mama, what's this?" Lu Xi pointed at the diamond necklace half-suspended in its setting inside the case.
"That's a necklace Mama made. It's called 'Light Traces.'"
"Why is it called 'Light Traces'?"
"Because light leaves traces behind. Look at this diamond — it hangs here, and when the light shines up from below, it casts a shadow across your collarbone. That shadow is the trace of the light."
Lu Xi tilted her head and thought about it. "Then if I stand here looking at Mama's work, am I a trace of light too?"
Su Qingyuan froze for a moment, then crouched down and looked into her daughter's bright, sparkling eyes. "Yes. You're Mama's most important trace of light."
Lu Xi smiled and threw her arms around Su Qingyuan's neck. "Mama, when I grow up I want to be someone just like you."
Su Qingyuan patted her back. "You don't have to be like Mama. Just be like yourself."
After the exhibition opened, Su Qingyuan gave an interview to a media outlet. The reporter asked, "Ms. Su, your work has gone from 'First Light' to 'Light Traces' to 'Roots,' and now your recent 'Everyday' series. The themes keep changing. What is it that sustains your creativity?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it. "Life."
"Life?"
"Mm. I used to think that creating took inspiration, took talent, took hard work. Later I realized what it really takes is life. You live your days well, you eat with care, you walk with care, you love someone with care — and all those feelings turn into work on their own."
The reporter laughed. "Then what inspiration has your life now given to your next piece?"
Su Qingyuan glanced at Lu Shiyan, standing in a corner of the hall — he was holding Lu Xi's hand, helping her look at the pieces in the cases. Father and daughter had their heads bowed, leaning together, murmuring about something. Suddenly Lu Xi burst out laughing, a crisp, clear sound, like a wind chime.
"My next piece," Su Qingyuan turned back to face the reporter, "might be called 'Xi.'"
The year Lu Xi turned seven, she learned to ride a bicycle.
Su Qingyuan stood in the courtyard, watching her wobble around in circle after circle, while Lu Shiyan ran along behind, holding the seat, drenched in sweat.
"Papa, don't let go!" Lu Xi shouted.
"I won't!" Lu Shiyan shouted back — though he had already quietly let go.
Lu Xi had ridden a dozen meters before she realized there was no one behind her. She panicked, the handlebars veered, and she went toppling — bike and all — into the grass at the side of the path. Su Qingyuan ran over and fished her out of the weeds. Lu Xi didn't cry; she just sat there, dazed, staring at the bicycle lying on the ground.
"Does it hurt?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"No."
"Then why aren't you crying?"
Lu Xi lifted her head and looked at Su Qingyuan. "Papa said crying doesn't solve anything."
Su Qingyuan turned and shot Lu Shiyan a look. He was just walking over, a guilty flicker on his face. She smiled and reached out to brush the bits of grass off Lu Xi. "Papa's right — but when you feel like crying, you can cry too. Cry it out first, then solve the problem."
Lu Xi thought about it. "Then I want to cry now."
"Then cry."
Lu Xi's lips puckered, and the tears came pattering down. Su Qingyuan gathered her into her arms and gently patted her back. Lu Shiyan stood off to the side, at a loss for what to do. Su Qingyuan glared at him. "Go pick up her bike." He hurried off to right the bicycle.
When Lu Xi had finished crying, she wiped her eyes, stood up, and said, "Mama, I want to ride again."
"Okay. Mama will hold it for you."
"I don't want Mama to hold it. I want to ride by myself."
Looking at her red-rimmed eyes and that stubborn expression, Su Qingyuan suddenly felt that she had grown up. Not in body — in spirit. That certainty of "I can do it," so much like her own self back in the day.
Lu Xi rode off. This time she didn't fall. She rode circle after circle, the wind tossing her hair into a tangle, laughing with delight. Su Qingyuan stood in the courtyard, watching her daughter's figure on the bike, and Lu Shiyan came over to stand beside her.
"Like you," he said.
"How am I like that?"
"That refusing-to-lose look."
Su Qingyuan laughed. "Like you. You're the same when you negotiate a deal — you fall, you climb right back up."
Lu Shiyan reached out and pulled her against his shoulder. "It's us."
In the distance, Lu Xi rode her bicycle and shouted toward them, "Papa! Mama! I can ride!" Su Qingyuan waved at her, the corners of her mouth curving up.
The year Lu Xi turned eight, Su Qingyuan received a letter from Australia.
Not from Su Yurou — Su Yurou hadn't written in a long time. Once in a while she'd post a photo of the Melbourne sky to her social feed, and Su Qingyuan would give it a like, occasionally commenting "Beautiful." This letter was from Chen Xiaoman. Inside was a photograph: a small restaurant with a wooden sign hanging by the door that read "Qingyuan's Little Kitchen."
Su Qingyuan stared at that wooden sign for a long while. On the back of the photo was a line of writing: "Sister Qingyuan, my restaurant has opened. I used your name for it — you don't mind, do you? I just wanted to say, without you, there would be no me today. — Xiaoman."
Su Qingyuan showed the photo to Lu Shiyan. He looked at the wooden sign and was silent for a few seconds. "Do you mind?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it. "I don't mind. If she could use my name, it means she thinks I'm a good person."
Lu Shiyan looked at her. "Of course you're good."
Su Qingyuan smiled, picked up her phone, and sent Chen Xiaoman a message: "Got it. The name is lovely. May business flourish."
Chen Xiaoman's reply came quickly: "Thank you, Sister Qingyuan. I'll do my very best."
Su Qingyuan set down her phone and stuck the photo on the wall of her studio. The wall was already covered with photos — Lu Xi's one-month portrait, the scene at Zhou Wanyin's painting exhibition, Qu Ya's new work, the Nordic aurora, the Wall of Love in Paris, the Empire State Building in New York. Every single photo was a light in her life.
The year Lu Xi turned nine, she asked a question.
"Mama, did you used to suffer a lot?"
Su Qingyuan was making osmanthus jam, and her hands paused. "Who told you that?"
"Auntie Wanyin. She said Mama had a hard time before, that you went through a lot of suffering."
Su Qingyuan set down the spoon and looked at her daughter. "Do you think Mama suffers?"
Lu Xi thought about it. "Before, you suffered. Now you don't anymore."
"Why?"
"Because now you have Papa, and you have me."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot. She reached out and gently ruffled her daughter's hair. "Mm. Now I don't suffer anymore."
Lu Xi looked up at her. "Mama, when I grow up I want to be like you too. Not afraid of suffering, as long as it can turn into something sweet."
Su Qingyuan's tears fell. She held her daughter close and buried her face in her small shoulder. "You've already turned into something sweet."
Lu Xi reached out and clumsily patted her back. "Don't cry, Mama. Crying makes you not pretty."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. She let go of her daughter, wiped her eyes, and said, "Mama isn't crying. It's the osmanthus jam — it's too sweet, so sweet it makes Mama's eyes sting."
Lu Xi looked at her and didn't call out the lie. She scooped up a spoonful of the osmanthus jam, put it in her mouth, and smacked her lips. "It really is sweet."
Looking at her daughter's bright, sparkling eyes, Su Qingyuan suddenly felt that all that bitterness really had turned to sweetness.
The year Lu Xi turned ten, Su Qingyuan planted a new osmanthus tree in the courtyard.
Not the original one — that one was still there, blooming every year, fragrant every year. This new one stood beside the old osmanthus tree, small, no taller than a person. Lu Xi asked, "Mama, why are you planting a new one?"
Su Qingyuan crouched down and pressed the soil firm. "Because your Papa planted the old one. This one is for you."
"For me?"
"Mm. When you grow up, it'll grow up too. When you bloom, it'll bloom too."
Lu Xi crouched down and looked at the little osmanthus tree. "Then when I grow up, I want to be just like it."
"Like what?"
"Standing here. Blooming. All fragrant."
Su Qingyuan smiled and pulled her daughter into a hug. "Okay. Standing here. Blooming. All fragrant."
Lu Shiyan walked out of the house carrying two cups of tea. Looking at the mother and daughter crouched together in the courtyard, hands covered in mud, the corners of his mouth lifted. He set the tea on the stone table, walked over, and crouched down. "All planted?"
"Done." Su Qingyuan stood up and brushed the soil off her knees.
Lu Shiyan looked at the little tree. "When will it bloom?"
"Next year."
"Then we'll all watch it together next year."
"Okay."
Lu Xi stood up and tugged at the hem of Lu Shiyan's shirt. "Papa, crouch down."
Lu Shiyan crouched down. Lu Xi rose onto her tiptoes and kissed him on the cheek. "Thank you, Papa, for planting the old osmanthus tree. Thank you, Mama, for planting the new osmanthus tree. Thank you both, for letting me be here."
Lu Shiyan went still. Su Qingyuan went still too. Then the two of them smiled at the same moment, and as they smiled, their eyes turned red.
Lu Xi looked at them. "Why are you crying?"
"We're not crying," Lu Shiyan said.
"What about Mama?"
"Mama isn't crying either." Su Qingyuan wiped the corner of her eye.
Lu Xi looked at them and smiled. "You grown-ups really aren't honest."
Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan exchanged a glance, and both of them laughed.
That night, Su Qingyuan leaned against Lu Shiyan's shoulder, sitting beneath the osmanthus tree in the courtyard. The moonlight was lovely, lighting the tree full of osmanthus blossoms until they looked like scattered gold. Lu Xi was already asleep, and the courtyard was very quiet, with only the calls of the autumn insects.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Do you think — what will Xi-Xi do when she grows up?"
"Whatever she does, she'll do it well."
"Why do you always say that?"
"Because I always feel that way."
Su Qingyuan laughed. She reached out and clasped his hand. "Lu Shiyan, thank you."
"Thank me for what?"
"Thank you for stopping the car that day."
Lu Shiyan lowered his head to look at her. The moonlight fell across her face, and there was light in her eyes — exactly the same as that day outside the glass door.
"You're welcome."
He lowered his head and kissed her forehead. Osmanthus blossoms fell onto their shoulders, like golden snow. In the distance, the lights of the city merged into a single warm sea of light. In the courtyard, the old osmanthus tree and the newly planted one stood side by side, one tall, one small. Like them — and like the two of them as well.
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes and breathed in the fragrance of the osmanthus. She thought back to many years ago, when she sat on the attic floorboards of the old Su family house, drawing. She had drawn light. Back then she hadn't known that one day, that light would become real.
Now she knew.
That light had become Lu Shiyan, had become Lu Xi, had become this courtyard full of osmanthus fragrance, had become the scalding, real happiness she held in her hands.
