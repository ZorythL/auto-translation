Chapter 27 — Roots and Undercurrents: On Her Home Turf, Her Word Is Law
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626229.html

Su Yurou's photograph was like a stone dropped into a lake—it sent out a single ripple, then sank back into stillness. Su Qingyuan didn't press for answers, didn't investigate, didn't even mention it to anyone again. She simply locked the envelope away in a drawer and carried on with her daily work—as if that letter had never existed at all.
But that silence was, in itself, a kind of answer.
Su Yurou wanted her to be afraid, to panic, to go running all over town asking, "How does she have an old photo of me?" Su Qingyuan did nothing. Her lack of reaction stung Su Yurou far more than any furious outburst could have. Because when you've gone to all that trouble to hand someone a knife, and they won't even spare it a glance—that's the cruelest cut of all.
A few days later, Su Qingyuan received a text. The number was new, but the tone was one she knew far too well.
"Aren't you the least bit curious who took the photo?"
Su Qingyuan looked at it for two seconds, then typed back two words: "Not curious."
The other end went silent for a full ten minutes. Then came another message: "You weren't always like this. You used to get hurt, used to cry, used to ask me why I treated you that way."
This time Su Qingyuan's reply was even shorter: "That was then."
The silence on the other end stretched longer still. Su Qingyuan didn't wait for more. She slipped the phone into her pocket and went back to polishing the first sample piece of her "Roots" series.
She didn't tell Lu Shiyan about the text. Not to hide it—it simply wasn't worth mentioning. What Su Yurou was doing now was like a child waving a plastic knife in front of an adult: alarming to look at, but incapable of so much as scratching her. The real threat wasn't these childish probings—it was Gu Changfeng, who lurked behind Su Yurou and had yet to show his face.
He was too quiet.
After the Hengsheng affair, Gu Changfeng had vanished as if into thin air. He appeared at no public events, commented on nothing, and had even moved Dingfeng Group's internal meetings online. That kind of quiet wasn't normal. It was the suffocating, heavy stillness that comes before a storm.
Lu Shiyan had clearly noticed it too. Lately he'd been coming to the studio more often—sometimes not even in the evening; he'd show up suddenly in the afternoon, settle onto the sofa with his documents, and work there for hours at a stretch. Su Qingyuan knew it wasn't because he was free—it was his way of standing guard at her side without intruding on her work.
She didn't call him out on it. She just brewed an extra pot of tea each day, set it on the side table by the sofa, and went on with her own work. The two of them, each absorbed in their own tasks, would occasionally glance up, catch each other's eye, share a smile, then bow their heads again.
That kind of unspoken understanding was more moving than any sweet words could ever be.
The first piece of the "Roots" series was finished late one Thursday night.
It was a brooch, its main body a cross-section of a tree—growth rings, fissures, scars, all faithfully recreated through micro-pavé work. Each ring was a different gemstone: at the very center sat a deep brown diamond, representing the beginning; moving outward came dark green emeralds for growth; then golden sapphires for the light that settled out of all the struggle; and the outermost ring was a layer of near-transparent white diamonds, thin as frost, like a film of protection.
The rings weren't perfect circles. There were several obvious "wounds"—and in those places she'd used darker stones, not to disguise them, but to let them become part of the rings.
Su Qingyuan cradled the brooch in her palm and studied it under the lamplight for a long time.
This tree was herself. That deep brown diamond at the very center was the attic of the old Su family house—cramped, dim, but the place where she had begun. Each ring spreading outward was a struggle, a stage of growth, a process of new flesh growing out of old wounds. That thin outermost layer of white diamonds was her life now—calm, cool, yet shot through with light.
She took a photo of it, sent it to no one, just saved it into an album on her phone titled "Roots."
Then she wrote a single line for the series: "Roots aren't the place that traps you. They're the place you grew out of."
That line, she decided, would go on the very first page when the series launched.
Having finished the first piece of the "Roots" series, Su Qingyuan did something rare and gave herself a day off. Not to rest—but to see an exhibition.
The Nancheng Art Museum was hosting a contemporary art exhibition called "Land and Bloodline." The participating artists came from all over the world, and the theme of every work was "roots"—homeland, family, memory, identity. When Su Qingyuan saw the exhibition listing online, something in her stirred. Her own "Roots" series was still in the works, and seeing this show right now might just spark some new inspiration.
Lu Shiyan couldn't accompany her—he had a board meeting today that he absolutely had to attend. He arranged for his driver to take her, and Su Qingyuan didn't refuse, because she genuinely wanted to focus on the exhibition and didn't want to waste time in traffic.
The museum wasn't crowded—it was a weekday, after all. Su Qingyuan walked alone through the spacious galleries, looking at the paintings on the walls, the installation pieces, the video works, her steps slow, examining each one carefully.
When she reached a corner deep in the gallery, she stopped.
On the wall hung an enormous black-and-white photograph—the roots of a tree. Not the lush canopy of leaves and branches above ground, but the part below the surface, in the darkness: roots crossing and tangling together. They burrowed deep into the soil, some thick and sturdy, some slender, some already rotting away, some just putting out new shoots.
The photograph was titled: "The Part You Can't See."
On the placard beside it, the artist had written a single line: "People are always quick to praise the leaves and branches, yet forget that it's the roots that hold the tree upright."
Su Qingyuan stood before that photograph for a long time.
She suddenly remembered the old pagoda tree in the back courtyard of the Su family house. As a child, when she'd been beaten and scolded, ignored and left out, locked in the attic and forbidden to eat, she would sneak off to that tree, sit on the roots exposed above the ground, bury her face in her knees, and cry. Those roots were rough and hard, digging painfully into her legs—but to her they were the one thing in that whole house that would never hurt her.
She took out her phone, meaning to photograph the piece. Just as she raised the lens, a voice came from beside her.
"You like this one too?"
Su Qingyuan turned to see a woman in her mid-forties: short hair, no makeup, dressed in loose linen clothes, a sketchbook in her hand. Her eyes were bright, as if they held a great many stories.
"Very much," Su Qingyuan said, lowering her phone. "'The Part You Can't See'—it's a wonderful title."
The woman smiled. "I came up with it. Because I'm the one who took this photograph."
Su Qingyuan paused, then took a fresh look at the woman before her. "You're... Qu Ya?"
She remembered the lead artist of "Land and Bloodline" from the exhibition brochure—Qu Ya, an internationally renowned photographic artist whose work centered on the theme of "root systems," and who had held dozens of solo exhibitions around the world.
Qu Ya nodded with a smile. "You know me?"
"I've seen your work. I never imagined I'd run into you here."
"You came to see the exhibition?"
"Mm. I'm a jewelry designer. I've been working on a series about 'roots' lately, and I wanted to come look for inspiration."
Qu Ya's eyes lit up. "A jewelry designer? What's your name?"
"Su Qingyuan."
Qu Ya thought for a moment, then suddenly let out an "Ah!" "You're the designer behind the 'First Light' collection? My daughter bought your 'First Snow' earrings—she's absolutely smitten with them, wears them every single day."
Su Qingyuan was a little surprised; she hadn't expected an artist of Qu Ya's caliber to know her name.
"Thank you—I'm glad your daughter likes them."
"Not 'likes'—obsessed," Qu Ya said with a laugh, shaking the sketchbook in her hand. "She's a hard child to please. For her to wear something every day, it has to be no ordinary thing."
The two of them fell into conversation. From "The Part You Can't See" to the philosophical meaning of "roots," from photography to jewelry design, from creative work to life itself. Qu Ya was a talkative person, but never grating—every word she spoke carried weight.
"You said you're working on a 'Roots' series," Qu Ya said, opening her sketchbook to show Su Qingyuan the drawings inside. "This is a new set of pieces I've been shooting lately. The theme is 'the roots of the city'—the people who've lived in a city for many years yet have never felt they belong here. Their roots aren't in the soil; they're in memory."
Su Qingyuan looked at those sketches, and something struck her right in the heart. The roots of the city, in memory. Wasn't she exactly that kind of person? She'd lived in Nancheng for more than twenty years, yet had never once felt this was her true home. Her roots weren't Nancheng, weren't the old Su family house—they were those memories: the good and the bad, the sweet and the bitter, all added together, forming who she was.
"Teacher Qu, would you be interested in... collaborating, just once?" Even Su Qingyuan herself was a little startled to hear the words come out. She wasn't someone who extended invitations lightly.
Qu Ya looked at her, curiosity in her gaze. "Collaborate how?"
"You photograph 'roots,' I make 'roots' in jewelry. The same theme, two different ways of expressing it. Maybe we could put on an exhibition together."
Qu Ya was silent for a few seconds, then smiled. "I've done photography exhibitions for twenty years, and no one has ever proposed this kind of collaboration to me." She closed her sketchbook and held out her hand. "Su Qingyuan, I'm looking forward to it."
Su Qingyuan took her hand, her heart beating a little fast.
This was no ordinary collaboration offer. Qu Ya was an internationally renowned photographic artist. To work with her meant that the "Roots" series, before it had even debuted, would already carry a top-tier artistic endorsement.
The two exchanged contact details and agreed to meet next week at Qu Ya's studio to discuss the details. As Su Qingyuan walked out of the museum, the sun was just right, falling warm on her shoulders. She looked up at the sky—it was high and blue, with a few white clouds drifting lazily by.
She suddenly felt that the heavens seemed to be helping her. Not the kind of help where good fortune falls from the sky, but the kind where—once you're ready, the opportunity comes. Before, she hadn't been ready, so opportunities had slipped right past her without her even knowing. Now she was ready, and she caught every chance squarely in her hands.
She took out her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message:
[Ran into the photographer Qu Ya at the museum today. Her daughter's a fan of yours? No, wait, her daughter's a fan of mine? Anyway, she really loves the "First Snow" earrings. We talked about the possibility of collaborating—doing a joint "Roots" exhibition together.]
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: [Qu Ya? Tickets to her solo exhibitions sell out three months in advance. How did you run into her?]
Su Qingyuan: [Fate?]
Lu Shiyan: [Not fate. It's that you now stand at a height where she'd notice you.]
Su Qingyuan stared at that line, the corners of her mouth curving up.
Once, she had looked up to people like Qu Ya, thinking them impossibly out of reach. Now Qu Ya had approached her first, exchanged contact details first, asked to meet first. It wasn't that Qu Ya had changed—it was that she had. She stood in a different place now, saw a different view, and the people who saw that view were different too.
Su Qingyuan: [Are you done with your meeting?]
Lu Shiyan: [Just finished. Where are you? I'll come get you.]
Su Qingyuan sent her location. Fifteen minutes later, Lu Shiyan's car pulled up at the museum entrance.
As Su Qingyuan got in, she noticed a white paper bag on the back seat, printed with a logo she'd never seen before.
"What's this?"
"Passed by a new dessert shop and picked it up on the way," Lu Shiyan said, starting the car, his tone as casual as if he were remarking that the weather was nice today.
Su Qingyuan opened the bag. Inside was an exquisite strawberry cake, the cream adorned with gold leaf and edible flower petals, too pretty to look like food. She picked up her phone and snapped a photo, then scooped a spoonful into her mouth. The cream was light, the cake fluffy, the sweet-tart of the strawberry melting on the tip of her tongue.
"It's good."
"Mm."
"This dessert shop you 'passed by'—what's it called?"
Lu Shiyan gave the name. Su Qingyuan made a mental note of it. Not because she wanted to buy more—but because she wanted to remember that behind every one of his "on the way" gestures, nothing was ever on the way.
As the car turned into the creative park district, Su Qingyuan saw someone standing at the entrance to her studio.
Her smile slowly faded.
It was Su Yurou.
Not the Su Yurou of before, with her flawless makeup and meticulous outfits. She wore a black down jacket, jeans, canvas shoes, her hair loose and unkempt, no makeup on her face. She looked like an ordinary young woman who'd been waiting a long time in the cold wind. But her eyes gave her away—what lay in those eyes was exactly the same as the day they'd torn off all pretense in the hotel lobby months ago, only more intense now.
Lu Shiyan had clearly seen her too. He didn't stop the car; he kept driving forward.
"Not stopping?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"Not stopping. She's come to find you—she won't come just once. See her today, and she'll be back tomorrow. Let her wait."
Su Qingyuan glanced at him and said nothing. She knew he was right. Su Yurou hadn't come today to apologize, hadn't come to make peace—she'd come to test the waters, to see whether Su Qingyuan still had any feelings left toward her. If there were feelings, it meant she still cared. To not care at all—that was the ultimate indifference.
The car circled the park district once, then stopped in a small alley behind the studio. Lu Shiyan cut the engine and turned to look at her.
"Do you want to see her?"
Su Qingyuan thought it over, then shook her head. "No."
"Then don't."
"But she'll keep waiting at the door."
"Then let her wait." Lu Shiyan's voice was very calm. "Her time is hers, your time is yours. You're under no obligation to spend your time answering hers."
Su Qingyuan leaned back against her seat, looking out at the gray, overcast sky beyond the windshield. The two of them just sat there in the car, neither saying a word. At the studio door, Su Yurou was still waiting. Every so often she'd lower her head to check her phone, then look up and glance around, pacing back and forth on the spot, looking restless and on edge.
Forty minutes later, she finally left. As she went, she looked back once at the studio door, her expression carrying both resentment and a stubborn "I'll be back."
Su Qingyuan waited until she was well out of sight before pushing open the door and getting out.
Inside the studio, everything was as usual. The butterfly orchid on the windowsill had bloomed with new flowers, the tools on the workbench were laid out neatly, the sketchbook open to the page she'd drawn up to yesterday.
She walked to the window, looked toward where Su Yurou had disappeared, was silent for a few seconds, then drew the curtains closed.
Not avoidance—a choice. Her gaze was to be saved for things worth looking at.
That evening, Lu Shiyan drove her home. In the car, Su Qingyuan suddenly asked, "Do you think Su Yurou came today on her own idea, or because Gu Changfeng sent her?"
Lu Shiyan tapped the steering wheel twice with his fingers. "Her own idea. Gu Changfeng wouldn't have her do something this stupid—coming straight to you with no preparation at all. Gu Changfeng's style is: either he doesn't move, or he strikes for the kill in one blow. What Su Yurou did today was far too amateur."
Su Qingyuan nodded. She'd thought the same. But Su Yurou's "amateurism" worried her all the more—because amateurs are unpredictable. A professional has methods, patterns, traces to follow; an amateur is capable of anything, because she herself doesn't even know what she's doing.
"I'll keep an eye out," Su Qingyuan said.
Lu Shiyan reached over and gave her hand a squeeze, saying nothing more.
The car stopped below her apartment building. Before getting out, Su Qingyuan suddenly leaned over and kissed him on the cheek.
"The cake today was delicious. Thank you."
Lu Shiyan touched the spot where she'd kissed him, the corner of his mouth lifting slightly. "I'll buy more tomorrow."
"You don't have to buy it every day—I'll get fat."
"You'd still look good even if you did."
Cheeks flushed, Su Qingyuan pushed the door open and got out, walking briskly into the building's entrance without looking back. But she knew he was surely watching her. It was always like this—she would leave, and he would keep watching, until the light came on in her window, before he'd start the car and drive off.
She went upstairs, turned on the light, walked to the window, and looked down. Sure enough, his car was still parked there, headlights off, but the window half-down, so she could see his profile. She picked up her phone and sent him a message: [I'm home. Go on back now.]
Lu Shiyan's reply: [Mm. Close your window tight—it's getting colder tonight.]
Su Qingyuan looked at that line, the corners of her mouth curving up, and closed the window. Then she drew the curtains, leaned against the wall by the window, and listened to his car start, drive off, and disappear into the night.
She lowered her head and looked at her own hand. When she'd met Qu Ya at the museum today, this hand had held her phone, her heart racing. When Su Yurou stood at the studio door, this hand hadn't trembled. When Lu Shiyan took her hand, this hand had been wrapped in his palm, so warm.
This hand had drawn thousands of sketches, polished hundreds of stones, signed Cartier's contract, and held Qu Ya's hand. It wasn't big enough yet, but it was already strong enough. In time, it would only grow stronger.
