Light Years Away
The year Lu Xi turned eighteen, two university acceptance letters arrived. One was from Central Saint Martins in London, the other from the Cartier Design Academy in Paris. She sat beneath the osmanthus tree in the courtyard, turning the two letters over in her hands again and again.
Su Qingyuan came out carrying two cups of tea and handed her one. "Have you decided?"
"No." Lu Xi took the tea and sipped it. "Mom, how did you choose, back then?"
Su Qingyuan sat down beside her and thought for a moment. "I didn't choose. Back then there was only one option—to keep moving forward."
Lu Xi was quiet for a while. "So should I pick the better one?"
"Which one is better?"
"The Cartier Design Academy. They offered a full scholarship, and after I graduate I could go straight to work at Cartier."
"Do you want to go?"
Lu Xi lowered her head, her fingers tracing the edge of the envelope. "I do. But I'm afraid people will say I got in because of my mother's connections."
Su Qingyuan looked at her and smiled. "Xixi, do you know what I was most afraid of, back then?"
"What?"
"I was most afraid people would say I was leaning on your father. Later I figured it out—what other people say doesn't matter. What matters is whether you yourself know what it is you're standing on."
Lu Xi lifted her head. "What am I standing on?"
"Your hands." Su Qingyuan took hold of her hand. "Your hands can carve stone, can sketch designs, can turn the light inside you into something real. That—no one can ever take it from you."
Lu Xi looked at her own hands. There were calluses on her palms, in exactly the same place as the calluses on Su Qingyuan's. She curled her fingers into a fist. "Mom, I understand now. I'll go to London."
"Why London?"
"Because Central Saint Martins is harder to get into. A place that's hard to get into proves I made it on my own."
Su Qingyuan smiled and reached out to ruffle her daughter's hair. "Good. Mom supports you."
The day Lu Xi left, a light rain was falling over Nancheng. Su Qingyuan stood in the airport departure hall, adjusting the scarf around Lu Xi's neck. "Call me when you land."
"Okay."
"Don't skip meals every day."
"Okay."
"Put on more clothes when it gets cold."
"Okay. Mom, if you keep going, I'm going to cry."
Su Qingyuan's eyes had already gone red. She didn't cry; she just reached out and held her daughter. "Xixi, Mom is proud of you."
Lu Xi's tears fell. She buried her face against Su Qingyuan's shoulder and cried for a while. Then she let go and turned to look at Lu Shiyan. He stood a step away, pushing the suitcase, his expression calm. But Lu Xi saw that his eyes had reddened too.
"Dad."
"Mm."
"I'm leaving."
"Mm. Call when you land."
"Okay."
Lu Shiyan stepped over and put his arms around his daughter. He held her tightly—tighter than any time before. Lu Xi froze for a moment, then hugged him back just as tightly.
"Dad, I've grown up."
"Mm. You've grown up."
"Don't worry about me."
"Okay."
Lu Xi released him, stepped back, wiped her tears, and smiled. "I'm going now. Take care of yourselves."
She turned and wheeled her suitcase toward the security checkpoint. Su Qingyuan watched her retreating figure—thin, her back held very straight. She remembered, years ago, how she too had wheeled a suitcase away from the old Su family house. Back then no one had seen her off, no one had told her to "call when you land." But her daughter had that. She had a mother who loved her, a father who loved her, and a whole courtyard of osmanthus-tree memories.
"She'll be just fine," Lu Shiyan said, standing beside her.
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder. "Mm. She'll be just fine."
In her first week in London, Lu Xi posted a long update on her social feed. She wrote about London's weather, the school cafeteria, her dorm roommates, and how she'd felt after giving her first presentation in English. The last paragraph she wrote for Su Qingyuan: "Mom, today I used the carving knife you gave me. A classmate said it was beautiful and asked where I bought it. I told them: it's my mother's. When she was young, she used this knife to carve out her own light. Now I'm going to use it too—to carve out mine."
Su Qingyuan read that post and cried. Lu Shiyan handed her a tissue. "She's like you."
Su Qingyuan wiped her tears. "She's braver than I was."
"You were brave too."
"No. When I went to Paris back then, I was so nervous my palms were sweating."
"But you still went."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "Mm. I still went."
The year Lu Xi turned twenty-two, she graduated. She didn't return to Nancheng; she stayed in London and joined an independent jewelry studio. The studio was small—only five people, including her—but the work they made had real soul. Su Qingyuan saw her pieces online and sent her a message: "Very good. Better than I was at your age."
Lu Xi's reply came quickly: "Impossible. Your 'First Light' collection from back then—I couldn't make that in a lifetime."
Su Qingyuan: "You can. Your light is just different from mine."
Lu Xi: "Different how?"
Su Qingyuan: "My light grew out of darkness. Your light grew up in sunlight. Both are bright—but they're not the same."
Lu Xi was silent for a long time. Then she replied: "Mom, thank you for raising me in the sunlight."
Su Qingyuan looked at those words, and the tears fell again.
The year Lu Xi turned twenty-five, she started dating a boyfriend. An Englishman named James, an architecture student, who wore round-framed glasses and spoke in a slow, deliberate way. The first time Su Qingyuan had a video call with him, he was so nervous he spilled his coffee on his white shirt.
"Hello, Auntie. I'm James."
"Hello. Are you drinking coffee?"
"I just spilled it. I'll skip it."
Su Qingyuan laughed. Lu Xi, beside him, was doubled over laughing. James turned red. "Xi, stop laughing."
Lu Xi only laughed harder. Lu Shiyan walked into frame from off-screen, holding a cup of tea. "Who's this?"
"Xixi's boyfriend," Su Qingyuan said.
Lu Shiyan glanced at the red-faced young man on the screen with the coffee stain on his shirt and was silent for a few seconds. "Hello."
"Hello, Uncle."
"Would you like some tea?"
"No… no thank you, Uncle."
Su Qingyuan smiled and ended the call. Lu Shiyan sat down beside her. "What do you think?"
"Think of what?"
"That James."
"He's nice. Sincere."
Lu Shiyan considered it. "Mm. Sincerity matters most."
That autumn, James came to Nancheng. He brought two bottles of red wine, a box of chocolates, and a bouquet of white chamomile. When Su Qingyuan saw the chamomile, she froze. "How did you know I like these?"
James pushed up his glasses. "Xi told me."
Su Qingyuan turned to glance at Lu Xi, who stuck out her tongue. Su Qingyuan smiled and took the flowers. "Come in."
James stayed in Nancheng for a week. Su Qingyuan took him to the creative park, the lobby of the Lu Group building, that Hunan restaurant, and the replica of the Montmartre Wall of Love—there was a small park in Nancheng with a Wall of Love too, built with donations from Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan. James stood before the Wall of Love, looking at the wall covered in "I love you"s, then suddenly turned to Lu Xi and said, in Chinese: "I love you."
Lu Xi froze. "When did you learn that?"
"Yesterday. Uncle taught me."
Lu Xi turned to look at Lu Shiyan. He stood a little way off, expression calm. Su Qingyuan stood beside him, holding back a laugh. "Dad!" Lu Xi cried. Lu Shiyan didn't change his expression. "He asked me. I just told him how to pronounce those three words." Su Qingyuan finally laughed out loud.
The year Lu Xi turned twenty-eight, she got married. The wedding was held at that manor in Nancheng—the same place where Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan had married, years ago. Beneath the osmanthus trees, white banquet tables, the whole courtyard filled with sweet fragrance. Lu Xi wore a wedding gown of her own design—white satin, osmanthus branches embroidered along the hem, much like her mother's dress from years before, but not quite the same. On her hem there was one small extra butterfly. She said the butterfly meant freedom.
James stood beneath the osmanthus trees, watching Lu Xi walk toward him, his eyes reddening. He wiped his eyes, and they reddened again. Su Qingyuan sat in the first row, watching her daughter walk step by step toward that young man, gripping Lu Shiyan's hand very tightly.
"Are you nervous?" Lu Shiyan asked quietly.
"I'm not nervous."
"Then why is my hand about to be crushed?"
Su Qingyuan smiled, loosened her grip, then took his hand again. "Xixi looks so beautiful today."
"Mm. Like you."
Lu Xi reached James, and the two of them stood face to face. There was no officiant, no long vows. Lu Xi said, "James, thank you for coming all that way to find me." James said, "Xi, thank you for letting me find you." Then they exchanged rings—Lu Xi gave James a plain platinum band, and James gave Lu Xi a moonstone ring with four layers of glow. The very center was deep blue, then silver-white, then warm gold, and the outermost layer was a ring of transparent, almost invisible light. Lu Xi looked at the ring and froze. "This is…"
"Your mother's formula. I asked Uncle for it." James's voice trembled a little. "The fourth layer of light—she said it was called 'Future.' But I think 'Xi' suits it better."
Lu Xi's tears fell. She turned to look at Su Qingyuan. Su Qingyuan sat there, smiling, her tears falling too.
The year Lu Xi turned thirty-two, she gave birth to a pair of twins. A boy and a girl—the elder brother named Lu Yinuo, the younger sister named Lu Yiyan. Su Qingyuan had become a grandmother.
She held her little granddaughter, standing beneath the osmanthus tree in the courtyard. The little one's round eyes were wide open, gazing at the canopy full of small golden flowers, reaching out to grab at them. Su Qingyuan smiled. "You like osmanthus too?"
The little one babbled back at her.
Lu Shiyan stood beside her, holding their little grandson. Yinuo was very quiet, leaning against his shoulder, and before long he had fallen asleep.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Who do you think Yinuo takes after?"
"You. Quiet, but with light inside."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "And Yiyan?"
"Xixi. Lively, but focused."
"How do you always see everything?"
"I've been watching for thirty years. If I still couldn't see it, all that watching would have been wasted."
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder, looking at the little granddaughter in her arms. The little one had already fallen asleep, her tiny hand still clutching a single osmanthus flower.
"Lu Shiyan, what do you think they'll do when they grow up?"
"Whatever they do, they'll be wonderful."
"Why do you always say that?"
"Because every time, that's how I feel."
Su Qingyuan smiled. She looked at the osmanthus trees in the courtyard—the old one had grown taller, its crown spread open like an enormous umbrella. The newly planted one had grown up too, standing shoulder to shoulder with the old tree, their branches and leaves interlacing until you couldn't tell one from the other.
In the distance, Lu Xi and James were playing on the lawn with Yinuo and Yiyan. Their laughter carried over, crisp and clear, like wind chimes.
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, breathing in the fragrance of the osmanthus. She remembered, years ago, that rainy night when she crouched at the side of the road, soaked through. A car had stopped, the window rolling down to reveal his face. He'd said, "Get in." She got in, and from that moment on she never got out again.
That year, she was twenty-four. This year, she was fifty-six. Thirty-two years. In thirty-two years she had made many works, won many awards, traveled to many places. But the work she was proudest of was not "First Light," not "Light Traces," not "Roots"—it was Lu Xi. It was Lu Yinuo and Lu Yiyan. It was this courtyard, this osmanthus tree, and this man who had stood by her side for thirty-two years.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"When we're old, what do you think we'll be like?"
"We'll watch the osmanthus together. Drink tea together. Wait together for Xixi to come home."
"And then?"
"And then we'll turn into osmanthus trees together."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "Osmanthus trees have no feet. They can't wander around."
"Then I'll become the osmanthus tree beside you. Wherever you want to go, the wind will carry my words to you."
Su Qingyuan's tears fell. She leaned against his shoulder, looking up at the osmanthus blossoms covering the tree. Sunlight slipped down through the gaps in the leaves, falling on the two of them, on the little granddaughter in her arms, on the golden petals scattered across the ground.
Everything was quiet, everything was beautiful.
From the lawn in the distance, Lu Xi's voice carried over: "Mom! Come take a photo!"
Su Qingyuan wiped her tears. "Coming."
She stood up and took Lu Shiyan's hand. The two of them walked side by side toward that patch of sunlight. Behind them, the osmanthus trees swayed gently in the wind, petals rustling down like golden snow.
Many years later, someone asked Su Qingyuan: "What do you think is the finest work of your life?"
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment, then said: "Light."
"What light?"
She didn't answer. She looked out the window, where the two osmanthus trees in the courtyard were glittering in the sunlight. On the swing beneath them, Yinuo and Yiyan were squabbling over a single flower. Lu Xi came out carrying a plate of sliced fruit, James following behind with two cups of juice in his hands. Lu Shiyan came out from inside the house and draped a coat over her shoulders. "The wind's picking up."
She smiled. Then she turned back and said to the person who had asked: "The finest works are the light that shines into your life. And the light that you become."
