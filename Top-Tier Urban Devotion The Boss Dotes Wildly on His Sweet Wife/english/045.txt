Chapter 45 — The Direction of Light (Side Story)
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626247.html

The year Lu Xi turned twelve, she picked up a carving knife for the first time.
Not a toy—it was a carving knife Su Qingyuan took down from her workbench, one she'd used for many years. The handle had been worn smooth, and the blade gave off a cold, white gleam under the lamp. When Su Qingyuan handed her the knife, her expression was more solemn than at any launch she'd ever held for her own work. "This knife has been with Mama for a long time. Today I'm lending it to you."
"Why lend it?"
"Because you're going to use this knife to carve out the thing inside your heart."
Lu Xi took the carving knife. It was heavy—heavier than she'd imagined. She sat down at the workbench. In front of her lay a moonstone—not large, ordinary in quality, its surface threaded with fine cracks. Su Qingyuan hadn't picked the material for her; Lu Xi had chosen it herself from the pile of rough stones. She'd searched for a long time, and in the end picked up this small, unremarkable stone. "Mama, I want to carve this one."
Su Qingyuan glanced at the stone and didn't ask why. "All right. First sketch a draft on paper, and once you've finished it, then set the knife to the stone."
Lu Xi held the pencil and drew in her sketchbook for a long while. Drawing and erasing, erasing and drawing, over and over again. Su Qingyuan didn't rush her; she sat beside her, quietly sipping tea. Sunlight poured in through the window and fell over mother and daughter, warm and golden. An hour later, Lu Xi turned the sketchbook around to show Su Qingyuan.
On it was a tree—not a whole tree, but the roots of one: thick, sturdy roots driving deep into the soil, crisscrossing and entwined, like blood vessels. It was very much like that photograph of Qu Ya's, but the lines were more childlike, more tender, carrying the particular touch of a twelve-year-old girl.
"You want to carve this tree?"
"Mm. I want to carve roots."
Su Qingyuan looked at her, silent for a few seconds. "Why do you want to carve roots?"
Lu Xi thought it over. "Because the roots are the part you can't see. But they're very important."
Su Qingyuan's nose stung. She remembered the year she was twenty-six, creating the "Roots" series—she'd thought the very same thing. She reached out and gently ruffled her daughter's hair. "All right. Mama will teach you."
The first cut was the hardest. Lu Xi held the carving knife, her hand trembling a little. Su Qingyuan didn't grip the knife for her; she only laid her own hand over the back of Lu Xi's, steadying it. "Take your time. If one cut isn't enough, make another. It doesn't matter if you carve it wrong—the stone can be replaced. But the thing in your heart, you can't leave it uncarved just because you're afraid of carving it wrong."
Lu Xi drew a deep breath and made the first cut. The feel of the knife biting into the stone traveled up through her fingertips—a little rough, a little cool. She watched the blade leave a shallow trace on the stone's surface, her heart pounding fast, but her hand no longer trembling.
That afternoon, Lu Xi carved at that moonstone for a long time. Su Qingyuan stayed beside her the whole while, offering a word of guidance now and then, but most of the time simply watching in silence. When Lu Shiyan came home from work, he stood in the doorway of the studio, saw mother and daughter with their heads bent close together over the workbench, and didn't go in. He leaned against the doorframe and watched for a long time. Not until Lu Xi lifted her head and called out to him: "Papa! Look what I carved!"
He walked over and bent down to look at the stone. The lines were still clumsy, the composition not yet balanced, but in those cut-by-cut traces there was something very familiar to him—seriousness. The exact same seriousness as her mother's.
"It's beautiful," he said.
"Really?"
"Really. A lot better than the first time Papa ever cooked."
Lu Xi laughed and turned to look at Su Qingyuan. "Mama, can I carve again tomorrow?"
"You can. I'll keep lending you this knife."
"I don't have to give it back?"
"When you're done with it, just return it to Mama."
Lu Xi carefully set the carving knife down on the workbench, hopped off her chair, and ran outside to play.
Su Qingyuan watched her daughter's retreating figure and smiled. Lu Shiyan stood beside her. "Is she going to study jewelry design?"
"Not necessarily. Maybe it's just a passing whim."
"It won't be," Lu Shiyan said. "She's like you. Once she's set her mind on something, she won't let go of it easily."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "How do you know?"
"Because I watched you do it."
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder, looking at the carving knife on the workbench. Fine stone dust still clung to the blade, glittering under the lamp. She remembered, long ago, that she too had a carving knife, bought with money she'd saved up over a long time. She'd used that knife for many years, and later gave it to a young apprentice at Zhou Wanyin's studio. She didn't know how that apprentice was doing now, but her carving knife was surely still somewhere, carving out the light in someone's heart.
The year Lu Xi turned thirteen, she carved her first complete piece.
Still the roots of a tree, but far more mature than that sketch from a year before. The lines were more fluid, the composition more balanced, the details richer—at the place where the roots crossed she'd carved a few fine cracks, like wounds, and also like the traces of healing. Su Qingyuan looked at the piece and was silent for a long time.
"Mama, is it no good?" Lu Xi was a little nervous.
"It's not that it's no good." Su Qingyuan picked up the piece and held it to the light. "It's that it's too good."
"Too good?"
"Mm. At thirteen, you've carved what Mama only managed to carve at twenty-six."
Lu Xi was stunned. She lowered her head and looked at her own hands. There were calluses in her palms—growing in the very same spot as the calluses on Su Qingyuan's hands.
"Mama, can I keep going?"
"You can. But you have to promise Mama one thing."
"What thing?"
"Don't carve for other people. Carve for yourself. For the light in your own heart."
Lu Xi nodded. "I promise."
Su Qingyuan placed the piece in the display case, setting it alongside her own "Roots" series. One large and one small, one tall and one short, like two trees—one an old tree, one a fresh seedling. When Lu Shiyan came home from work and saw a new piece in the display case, he crouched down and looked at it for a long time.
"Xixi carved this?"
"Mm."
"When did she carve it?"
"These past few months. After school, on weekends. When you weren't home."
Lu Shiyan stood up and looked at Su Qingyuan. "You taught her?"
"She taught herself. I only gave a little guidance now and then."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a few seconds, then said: "Su Qingyuan, you have a wonderful daughter."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "So do you."
The year Lu Xi turned fourteen, Su Qingyuan received an email from the Biennale organizing committee. It wasn't an invitation for her—it was an invitation for Lu Xi. A "Young Artists Special Program," open to young creators aged fourteen to eighteen from all over the world; those selected could hold a solo exhibition during the Biennale.
Su Qingyuan showed Lu Xi the email. Lu Xi read it and was silent for a long while.
"Mama, I want to take part."
"All right."
"You're not going to ask whether I'm confident?"
"No. Because if you dare to take part, that means you're ready."
Lu Xi held her phone, her eyes reddening. "Mama, I'm afraid I won't do it well."
Su Qingyuan crouched down to meet her at eye level. "Xixi, Mama has been making pieces for all these years, and every single one has its regrets. There's no such thing as a perfect work—only a sincere one. If you're sincere, others will see it."
Lu Xi nodded and wiped away her tears. "I understand."
Lu Xi spent three months preparing her work for the Biennale. She didn't make jewelry—she made an installation piece: the roots of a tree. Not carved stone, not metal, but real tree roots she'd gathered from the hill behind her school. She'd collected many, large and small, all different in shape. She washed them, dried them, and bound them together with fine wire to make a new "tree." This tree's roots were aboveground and its branches and trunk below—she hung the roots upside down so that what viewers saw were the roots, not the branches and leaves.
The piece was titled "The Part You Can't See." The theme was the same as her mother's "Roots" series, but the expression was entirely different. When Su Qingyuan saw the piece, she cried. Not from sorrow—it was the kind of tears that come from "the child has grown up," both proud and reluctant to let go.
Lu Shiyan stood behind her and handed her a tissue. "She's like you."
Su Qingyuan wiped her tears. "She's braver than I am."
"You're very brave too."
"No. Back when I made 'Roots,' I was so nervous I couldn't sleep."
Su Qingyuan looked at the piece and smiled. "But I still made it."
The Biennale was in Venice. It was the first time Lu Xi traveled abroad on her own; Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan went with her. On the day of setting up the exhibition, Lu Xi wouldn't let Su Qingyuan help. "Mama, I'll do it myself." She climbed up and down, adjusting the angles of those tree roots, her hands covered in dust, her forehead covered in sweat. Su Qingyuan stood outside the exhibition hall, watching her through the glass door. Lu Shiyan stood beside her. "Are you nervous?"
"I'm not nervous. She can do it."
"How do you know?"
"Because she's our daughter."
Lu Shiyan laughed.
On opening day, many people came to see Lu Xi's piece. A white-haired old lady stood before "The Part You Can't See" for a long time, then asked Lu Xi: "Child, why did you put the roots on top?" In her not-quite-fluent English, Lu Xi said: "Because people always see the branches and leaves, and forget the roots. I wanted them to see the part you can't see." The old lady nodded, her eyes reddening. "I'm eighty years old, and this is the first time anyone has let me see the roots."
Lu Xi was stunned. She turned to look toward the entrance of the hall, where Su Qingyuan stood, giving her a smile. She smiled too, her eyes curving into little crescents.
After returning from Venice, Lu Xi donated "The Part You Can't See" to her school. The school placed it at the entrance of the library, where everyone passing by would see those upside-down tree roots. Someone asked Lu Xi: "Doesn't it hurt to give it away? Once you've donated it, it's gone." Lu Xi said: "It means more sitting there than sitting at my home."
Su Qingyuan knew about this. She didn't say "you're amazing," and she didn't say "are you sure you can part with it." She simply cooked a meal—all of Lu Xi's favorite dishes. Sweet-and-sour ribs, stir-fried seasonal greens, steamed sea bass, tomato and egg-drop soup.
Lu Xi looked at the table full of food. "Mama, what's the occasion today?"
"Today is the day you gave your light to someone else."
Lu Xi lowered her head, picked up her chopsticks, and took a piece of rib. "Mama, thank you."
"Thank me for what?"
"Thank you for lending me that carving knife."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot, but she didn't cry. She placed a piece of fish in Lu Xi's bowl. "You're welcome. From now on, lend your carving knife to others too."
Lu Xi nodded.
That night, Su Qingyuan leaned against Lu Shiyan's shoulder, sitting beneath the osmanthus tree in the courtyard. The moonlight was lovely, lighting the tree full of blossoms until they looked like scattered gold. Lu Xi was already asleep, and the courtyard was very quiet, with only the sound of the autumn insects.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Tell me, what do you think Xixi will do someday?"
"I don't know. But she already knows to give her light to others."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "Mm. She's better at it than I am."
"You're good at it too. You've given your light to so many people—me, Xixi, Wanyin, Qu Ya, Xiaoman, and all the people who wear your work."
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder and closed her eyes. "So have you. You're my light."
Lu Shiyan lowered his head and kissed her forehead. "And you're mine."
Far off, the city's lights merged into a warm sea of light. In the courtyard, the old osmanthus tree and the newly planted one stood side by side, one tall, one short. The moonlight fell over them, as if plating them with a layer of silver.
Su Qingyuan opened her eyes and looked at the two trees. "Lu Shiyan, when Xixi grows up, she'll leave home and go somewhere far away."
"Mm."
"Will it be hard for you to let her go?"
"It will."
"Then what do we do?"
"Let her go. And then wait for her to come back."
Su Qingyuan smiled and took his hand. "You're always so easy about things."
"It's not that I'm easy about it. It's that I know—no matter how far she goes, this place is her roots."
Su Qingyuan looked at him; the moonlight fell across his face, his profile still as handsome as ever. She suddenly remembered, many years ago, a rainy night—she'd been crouching at the side of the road, and a car had stopped. The window came down, revealing his face. He'd said: "Get in." She got in, and from then on she never got out again.
That night was the end of the storm, and also the beginning of the dawn.
And the dawn has been here ever since.
