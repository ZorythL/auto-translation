Chapter 20 — The Brand's Arrogance, Her Quiet Composure

The next morning, Su Qingyuan allowed herself a rare lie-in.

After returning to the apartment the night before, she had worked until two in the morning—although the "First Light" collection had already launched, demand for follow-up custom orders far exceeded expectations. Many clients had left comments on the pre-sale page, hoping she would create bespoke designs based on their personal stories.

Su Qingyuan read through every single one of those comments, her heart filling with something warm and overwhelming.

Among them were daughters wanting to gift their mothers, husbands wanting to gift their wives, girls wanting to gift themselves. Behind every story was a beam of light waiting to be kindled.

She made up her mind on the spot: alongside the "First Light" collection, she would open a custom line, taking only three commissions a month, each one personally crafted by her own hand.

After the announcement went out through the official account of Zhou Wanyin's studio, applications passed three hundred within ten minutes.

Su Qingyuan rubbed her bleary eyes, picked up her phone, and found the screen piled with messages.

Zhou Wanyin: [You up? Two o'clock this afternoon, Cartier Greater China headquarters. Don't be late.]

Lu Shiyan: [Good morning. Remember to eat breakfast. There's congee in the kitchen, in the warming cabinet.]

Su Qingyuan stared at Lu Shiyan's message for a stunned moment, then ran barefoot into the kitchen.

On the stovetop sat a white insulated clay pot, quiet and waiting. She lifted the lid and the aroma of century egg and lean pork congee wafted up at her, still warm.

She paused.

When had he come? She hadn't gone to sleep until two the night before—there was no way he'd come this late to deliver congee... unless he'd made a special trip first thing in the morning.

Su Qingyuan ladled out a bowl of congee, sat down at the dining table, and slowly ate.

The congee tasted wonderful—nothing like takeout. The grains were soft and tender, the savory richness of century egg and lean pork pitched just right.

She picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message:

[Got the congee. When did you come by?]

Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: [Seven this morning. You were still asleep, so I didn't wake you.]

Su Qingyuan: [You came at seven to drop off congee, then went to work? Don't you need sleep?]

Lu Shiyan: [I slept. Got back last night, handled some things, went to bed at one.]

Su Qingyuan's chest tightened. To bed at one, here at seven to deliver congee—that meant he'd only slept six hours?

She was about to say something more when another message arrived:

[Is the congee good?]

Su Qingyuan looked at the line of text and suddenly felt a sting in her nose. This man was always like this—when he himself hadn't rested properly, what he asked was "Is the congee good?"

[It's good. Thank you.]

Lu Shiyan: [Good is all that matters. You're seeing Cartier this afternoon. Want me to come along?]

Su Qingyuan thought it over and replied: [No need. This is a professional setting, I want to go on my own. If you show up, they'll just assume I'm relying on connections.]

Lu Shiyan: [Alright. Tell me how it goes afterward.]

Su Qingyuan set down her phone, drank the bowl of congee clean, then washed up, tidied the kitchen, and changed her clothes.

She stood before the mirror, studying herself.

A white shirt, black high-waisted wide-leg trousers, simple silver stud earrings—a sample piece from her own "First Snow" collection. Hair in a low ponytail, makeup clean and understated.

She looked sharp, professional, unflashy, yet every detail spoke of quality.

She gave herself a nod in the mirror, picked up her bag, and headed out.

Cartier's Greater China headquarters stood in the heart of Nancheng's most bustling CBD district.

The exterior of the entire building was understated yet luxurious, its dark-gray glass curtain wall mirroring the clouds in the sky. Red silk ribbons were tied around the necks of the two stone lions flanking the entrance—a blend of East and West, imposing without being ostentatious.

Su Qingyuan walked into the lobby. The reception staff had clearly been notified, and a smiling attendant led her upstairs. "Ms. Su, this way please. The brand director is waiting for you on the thirty-eighth floor."

As the elevator rose, Su Qingyuan looked at her own reflection in the mirrored surface and took a deep breath.

Cartier.

One of the world's top jewelry houses.

A few months ago, she wouldn't even have been qualified to set foot in this building. Now she was here as an invited guest.

The elevator doors opened. At the end of the corridor stood a pair of dark wooden double doors. The attendant knocked, pushed them open, and stepped aside to let Su Qingyuan enter first.

Inside was a spacious office, the floor-to-ceiling windows framing Nancheng's skyline. Behind an enormous dark solid-wood desk sat a blonde woman in her forties—Anna Schmidt, Cartier's Greater China brand director, widely known in the industry as the "Iron Lady."

Seeing Su Qingyuan, Anna stood, a professional smile on her face, and extended her hand. "Ms. Su, I've heard a great deal about you."

"Ms. Schmidt, it's a pleasure." Su Qingyuan shook her hand—firm but measured, neither obsequious nor arrogant.

The two of them sat down. Anna's assistant brought in coffee, then withdrew without a sound.

Anna got straight to the point, skipping the pleasantries. "Ms. Su, I've seen all the work in your 'First Light' collection, and I've followed the news of your Biennale award. Frankly, your talent has left a deep impression on me."

"Thank you."

"However," Anna's tone shifted, the smile unchanged but her words now carrying a note of scrutiny, "talent is one thing, and commercial value is another. When Cartier chooses a designer to collaborate with, we look not only at design ability, but also at the designer's stability, their fit with the brand image, and—their resilience to risk."

Su Qingyuan's hand, holding the coffee cup, didn't move. She waited calmly for Anna to finish.

"Some of your recent news," Anna's fingers tapped lightly on the desktop, "including your adoptive father buying marketing accounts to spread rumors, your sister's suspected plagiarism, and your relationship with your boyfriend—President Lu of the Lu Group—these are all talking points in the public eye. For Cartier, talking points can be a good thing, or they can be a risk."

Su Qingyuan set down her coffee cup and met Anna's gaze.

"Ms. Schmidt, I completely understand everything you've said. Risk assessment is a necessary step when a brand chooses its partners. But I'd ask you to distinguish between two things—'news one is passively dragged into' and 'news one actively manufactures.'"

Anna arched an eyebrow slightly.

"My adoptive father spreading rumors, my sister plagiarizing—these were not things I chose. They happened to me because I chose to stand up and protect my own rights. If being 'harmed' makes someone 'a risk,' then that logic is unfair to every victim."

Su Qingyuan's voice wasn't loud, but every word landed steady and sure.

"As for my relationship with Mr. Lu Shiyan—we went public not to drum up hype, but because someone secretly photographed us and tried to use it to hurt us. We chose to respond head-on because we didn't want our private affairs to become leverage in someone else's hands."

Anna looked at her, and the scrutiny in her eyes slowly gave way to something else—curiosity.

"Ms. Su, the way you speak doesn't sound like someone negotiating with a brand for the first time."

"Because I've never had the chance to negotiate with a brand before," Su Qingyuan said frankly. "But I've always known exactly who I am, and what my work is worth."

Anna was silent for a few seconds, then suddenly smiled. This smile was a touch more genuine than the professional one from before.

"Alright, Ms. Su, I'll speak plainly. Cartier wants to work with you—but not as a brand designer. As a 'guest artist.' Once a year, you design a capsule collection for Cartier, fully led by you. Cartier only provides the supply chain and sales channels. Profit split, fifty-fifty."

Something stirred in Su Qingyuan's heart.

Guest artist. Not a designer absorbed into the brand, but an equal, independent collaborator.

That status carried more dignity than a "signed designer," and more of a voice as well.

"The profit split," Su Qingyuan said, "I want to negotiate."

Anna clearly hadn't expected her to negotiate the split outright, and paused. "What were you thinking?"

"Sixty-forty. Sixty to me, forty to you."

The office fell silent for a moment.

Anna set down the pen in her hand and leaned slightly forward. "Ms. Su, do you know that Cartier has never once given a sixty-forty split to any designer it's worked with?"

"I know." Su Qingyuan didn't flinch or look away. "But no other designer has set a record of fifty-three million in pre-sales within five minutes, either."

Anna looked at her, her gaze so sharp it seemed to want to see straight through her.

Su Qingyuan didn't shy from that gaze. Her spine was perfectly straight, her eyes clear and resolute.

After a long while, Anna leaned back into her chair and let out a soft laugh.

"Ms. Su, you really are a lot like your boyfriend."

Su Qingyuan blinked. "What do you mean?"

"Your negotiating style." Anna picked up her coffee cup, a trace of resignation and admiration in her tone. "Neither of you gives an inch."

Su Qingyuan curved her lips but said nothing.

The air in the office shifted from tense to relaxed. Anna picked up the documents on the desk, flipped through a few pages, then closed them again.

"A sixty-forty split—I'll need to discuss it with headquarters. But from what I know of them, they won't agree easily."

"I'll wait to hear from you." Su Qingyuan stood and extended her hand. "Whatever the outcome, today's conversation with you has been enormously valuable to me."

Anna shook her hand—and this time her grip was a little firmer than before.

"Ms. Su, I'll be honest. Before you came in today, my impression of you was 'a young designer who'd risen on connections and luck.' But now—" she let go, the corner of her mouth lifting, "I've changed my mind."

When Su Qingyuan walked out of the Cartier building, the autumn sunlight fell perfectly across her shoulders.

She stood on the steps, tilted her head back, and drew a deep breath of the sky.

Done.

Not that the collaboration itself was settled—but that she had held her ground at the negotiating table.

She hadn't shrunk three inches just because the other party was Cartier, hadn't lost her nerve before Anna's commanding presence, hadn't accepted every condition just because "they're a big brand."

She had negotiated what she ought to negotiate, and held what she ought to hold.

Her phone buzzed.

Lu Shiyan: [Done? How did it go?]

Su Qingyuan: [Done. Cartier wants to work together—guest artist status, one capsule collection a year. I negotiated a sixty-forty split, sixty to me, forty to them. Anna said she needs to talk to headquarters.]

Lu Shiyan's reply came fast: [You negotiated sixty-forty?]

Su Qingyuan: [Mm. Do you think it's too high?]

Lu Shiyan: [Not too high. Your value is worth it. Did she agree?]

Su Qingyuan: [She said she needs to talk to headquarters.]

Lu Shiyan: [I'll have someone quietly put in a word with headquarters. No pressure—just to let them know your 'First Light' pre-sale figures are real, not padded.]

Su Qingyuan looked at the message and couldn't help smiling.

She'd noticed a pattern—whenever she fought it out in a professional arena, Lu Shiyan was always there on the sidelines, silently passing her weapons. He didn't take the field for her; he made sure that when she did, she had a blade in her hand.

[Okay. Thank you.]

Lu Shiyan: [You're welcome. What do you want for dinner? Let's celebrate.]

Su Qingyuan thought for a moment, then typed out a line:

[Something you make.]

The other end was silent for a few seconds.

Lu Shiyan: [I can't cook.]

Su Qingyuan: [I know. But you'll learn. The congee last time was very good.]

The other end fell silent for even longer.

Then Lu Shiyan sent a voice message, his tone carrying a trace of being caught out and a smile he couldn't quite suppress:

"Fine. I'll learn. But you're not allowed to complain that it tastes bad."

Su Qingyuan played the voice message twice and saved it.

She stood on the steps outside the Cartier building, the sunlight on her, smiling like a little girl who'd just been given a promise.

[System: I said I'd stay behind the scenes. But this System truly can't help itself—Lu Shiyan, learning to cook? This man would walk into a kitchen for you. Do you even know that he used to have no idea which way a kitchen door swings open? Alright, System will shut up now. System has shut up.]

Su Qingyuan smiled and slipped her phone into her pocket, walked down the steps, and merged into the hurried crowd of the CBD.

People came and went all around her, suited and brisk, in a rush. No one knew that this young woman in a white shirt with a low ponytail had just pulled off a beautiful negotiation.

And even fewer knew that the man standing behind her was the strongest backing in all of Nancheng.

But she knew.

And she was using her own effort to make herself worthy of that backing—no, not worthy of it. To stand shoulder to shoulder with him.

That evening, when Su Qingyuan returned to the studio, she found a parcel at the door.

A white cardboard box, with no sender information, only a handwritten card in clumsy, crooked script:

"Sister Qingyuan, I'm sorry. —Chen Xiaoman"

Su Qingyuan looked at the name and froze for a moment.

Chen Xiaoman. The girl who had posed as Photographer Lin's assistant and secretly photographed her Ripple Cut technique.

She opened the box. Inside was a stack of photographs and a letter.

The photographs captured a scene of Su Yurou meeting with a middle-aged man. The man was in his fifties, dressed in a dark-gray bespoke suit, his hair graying but his spirit sharp, a high-handed arrogance between his brows that came from long years at the top.

Su Qingyuan didn't recognize the man, but she had a powerful intuition—he was Gu Changfeng.

The photos had been taken from a very concealed angle, as if shot from a distance, but every one was crisp and clear. Su Yurou and the man's expressions, gestures, even the way they shook hands—all of it was perfectly visible.

Su Qingyuan picked up the letter and unfolded it.

The handwriting was still clumsy and crooked, but every stroke was pressed down hard, as though written with great resolve:

"Sister Qingyuan:

My name is Chen Xiaoman. My sister was Su Yurou's college roommate. Su Yurou came to me and said she only needed me to take a few photos, that it wouldn't hurt anyone. She gave me fifty thousand yuan to apply to Photographer Lin's team and secretly photograph your workbench.

I did it. I photographed your work notes and the finished details of 'Daybreak,' and sent the photos to her.

I thought nothing would come of it. I thought it was just 'borrowing,' not stealing.

Later I saw the news, and only then did I learn she had stolen your technique and used it to enter a competition.

I was so afraid. I was afraid you'd sue me, afraid of going to prison. I secretly followed Su Yurou and photographed her with that man. I thought, if I gave these to you, maybe you'd forgive me.

Sister Qingyuan, I know I was wrong. I don't ask for your forgiveness, only that you don't sue me. My mother is ill, and I'm the only one in the family earning money. If I go to prison, there'll be no one to take care of her.

I'm sorry. I'm truly sorry."

Su Qingyuan held the letter, silent for a long time.

The sky outside the window darkened bit by bit. The studio lights were off, and she stood in the dim light like a quiet statue.

She felt no anger.

She even pitied Chen Xiaoman—a young girl lured by money, driven by fear, and in the end tormented by her own conscience.

She picked up her phone and dialed Lawyer Liu.

"Lawyer Liu, this is Su Qingyuan. I'd like to ask about something—if an accomplice voluntarily provides key evidence and admits their wrongdoing, how is that generally handled under the law?"

Lawyer Liu explained the relevant provisions in detail on the other end of the line. When Su Qingyuan had finished listening, she was silent for a moment.

"I understand. Thank you, Lawyer Liu."

After hanging up, she sent Chen Xiaoman a text:

"I've received the photos and the letter. I won't sue you. But what you did was wrong, and I hope you remember this lesson. Don't ever do something against your conscience for money again."

A few seconds later, Chen Xiaoman's reply came—just three words:

"Thank you."

Su Qingyuan set down her phone, put the photographs away carefully, and placed them in a drawer.

These photos were direct evidence of Su Yurou colluding with Gu Changfeng. She wouldn't use them for now, but she had to keep them—at the critical moment, they would be a fatal blow.

[System: The heroine chooses not to sue Chen Xiaoman. Not out of saintliness, but because she's letting it go. "Letting it go" is more powerful than forgiveness. Because "letting it go" means: I won't waste my time hating you. I'm too busy moving forward.]

Su Qingyuan silently said to the System in her heart: thank you for understanding.

[System: Even though the System has retreated behind the scenes, its reading comprehension is still intact. Alright, I won't bother you anymore.]

Su Qingyuan curved her lips, switched on the light, walked to her workbench, and picked up her carving knife.

A new design was waiting for her.
