Beyond the Light
Su Yurou saw that post on a rainy day in Melbourne. Lu Shiyan had put it up: in the photo, Su Qingyuan stood with a small girl in front of the Wall of Love, and the caption was just two words — "Mine."
She stared at the picture for a long time. The little girl had her hair tied up in two small bunches and wore a red coat, head tilted back as she looked up at the words on the wall, as if struggling to make out strokes she didn't yet recognize. Her profile was very like Su Qingyuan's, yet the outline of her brows and eyes plainly carried the shadow of that other man. Su Yurou turned her phone face-down on the table and closed her eyes. It was raining outside the café; Melbourne winters were full of rain, one shower after another, like old memories that wouldn't stop coming. She thought of an afternoon many years ago, the day Su Qingyuan first came to her home — it had been raining then too. A small, thin girl had stood in the doorway, her hair dripping wet, an old schoolbag in her hand. Her mother, Liu Xiulan, had said, "This is your big sister. She'll be living with us from now on." She had looked at the girl, and the girl had looked back at her. In the girl's eyes there was fear, there was hope, and there was something she couldn't name — later she would learn it was called wariness. The kind of carefulness everyone develops when they live under someone else's roof.
At the time she had thought it was fun. She'd run over and grabbed the girl's hand. "Big sister, what's your name?" The girl had said softly, "Su Qingyuan." She'd said, "Sister Qingyuan, I'll show you to your room." It was a tiny room, originally a storage closet, but it had been tidied up — there was a small bed, a small desk, a little lamp. The girl set her schoolbag on the desk, turned, and said, "Thank you." That was the first "thank you" Su Qingyuan ever said to her, as far as Su Yurou could remember. After that, Su Qingyuan said countless "thank yous" — thank you for helping with my homework, thank you for taking the blame for me, thank you for giving me the good food, thank you for not fighting me over Mom and Dad. Every "thank you" was a step backward, a concession. She had grown used to Su Qingyuan's concessions, used to her good temper, used to the fact that she never seemed to get angry. So when Su Qingyuan finally stopped giving way, she panicked. She had done so many terrible things — stealing her designs, stealing her boyfriend, falsely accusing her of plagiarism. She'd thought Su Qingyuan would hate her, take revenge, make her wish she were dead. But Su Qingyuan did nothing of the kind. She simply removed her from her life, cleanly and completely, like deleting someone you'll never contact again.
That was the cruelest revenge of all — you're still busy hating, and she's already moved on.
Su Yurou picked up her phone, typed a few lines, deleted them, typed again, deleted again. In the end she sent just one thing: "It's raining in Melbourne. How about where you are?" She didn't wait for a reply. She slipped the phone into her pocket, took up her umbrella, and walked out of the café.
The Melbourne rain was still falling, pattering against the canopy of her umbrella.
Back at her rental flat, she changed into dry clothes and cooked herself a bowl of noodles. The noodles were dried ones from the supermarket; the broth was just water and salt, a few slices of luncheon meat, a poached egg nestled on top. She carried the bowl to the table and sat watching the rain outside the window. Melbourne winters were truly cold — colder than a southern hemisphere had any right to be. Her phone lit up. Su Qingyuan's reply was a single sentence: "It's raining in Nancheng. Xixi says rain is the sky watering its flowers."
Su Yurou looked at the line of text, and her tears dropped into the noodle bowl. She raised a hand to wipe them away, sniffled, and wrote back: "Then it must be your sky doing the watering. The Melbourne sky forgot to water today." After she sent it she set the phone down and finished the noodles. They'd gone soft and clumped, the broth had cooled, but she ate every last mouthful.
That night the rain stopped. Su Yurou stood at the window, looking at the distant sky. A seam had torn open in the cloud cover, and moonlight leaked through it, falling onto the wet streets and glittering. She picked up her phone and scrolled to Su Qingyuan's post. The photo before the Wall of Love was still there, the little girl's face lit by sunshine, laughing until her eyes curved into crescent moons. Su Yurou looked at it for a long while, and then she tapped "like." It was the first time she had ever liked one of Su Qingyuan's posts. Before, she hadn't dared; later, she'd been too ashamed; today, for some reason she didn't understand, she simply wanted to. Maybe it was because the Melbourne rain had stopped, maybe because she'd eaten that bowl of noodles, or maybe because — she could finally admit that Su Qingyuan was doing well, and that she, too, was doing all right.
Chen Xiaoman's restaurant was in a small town outside Melbourne, a two-hour drive from the city. It wasn't large — a dozen-odd tables — and it served Chinese food: home-style dishes, nothing fancy, but good. Most of the customers were regulars from nearby who'd order a plate of sweet-and-sour pork ribs, a plate of tomato and scrambled eggs, a bowl of rice, then wipe their mouths and go. Chen Xiaoman was busy every day — shopping for ingredients in the morning, prepping, opening for the lunch rush, resting a while in the afternoon, then back to it in the evening. When the day was done, after she'd closed up, she would cook herself a bowl of noodles and sit on the front steps, looking out at the quiet streets of the little town.
Sometimes she would think of Su Qingyuan — of that studio, of the moonstone that had glowed with three layers of halo under the light. She had secretly photographed that stone and sent the picture to Su Yurou in exchange for fifty thousand yuan. She hadn't spent a single cent of that money; later she'd tried to return it to Su Qingyuan. Su Qingyuan wouldn't take it. "Your mother's ill. Keep it for that," she'd said at the time. In the end her mother passed away anyway. She used that fifty thousand to open this restaurant. Her mother had always said: live so you can face your own conscience. She didn't know whether this restaurant could face her conscience — she hadn't stolen, hadn't cheated, every dish was cooked by her own hands, every cent earned by herself. But she knew that without Su Qingyuan's words, "keep it for that," she might still be drifting along somewhere in a daze.
One day Chen Xiaoman saw news of Su Qingyuan online — the "Light Traces" collection on a global tour: New York, Tokyo, Shanghai, Paris. She looked at the photo of that woman in a white gown, standing in the exhibition hall of Cartier's flagship store, and felt that they didn't belong to the same world. And yet they had once stood so close — close enough for her to photograph the secret on her workbench. She dug out that stolen photo, looked at it for a long time, and then deleted it. It wasn't that she feared leaving evidence; it was that the photo shouldn't exist at all. When she'd taken it, her heart had been full of money. Now her heart held something else — maybe guilt, maybe gratitude, or maybe just a plain, simple wish: I hope you're doing well.
She sent Su Qingyuan a message — not deliberately, but because Melbourne's sunset that evening happened to be lovely, and she'd snapped a photo. The caption: "The sunset here is pink. Can you see it over there?" Su Qingyuan's reply came late: "Nancheng's is blue. Xixi says the sky is the sea turned upside down."
Chen Xiaoman read the words and smiled. She sent back a smiley face, then set down her phone and went on washing the dishes.
Zhou Wanyin had lately been preparing an exhibition of her own — not a jewelry exhibition, but a painting show. She had studied painting when she was young, then changed paths and become a jewelry designer. Decades had passed, and now she'd picked up the brush again. The reason was Lu Xi, Su Qingyuan's daughter. Once, when Zhou Wanyin was visiting Su Qingyuan's home, Lu Xi had been painting in the living room — a painting of flowers, a messy, unidentifiable bouquet, but the colors were lovely: pink, yellow, blue, all mixed together, like an overturned box of paints.
"Xixi, what are you painting?"
"Flowers. For Auntie Wanyin."
Zhou Wanyin looked at the painting, and something stirred in her heart. She realized she hadn't painted in a long time — not for lack of time, but because she'd forgotten. Forgotten that feeling of painting for no purpose at all, just because you wanted to. She told Su Qingyuan she wanted to hold a painting exhibition. Su Qingyuan said, "Do it. I'll help you." Zhou Wanyin laughed. "Aren't you going to ask whether my paintings will even sell?" Su Qingyuan said, "Whether they sell isn't what matters. What matters is that you want to paint."
Zhou Wanyin's eyes went red. She hugged Su Qingyuan. "Qingyuan, thank you." Su Qingyuan patted her back. "Sister Wanyin, you're the one who taught me — doing what you love never wears you out."
The exhibition was held in a very small space in the south of the city, and quite a few of Zhou Wanyin's old customers and old friends came. Su Qingyuan brought Lu Xi. Lu Xi stood in front of one painting, head tilted, studying it for a long while. "Auntie Wanyin, what did you paint?"
"A sunset."
"Why is the sunset blue?"
"Because that's when the sky is about to go dark, when the sunset is almost gone, and the blue is about to cover over the pink."
Lu Xi thought about it. "I think pink is prettier."
Zhou Wanyin smiled. "Then next time I'll paint more pink."
Lu Xi nodded, satisfied, and ran off to find Su Qingyuan. Qu Ya had come too, and she stood before one painting, examining it for a long time. "Wanyin, you studied painting before?"
"I did. A long time ago."
"Why did you stop?"
"Because I felt I wasn't good at it."
"And now?"
"Now I feel whether I'm good or not doesn't matter. As long as I'm happy while I paint."
Qu Ya smiled. "You've finally figured it out." Zhou Wanyin looked at her. "Figured out what?" Qu Ya said, "Making art isn't about proving yourself. It's about expressing yourself." Zhou Wanyin was silent for a few seconds, then smiled. She looked at the paintings around the hall — every one of them had flaws: the composition not quite perfect, the colors not quite harmonious, the brushwork not quite practiced. But they were her paintings, the marks she had left on this world.
After the exhibition ended, Zhou Wanyin gave that blue sunset to Lu Xi. Lu Xi stuck it up at the head of her bed and looked at it for a while every night before sleep. Su Qingyuan asked her, "Xixi, which one do you like best?" Lu Xi said, "I like Auntie Wanyin's sunset best." Su Qingyuan said, "Why?" Lu Xi said, "Because blue sunsets are rare. And rare things are worth loving more."
Su Qingyuan went still for a moment, then smiled. She thought of long ago, when Lu Shiyan had seen her through a glass door and said there was something in her eyes — something very clean, very rare. She looked at her daughter's bright, shining eyes and felt that she too would one day meet someone — after she'd grown up, after she was ready, after she'd become her own light.
That someone would be like her father: he would see her through a glass door, and then he would wait. Wait until she was ready, wait until she turned around and saw him.
Sometimes Su Qingyuan would wonder: if not for that rainy night, where would she be now? Maybe still working in the Su family's shop, maybe married to some ordinary man, maybe still painting, but with no one to see it. She didn't know. She only knew that she was here now — in this courtyard with its osmanthus tree, in this room with its workbench, in this home with Lu Shiyan and Lu Xi.
Lu Xi was five this year. She asked Su Qingyuan, "Mama, will I meet someone like Papa one day too?" Su Qingyuan thought about it. "Not necessarily." Lu Xi looked a little disappointed. "Why?" Su Qingyuan crouched down and retied her crooked braid for her. "Because first you have to become someone like your mama. Once you've become yourself, that person will come."
Lu Xi nodded, half understanding, and ran off to play. Su Qingyuan watched her small figure disappear behind the osmanthus tree, then stood and turned back to her workbench. The sixth piece of the new collection still needed its final step. She picked up her engraving knife and went on polishing. Outside the window, the sunlight was beautiful. The osmanthus had bloomed especially lush this year, the whole tree golden, its fragrance sweet to the point of cloying. Lu Shiyan came out from inside carrying two cups of tea, one for her, one for himself.
"Where's Xixi?"
"Out in the back catching grasshoppers."
"You're not keeping an eye on her?"
"Let her play. When she can't catch any, she'll come back."
Su Qingyuan took the tea and sipped it. It was Longjing, newly bought this year; he said green tea was good to drink in spring.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"What do you think Xixi will do when she grows up?"
"Whatever she does, she'll be wonderful at it."
"Why?"
"Because she's our daughter."
Su Qingyuan laughed and leaned against his shoulder, looking at the osmanthus tree in the courtyard. Sunlight slipped through the gaps in the leaves and fell to the ground, scattering into a sheet of golden light.
From far off, Lu Xi's laughter drifted over. "Mama! I caught one!"
Su Qingyuan straightened up. "Caught what?"
"A grasshopper! A green one!"
"Bring it over and show Mama."
Lu Xi came running, a tiny green grasshopper cupped in her palms, hopping this way and that as she giggled. Su Qingyuan looked at her daughter's little face, glowing in the sunlight, and suddenly remembered many years ago, when she had sat on the attic floor of the old Su family house and painted — painted light. Back then she hadn't known that one day those lights would become real.
Now she knew.
