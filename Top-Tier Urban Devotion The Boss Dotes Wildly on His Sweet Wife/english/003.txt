Chapter 3 — Devotion in Silence
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626205.html

The instant Su Qingyuan opened the door, the motion-sensor light in the corridor flickered and dimmed—then blazed back to life just as Lu Shiyan lifted his head.

The dim yellow light fell across his face. Rainwater slid down the shoulder line of his overcoat, gathering into droplets at his cuffs that dripped soundlessly onto the worn cement floor.

The corridor of this building was far too narrow. Standing there, he seemed to take up the entire space. He was too tall—the crown of his head nearly grazed the bare fluorescent tube overhead—and the whole of him clashed with this shabby backdrop, like a high-end magazine ad that had been Photoshopped onto a demolition notice in some old slum district.

Su Qingyuan looked at him, opened her mouth, and for a moment found she had no idea what to say.

It was one in the morning. Rain fell outside. This man was dressed as though he had just stepped away from some corporate gala—a dark gray cashmere overcoat, a black turtleneck sweater beneath it, crisp and immaculate, not a single crease. But the shoulders of his coat were damp, and fine droplets clung to his hair; clearly he had stood in the rain for more than a moment.

"How did you know where I live?" she asked.

The words were barely out before she felt she'd asked something stupid.

He was prepared to buy the entire building and renovate it from scratch. Knowing which floor and which unit she lived in was nothing to him.

Lu Shiyan didn't answer the question. Instead he lowered his eyes slightly and looked at her.

She had finished showering not long ago; her hair still hung half-damp around her shoulders. She wore an old hoodie, black sweatpants below, and a pair of pilled cotton slippers on her feet. Her face was bare of makeup, her skin so pale it was almost translucent, faint shadows of blue-black beneath her eyes—the mark left by nights spent drafting.

She was a different person entirely from the woman bristling with thorns at the banquet the night before.

Right now she was soft, like a crumpled sheet of paper.

"Finished?" he asked.

Su Qingyuan was startled.

How did he know she'd been painting?

In the next instant she felt that question was also rather foolish. This man knew exactly what time she went to bed; the fact that she had spent the entire day drafting in her rented room had no doubt long since been reported to him by Qin Zhou.

"Mm," she said. "Finished."

"May I see it?"

Su Qingyuan hesitated.

It wasn't that she didn't want to show him. Quite the opposite—she very much wanted to show it to someone. This piece had cost her every ounce of her energy; she had never produced work this good. She wanted someone to know, wanted to hear someone say, "You really painted this beautifully," wanted to see the light kindle in someone's eyes because of her work.

But the drafts were scattered all over the room, strewn in a mess across the table, the floor, the bed. The curtains were a dull yellow striped cloth she'd dug up from a secondhand market, boxes of instant noodles were piled in the corner, and the whole room reeked of cheap laundry detergent.

She didn't want him to see any of this.

Not out of vanity—but because… never mind. She didn't know why either.

"The place is a bit of a mess," she said.

Lu Shiyan glanced at her, his gaze as calm as a still pool.

"It's all right."

He didn't say "I don't mind." He said "It's all right."

Su Qingyuan felt there was a subtle difference between the two. "I don't mind" implied he had noticed her embarrassment but chose to be tolerant of it. "It's all right" implied he didn't regard it as anything at all—whether the room was messy or not had simply never been a question requiring his minding or not minding.

She stepped aside from the doorway.

"Come in."

Lu Shiyan bent to step through the low door frame—he really was too tall; this door was like dressing a child in adult clothes, awkward no matter how you looked at it.

He stood in the entryway, his gaze sweeping across the entire room.

The thirty-square-meter space took it all in at a glance. The bed sat against the far wall, the blanket folded with perfect neatness. The desk by the window was piled high with design books, their spines frayed from constant flipping. In the corner stood a drawing board and an easel; pencil shavings and eraser crumbs littered the floor. On the walls were pinned some of her old practice sketches, several already yellowed with age.

His gaze came at last to rest at the very center of the room—where a makeshift worktable had been set up, on it forty-two drafts arranged in perfect order, from concept sketches to rendered images, from floor plans to detail nodes, every one of them complete.

Su Qingyuan followed behind him, watching as he walked toward the drafts.

He walked slowly, as though giving the room a moment to breathe. The black leather shoes pressed against the cement floor, making a faint sound. The hem of his coat brushed past the jacket she'd tossed carelessly on the floor—no pause, no frown, as though those things did not exist.

Lu Shiyan stopped before the worktable.

He looked down at the first draft—the overall rendering of "Listening to the Sea." A pure white building rose along the coastline, its curves as gentle as a stone caressed over and over by the sea wind. The glass curtain wall wasn't straight but tilted ever so slightly, so that sunlight would refract through it at a special angle, casting rippling patches of light across the floor, as if the sea had surged indoors.

He said nothing.

He went through them one by one, like reading a book. He lingered long over each image—composition to line, light and shadow to texture, missing not a single detail.

Su Qingyuan stood behind him, watching his back, her heart beating inexplicably fast.

She had confidence in her drafts, of course—but that was by her own standard. She didn't know whether, in the eyes of a man like Lu Shiyan, these things were good enough.

He looked for a long time.

Long enough that Su Qingyuan began to feel uneasy.

At last he finished the final detail-node drawing and turned around.

Su Qingyuan met his eyes and found in those jet-black pupils a kind of light she had never seen before. Not polite admiration, not a perfunctory "not bad," but something almost scrutinizing, earnest, carrying a certain solemn approval.

"Su Qingyuan," he said, "do you know what you've painted?"

His tone left her a little tense. "My work."

"No," Lu Shiyan said. "You've painted an era."

There was no exaggerated rhetoric in the words. He said it very calmly, as though stating a fact.

Su Qingyuan froze where she stood.

Instinctively she wanted to say "You flatter me," but the words caught in her throat. Because she saw his expression—he wasn't comforting her, nor was he flattering her. This man had no need to flatter anyone.

He truly believed it.

"The Yunlan Bay project—the original budget was twelve million, eight million for design and four million for construction." Lu Shiyan said. "Now I'm going to change the budget."

Su Qingyuan's heart sank.

Yes, she thought. Eight million was too much. She was an unknown newcomer; what right did she have to a design fee of eight million?

"Thirty million," Lu Shiyan said. "Twenty million for design, ten million for construction."

Su Qingyuan thought she'd misheard.

"What?"

"If your design were built on the original budget, it would be a desecration of it." Lu Shiyan's tone was still that maddening calm. "The structural curved surfaces require higher-grade concrete, the tilt angle of the glass curtain wall requires custom molds, and if the interior floor is to mimic the play of light on water, it must use a special imported Italian stone—all of which costs money."

Su Qingyuan opened her mouth. "How did you know I wanted to use that stone?"

Lu Shiyan glanced at her.

"The fourth of your detail-node drawings—the stone-jointing method you drew—I've only seen its match in a private collection room in the Carrara quarry district of Italy."

Su Qingyuan was utterly speechless now.

She knew that someone had collected that stone—she had stumbled across it while researching material for her thesis during graduate school. But that paper was written in Italian, published in a journal almost no one in the country subscribed to, let alone something read by a man in real estate investment.

Just how much had this man seen?

"The Yunlan Bay Art Center is not one of my real estate projects," Lu Shiyan continued. "It was my mother's last wish before she died. She was an architect."

Su Qingyuan's heart gave a violent jolt.

There was no ripple of emotion on Lu Shiyan's face, but she couldn't say why—she simply felt that his voice was different from a moment ago, as though something were flowing beneath a thin sheet of ice.

"Before she passed, she drew thirty-seven versions of the design, and she wasn't satisfied with any of them. She said this place was too special; it had to be designed by someone who truly understood the sea." Lu Shiyan's gaze fell on the drafts, his voice dropping low. "She never lived to meet that person."

The room fell silent for a few seconds.

The faucet was still dripping, drip after drip, like some ancient timekeeper.

"Your design is the answer she was searching for," Lu Shiyan said.

Su Qingyuan lowered her eyes, her lashes trembling faintly.

Not because she was moved—though she truly was. But because she suddenly understood why this man had appeared at the awards gala of the design competition, why he had stood up for her, why he had come through the rain to her rented room at one in the morning.

Not because of "devotion"—at least not entirely.

It was because he truly understood her design.

A tycoon who held half the world in his grasp, a man the business world called "the King of Hell," a man rumored to be cold, untouchable, a lone wolf who never went near women—and yet he understood her design.

That stirred her heart more than any words of love could.

"Where's the contract?" Su Qingyuan lifted her head.

Lu Shiyan drew a document from the inner pocket of his coat and handed it to her.

Su Qingyuan took it, didn't look at the second page, flipped straight to the last, picked up a pen, and signed her name.

Lu Shiyan watched the fluid grace of her movements, and rarely revealed an expression—one eyebrow lifting just slightly.

"You don't read the terms?"

"Whatever you've offered is certain to be the best terms." Su Qingyuan handed the signed contract back. "Besides, even if you weren't paying, I'd still paint it."

"Why?"

"Because that place is worth it."

Lu Shiyan took the contract and looked down at the elegant signature on the last page. Su Qingyuan. The three characters were written cleanly and crisply—no flourishes, no connecting strokes—just like the person herself: clean, clear.

"There's one more thing," he said. "The International Young Designers Competition, three days from now—I need you to enter."

Su Qingyuan was taken aback. "Hasn't registration for that competition already closed?"

"Lu Group Global is the title sponsor of the competition." Lu Shiyan's tone was as flat as if he'd said, "The rain's a bit heavy today." "A supplementary slot can be added anytime."

Su Qingyuan: "…"

Fine, very nice, very rich.

"Why do you need me to enter?"

"Because the global grand champion of that competition earns a solo exhibition at Milan Design Week." Lu Shiyan looked at her. "Your 'Listening to the Sea' deserves to be seen by the whole world."

Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat.

Milan Design Week. The ultimate dream of designers across the globe. A twenty-three-year-old newcomer designer from China holding a solo show at Milan Design Week—such a thing had never happened in all of history.

"I'll enter," she said.

The system's notification chime sounded in her mind right on cue.

【Ding—Main quest triggered: The Path to Global Apotheosis.

Quest description: Win the global grand championship of the International Young Designers Competition and secure the qualification for a solo exhibition at Milan Design Week.

Quest reward: Appearance +5, Intelligence +5, unlock Advanced Design Talent, unlock new skill: Advanced Language Talent (including native-level mastery of Italian, French, and English), unlock new skill: Advanced Piano Performance.

Stage quest rewards: reaching the global finals, winning the continental championship, and winning the global grand championship each carry additional rewards.】

Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath.

She knew that from this moment on, her path would no longer be one arranged for her by others.

Lu Shiyan put the contract away and glanced toward the door.

"It's late. You should rest."

Su Qingyuan nodded. She really was exhausted—from yesterday until now, she'd slept only three hours.

Lu Shiyan turned and walked toward the door.

Su Qingyuan followed behind to see him out.

When he reached the doorway, he suddenly stopped and turned around. The turn was so abrupt that Su Qingyuan couldn't check her step and nearly collided with his chest.

The distance between them had narrowed to barely a dozen centimeters.

She could smell that cold, crisp scent on him—not cologne, more like some expensive fabric-care product, clean, aloof, untouchable, just like the man himself.

Lu Shiyan lowered his head and looked at her.

From this angle she could clearly see the sharp arc of his jawline, see the shadow his lashes cast across his cheekbone.

"Su Qingyuan," he said, "you don't need to live here anymore."

Su Qingyuan was startled.

"Qin Zhou will arrange new lodgings for you. It's not charity—it's a condition of our partnership. The designer of the Yunlan Bay project cannot live in a condemned building. That's my bottom line."

His tone brooked no argument, like a business clause so reasonable it couldn't be refused.

Su Qingyuan opened her mouth, wanting to say "There's no need," but meeting his gaze, the words lodged in her throat.

Not because she was afraid.

It was because she saw something in his eyes—not sympathy, not pity, but a kind of very earnest, very solemn… care.

He wasn't helping her because he thought she was pitiable.

He thought she deserved to be treated well.

"…All right," she said.

The corner of Lu Shiyan's mouth moved ever so slightly, the curve a fraction larger than the one outside the hotel the night before.

"Good night."

He turned and left.

Su Qingyuan stood in the doorway, watching his back disappear around the bend of the corridor. His footsteps descended, one after another, the voice-activated lights flaring on floor by floor—then, as his steps receded into the distance, dimming again floor by floor.

Only when all the lights had gone out did she close the door.

Leaning back against that brand-new door fitted with a smart fingerprint lock, she slowly slid down to the floor and buried her face in her knees.

Her heart was beating too fast.

So fast it almost frightened her.

At eight o'clock the next morning, Qin Zhou appeared at the door right on time.

When Su Qingyuan opened the door, Qin Zhou's gaze swept quickly around the room—the forty-two drafts were already sealed in protective sleeves, stacked neatly in a single drawing tube. The suitcase was already packed, just one, an old one, its zipper mended with needle and thread.

"Good morning, Miss Su," Qin Zhou said with a polite nod. "The car is waiting downstairs. Here are the keys to your new residence; your fingerprint has already been registered, so you can use it directly. Also, Mr. Lu asked me to relay that the registration for the International Young Designers Competition has been taken care of—you don't need to do anything, just prepare your work."

Su Qingyuan took the keys and nodded.

She glanced once at the rented room she'd lived in for two years. The paintings on the walls hadn't yet been taken down, a nearly withered pothos sat on the windowsill, the kitchen faucet still dripping.

Nothing here worth missing.

"Let's go," she said.

On the way down, Qin Zhou took the initiative to carry her suitcase, walking ahead. Su Qingyuan followed behind, noticing that all the corridor sensor lights had been replaced—the broken ones repaired, the dim bulbs swapped for bright LEDs, every floor aglow.

A black business sedan was parked at the building entrance—not the Maybach from the night before.

Qin Zhou opened the car door. "Mr. Lu has an early meeting today and can't come in person; he asks for Miss Su's understanding."

Su Qingyuan wanted to say she hadn't expected him to come in person at all, but the words felt strange on the tip of her tongue, so she only gave a soft "Mm."

The car drove out of the old neighborhood in the north of the city, crossed downtown, and turned into a high-end apartment district on the east side of the Capital.

Su Qingyuan watched the increasingly unfamiliar streetscape outside the window and felt, faintly, that something was off.

The car finally stopped beneath an apartment tower in the core of the CBD. She recognized this building, of course—Yunxi, the most expensive residence in the Capital. The top-floor full-floor penthouse was said to sell for over two hundred million; those who lived inside were nothing if not rich and powerful.

Qin Zhou swiped the access card and took her up by elevator straight to the top floor.

When the elevator doors opened, Su Qingyuan froze.

The entire top floor was a single open-plan unit, easily over three hundred square meters by her estimate. The living room was a floor-to-ceiling glass curtain wall, overlooking the whole skyline of the Capital. The décor was minimalist modern, in shades of gray and white, nothing superfluous, every detail exuding a quality that whispered, "I'm very expensive, but I won't say so."

"This is a vacant property under Mr. Lu's name," Qin Zhou said, his tone as flat as if introducing the day's weather. "Mr. Lu says that with you living here, he can come discuss the project plans anytime."

He delivered "come discuss the project plans anytime" with utter naturalness.

Su Qingyuan stood in the middle of the living room, looking out at the panoramic view of the city, only one thought in her mind.

This man's way of doing things was just like his looks—it left a person utterly defenseless.

Her phone buzzed.

She glanced down—a message from Lu Shiyan.

"Are you satisfied with the place?"

Su Qingyuan thought for a moment, then typed a few words: "It's too big. A waste for me to live here alone."

The reply came instantly: "Not a waste. I have a three-thousand-square-meter one. That's far more of a waste."

Su Qingyuan: "…"

She stared at that message for five seconds, the corners of her mouth curving up beyond her control.

This business-world King of Hell, rumored to be cold and untouchable, ascetic and aloof—and he actually made jokes.

Phone in hand, she walked into the bedroom prepared for her—the sheets and bedding all new, in the gray-blue she liked. On the nightstand sat a bouquet of white chamomile, small and very understated, with a card that read: Welcome home.

Not "Welcome to your new home"—but "Welcome home."

Su Qingyuan turned the card over. On the back was a line in Lu Shiyan's handwriting.

The strokes were bold and forceful, just like the man himself.

"You deserve all the best things in this world. —Lu."

Su Qingyuan looked at that line of words, and her eyes suddenly reddened.

In all her life, no one had ever said this to her.

No one.
