His Point of View
The first time Lu Shiyan saw Su Qingyuan was in the autumn of that year.
The Nancheng Design Association's annual gala — he hadn't meant to go. That kind of event was crowded, noisy, and every person who came over to exchange pleasantries had an agenda. His assistant had said, "Mr. Lu, a brand under the Lu Group has been shortlisted for this year's Design Award. Just show your face." So he went. He spent less than twenty minutes in the banquet hall, dealt with the people who had to be dealt with, and was about to leave. But at the door, he stopped.
Outside the glass doors stood a girl.
She wore a sweater washed pale with age, her hair tied carelessly behind her head, a sketchbook in her hands as she bent over it, drawing something. She was utterly absorbed, her brow faintly furrowed, her lips lightly pressed together, the tip of her pencil darting swiftly across the paper. She had no idea anyone behind the glass was watching her. Lu Shiyan stood there and watched her for a long time. Long enough that his assistant came over and asked in a low voice, "Mr. Lu, the car is ready." He didn't answer. He kept watching. The girl finished her drawing, lifted her head, and looked into the banquet hall. There was no envy in her eyes, no longing — only a very quiet light. That light, he had never seen in anyone else's eyes: neither servile nor proud, neither grasping nor competing, simply looking, as if to say, "One day, I'll stand in there too."
"Find out who that girl is," he said. The assistant blinked. "Which one?" He gestured with his eyes. The assistant looked. "I don't know her. I'll go ask around." Later the assistant told him: the girl's name was Su Qingyuan. She wasn't an invited guest — she'd come to deliver documents. She'd been waiting at the door for nearly an hour.
By the time Lu Shiyan walked out of the banquet hall, she was already gone. The doorway was empty, only the night wind tumbling a few fallen leaves across it. He stood there a while. The assistant asked, "Mr. Lu, shall we go?" He said, "Let's go."
After that, he thought of her now and then. Not deliberately — it would be when he passed the creative district, when he saw a jewelry design magazine, on a rainy night. He would wonder: what was that girl doing now? Was she still drawing? Had anyone seen her light? He thought about going to find her, but he didn't. Because he knew she wasn't ready yet. Her eyes still held that "carefulness" — the kind everyone develops after living too long under someone else's roof. He didn't want to appear before she'd found her footing; that way he'd become her "backer" instead of her "fellow traveler." So he waited for her.
On the night of that rainstorm, he drove down that street. It wasn't coincidence — he knew she lived nearby. Ever since the glass doors, he'd had someone keep an eye on how she was doing. Not surveillance, just occasionally knowing that she was all right. The rain was heavy that night, his headlights cutting the road ahead, and he saw someone crouched at the roadside. Soaked through, hair plastered to her face, her expression impossible to make out — but that silhouette, he remembered it. He stopped the car, opened an umbrella, and walked over. She raised her head, rain mixed with tears, her face indistinguishable between the two. He recognized those eyes — the same as outside the glass doors. They held light.
"Get in the car," he said. Not "Why are you here," not "Let me take you home," but "Get in the car." Because he knew what she needed now wasn't a greeting, but an umbrella, a car, an "I'll take you away" that asked no questions.
She hesitated, then stood and went with him. In the car, she leaned back, trembling all over. He turned the heater up high, took off his coat, and handed it to her. She took it, draped it over her shoulders, and said, "Thank you. Sorry to trouble you." Four words, polite, distant. But he could hear the exhaustion beneath them — the exhaustion of someone who had held on for so long and could finally, at last, stop holding on.
He didn't speak, just drove quietly. He knew what she needed now wasn't comfort, but quiet.
Everything that followed felt like the natural course of things — and also like something he'd long been scheming toward. In the hotel lobby, she was set upon by her foster parents and her sister while he stood watching from a distance. He didn't step in right away, because she didn't need him to fight this battle for her. What she needed was to win it herself. And she won. He watched her back as she walked into the elevator, his heart racing faster than it ever had during any business decision.
He sent her a message: "Are you free tonight?" After he hit send, he felt like a green boy. But he didn't regret it. Because he'd waited too long for her, and didn't want to wait any longer.
At the salon, she wore a white knit dress and talked with the industry's heavyweights, neither servile nor proud, perfectly composed. He stood by the window, holding a glass of water, watching her profile. She didn't know he was watching her — no, she knew. She just didn't turn around.
The keys to her studio — he'd prepared them a long time. From the day she signed the contract with Zhou Wanyin, he had his assistant search the creative district for available spaces. The assistant found several, and he was satisfied with none. Finally the assistant said, "Mr. Lu, why don't you go look yourself?" So he went, and stood in that empty eighty-square-meter room, its windows facing an osmanthus tree, sunlight pouring in, bright and warm. He thought of the look in her eyes outside the glass doors — that "one day I'll stand in there too" light. This studio was her "in there."
He had it remodeled, the walls redone in a warm white, the floor laid with a pale grey self-leveling finish, fitted with a professional workbench and lighting. He put a small sofa in the rest area and set a pot of white chamomile on the windowsill. She liked clean, warm colors. She'd said so once, and he'd remembered.
The day he handed her the keys, she took them, her fingertips brushing his, and her ears turned red. He saw it, but said nothing. Some things don't need to be said; it's more interesting to let her discover them herself.
The proposal ring — he'd considered many designs. A diamond was too ordinary, not her style. He thought of her "Daybreak" — that moonstone with three layers of halo. That was her light, and it should become her ring too. He contacted Mr. Chen at Zhenyi and asked whether they could make a moonstone using the Ripple Cut technique. Mr. Chen said, "Mr. Lu, the parameters of the Ripple Cut are exclusively Miss Su's. I wouldn't dare ask her for them." He said, "Then you try. Try as many times as it takes. I'll cover the cost."
They tried for two months and ruined over a hundred moonstones before finally producing one. Three layers of halo — deep blue on the outside, silver-white in the middle, warm gold at the core. Exactly like the one he'd first seen on "Daybreak." Mr. Chen said, "Mr. Lu, this stone was harder to make than Miss Su's. Because there were no parameters." He looked at the moonstone and said, "Worth it."
The inside of the band was to be engraved, and he thought long about the words. In the end he engraved: "From the glass doors to the aurora — from now on, it's you." The glass doors were the beginning; the aurora was the promise he made her. From now on, it's you — not "I like you," not "I love you," but "from now on, it's you." Because "like" and "love" might change, but "from now on" wouldn't.
He left the ring at her door rather than handing it to her himself. Because he was afraid that if he were there, she'd be too embarrassed to cry. He wanted her to see it alone, cry alone, and put on the ring alone. And then, by the time he appeared, she would already have her answer ready.
She did cry, just as he'd expected. He hid around the bend of the stairwell, listening to her sobbing as she crouched at the door, and his heart ached a little. He waited a while, until she had finished crying, before stepping out. She was crouched there, the ring cradled in her hands, her eyes red, like a little cat that had just been picked up off the street. He asked, "You put the ring on. Does it fit?" She raised her hand, the moonstone glowing softly under the streetlight. "It fits." He asked again, "And the answer?" Before he could finish "Will you" — she cut him off. "I will. I have since the day you said, 'Tell me once you're sure.'"
In that moment, he felt he'd spent all the luck of his entire life on this one moment.
On their wedding day, she wore a white dress she'd designed herself, the osmanthus sprigs along the hem glittering in the sunlight. As she walked toward him, his eyes grew hot. Not from being moved, but because he thought of that girl outside the glass doors — in a sweater washed pale, standing at the door, waiting for an hour. No one knew she was standing there. No one knew what she was drawing. But he, behind the glass, had seen it. He had seen her light. Back then he hadn't known that beam of light would illuminate all the days of his life to come.
Now he knew.
The day Lu Xi was born, he stood outside the delivery room, his palms slick with sweat. He had negotiated countless times and never once been nervous. But in that moment, he was nervous. Not because he feared something would go wrong — he trusted the best doctors and the best equipment were all there. It was because the person he was about to meet was the "from now on" of him and Su Qingyuan.
When the nurse carried her out, the little thing was wrinkled, eyes shut, lips puckering in and out. He looked at that tiny face, and a voice inside him said — this is it. He reached out and awkwardly took her, like cradling a fragile treasure. She was so small, so light, so soft. His tears fell. When Su Qingyuan was wheeled out of the delivery room and saw him crying, she smiled. "You're crying?" she said. He wiped his eyes. "No." She said, "Liar." He didn't argue. He walked over and bent down to kiss her forehead. "You worked hard."
She said, "It wasn't hard." Then she reached out and touched their daughter's face. "She looks like you." He said, "Her eyes look like yours." She smiled, and after smiling she cried again. He held her hand and stayed with her, until she had finished crying.
When Lu Xi was three, she asked a question. "Daddy, how did you meet Mommy?" He thought for a moment. "Behind a glass door." Lu Xi didn't understand. "Behind a glass door?" He said, "Mm. Mommy was outside the door, and Daddy was inside. She didn't know Daddy was watching her." Lu Xi asked again, "Then why didn't you go out and find her?" He said, "Because Mommy wasn't ready yet." Lu Xi blinked, half understanding. "Is she ready now?" He smiled. "She's ready." Lu Xi nodded, satisfied. "Good, then."
He picked his daughter up and walked to the window. Down in the yard, Su Qingyuan was crouched beneath the osmanthus tree gathering fallen blossoms, picking them up carefully, one by one, and placing them in a bamboo basket. She'd said she was going to make osmanthus preserve to spread on Lu Xi's bread.
The sunlight fell over her, the wind tousling her hair a little, a few stray strands brushing the side of her face. She wore that old sweater — not the one from outside the glass doors, that one she'd long stopped wearing, but this one was white too. She liked white. As if she'd sensed his gaze, she lifted her head and smiled at him.
That smile was a world apart from the one outside the glass doors. Back then she wouldn't have smiled like that; back then her smile was careful, edged with a wish to please. Now her smile was open, sure, the smile of someone who knew she was loved.
He set his daughter down, walked out of the house, and went over to her. "How many have you gathered?"
"Half a basket. That's enough. Let's go back and make the preserve."
"I'll carry it for you."
She stood, handed him the bamboo basket, and patted the dirt off her knees. He took the basket, and with his other hand he took hers. She didn't pull away — she held his hand back.
The two of them walked back to the house, behind them a ground strewn with golden osmanthus and a whole yard full of sweet fragrance.
Sometimes Lu Shiyan wondered: if not for that rainy night, where would he be now? Perhaps still in his office on the eighty-eighth floor, looking out at the night view alone. Perhaps he'd think now and then of that girl outside the glass doors, but he wouldn't go find her, because he'd believe she wasn't ready yet. He didn't know that she had, in fact, always been ready. It was only that he had appeared at exactly the right moment — not too early, not too late, on a rainy night.
He was grateful it rained that day. Grateful she hadn't brought an umbrella. Grateful he'd driven down that street. Grateful that when she crouched at the roadside, he'd stopped the car. Grateful that she'd said "Sorry to trouble you," and he'd said "Get in the car." Grateful that all the gratitude had turned into "from now on."
