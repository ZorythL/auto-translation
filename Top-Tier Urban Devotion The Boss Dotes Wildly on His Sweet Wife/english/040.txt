Chapter 40 — Light and Everyday Life
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626242.html

After Venice, Su Qingyuan's life was filled with two things: the creation of her new collection, and—what Lu Shiyan called "wedding preparations." In truth, he did most of it; her job was only to nod or shake her head. Choosing the venue, she nodded; choosing the date, she nodded; choosing the invitation design, she looked at it for three seconds—"This one"—and went back to bending over her sketches. Lu Shiyan never rushed her. She knew he loved this state of things: her busy, him beside her.
One evening she lifted her head from her workbench and found Lu Shiyan sitting on the sofa, a notebook in his hand, writing something.
"What are you writing?"
"The guest list."
"Let me see."
Lu Shiyan handed the notebook over. Su Qingyuan took it and looked—it was crammed full of names. Zhou Wanyin, Qu Ya, Anna, Jean-Pierre, President Chen, Secretary-General Bai, the director of the Cultural Bureau… She looked at those names and suddenly realized how large her world had become. A year ago, her phone's contact list had held only a handful of names; now her wedding could fill an entire hall.
"Isn't that too many people?" she asked.
"You want fewer?"
"Not exactly. I just feel… it's a little unreal."
Lu Shiyan looked at her. "What part is unreal?"
"A year ago, I was on my own. Now I'm about to marry you."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a second, then said, "A year ago, you weren't on your own either. It was just that I wasn't beside you yet."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot. She handed the notebook back and turned to keep sketching. What she was drawing was the jewelry she would wear at the wedding—not for anyone else, for herself. A necklace, its pendant a single moonstone, cut in the Ripple Cut—but this time the halo wasn't three layers, it was four. At the very center was deep blue, then silver-white spreading outward, then warm gold beyond that, and the outermost layer a ring of clear, almost invisible light. She wanted to call that fourth layer "the future"—an unknown light, but a light that existed.
The wedding was set for late September, when the osmanthus bloomed. The venue was a manor on the outskirts of Nancheng, not large, but with one enormous osmanthus tree, its canopy spread open like a golden umbrella, its fragrance so thick it would not dissolve. The first time Su Qingyuan went to see the place, she stood beneath that osmanthus tree and tilted her head up at the little golden-yellow flowers crowding the branches, and suddenly remembered the autumn before, the day she had just moved into her studio—the osmanthus by the door had smelled just like this.
"Here," she said.
"All right." Lu Shiyan stood behind her, looking at that osmanthus tree too.
The wedding was small; only some fifty-odd people were invited. Su Qingyuan didn't wear a bridal gown. She wore a white floor-length dress of her own design, its hem embroidered with osmanthus branches in real silk and silver thread, so that when she walked the sprays of blossom seemed to sway gently in the wind. The necklace was made by her own hands—the four-layered moonstone, glowing with a soft light in the sun.
Lu Shiyan wore a black suit, no tie, the top button of his shirt left undone—she'd told him to leave it. "Too tight," she said. "It's not like you're going to a meeting." He did as she said.
The ceremony was simple. No officiant, no long-winded vows. They stood beneath the osmanthus tree, face to face, and said only a few words.
Lu Shiyan said, "Su Qingyuan, from that glass door to today, I've thought many times about what I'd say to you. But standing here, I find there's nothing I need to say. Because everything I wanted to say, you already know."
Su Qingyuan's tears fell.
She said, "Lu Shiyan, I used to think I'd used up all the luck of my entire life. Later I found out I hadn't. The best one was saved for the very end."
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently wiped the tears from her face. Then he lowered his head and kissed her.
Osmanthus flowers fell on their shoulders, like golden snow.
Zhou Wanyin stood off to the side, crying harder than Su Qingyuan. Qu Ya handed her a tissue; she took it, wiped her eyes, and cried again. Anna stood at the back, taking a few photos with her phone and posting them to the Cartier headquarters group chat. Jean-Pierre replied with a thumbs-up.
After the ceremony, everyone sat beneath the osmanthus tree to eat. The food was Lu Shiyan's choosing—not the standard hotel banquet sort, but the most authentic home-style cooking of Nancheng: sweet-and-sour pork ribs, stir-fried seasonal greens, red-braised pork, tomato and egg-drop soup. Every dish was something Su Qingyuan loved.
Zhou Wanyin drifted over to Su Qingyuan's side, a glass of red wine in hand. "Qingyuan, you look beautiful today."
"Thank you, Wanyin."
"I'm not being polite. You really do look beautiful. Your eyes used to always have that 'I need to prove myself' light. Today it's gone. Today it's the light of 'I know who I am.'"
Su Qingyuan looked at Zhou Wanyin and smiled. "Wanyin, thank you. Without you, there'd be no me as I am today."
"Don't say that. Without me, you'd have gotten here too. Just a little slower."
Qu Ya came over as well and clinked her glass against Su Qingyuan's. "Qingyuan, the 'Roots and Light' joint exhibition—do you want to do a second season next year?"
"Sure. What's the theme?"
"Haven't thought of one yet. Tell me once you've figured it out."
"All right."
Anna said a blessing in French, and Jean-Pierre translated it into English: "He says Cartier is waiting for your next collection." Su Qingyuan smiled and nodded.
The guests left one by one. The sky gradually darkened, and the lights of the manor came on one after another. The long table beneath the osmanthus tree was littered with cups and dishes, but it was warm and cozy. Su Qingyuan sat under the tree, leaning against Lu Shiyan's shoulder, watching the little golden-yellow flowers glittering across the whole tree in the lamplight.
"Tired?" he asked.
"No."
"Really?"
"Really. Today is the least tired day of my whole life."
Lu Shiyan smiled and reached out to pick an osmanthus blossom from her hair.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"From now on, every year at this time, let's come here to see the osmanthus."
"All right."
"Every year."
"Every year."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes and breathed in the fragrance of osmanthus. She thought of the autumn before, the day she first went to the studio—there had been an osmanthus tree by the door then too, and he had stood beneath it waiting for her, a key in his hand. He'd said, "The key's here. Come look anytime." Back then she'd thought he was just being kind, helping her find a studio; only later did she learn that, for that one studio, he had taken control of the entire creative park.
She opened her eyes and looked at him. "Lu Shiyan, when did you start liking me?"
Lu Shiyan thought about it. "The glass door."
"That early?"
"Mm. I didn't know it then—that it was liking you."
"When did you know?"
"The rainy night. You were crouched at the side of the road, soaked through. I thought: this person, I can't let her be alone."
Su Qingyuan's tears fell again. She reached out and held him, burying her face against his chest. "Lu Shiyan, thank you for not giving up on me."
Lu Shiyan tightened his arms, his chin resting on the top of her head. "Giving up on you was never one of my options."
Beneath the osmanthus tree, the shadows of the two embracing fell on the ground, very close together.
After the wedding, Su Qingyuan didn't change her surname. Lu Shiyan never brought it up either. Once Zhou Wanyin asked her, "Your husband, President Lu, didn't make you change your name?" Su Qingyuan smiled. "He said, Su Qingyuan is Su Qingyuan. Change it and you wouldn't be you anymore." Zhou Wanyin sighed, "Did you save the galaxy in a past life or something?"
Su Qingyuan thought about it. "Maybe. But I think it's just that I got lucky in this life."
The first year after the wedding, they went to Northern Europe to see the auroras. The second year, they went to Paris and took another photo together in front of the Wall of Love. The third year, they went to New York, and on the observation deck of the Empire State Building, Su Qingyuan's belly had already begun to swell a little. She was pregnant.
On the day she found out she was pregnant, Su Qingyuan was in the studio polishing a piece for her new collection. She set down her carving knife and made a trip to the hospital. When she got the results, she sat on the long bench in the hospital corridor and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "You're going to be a father." There was a full minute of silence on the other end. Then the phone rang. His voice shook a little. "Really?"
"Really."
"Where are you?"
"The hospital."
"Don't move. I'll come get you."
Su Qingyuan smiled. "You don't have to come get me. I can go home on my own."
"Don't move. I'll come get you."
He hung up. Su Qingyuan sat on the bench, looking at that message on her phone screen—"You're going to be a father"—and the corners of her mouth curved up. After about twenty minutes, Lu Shiyan appeared at the far end of the hospital corridor, his suit jacket askew from running, his tie loosened, his hair blown into a mess by the wind. He had never been so disheveled, and he had never looked so good. He came over, crouched in front of her, and took her hands. "How far along?"
"Six weeks."
"Are you all right? Anything feel uncomfortable?"
"No. Just a little tired."
"Then let's go home. Rest."
Su Qingyuan looked at how nervous he was and smiled. "Lu Shiyan, you're more nervous than I am."
"Mm. Nervous."
He helped her up, his arm circling her waist, and they walked very slowly, as if he were afraid she might fall. Su Qingyuan leaned against him, breathing in his familiar scent, and felt that this life had been worth it.
Through the pregnancy, Su Qingyuan didn't stop working. She said using her brain was good for the baby. Lu Shiyan didn't try to stop her; he only made nutritious meals every day, varying them constantly, and watched over her while she ate. She put on fifteen pounds; he lost ten.
At seven months pregnant, Su Qingyuan completed the fifth and final piece of her new collection. She named this piece "Beginning"—the same character as in "First Light." She said the child's arrival was another beginning.
On the day of the delivery, Lu Shiyan stayed with her at the hospital for a full twenty hours. Su Qingyuan was in so much pain she couldn't speak, so he held her hand and said, over and over, "I'm here." She didn't cry; he did.
Their daughter was born at dawn, the sky outside just brightening, morning light leaking through the gap in the curtains to fall on her wrinkled little face. Su Qingyuan looked at her, tears streaming unstoppably.
"She's like you," Lu Shiyan said, his voice hoarse in a way that didn't sound like him.
"Like me how?"
"Her eyes. The way you looked at me that first time outside the glass door—that light."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. She reached out a finger and gently touched her daughter's tiny hand. That little hand instantly gripped her finger, tight.
Their daughter's name was Lu Xi. Lu Shiyan chose it. Xi—morning light. The same meaning as the "Light" in "First Light." He said she was born in the morning light, and she was their light.
After her confinement month, the first thing Su Qingyuan did was go to the studio. After more than a year without working in earnest, a thin layer of dust had settled on her workbench. She opened the window, and the autumn wind surged in, sweeping away the stale air in the room. Outside, the osmanthus tree was covered in blossom, the fragrance sweet to the point of cloying.
She sat at her workbench, picked up her carving knife, and began to polish a new piece—a small brooch, shaped like a door, half-open, with light spilling through the crack. When she finished, she set it at the head of her daughter's crib. When she grew up, she would tell her—this door is the door where Papa waited for your mama to come home. And it's the door where Mama waited for you to come into this world.
Sometimes Su Qingyuan would think of that rainy night. Of how she had crouched at the side of the road, soaked through, thinking her life was over. She hadn't known then that that rain was the end of the storm, and also the beginning of dawn.
Now she knew.
She sat beneath the osmanthus tree, Lu Xi in her arms, Lu Shiyan beside her with a cup of tea in his hand. Sunlight leaked down through the gaps in the leaves, falling over all three of them, like gold shattered across the ground.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"What do you think Lu Xi will do when she grows up?"
"I don't know. But whatever she does, it'll be wonderful."
"Why?"
"Because she's your daughter."
Su Qingyuan smiled and lowered her head to look at Lu Xi, already asleep in her arms. The tiny mouth was pursed slightly, the breathing soft, the lashes long and curled. She reached out and gently touched her cheek.
Lu Shiyan watched the two of them, the light in his eyes gentler than it had ever been.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"Thank you."
"Thank me for what?"
"Thank you for letting me wait until I found you."
Su Qingyuan looked up at him. Sunlight fell across the side of his face; his profile was still as handsome as ever.
"You're welcome."
She leaned against his shoulder, holding their daughter, breathing in the fragrance of osmanthus. In the distance the city glittered in the autumn sunlight; everything was quiet, everything was beautiful.
Su Qingyuan suddenly thought of the words she had written long ago, in that eighty-square-meter studio—"All the darkness will pass. The sky will always brighten in the end."
The sky had brightened. And it would go on brightening, forever.
