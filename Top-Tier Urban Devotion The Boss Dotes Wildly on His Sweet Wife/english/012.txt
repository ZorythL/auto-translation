During the three-day burst of inspiration, Su Qingyuan practically lived in her studio.
She left at the crack of dawn every morning and didn't come home until deep in the night, surviving the hours in between on takeout and coffee. Her worktable was buried under design sketches, one after another, pencil lines evolving from rough to refined, from blurry to crisp.
Over those three days, the prototype of the "First Light" series went from nothing to something, from zero to complete.
Seven pieces in all.
A necklace, a bracelet, drop earrings, a ring, a brooch, a hair ornament, and cufflinks.
At the heart of every piece was a single shaft of light—light breaking through the clouds, light piercing the deep sea, light shining into the abyss, the first ray before dawn.
Each of the seven pieces stood on its own, yet together they told one complete story: struggling out of the darkness, meeting that first ray of light, being wrapped in light, and finally becoming the light herself.
Su Qingyuan looked at the seven finished drafts spread across her worktable, her eyes faintly stinging.
Not from exhaustion, but from that particular kind of sting—the relief of finally pulling out something she'd kept bottled up in her heart for so long.
These seven drafts weren't drawings of jewelry.
They were her.
From being exploited, betrayed, and trampled into the dirt, to encountering that rainy night, that car, that man, to standing here now, brush in hand, becoming her own light.
Her phone buzzed.
Lu Shiyan: [Three days, no word. Still alive?]
Su Qingyuan looked at the message, smiled, and snapped a photo of her worktable to send over.
In the photo, the seven design drafts were lined up neatly, surrounded by scattered pencil shavings, an eraser, and a half-cup of coffee gone cold.
Lu Shiyan's reply came fast:
[This is the fruit of three sleepless nights?]
Su Qingyuan: [I slept. Four hours a day.]
Lu Shiyan: [……]
Lu Shiyan: [I'm coming over now.]
Su Qingyuan blinked, and just as she was about to reply "no need," another message came through:
[Not to see you. To collect rent. First quarter's.]
Su Qingyuan looked at those two words—"collect rent"—and simply couldn't press the corners of her mouth back down.
When this man made up an excuse, could he at least put a little more heart into it?

Forty minutes later, Lu Shiyan appeared at the studio door.
He didn't come empty-handed.
A bag of fruit in his left hand, a bag of snacks in his right, and an assistant carrying an insulated bag trailing behind him.
Su Qingyuan opened the door, took in the whole spectacle, and froze for a second. "Are you… moving in?"
Lu Shiyan didn't answer. He stepped past her and walked in, his gaze sweeping across the worktable, lingering for a few seconds on the seven design drafts before turning to her.
Three days apart, and she'd gotten visibly thinner. Faint shadows under her eyes, slightly chapped lips, hair carelessly tied back with a few stray wisps falling against her cheek, and pencil dust smudged on her work clothes.
But her eyes were bright.
That weary yet exhilarated brightness of someone who'd stayed up all night to finish an important piece.
Lu Shiyan looked at her like this, his brow furrowing almost imperceptibly, but he said nothing. He simply set the fruit down on the little round table in the lounge area, told the assistant "put it there," and the assistant placed the insulated bag on the table and tactfully withdrew.
The door closed.
Only the two of them were left in the studio.
"Come eat." Lu Shiyan opened the insulated bag and took out one container after another.
Su Qingyuan walked over and looked—chicken soup, stir-fried seasonal vegetables, blanched shrimp, and a small bowl of rice.
Not takeout.
Cooked at home, packed in an insulated bag, and driven over forty minutes to be delivered here.
"Did you make this?" Su Qingyuan looked up at him.
Lu Shiyan undid his shirt cuffs and rolled his sleeves up to his forearms, calmly setting out the utensils as he spoke. "My housekeeper made it. I can't cook."
Watching the way he rolled up his cuffs, Su Qingyuan suddenly felt that this man could even say "I can't cook" with such unabashed confidence, and look unfairly good doing it.
"Sit down." He handed her the chopsticks.
Su Qingyuan obediently sat down and took a sip of the chicken soup. The temperature was just right, neither scalding nor cold, and so savory she nearly swallowed her own tongue.
She'd drunk nearly half the bowl before she remembered to ask, "Didn't you say you came to collect rent?"
Lu Shiyan sat down across from her, leaning back in his chair, posture relaxed, voice casual: "Eat first. We'll talk about the rent after."
Su Qingyuan shot him a look, more and more certain that the real reason he'd come today had nothing to do with rent.
But she didn't call him out, just lowered her head and ate in earnest.
Because she really was hungry.
Three days of running on coffee and sandwiches, and her stomach had long since started protesting.
The way she ate wasn't exactly graceful, but it was earnest. She chewed every bite carefully, as if completing some important task.
Lu Shiyan watched her eat, saying nothing, doing nothing. He just sat there, quiet and patient, waiting for her to finish.
The feeling of being watched without being rushed made some place deep inside Su Qingyuan go soft, so very soft.

Su Qingyuan finished eating, packed up the containers, washed her hands, and returned to the worktable.
Lu Shiyan stood before the seven design drafts, hands tucked into his trouser pockets, gazing down at them.
"This is the 'First Light' series." Su Qingyuan walked to his side and pointed at the first one. "The inspiration comes from… myself."
Lu Shiyan turned his head to look at her.
Su Qingyuan didn't look at him; she kept her eyes on the drafts, her voice low. "Clawing out of the darkness, meeting the first ray of light, and then discovering the light was on you all along. This story—you should know it well. Because you saw it from the very start."
The studio fell silent for a few seconds.
Outside the window, the branches of the osmanthus tree swayed gently in the wind, their shadows falling across the paper like dancers.
"This story," Lu Shiyan's voice was very low, low enough that it seemed meant for her ears alone, "I'm in it too."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him.
He wasn't looking at her; his eyes were lowered to the paper, the line of his profile cold yet tender.
"You said you met the light." He paused. "If that light is me, then I'm honored."
Su Qingyuan's heart suddenly began to race, so fast she was sure he could hear it.
She wanted to say something—wanted to say "not just you, but myself too," wanted to say "you're not just a ray of light, you're the warmest person I've ever met," wanted to say "could you please stop saying things that make a person's heart race like this."
But she didn't get any of it out.
Because Lu Shiyan had already turned around and was walking toward the door, his tone returning to its usual coolness: "That's enough for today. I'll come check on your progress again tomorrow."
He pulled open the door, and the scent of osmanthus came flooding in.
"By the way," he glanced back at her, "the rent—we'll talk about it next time."
The door closed.
Su Qingyuan stood where she was, staring at the closed door, and looked down at her own racing heart—if a heartbeat had a sound, the whole building could probably hear it.
She turned and walked back to the worktable, picked up her pen, and tried to keep drawing.
But the tip of the pen hovered above the paper, refusing to come down.
Her mind was full of that one line of his: "If that light is me, then I'm honored."
Su Qingyuan put the pen down and buried her face in both hands.
It was over.
Completely over.

The next afternoon, Su Qingyuan was polishing the first sample of the "First Light" series in her studio when a burst of frantic knocking suddenly came at the door.
Not Lu Shiyan's polite three knocks.
The kind that meant—someone hostile, frantic, knocking like they wanted to smash the door down.
Su Qingyuan set down her tools and went to open it.
Two uniformed police officers stood outside.
"Are you Su Qingyuan?"
"I am."
"Someone has filed a report accusing you of plagiarizing and stealing another person's original design work. Please come with us to the station to cooperate with the investigation."
A "buzz" went off in Su Qingyuan's head, but her face showed no panic.
"Who filed the report?"
"The complainant prefers not to disclose that. Please cooperate."
Su Qingyuan took a deep breath, turned to grab her phone and keys, locked up the studio, and got into the car with the officers.
In the car, she sent Lu Shiyan a message:
[Someone reported me to the police for plagiarism. I'm on my way to the station now.]
This time there was no instant reply.
Just a dozen-odd seconds, and his call came through.
"Which station?" His voice was low and steady, but Su Qingyuan caught the tension beneath it.
She told him the name of the station.
"Don't panic. Don't give any statement to anyone alone—wait until I get there. Twenty minutes, at most."
The call ended.
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, watching the streetscape rush backward outside the window, her heart pounding but her mind oddly calm.
She had a gut feeling.
The person who'd called the police was Su Yurou.
---
The police station.
Su Qingyuan was led into an interrogation room where someone was already seated.
Su Yurou.
She wore a white dress and delicate, light makeup, her eyes red as if she'd been crying. Beside her sat a middle-aged man in a gray suit—presumably the lawyer she'd hired.
The moment she saw Su Qingyuan walk in, Su Yurou shot to her feet, pointed at her, and said in a trembling voice, "Officers, it's her! She stole my design!"
Su Qingyuan looked at her expressionlessly.
"Su Yurou, filing a false police report carries legal consequences."
"I didn't file a false report!" Su Yurou's tears spilled over. "The 'First Light' series was my idea first! I have a whole notebook of design sketches! It was you—you stole it from me!"
As she spoke, she pulled a sketchbook out of her bag, flipped it open, and handed it to the officers.
Su Qingyuan's gaze swept over it, her pupils contracting slightly.
The sketchbook did indeed contain rough drafts resembling the "First Light" concept. The theme of light and shadow, the motif of breaking free from a cocoon—a few of the compositions even looked very much like the ones she'd drawn.
But the strokes in those sketches were clumsy and the structure scattered, clearly the work of an amateur copying from some template.
To be precise, Su Yurou had peeked at her sketchbook when she wasn't paying attention, then drawn it from memory.
The one who'd stolen the design was biting back, accusing the victim of being the thief.
Su Qingyuan let out a cold laugh.
"Su Yurou, when did you draw this sketchbook?"
"Three months ago!" Su Yurou wept harder. "I worked on it for a long time, I'd meant to enter a competition with it, never imagining you'd beat me to publishing it…"
"Three months ago?" Su Qingyuan's tone was as calm as if she were remarking on the nice weather. "Then do you know that the core technique of the 'First Light' series is a bidirectional cutting process for moonstone? I only developed that technique last month. So your three-month-old draft already depicted this exact cutting method?"
Su Yurou's expression stiffened for a moment.
"I… mine were just concept sketches back then, I didn't draw them in that much detail…"
"Concept sketches?" Su Qingyuan pulled up a photo on her phone and handed it to the officers. "This is my sketchbook from three years ago, and one of the pages has the earliest prototype of 'First Light.' Three years ago, Su Yurou, you were still studying marketing in university and had never touched jewelry design. How did your concept fly onto a page in my sketchbook from three years ago?"
The color drained completely from Su Yurou's face.
The lawyer beside her leaned in to look at the photo, his brow knitting.
The officers looked at the photo, then at Su Yurou's sketchbook, and their expressions shifted.
"Ms. Su, you say you drew this three months ago, but the evidence here shows a similar concept existed three years ago. How do you explain that?"
"I… back then I…" Su Yurou stammered incoherently. "Maybe I… happened to catch a glimpse of her draft, and then I just…"
"So you're admitting that you saw her design?"
"No! I didn't! She stole mine!"
Su Yurou's voice turned shrill, every trace of her earlier piteous fragility now gone.
At that very moment, the door of the interrogation room was pushed open.
Lu Shiyan walked in.
Behind him followed a man in a tailored suit and gold-rimmed glasses—unmistakably a top-tier lawyer.
The air pressure in the entire room shifted in an instant.
Lu Shiyan didn't spare Su Yurou a glance. He walked straight to Su Qingyuan's side, lowered his head, and asked, "Are you all right?"
"I'm fine." Su Qingyuan looked up at him and, inexplicably, felt herself settle.
Only then did Lu Shiyan turn to the officers, his tone not heavy, yet every word carrying an unquestionable weight: "I am Su Qingyuan's representative. She will give no statement until my legal counsel is present. Furthermore, regarding the false police report, we reserve the right to pursue the matter."
The lawyer behind him stepped forward and offered his card: "I'm a senior partner at the Jintiancheng Law Firm. My surname is Gu. From this moment on, I represent Ms. Su Qingyuan in this case."
Su Yurou's lawyer took the card, glanced at it, and his face instantly turned grim.
Jintiancheng. One of the top three law firms in Nancheng. This Lawyer Gu had never lost an intellectual property case.
Lu Shiyan picked up Su Yurou's sketchbook, flipped through two pages, and handed it to his own lawyer, his voice as light as if discussing some trivial little matter:
"Lawyer Gu, I need you to do two things for me. First, have the ink in this sketchbook dated. Second, pull the surveillance footage from Su Qingyuan's studio and apartment to prove that Su Yurou entered her spaces while she was away."
He paused and looked at Su Yurou.
That single glance was as cold as if he were watching an insect in its dying throes.
"Ms. Su, you should count yourself fortunate that what you did today was call the police, rather than take direct action."
"Otherwise, you wouldn't be sitting in a police station right now."
Su Yurou trembled from head to toe, her teeth chattering.
She suddenly realized that this gamble of hers had backfired.
And backfired catastrophically.

The matter resolved faster than anyone had anticipated.
The ink-dating results showed that the earliest page of the sketches in Su Yurou's notebook had been drawn fifteen days ago, and the latest just three days ago. The so-called "three months ago" was nothing but a lie.
The surveillance footage showed that Su Yurou had, on three separate occasions, entered Su Qingyuan's apartment while she was out—using a key she'd secretly copied back when she still lived in the Su household.
The evidence was ironclad.
Su Yurou was lawfully detained on suspicion of malicious false accusation and copyright infringement.
By the time Su Qingyuan walked out of the police station, the sky had already darkened.
She stood on the steps as the night wind blew past, carrying a slight chill.
Lu Shiyan stood behind her and draped his coat over her shoulders.
Su Qingyuan didn't refuse.
After a moment of silence, she said, "I thought she would let it go."
"She won't." Lu Shiyan's voice was calm. "People like her never reflect on themselves; they only blame others. The better off you are, the more she'll hate you."
Su Qingyuan nodded and pulled the coat closer around her. It carried the crisp, pleasant scent of him.
"Just now you said she should count herself fortunate it was the police she called, and not direct action. What did you mean by that?"

"Just what it sounds like."

From the night of that rainstorm onward, Lu Shiyan hadn't simply been good to her.
He'd been shielding her from things all along.
