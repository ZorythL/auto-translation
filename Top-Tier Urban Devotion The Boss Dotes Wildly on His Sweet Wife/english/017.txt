Chapter 17 — Undercurrents at the Biennale, and She Meets Them with Composure
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626219.html

Three days after the launch, the news became official: "Daybreak" had made the final shortlist of the Biennale.
Announced alongside it were the other eleven shortlisted pieces. Twelve works, from nine countries and regions — and Su Qingyuan was the youngest designer among them, and the only one to make the cut as an independent designer.
Beneath the official announcement, the comment section was as lively as a festival.
"Su Qingyuan is incredible!!! The Ripple Cut has finally been recognized internationally!"
"The only Asian among the twelve works — go for it!!"
"Wait — look at that piece on the shortlist, 'Undertow' — the designer is Su Yurou???"
That comment dropped like a depth charge and blew the whole thread wide open.
Su Qingyuan saw it while she was brewing tea in her studio. Her fingers froze, and she tapped open the shortlist to read it carefully.
"Undertow," designer: Su Yurou (China).
Her pupils contracted slightly.
Su Yurou. The one who'd been out of detention for less than a week. The one the entire industry had spat on, the one the brands had cut ties with, the one the whole internet had mocked.
She had made the Biennale final shortlist.
Su Qingyuan set down her phone, carried her teacup to the window, and took a slow sip.
Something was off.
She knew Su Yurou's design ability better than anyone — shaky fundamentals, mediocre creativity, and the only "talent" she had was stealing other people's work. Even if she'd studied like a madwoman after landing in detention, there was no way she could have produced a piece worthy of the Biennale shortlist in such a short span of time.
Unless that piece wasn't hers.
Su Qingyuan put down the teacup and dialed Zhou Wanyin.
"Wanyin-jie, have you seen the shortlist?"
"I have." Zhou Wanyin's voice was heavy. "I'm looking into it now. Su Yurou's piece, 'Undertow' — there's no prior public record of it anywhere. It just appeared out of nowhere and went straight into Biennale review. Something's wrong here."
"Can you find out who her sponsor was?"
"I'm digging." Zhou Wanyin paused. "Qingyuan, there's something I have to tell you — on the Biennale's judging committee, there's a juror who's a distant relative of Su Yurou's mother, Liu Xiulan. I'm not saying there's definitely something shady going on, but that connection is enough to put me on guard."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, her mind racing.
"Wanyin-jie, can you help me get hold of the materials for 'Undertow'? I don't need detailed specs — just the style, the craftsmanship, the theme."
"I'll try. But the review materials are confidential. I can only ask around through personal contacts, and I might not get anything."
"All right. Thank you for the trouble."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan stood at the window, looking at the osmanthus tree outside. Most of the blossoms had already fallen, leaving only a few sparse clusters, but the fragrance lingered, faint and elusive.
Her phone buzzed.
Lu Shiyan: [Saw the shortlist. I've got people looking into the Su Yurou matter.]
Su Qingyuan: [How did you know I was thinking about it right now?]
Lu Shiyan: [Because whatever you're thinking, you write it into your silences.]
Su Qingyuan looked at the line of text and curved her lips. She put down the phone, sat back at her workbench, and picked up her carving knife.
No matter what tricks Su Yurou pulled, there was only one thing she could do — polish her own work to perfection.
Let the work speak for itself.
The rest, she'd leave to time, and to Lu Shiyan.
Three days later, Zhou Wanyin came with news.
"I got it. A friend of mine in the judging committee secretariat secretly photographed the brief for 'Undertow' and sent it to me." She handed her phone to Su Qingyuan, her expression complicated. "See for yourself."
Su Qingyuan took it, and after a single glance her fingers tightened.
The centerpiece of "Undertow" was a black opal, and the cutting method was… the Ripple Cut.
Not "similar." Not "inspired by." It was completely, identically, the Ripple Cut.
The halo radiated outward from the core in ring after ring, three graduated layers — deep blue on the outer ring, silver-white in the middle, warm gold at the core.
Exactly like "Daybreak."
But "Undertow" was darker, more oppressive in its overall design. The black opal's base tone swallowed most of the halo, leaving only that inner warm gold like the last breath escaping a throttled throat.
The piece's description read: "Undertow is the last light at the bottom of the abyss. Not everyone is worthy of seeing it."
Su Qingyuan stared at that line, her fingers trembling slightly.
Not out of fear. Out of fury.
Su Yurou had stolen her craft.
Not stolen a design — a design could be imitated. But the Ripple Cut's cutting parameters, the angle of the blade's entry, the sequence of polishing — those were core techniques she'd worked out by ruining more than thirty pieces of moonstone. She hadn't applied for a patent, because the technique was too novel and the patent process took too long, and she couldn't afford to wait.
Su Yurou must have gotten hold of her craft parameters some way or other.
"How did she do it?" Su Qingyuan's voice was cold.
Zhou Wanyin's face wasn't pleasant either. "I checked the timeline. The day you finished 'Daybreak,' photographer Lin came to shoot the finished piece. Was your studio door unlocked that day?"
Su Qingyuan thought back, and her expression changed abruptly.
That day photographer Lin had come to shoot, hauling equipment in and out, and she really hadn't locked the studio door. And photographer Lin's assistant — she remembered a young girl, wearing a mask, hardly speaking, always tidying gear in the corner.
"That assistant."
"I suspect her too." Zhou Wanyin nodded. "Photographer Lin told me afterward that the assistant was someone he'd hired on short notice. Her résumé said she was a photography student, but she only worked one day and then vanished. The phone number was a burner — no identity could be traced."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes and took a deep breath.
A setup. It had started back then.
Su Yurou hadn't been idle in detention — she'd been planning. Before she got out, she'd already had someone lay the trap. That assistant had obtained the finished details of "Daybreak," and may even have secretly photographed Su Qingyuan's technical notes on the Ripple Cut sitting on her workbench.
Then, in the shortest possible time, Su Yurou had reproduced a piece and submitted it just before the Biennale registration deadline.
She knew Su Qingyuan's "Daybreak" was certain to make the shortlist. What she wanted wasn't to win — it was to compete on the same stage. To stand on the same platform as Su Qingyuan, in front of the whole world.
Lose, and she'd be "the newcomer who lost but is still very impressive." Win, and she'd turn everything around completely, climbing back up by stepping on Su Qingyuan.
No matter how you ran the numbers, she came out ahead.
"Wanyin-jie, do the Biennale's judging criteria have any rules about originality of craft?"
"They do. If two pieces use the same original technique, the committee will investigate who the technique originally belongs to. Whoever created it earlier is the originator." Zhou Wanyin paused. "Is your record of 'Daybreak's' creative process complete?"
"Complete. From the first sketch to every step of the process, I have timestamped records."
Zhou Wanyin let out a breath of relief. "Good. Get the materials ready and I'll help you submit them to the committee. Su Yurou likes playing dirty, doesn't she? Then let the international judges decide who the real originator is."
Su Qingyuan nodded, opened her laptop, and began compiling the creative archive of "Daybreak."
From the first crude concept sketch, to the first experimental log of the Ripple Cut, to the parameter log of the thirty-first successful experiment, to the final photographs and videos of the finished piece. Every entry had a timestamp precise to the second, and some even carried system-generated proof of time — although she couldn't reveal the System's existence, the timestamps it generated were blockchain-grade and impossible to tamper with.
She was halfway through compiling when Lu Shiyan arrived.
He didn't knock; he pushed the door open and walked straight in — Su Qingyuan had given him a key, and he was the only one who had one.
"Compiling evidence?" He glanced at her screen, unsurprised.
"You already know about Su Yurou?"
"I know. I tracked down that assistant's real identity." Lu Shiyan set a USB drive on the desk. "Her name is Chen Xiaoman. She's the younger sister of Su Yurou's college roommate. On the third day Su Yurou was in detention, Chen Xiaoman started submitting her résumé on job sites, specifically targeting temporary positions related to jewelry photography. She was aiming for photographer Lin's team, because Lin is acknowledged in the industry as the top jewelry photographer — Su Qingyuan was bound to hire him."
Su Qingyuan looked at the USB drive and was silent for a few seconds.
"You're saying that even while she was in detention, Su Yurou was already plotting all of this?"
"Phones aren't allowed in detention, but she could see her lawyer. Her lawyer visited twice a week." Lu Shiyan's voice was cold. "She passed information through her lawyer and had people on the outside carry it out."
Su Qingyuan leaned back in her chair, gazing at the ceiling, and suddenly laughed.
"Lu Shiyan, do you think that if she'd put all this scheming into honest work, she'd have become a master long ago?"
"No." Lu Shiyan sat down across from her. "People who bend their cleverness the wrong way can never become masters. Because a master needs more than intelligence — they need integrity. She has none."
Su Qingyuan looked at him, and the resentment in her chest suddenly dissolved.
That was true. She had no integrity. So even if she made a hundred pieces with the same technique, she would never be a master.
Because what she'd stolen would always be stolen.
"I'll finish compiling the materials tonight and submit them to the committee tomorrow." Su Qingyuan sat up straight again. "There's still a month until the Biennale final. In that month, I'll file for a provisional patent on the Ripple Cut's craft parameters. At least internationally, I'll establish originality first."
Lu Shiyan nodded. "I'll arrange the legal side for you."
"All right." Su Qingyuan didn't refuse this time. For this, she really did need his help.
The appearance of "Undertow" caused no small tremor within the industry.
It wasn't an official announcement — someone had seen the shortlist on the Biennale organizing committee's website, spotted Su Yurou's name, screenshotted it, and posted it online.
The hashtag #SuYurouShortlistedforBiennale shot up the trending list.
The comments were sharply divided.
"Didn't she just get out of detention? How can she still enter an international competition?"
"Detention and competing don't conflict — it wasn't a criminal conviction. Legally she only got administrative detention."
"The point is her work! Wasn't she caught dead to rights stealing Su Qingyuan's designs? This piece had better not be stolen again."
"Can you all stop trashing Yurou for everything? She's a victim too — wasn't the earlier thing reversed?"
"What reversal? The court verdict is out — malicious false accusation. Can't you read Chinese?"
"But if this piece really is her own work, that means she does have talent."
"Whether she has talent or not, we'll know once the final results are out."
Su Qingyuan didn't join the online debate. She turned off all her social media notifications, shut herself in the studio, and started on something new.
Not designing a new piece.
Writing a letter.
A letter to the Biennale judging committee.
There were no accusations in it, no denunciations, no emotional language. She simply stated a fact, objectively: the Ripple Cut was a cutting technique she had originated, and its creative process was fully documented with timestamps. Su Yurou's "Undertow" used the same technique, and was created later than her "Daybreak."
She attached an index of all the evidence and formally requested that, in the final round, the committee conduct an independent assessment of the originality of the craft in both pieces.
At the end of the letter, she wrote this:
"I am not accusing Su Yurou of plagiarism. I am only stating a fact: this technique is something I ground out, bit by bit, a month ago, in an eighty-square-meter studio. If it appears on another piece, I hope the committee can confirm whether the creator of that piece obtained my authorization. Thank you."
No drawn swords, no aggression.
Just the facts laid out, for professionals to make a professional judgment.
After reading the letter, Zhou Wanyin was silent for a long time, then said one thing: "This letter has more power than any accusation."
Su Qingyuan handed the letter to Zhou Wanyin, who, as the sponsor, would forward it to the committee.
Then she went back to work.
As if nothing had happened.
Of course Su Yurou wouldn't quietly wait for the verdict.
On the third day after the letter was submitted, Su Qingyuan received a long message from a new number — but the tone was one she knew far too well.
"Qingyuan-jie, did you write a letter to the committee? Do you think that just because you drew up the Ripple Cut first, the technique is yours? Did you know someone did a similar halo cut before you? You're not the originator — you just got lucky and found that angle first. I was only a little later than you. Make a big scene of this, and in the end you're the one who'll lose face."
Su Qingyuan read it and replied without expression:
"Su Yurou, do you have evidence that you independently developed the Ripple Cut? If not, I'll see you in court."
The other side fell silent.
Su Qingyuan put down her phone and went on polishing the moonstone in her hand.
She didn't tell Su Yurou that she'd already had Lu Shiyan's legal team prepare the litigation materials. If, after the Biennale's verdict came out, Su Yurou was found to have infringed, she would sue directly.
Not because she hated Su Yurou.
But because the Ripple Cut was something she'd ground out over thirty-some moonstones, through countless sleepless nights, with Lu Shiyan's inspiration. It deserved to be protected.
Just as her life deserved to be respected.
A month passed quickly amid all the busyness.
The day before the Biennale final, Su Qingyuan flew to the host city of the exhibition — Paris.
Lu Shiyan went with her. He cleared all his work and flew more than ten hours just to accompany her.
On the plane, Su Qingyuan leaned on his shoulder, gazing out the window at the sea of clouds, and suddenly said, "Lu Shiyan, have you ever thought about what we'd do if I don't win anything at this Biennale?"
"No." Lu Shiyan's voice came from above her head, low and steady.
"Why not?"
"Because you will win." He paused. "Not because I blindly believe in you. Because 'Daybreak' deserves it. No matter how the committee judges, its value won't change. Your value won't change."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes and curved her lips.
"Lu Shiyan, you're starting to sound more and more like an inspirational influencer."
"You become like the company you keep."
"And who's the good company?"
"You."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a light punch, but didn't lift her head from his shoulder.
The plane passed through the clouds, and sunlight came flooding in, falling over the two of them.
Through more than ten hours of flying, she didn't feel tired.
Because he was there.
On the day of the final, the Grand Palais in Paris.
Su Qingyuan stood in the exhibition hall, looking at the twelve shortlisted pieces lined up in a row, each carefully displayed in its own glass case — the lighting, the angles, the backdrops all meticulously designed to present the work's beauty to the fullest.
"Daybreak" was in the very center of the hall.
Under the professional lighting, its three layers of halo flowed and shimmered, as if dawn itself had truly been sealed inside a stone.
"Undertow" was at the other end of the hall. Under the lights, the black opal looked deep and oppressive, the Ripple Cut's halo mostly swallowed by the black base, leaving only that inner thread of warm gold, like a cornered beast's struggle.
Su Qingyuan stood for a while before the case holding "Undertow."
She had to admit Su Yurou's design concept had its bright spots — using darkness to devour light, leaving only a sliver of life. That theme formed a stark contrast with the "breaking out of darkness" of her own "Daybreak."
One was "light coming out of dark."
The other was "dark devouring light, leaving only the last trace."
If the Ripple Cut had been Su Yurou's own, Su Qingyuan might even have admired this piece.
A pity it wasn't.
"Ms. Su."
An unfamiliar voice came from behind her.
Su Qingyuan turned and saw a blonde woman in her fifties, wearing a sharply tailored gray suit, with the Biennale judging committee's badge pinned to her chest.
"I'm the chair of the judging committee, Isabelle Morillet." The woman spoke in English, her gaze settling on Su Qingyuan with scrutiny, and with appreciation. "I read the letter you wrote. The committee conducted an independent assessment of the Ripple Cut's originality, and the result is in."
Su Qingyuan's heartbeat quickened, but her face stayed calm. "And what is the result?"
Isabelle looked at her and suddenly smiled.
"The result is — the Ripple Cut is yours. All the evidence points to you. The timestamps, the creative logs, the evolution of the craft parameters — irrefutable." She paused. "And Ms. Su Yurou could provide no evidence whatsoever that she independently developed this technique."
Su Qingyuan said nothing, waiting for the "but."
Sure enough, Isabelle's tone shifted. "But the committee has decided to retain 'Undertow's' shortlist qualification and not revoke its right to compete. There are two reasons. First, Ms. Su Yurou's lawyer submitted a statement claiming she 'drew reference' from your technique without knowing, and that it was not malicious plagiarism. Second, the committee feels that although the two pieces use the same technique, their design concepts are entirely different and can be evaluated separately."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a few seconds, then nodded. "I understand. Thank you for telling me this."
Isabelle looked at her, a flicker of surprise in her eyes. "You're not angry?"
"Anger doesn't solve problems." Su Qingyuan said calmly. "The committee has its rules, and I respect them. But I reserve the right to pursue my claim afterward."
Isabelle gave her a long, deep look, nodded, and turned to leave.
Su Qingyuan stayed where she was, watching the crowd flow back and forth through the hall, and took a deep breath.
She wasn't angry.
She just felt that — the fact that Su Yurou's lawyer could submit that statement meant there was a master strategist behind her. That person had told her not to go head-to-head, to "admit fault but not guilt," to blur the lines with "drawing reference," making it hard for the committee to come down hard.
It was a clever move. Very clever.
"What are you thinking about?" Lu Shiyan walked up beside her.
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him. "Thinking about who's helping Su Yurou behind the scenes."
Lu Shiyan's gaze flickered slightly. "I'm looking into it too. No clear result yet, but there's one lead — Su Yurou changed lawyers. The new lawyer's surname is Fang. He's a partner at a mid-sized law firm in Nancheng, specializing in intellectual property. Behind that firm is the shadow of an investment company, and that investment company…"
He paused.
"Is connected to you?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"It has nothing to do with me." Lu Shiyan's voice dropped. "But it has something to do with a competitor of the Lu Group."
Su Qingyuan's heart sank.
This was no longer just Su Yurou's personal revenge.
Someone was using Su Yurou as a blade to stab at Lu Shiyan.
Because she was Lu Shiyan's girlfriend. To strike at her was to strike at Lu Shiyan.
"Lu Shiyan, this matter—"
"Leave it to me." Lu Shiyan cut her off, his voice soft but firm. "You go and claim your award. I'll handle the rest."
Su Qingyuan looked at him and wanted to say "we'll handle it together," but seeing the look in his eyes, she didn't say it.
That look told her: there were some storms he was determined to shield her from.
Even when she herself wanted to charge into them.
The award ceremony began at eight in the evening.
The main exhibition hall of the Grand Palais had been transformed into a ceremony venue — the lighting, the sound, the stage, every detail breathing French elegance and grandeur.
Su Qingyuan wore a champagne-colored gown, her hair swept up, revealing her delicate collarbones and that pair of "First Snow" earrings — her own design.
Lu Shiyan sat in the second row of the audience, off to the side. He didn't take a seat in the front row, because that row was reserved for the award winners and the committee. He didn't want to occupy a seat that might belong to an industry professional simply as "family."
Su Qingyuan waited backstage, her phone in her hand, the screen showing a message Lu Shiyan had sent five minutes earlier:
[Don't be nervous. You're already a winner. No matter the result.]
She replied with a single word: [Mm.]
The host took the stage, delivering remarks alternately in French and English. The ceremony officially began.
The awards started with the bronze.
The bronze medalist was an Italian designer, whose work was an ocean-themed sapphire brooch. Applause filled the hall.
The silver medalist was a veteran Japanese designer, whose work was a necklace set themed on the four seasons. The applause grew warmer still.
The gold.
The whole hall fell quiet.
The host opened the envelope, glanced at it, and a meaningful smile spread across his face.
"The gold medalist is—"
"Su Qingyuan, from China! For her work, 'Daybreak'!"
A "buzz" went off in Su Qingyuan's head.
She heard the applause, heard her own name, heard the congratulations of the people around her. But she didn't move.
It wasn't until a staff member gave her a gentle nudge that she came back to herself, took a deep breath, and walked up onto the stage.
The spotlight chased her, every step landing in light.
She took the trophy from the host's hands — a transparent crystal sculpture carved with the Biennale's emblem, heavy, pressing down into her palm, as if pressing down all her effort and persistence.
"Please share your acceptance speech," the host said with a smile.
Su Qingyuan stood at the microphone, looking out at the dense crowd below.
She saw Zhou Wanyin in the front row, her eyes red, clapping with all her might.
She saw the industry people who had once doubted her, mocked her, said she'd "risen on connections" — all of them now standing to applaud.
She saw, in a corner at the very back of the hall, Lu Shiyan standing there.
He hadn't taken his seat in the second row. He'd stood up and gone to the very back. Because from that angle — farthest from the stage, yet closest to her — he could see the whole of her, unblocked by anyone's back.
Across the entire length of the auditorium, their eyes met.
Su Qingyuan curved her lips and said into the microphone:
"Thank you to the committee, thank you to the Biennale. Thank you to everyone who has helped me. Thank you to my teacher, Zhou Wanyin — without her trust, I wouldn't be standing here."
She paused, her voice softening:
"And finally, thank you to one person. He never drew a single line of my designs, never polished a single stone for me, but he shielded me from every storm, so that I could sit quietly in that eighty-square-meter studio and carve the light in my heart, bit by bit, into stone."
"This piece is called 'Daybreak.' He is my daybreak."
The applause below thundered.
Su Qingyuan bowed deeply, and as she straightened, the tears finally fell.
She didn't wipe them away, letting them slide down her cheeks.
Because this time, they were tears of joy.
The joy she had earned, the joy she deserved, joy that was hers in the full light of day.
After the ceremony, Su Qingyuan was surrounded by a swarm of reporters.
The camera flashes nearly blinded her, and the questions surged over her like a tide:
"Ms. Su, what does the gold medal mean to you?"
"Will the Ripple Cut become your patent?"
"What are your thoughts on Su Yurou's 'Undertow'?"
Su Qingyuan answered each one, neither humble nor haughty, neither dodging nor provoking.
The last question came from a Chinese reporter: "Ms. Su, is there anything you'd like to say to Su Yurou? If she happens to be listening right now."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a second, looked into the camera, and said in a calm voice:
"Su Yurou, if you're listening — the Ripple Cut is mine. You can keep using it, but remember this: it's something I ground out over thirty-some moonstones, through countless sleepless nights, with one person's inspiration. You can steal the craft, but you can't steal my time, you can't steal my persistence, you can't steal every drop of sweat I shed in that studio. Any work you make with my technique will always carry my shadow. That's not your light. That's the dark inside my light."
Those words were relayed back home in real time and instantly set every social media platform ablaze.
The hashtags #SuQingyuanGoldMedalSpeech and #SuQingyuanSaysThatsTheDarkInsideMyLight shot up to first and second on the trending list at the same time.
The comments were overwhelmingly in Su Qingyuan's favor:
"So fierce!!! This is the confidence of the real deal!"
"Stolen craft is stolen craft — no amount of spin can wash it clean."
"What's Su Yurou going to do now? Still dare to call herself the originator?"
"That last line of Su Qingyuan's — 'that's the dark inside my light' — devastating. Even when educated people throw shade, they do it with this much class."
When Su Yurou saw those words, she was in a rented room in Nancheng.
She stared at her phone screen, lips pressed into a thin line, her face pale as paper.
Lawyer Fang sent a message: [Don't respond. Don't post anything. Wait for word from me.]
Su Yurou threw her phone onto the sofa and buried her face in both hands.
She'd lost.
She'd lost again.
But she didn't cry.
Because Lawyer Fang had said — "Winning or losing isn't settled in a single hand. As long as you're still at the table, there's a chance to turn it around."
She lowered her hands, picked up her phone, and replied to Lawyer Fang: [Understood. What's the next move?]
Lawyer Fang's reply came quickly: [Wait. Wait for the heat to die down. Wait for Su Qingyuan to slip.]
Su Yurou looked at the line of text and slowly curved her lips.
Wait.
What she was best at was waiting.
All her life, she had waited — waited more than ten years, waited for every concession from Su Qingyuan, every softening of her heart, every time she'd been trampled underfoot.
This time, too, she could wait.
Su Qingyuan, just you wait.
Your light will dim one day.
And my dark will be there, on and on and on.
Paris. Deep night.
Su Qingyuan sat on the balcony of her hotel room, the trophy on the round table beside her, the crystal refracting fine fragments of light in the moonlight.
Lu Shiyan came out from the room, a cup of warm milk in his hand, and held it out to her.
"Not sleeping yet?"
"Can't sleep." Su Qingyuan took the milk and cupped it in her palms. "Too excited."
Lu Shiyan sat down in the chair beside her, and the two of them watched the Paris night side by side. The Eiffel Tower glittered in the distance, like an enormous lighthouse.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"That line I said on the stage today — did you hear it?"
"Which line?"
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him, the moonlight falling on his face, casting his profile in something tender and deep.
"You are my daybreak."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze deep, deep.
Then he reached out, gently took her hand, and laced their fingers together.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"You're my daybreak too."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips, leaned on his shoulder, and looked at the lights in the distance.
The Paris night was quiet, so quiet you could hear each other's heartbeat.
One beat, then another, steady — like a kind of promise.
