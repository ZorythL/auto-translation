Chapter 26 — Yurou's Desperate Gamble, Lu Shiyan's Thunderbolt Tactics

The Hengsheng affair was put to rest faster than anyone had expected.
The Zhenyi factory completed its production-line switchover within twenty-four hours, and the first batch of replacement products had a sample ready by the third day. Su Qingyuan flew to Shenzhen to inspect the goods herself, and the moment she opened the sample box, the worry that had been hanging over her finally settled back into place. Zhenyi's craftsmanship was a full cut above Hengsheng's—the precision of the diamond settings, the fineness of the metal polishing, even the heft and texture of the packaging box—all of it hit the level of an international top-tier brand.
"Director Chen, how much higher is the cost on this batch of samples compared to Hengsheng?" Su Qingyuan asked.
Director Chen quoted a figure. Su Qingyuan ran the numbers in her head, and at that cost the profit margin on the "First Light" collection would drop by nearly ten percentage points. Ten percentage points was no small thing for an order of this volume.
"Director Chen, can the cost come down? Not by cutting quality—by streamlining the process."
The look in Director Chen's eyes shifted. Most designers, hearing that costs had gone up, either gritted their teeth and accepted it or switched to a cheaper supplier. Very few would say "streamline the process"—which meant Su Qingyuan wasn't just a designer. She understood production, understood supply chains, understood how to control costs without sacrificing quality.
"It can. Give me a week and I'll rework the production schedule. Some steps can run in parallel; some can be outsourced to our long-term partner workshops. Quality won't drop, and I can bring the cost down five points."
"Five points isn't enough. I want eight."
Director Chen was silent for a moment, then suddenly laughed. "Miss Su, you're really a lot like Director Lu. Back when he was negotiating the OEM agreement with us, he said the exact same thing—'Eight points, not one less.'"
Su Qingyuan blinked, then curved her lips. "And did he get them in the end?"
"He did. Which is why we can sit here talking today." Director Chen stood up and extended a hand. "Eight points—I'll try. No guarantees, but I'll give it my best."
"Thank you, Director Chen."
On the flight back from Shenzhen, Su Qingyuan sat by the window with a tablet in her hands, scrolling through the production-progress report for the "Light Traces" collection. Cartier's supply chain was far more mature than the one she'd cobbled together herself—every link had a standardized process and strict timelines, leaving her nothing to worry about.
A strange feeling came over her. She used to think she had to do everything herself, because there was no one she could lean on. Now she had Lu Shiyan, she had Zhou Wanyin, she had partners like Director Chen—and yet she was busier than ever. It wasn't that there was more to do. It was that there was more she could do.
The plane broke through the cloud layer, and sunlight poured in, lighting up the whole cabin.
She picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: [On the flight back to Nancheng. Should land around three this afternoon.]
Lu Shiyan replied quickly: [I'll come get you.]
Su Qingyuan: [Don't you have a meeting this afternoon?]
Lu Shiyan: [Pushed it.]
Su Qingyuan looked at those two words—"pushed it"—and curved her lips. She remembered a saying she'd once heard: to tell whether someone truly cares about you, don't look at the special things they do for you—look at the important things they give up for you. Lu Shiyan's meetings were never ordinary meetings. The only person who could make him push one aside to pick her up from the airport was, probably, her.
When the plane landed, a light rain was falling over Nancheng.
The late-autumn rain was chilly, pattering against the cabin windows and drawing long, thin streaks of water down the glass. Su Qingyuan walked out into the arrivals hall and spotted Lu Shiyan at a single glance.
He stood by the exit in a black overcoat and a gray scarf, holding a black long-handled umbrella. He looked like he'd stepped straight out of a magazine; travelers passing by all gave him a second glance, but no one dared approach—because his expression said "keep your distance."
But the instant he saw Su Qingyuan, that "keep your distance" expression shattered. He came toward her and tilted the umbrella over her, leaving half his own shoulder out in the rain.
"Didn't you say you pushed the meeting? Why are you still in a suit?" Su Qingyuan looked at his rain-dampened shoulder and reached out to brush it off.
"Came straight over after the meeting." Lu Shiyan wrapped an arm around her shoulders and headed for the parking lot. "Couldn't push it, so I moved it online. Ran it from the car."
Su Qingyuan leaned into his embrace, listening to the steady beat of his heart amid the sound of the rain, and suddenly all her Shenzhen weariness melted away.
In the car, Lu Shiyan didn't start the engine right away. Instead he reached into the back seat and handed her a paper bag.
"What's this?"
"Bought it at the airport. You said last time you wanted croissants."
Su Qingyuan opened the bag. Inside were two golden, crisp croissants, still warm. She picked one up and took a bite; the flaky pastry crumbled between her teeth, and the scent of butter filled her mouth.
"It's good," she said, mouth full.
Lu Shiyan watched her eat, the corners of his lips lifting.
"How were things in Shenzhen?"
"Great. Zhenyi's Director Chen is very professional—we can bring the 'First Light' cost down eight points without dropping quality." Su Qingyuan took another bite of croissant. "Oh, right—Director Chen said that back when you negotiated the OEM agreement with them, you asked for eight points too. You two have worked together before?"
"Mm. Lu Group has a high-end watch brand, and Zhenyi is one of its suppliers." Lu Shiyan started the car. "Director Chen is a hard man to negotiate with. It took me three months to bring the price down."
Su Qingyuan tilted her head toward him. "Well, I got it down in a single day—so does that make me better than you?"
Lu Shiyan glanced at her, a smile in his eyes. "Mm. Better than me."
That "mm" left Su Qingyuan feeling a little guilty. She knew a large part of why Director Chen was willing to deal with her came down to Lu Shiyan's standing. But he didn't say, "That's because of me." He said, "Mm, better than me." This man handed every bit of the credit to her and stood between her and every storm.
She lowered her head and went on eating the croissant, the tips of her ears turning faintly red.
The car pulled off the airport expressway and merged into Nancheng's traffic. The rain came down harder, the wipers sweeping fast across the windshield. Su Qingyuan finished her croissant, settled back into her seat, and as she watched the blurred streetscape outside, something suddenly occurred to her.
"Has Su Yurou made any moves lately?"
Lu Shiyan tapped his fingers lightly twice on the steering wheel. "Yes. She's moved to a new address—an ordinary apartment complex on the outskirts of Nancheng. She hasn't looked for a new job, and she hasn't contacted anyone from her old circles. Lawyer Fang's side has terminated their retainer too."
Su Qingyuan frowned. "Terminated? Gu Changfeng isn't helping her anymore?"
"He is. But the method has changed. After Lawyer Fang dropped her, Su Yurou didn't hire a new lawyer—she's handling everything herself. That tells me Gu Changfeng is having her 'disappear'—no public appearances, no traceable handle left behind."
"Then how is she living?"
"Someone's wiring her money. The account is well hidden, but Lu Group's risk-control department found a trace of it—the money comes from an offshore account, and the ultimate source points to a subsidiary of Dingfeng Group."
Su Qingyuan fell silent. Gu Changfeng hadn't abandoned Su Yurou; he'd moved her from out in the open into the shadows. A piece hidden in the dark was harder to deal with than an enemy in plain sight.
"Lu Shiyan, what do you think Su Yurou will do next?"
"I don't know." Lu Shiyan's voice was low. "But she won't do nothing. Su Yurou is the kind of person whose hatred outlasts her love. She can wait a long time."
Su Qingyuan nodded and asked nothing more. She watched the rain outside and suddenly said, "If she comes for me, I'll face her."
Lu Shiyan said nothing, only reached out and took her hand.
The wipers kept sweeping, one stroke, then another, clearing the rain from the windshield again and again, only to have it covered again and again by fresh rain.
Just like their life—trouble kept appearing, and again and again the two of them joined hands and dealt with it.
On the third day after returning to Nancheng, Su Qingyuan received an unexpected phone call.
It was Cartier headquarters' creative director, calling in person—a Frenchman with a low, gentle voice. He said the three sample pieces of the "Light Traces" collection had completed all their testing in Paris, and the results exceeded expectations. He asked whether Su Qingyuan would be willing to come to Paris next spring for the global launch event of the "Light Traces" collection.
Su Qingyuan gripped the phone, her heart racing, but her voice steady. "I would. Thank you for the invitation, Director."
"Just call me Jean-Pierre." The man let out a soft laugh. "Miss Su, I've designed jewelry for thirty years and met countless designers. You're the first one to make me feel that 'this industry still has hope.'"
Su Qingyuan was taken aback, then said earnestly, "Jean-Pierre, those words make me happier than winning a gold medal."
"Why?"
"Because a gold medal is for the past. Those words are for the future."
There was a moment's silence on the other end, and then Jean-Pierre's laughter grew more pronounced. "Miss Su, I'm beginning to understand why Cartier wanted to work with you. It's not because of how talented you are. It's because you have a soul."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan sat at her worktable and didn't move for a long while.
Jean-Pierre had said, "You have a soul." Those few words weighed more than any award. Because an award can come down to skill, to luck, to timing—but a soul can't be faked. It's the mark left on your work by what you've lived through, what you believe, and the person you've become.
She looked down at her own hands. These hands had drawn thousands of sketches, ground hundreds of stones, been cut by carving knives more times than she could count. Every scar was a story. And those stories were her soul.
Su Qingyuan opened her sketchbook and began drawing new drafts. Inspiration came fast, like water bursting through a dam; the pen tip raced across the paper, lines moving from chaos to clarity, from blur to certainty.
What she was drawing was a series—not "First Light," not "Light Traces," but an entirely new one with no name yet. The theme was "Roots."
Something suddenly came clear to her. She used to fight desperately to escape the old Su family house, to flee the place that had trapped her, believing that escape was freedom. But now she knew: true freedom wasn't escape. It was carrying everything from the past—the good and the bad, the sweet and the bitter—and walking forward, neither trapped by it nor pretending it didn't exist.
The "Roots" series was about exactly that.
She drew deep into the night, forgetting to eat, forgetting to drink, forgetting to message Lu Shiyan. Until the studio door was pushed open and Lu Shiyan walked in carrying an insulated bag. Seeing her hands covered in pencil dust yet her eyes shining like stars, he let out a sigh.
"You didn't check your phone?"
Su Qingyuan glanced down at her phone. The screen showed more than a dozen unread messages, all from Lu Shiyan.
The first: [What do you want to eat tonight?]
The second: [I'll bring it for you.]
The third: [Busy?]
The fourth: [Reply when you see this.]
The fifth: [I'm just coming over.]
The sixth: [Your studio lights are still on.]
The last: [On my way.]
Su Qingyuan looked up at him, a little guilty. "I… forgot."
Lu Shiyan set the insulated bag on the table, came over, and took a look at her sketchbook. He didn't ask, "What is this." Instead he gazed at it in silence for a long while, then said, "This series will be better than 'First Light.'"
Su Qingyuan was stunned. "You can tell?"
"I can't." Lu Shiyan moved his gaze from the sketchbook to her face. "But I can read your eyes. When you were drawing 'First Light,' your eyes held 'breaking free.' Drawing this, your eyes hold 'acceptance.' The latter is harder than the former."
Su Qingyuan's nose stung. This man didn't understand jewelry, didn't understand design, but he understood her. He understood the emotion behind every one of her drawings, because what he was looking at wasn't the drawing—it was her.
"Lu Shiyan, don't come anymore from now on."
Lu Shiyan's brows knit slightly.
"Every time you come, I want to put down my work and talk to you. But I don't want to put down my work."
"Then work and talk to me at the same time."
"I'll get distracted."
"Distracted is fine. I'll be right beside you, not getting in your way."
Su Qingyuan looked at his earnest expression and couldn't help laughing. "You really know how to talk people around."
Lu Shiyan pulled out a chair and sat down, taking the food out of the insulated bag and laying out each dish one by one.
"Eat first. Draw after you've eaten."
Su Qingyuan obediently sat down and picked up her bowl, then suddenly remembered something. "Oh, right—Cartier headquarters' creative director called me today and invited me to Paris next spring for the global launch of the 'Light Traces' collection."
Lu Shiyan's hand, in the middle of putting food into her bowl, paused. "When?"
"March next year."
"I'll go with you."
"Aren't you really busy?"
"March isn't busy."
Su Qingyuan watched him say "March isn't busy" without batting an eye, and knew perfectly well—Lu Shiyan's March was never not busy. He was simply making time for her.
"Okay." She didn't call out his lie. "We'll go together."
The corners of Lu Shiyan's lips lifted, and he picked out another piece of ribs for her.
At some point the rain outside had stopped. Nancheng's night was quiet and deep, the studio lights a warm amber. The two of them sat across from each other eating, exchanging a word or two now and then, sometimes saying nothing at all.
This kind of quiet brought more peace than any noise ever could.
Meanwhile, in a rented room in a run-down apartment complex on the outskirts of Nancheng.
Su Yurou sat in front of a computer, the glow of the screen lighting up her gaunt, pale face. She wore a pilled gray loungewear set, her hair carelessly tied back, looking for all the world like an ordinary young woman scraping by—if you ignored the obsessive light in her eyes.
On the screen was the interface of an encrypted chat app. The other party's avatar was a blank, their handle a meaningless string of letters.
Su Yurou typed:
[I've thought it over—the thing Director Gu mentioned. I'll do it. But I have a condition.]
The reply came quickly:
[Go on.]
Su Yurou bit her lip, her fingers hovering over the keyboard for a few seconds, then rapidly tapped out a line:
[Once it's done, I want a sum of money. Enough to leave the country and start over. I'm not staying in China anymore—there's no place left for me here.]
The other side was silent for a moment.
[How much?]
Su Yurou quoted a figure.
The reply came fast:
[Fine. But if you fail, this has nothing to do with me. Director Gu will deny any connection to you whatsoever.]
Su Yurou stared at the line, and a cold arc slowly curved at the corner of her mouth.
[I know. I never expected him to admit anything in the first place.]
The other party didn't reply again.
Su Yurou closed the chat app and leaned back in her chair, eyes shut. The room was very quiet, broken only by the occasional gurgle of the heating pipes. Her breathing was light—light enough to seem nonexistent.
She thought back to many years ago, when she was still a little girl. Su Qingyuan had just been adopted not long before, scrawny and small, curled up in a corner too afraid to speak. Yurou had found it amusing and went to tease her—snatching her toys, tearing up the pictures she'd drawn. Su Qingyuan never fought back, only stared at her with reddened eyes, then silently picked up the torn scraps and pieced them back together.
At the time, she'd thought: this one is so easy to push around.
Later they grew up, and she discovered Su Qingyuan's drawing kept getting better, the teachers always praising her, while she herself was forever just "Su Qingyuan's little sister." That feeling of being outshone was like a thorn driven into her heart, deeper and deeper.
Later still, she discovered a secret—Su Qingyuan wasn't their parents' biological daughter. That secret made her feel she finally had the upper hand. You were brought in; I was born here. In this family you're an outsider, and I'm not. She used that advantage to grind Su Qingyuan down again and again. She stole her design drafts, stole her boyfriend, stole everything.
She'd thought she would keep on winning.
But she'd lost. Lost utterly and completely.
Su Yurou opened her eyes, looked at a blackened water stain on the ceiling, and murmured, "Sister Qingyuan, do you know why I hate you?"
"It's not because you're better than me."
"It's because you never once hated me."
"I stole your things, and you didn't hate me. I stole your boyfriend, and still you didn't hate me. The more you refused to hate me, the more I felt like a joke."
Her voice was light as a sigh.
"So this time, I'm going to make you hate me."
"Even if it's only once."
She turned off the light, and the room sank into darkness. Only the computer's power indicator stayed lit—like a red, unblinking eye glowing in the dark.
The next day, when Su Qingyuan arrived at her studio, she found an envelope set by the door.
White, with no sender information—only three characters written on it: Su Qingyuan (To).
She opened the envelope. Inside was a photograph.
The photo was of herself—but not as she was now. It was from a few years ago. Back then she was still working at the Su family's shop, dressed in faded work clothes, hair tied in a messy knot, heavy dark circles under her eyes, holding a half-finished stone, absorbed in grinding it.
The angle of the shot was furtive, as if taken in secret.
Su Qingyuan turned the photo over and saw a line of crooked handwriting on the back:
"Do you still remember the way you looked back then? I remember. I've never forgotten.—Su Yurou"
Su Qingyuan looked at that line and was silent for a long time.
She felt no anger, no fear—only a strange calm, something close to "so it's finally here."
Su Yurou wouldn't lunge out of the dark and bite her all at once. Su Yurou would slowly, bit by bit, plant unease in her heart. This photo was the first seed.
She was telling her: I've been watching you all along. It started a very long time ago. You never knew, but every step you took, I was watching.
Su Qingyuan tucked the photo back into the envelope and put it in a drawer—locked away together with Chen Xiaoman's photo and Lin Haoyu's USB drive.
She wouldn't throw it away. Because she meant to keep them and, at the most crucial moment, lay them all out at once.
Then she sat back down at her worktable, picked up her carving knife, and went on grinding the first sample piece of the "Roots" series.
The sound of the carving knife scraping across metal was fine and fragmented, yet resolute.
The footsteps outside the door, the wind, the distant blare of car horns—none of it had anything to do with her.
She immersed herself in her own world, like a ship sailing into the deep sea—calm on the surface, all power underneath.
That evening, when Lu Shiyan came to pick her up, Su Qingyuan told him about the envelope.
Lu Shiyan listened and stayed silent for a long while. His silence made Su Qingyuan a little uneasy—he usually responded the instant she finished speaking, even if only with "Mm" or "I see." But this time, he didn't.
"What's wrong?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"Su Yurou is testing your limits." Lu Shiyan's voice was low. "She wants to know what it takes to make you lose control. She won't hurt you directly, but she'll do plenty of things to unsettle you—mailing photos, sending messages, leaving traces of herself in the places you least expect. Boiling a frog in slow water; once you've grown used to it, she'll strike hard."
Su Qingyuan nodded. "I know."
"What do you plan to do?"
"Nothing." Su Qingyuan watched the neon flowing past outside the window. "She mails a photo, I'll keep it. She sends a message, I'll read it and delete it. The traces she leaves, I'll see them but pay them no mind. She wants me to lose control—and I just won't."
Lu Shiyan reached out and took her hand.
"Good. Then that's how it'll be."
The car stopped at a red light. Su Qingyuan turned to look at him and suddenly said, "Lu Shiyan, what do you think Su Yurou actually wants?"
Lu Shiyan thought for a moment, then said, "She wants you to become her. To hate her, envy her, suffer the way she does. That way she won't have to suffer alone."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a long time.
The light turned green, and the car moved on.
"Then I won't give her what she wants," she said, her voice soft but firm.
Outside, Nancheng's night grew ever deeper. The streetlights joined into a warm river of light, illuminating the road home.
