Supply-Chain Warfare: Her Thunderous Counterstrike
The promise made at the Japanese restaurant was like a seed that took root in Su Qingyuan's heart.
The aurora. She had been only a teenager when she said those words, tucked away in the cramped attic of the old Su family house, flipping through a tattered travel magazine. Outside the window was Nancheng's grey, overcast sky; inside, her foster mother Liu Xiulan's voice nagging at her to go cook. She had curled up in the corner, staring at the photo of the aurora over and over, and silently told herself one thing — in this life, I have to go see it, just once.
Later she grew up, and the crushing weight of life buried that wish in the deepest recesses of her memory, until even she had forgotten it.
But he remembered.
When Su Qingyuan arrived at the studio the next day, she was still in good spirits. She brewed a cup of tea, sat down at her workbench, and got ready to keep polishing the samples for the "Light Traces" collection. The orders for "First Light" had already entered mass production; the first batch of finished pieces had shipped just yesterday, and customer feedback was excellent. Social media was flooded with posts showing them off.
She opened Weibo and saw one comment pinned to the very top:
"Got my 'First Snow' earrings — they're ten thousand times more beautiful in person than in the photos. Su Qingyuan, thank you for giving me the best gift on my thirtieth birthday. I always used to feel that nice things weren't meant for me. The moment I put these earrings on, I felt — I'm worth it."
Su Qingyuan read it three times, set down her phone, and drew a deep breath.
This was exactly why she did what she did.
At ten in the morning, the phone rang. Not her cell — the studio's landline. Only business partners had that number.
"Miss Su, this is Old Jiang from Hengsheng Jewelry." There was a trace of unnatural unease in the voice on the other end. "There's something I need to tell you about."
Su Qingyuan gripped the receiver, her heart giving a small lurch, but her voice stayed steady. "Go ahead, Mr. Jiang."
"Things over at Hengsheng have... hit a snag. Several of our production lines have to halt for environmental compliance inspections, so the timeline may slip. That batch of orders for your 'First Light' collection — I might not be able to deliver on schedule."
Su Qingyuan's fingers tightened slightly.
"Mr. Jiang, was this environmental inspection notice issued out of nowhere today?"
"Yes. We only got the paperwork this morning."
"And how long do you expect the delay to be?"
"At least... a month."
A month. Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, her mind racing. The first batch of the "First Light" collection had already shipped, but the second and third batches were still in production, with a promised delivery time of fifteen days. A month's delay would mean several thousand customers waiting forty-five days. In the jewelry industry, a delivery delay was a serious blow to a brand.
"Mr. Jiang, tell me the truth — is this environmental inspection a real reason, or did someone tell you to say it?"
The line went silent.
"Miss Su, I..."
"Is it Gu Changfeng?"
Silence again. Then Mr. Jiang sighed, his voice very low. "Miss Su, I'm sorry. I owe Mr. Gu a favor. He asked me to help him out this one time, and I couldn't refuse. I know it's wrong, but I... I really had no choice."
Su Qingyuan opened her eyes, her voice frighteningly calm. "Mr. Jiang, you owe Gu Changfeng a favor — so why are you using my customers to pay it back?"
"I—"
"Have you thought about what those thousands of customers will think of me when they wait forty-five days and still don't get their goods? They'll think Su Qingyuan can't be trusted, that the 'First Light' collection is a scam. What you're destroying isn't a batch of orders — it's a brand."
Mr. Jiang didn't answer. All that came down the line was heavy breathing.
"I understand, Mr. Jiang." With that, Su Qingyuan hung up.
She sat at her workbench, looking out at the grey sky, and took three deep breaths. The first pushed down her anger, the second her panic, the third let her settle into calm.
Then she picked up her phone and dialed Lu Shiyan.
"Hengsheng's a problem. Gu Changfeng got Mr. Jiang to delay a month, using environmental inspections as the excuse."
Lu Shiyan's voice was steady, without the slightest ripple. "The backup supplier is already in place. How many pending orders do you have?"
"Second batch, three thousand two hundred pieces; third batch, four thousand one hundred. Promised delivery is fifteen days from now."
"Send me the order data and the technical specs. I'll have the backup supplier start scheduling production this afternoon. The line switchover will be done within twenty-four hours — there won't be any delay."
That set most of Su Qingyuan's mind at ease. "How does the backup supplier's craftsmanship compare to Hengsheng's?"
"Higher. Hengsheng is top-tier domestically; the backup is a top-tier international contract manufacturer that's produced for several luxury brands. Costs will run a bit higher, but I'll cover the difference."
"You don't need to cover it." Su Qingyuan's tone was firm. "This is a cost of the 'First Light' collection — it should be on me. Give me the supplier's contact, and I'll negotiate with them myself."
There was a pause on the line. Then Lu Shiyan said something that warmed her right to the core. "All right. You negotiate. Whatever you can't close, I'll make up."
He wasn't the kind of man who said, "You can't handle it, so I'll take over." He was the kind who said, "You go first, I'll be right behind you, and if you can't hold on, you've got me." That kind of trust was more precious than any protection.
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan immediately contacted the backup supplier — a factory called "Zhenyi" in Shenzhen that specialized in contract work for top-tier international jewelry brands. The person in charge, surnamed Chen, was a woman in her forties who spoke crisply and to the point.
"Miss Su, Mr. Lu already gave us a heads-up. Send over your technical files and we'll start prototyping this afternoon. Once the samples are confirmed, mass production can launch in parallel."
"Director Chen, about the cost—"
"Mr. Lu said to work to your budget, and he'd cover the difference."
"No." Su Qingyuan cut her off. "I'll cover the difference. Quote me a real price. If I can accept it, I'll sign; if not, we'll keep talking. I don't want anyone thinking Su Qingyuan gets things done on subsidies."
The line was silent for a second, and then Director Chen laughed. "Miss Su, you're just like Mr. Lu said. He told me you'd say exactly this. Fine — I'll send the real price to your email, and after you've looked it over, we'll talk."
Su Qingyuan hung up, opened her computer, and began organizing the technical files.
Outside, the sky grew darker and darker, the cloud layer pressing low, as if it were about to snow. In the studio there was nothing but the clatter of her keyboard and the occasional rustle of a page turning.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan:
[Gu Changfeng went after Hengsheng — he won't stop at just this one point. He may make a move on the public-opinion front too. Be ready.]
Su Qingyuan replied: [I know. Let him come.]
When she said "let him come," there was no bluster in her heart. Because she held trump cards — the chat records Lin Haoyu had given her, plus her own complete documentation of her creative process. If Gu Changfeng dared to sling mud in the court of public opinion, she'd dare to throw the evidence in his face.
Sure enough, at three in the afternoon, a wave of orchestrated chatter began appearing on Weibo.
The hashtag #TheInsideStoryOfSuQingyuansCartierDeal# quietly climbed onto the trending list. The gist of it: Su Qingyuan had only landed the Cartier collaboration because Lu Shiyan pressured Cartier's headquarters; Cartier China had been unwilling at first, but under the commercial pressure of the Lu Group, they'd had no choice but to accept.
No hard proof, no screenshots — just one blurry "insider tip-off" chat log. But that's how public opinion works: it doesn't need evidence, only a "maybe."
Su Qingyuan's comment section was immediately flooded with voices full of doubt:
"Just as I thought — she's relying on a man after all."
"That gold medal was probably won by Lu Shiyan's pressure too, huh? How dull."
"And here I thought she had real talent. Turns out it's all backers behind the scenes."
"Cartier really has no backbone, ditching the brand's bottom line for a business deal?"
But plenty of people spoke up for her too:
"Do you have any proof? You're treating one chat log as gospel truth?"
"Su Qingyuan's 'First Light' collection is right there in front of you — is that level of craftsmanship something you can squeeze out by 'pressure'?"
"The Biennale gold medal was judged by an international panel. Lu Shiyan's arm isn't long enough to reach all the way to Paris, is it?"
The two camps quarreled endlessly.
Su Qingyuan looked at the comments and felt no anger, no grievance — only a clarity so calm it bordered on cold. She knew this chatter wasn't something netizens had stirred up on their own; it was an organized troll army. Gu Changfeng was testing her reaction — seeing whether she'd panic, whether she'd cry, whether she'd silently endure it like she used to.
She wouldn't anymore.
She opened Weibo and drafted a long post, but didn't rush to send it. Instead she checked it over three times, making sure every single word would hold up. Then she sent Lu Shiyan a message: [I want to post a Weibo responding to the chatter. Take a look.]
Lu Shiyan read it and replied: [Post it. I'll have the PR team back you up.]
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath and pressed send.
"About the rumors circulating lately, I only have a few things to say:
1. The Biennale gold medal was decided by independent votes from twelve international judges. The entire judging process was recorded and can be verified. I did not — and do not need to — pressure anyone.
2. The Cartier collaboration was one I pitched myself, design proposals in hand. I negotiated every clause of the contract one by one — a sixty-forty split, sixty to me, forty to them. This is the first time Cartier has ever offered such terms to a Chinese designer. If that's the result of 'pressure,' then it's my design proposals that did the pressuring.
3. My 'First Light' collection — from concept to finished product, every sketch, every revision, every step of craftsmanship — is documented with timestamps. I have already retained a lawyer to reserve the right to pursue legal action against those spreading rumors.
4. Finally, I want to say thank you to everyone who supports me. To those who don't believe in me — I don't need you to. Time will prove everything.
That's all."
After the long post went out, the tide in the comment section began to turn.
"She's so fierce! Straight-up posting the contract terms?"
"Sixty-forty? Sixty to her, forty to Cartier? Damn, that's a first for Cartier, isn't it?"
"The Biennale judging process can be verified — haters, zip it."
"I believe in her. Anyone who can make 'First Snow' doesn't need to rely on a man."
But some haters kept up their stubborn front: "Got the guts to post the judging records, then? What good is just saying it 'can be verified'?"
Su Qingyuan didn't reply to that comment. She didn't need to spar with every hater. She only needed to lay out the facts; the rest, she'd leave to time.
Meanwhile, Zhou Wanyin reposted her Weibo with the caption: "I've been involved in the 'First Light' collection from day one. Qingyuan's talent doesn't need anyone's endorsement. I'll stake my career on it."
A few minutes later, Cartier's official account also posted a brief message: "We chose to collaborate with Ms. Su Qingyuan because of her work. There is no other reason."
The two reposts landed like two resounding slaps across the face of the rumor-mongers.
Looking at Cartier's official statement, Su Qingyuan felt a warmth well up inside her. She knew the statement wasn't entirely the brand's own doing — Lu Shiyan must have done some work behind the scenes — but the content of the statement was true. Cartier really had chosen her because of her work. Without that factual foundation, no amount of "work" would have mattered.
Within a few hours, the tide of public opinion reversed completely. The trending hashtag changed from #TheInsideStoryOfSuQingyuansCartierDeal# to #SuQingyuanResponds#, and then to #SuQingyuansSixtyFortySplit#. By evening, the tone of the comment section had shifted entirely:
"Sis is so badass!"
"As of today, I'm a fan of Su Qingyuan's career!"
"Even Cartier came out to back her — what do the haters have left to say?"
Su Qingyuan closed Weibo and rubbed her aching eyes.
It wasn't that she didn't care about public opinion. She did — but she'd learned not to lose herself in that caring.
The studio door swung open. Lu Shiyan walked in, carrying an insulated bag.
"You haven't had dinner yet, have you?" He set the bag down on the table in the lounge area. "Brought some congee and side dishes."
Su Qingyuan looked at him, and suddenly her nose stung. Not the sting of grievance — the kind that comes from fighting a hard battle out in the world, then coming home to find someone left a light on for you, and brought hot congee besides.
"You saw it?" She walked over and sat down on the sofa.
"I saw it." Lu Shiyan ladled out the congee and set it in front of her. "That Weibo post you put up — it was good. I read it three times."
Su Qingyuan picked up the congee and took a sip. Century egg and lean pork congee — the same taste as last time. He'd made it.
"Did you learn another dish?" she asked.
"Mm. Congee doesn't count as a dish." Lu Shiyan sat down across from her. "But it's better than last time. Try it."
Su Qingyuan took another sip — it really was better than last time. The grains were softer and stickier, the seasoning better balanced.
"Lu Shiyan, did you secretly sign up for a cooking class?"
"No. Learned it from videos."
"You can learn this well just from videos?"
"Talent." Lu Shiyan said the word without batting an eye, and Su Qingyuan nearly choked on her congee.
She set down her bowl, looked at him, and suddenly laughed. "You — you've got a talent for everything. Talent for congee, talent for negotiation. Even the people spreading rumors know your name is useful — when they smear me, they don't say 'Su Qingyuan relies on a man,' they say 'Su Qingyuan relies on Lu Shiyan.' Which means in their minds, your name is synonymous with 'backer.'"
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze gentle. "Does that bother you?"
"Bother me how?"
"Bother you, people saying you 'rely on me.'"
Su Qingyuan thought about it seriously, then shook her head. "No. Because I know I don't. What others say can't change the facts. And besides—" she paused, her voice softening, "if relying on you means I take a few less detours, I don't mind being talked about. Because I've already walked enough crooked roads."
Lu Shiyan reached out and took her hand where it rested on the table, his thumb slowly tracing circles on the back of it.
"Su Qingyuan, you're stronger than I thought."
"It's not strength." Su Qingyuan clasped his hand back. "It's that I'm not afraid anymore. I used to be afraid people would say I relied on connections, so I tried desperately to prove myself. Now I'm not afraid, because I know who I am. You just let me know it a little faster — without you, I'd have figured it out eventually anyway. It would only have been slower."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, the light in his eyes as deep as night.
"Then from now on, can I keep 'letting you know it a little faster'?"
Su Qingyuan laughed, her eyes curving into crescents.
"You can. But not too much. I'm afraid if I get used to it, I won't be able to quit it."
"Then don't quit."
The two of them looked at each other, and both laughed.

Outside, the sky had gone completely dark. The Nancheng wind howled, and the congee in the insulated bag was still steaming. Su Qingyuan leaned back against the sofa, pulled Lu Shiyan's hand over to rest on her knee, then leaned against his shoulder.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"What do you plan to do about the Hengsheng situation?"
"Nothing." Lu Shiyan's voice was light. "Mr. Jiang chose to help Gu Changfeng, so he has to bear the consequences. Hengsheng has lived off the Lu Group's contracts for five years. Now that he's torn up our agreement, none of the Lu Group's business will ever work with him again. With his factory losing its biggest client, it won't last half a year."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a few seconds. She didn't pity Mr. Jiang. An adult's choices are all his own to pay for.
"And Gu Changfeng?"
"His move against Hengsheng this time was to probe the resilience of your supply chain. Now that he's found you have a backup plan, and that you switched over this fast, he won't go after the supply chain next time." Lu Shiyan's fingers slid through her hair. "He'll switch to another method."
"What method?"
"I don't know. But he'll definitely strike again. His IPO plan was thrown off by losing that bid — he needs a new opening." Lu Shiyan paused. "And in his eyes, you're my opening."
Su Qingyuan straightened up off his shoulder and looked into his eyes.
"Then I won't be his opening."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, waiting for her to go on.
"I'll be his end point." Su Qingyuan's voice was quiet, but every word was crystal clear. "Every time he tries to use me to get at you, he'll slam into a wall. And that wall isn't me — it's my work, my bottom line, my principles. Those are things he can't break through."
Lu Shiyan looked at her for a long, long while. Then he leaned in and dropped a feather-light, infinitely tender kiss on her forehead.
"That wall is mine too."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, feeling the warmth of that kiss.
Outside, the wind grew stronger. The weather forecast said there might be sleet tonight.
But inside the studio, the heat was on, the congee was still warm, and the two of them leaned against each other like two trees standing side by side — roots intertwined underground, branches touching in the wind.
Nothing could blow them down.
