His private domain holds traces of her.
The Nancheng financial district.
Here the city's skyline reached its highest point. The headquarters of the Lu Group rose like a drawn sword piercing straight into the clouds, its glass curtain wall reflecting the afternoon sun—dazzling and cold.
The taxi pulled up at the base of the building. Su Qingyuan looked up and drew a deep breath.
She had passed this building countless times before. Each time she would tilt her head back for a single glance, then lower it and hurry past. She had never imagined that one day she would walk inside—much less that she would be invited in.
"Miss, we're here," the driver said.
Su Qingyuan paid the fare and pushed the door open to get out.
She wore the beige skirt suit she'd worn to the salon that day, carrying a black tote bag stuffed with her portfolio materials. Standing before the building, her slender figure looked small against the colossal structure, yet her spine was held perfectly straight.
There was a security booth at the entrance. The guard in his crisply pressed uniform spotted her and was about to step forward to ask her business, when a woman in business attire strode briskly out from the direction of the front desk.
"Excuse me, are you Miss Su Qingyuan?" The woman's smile was perfectly poised.
"I am."
"President Lu gave instructions. This way, please." The woman turned to lead the way, her gestures respectful without being obsequious. "President Lu is on the top floor. I'll take you up."
Su Qingyuan followed her across the spacious, brightly lit ground-floor lobby. Marble beneath her feet, a ten-meter-high crystal chandelier overhead—every detail breathed the grandeur of a top-tier enterprise.
A few young women at the front desk stole glances her way, their eyes brimming with gossip.
"Is that the person President Lu's been waiting for?"
"Oh my god, she's gorgeous, what an aura."
"No wonder President Lu pushed back all his meetings this afternoon…"
The whispers drifted into her ears, but Su Qingyuan's expression never changed, her pace neither hurried nor slow.
The elevator was a private one—it required a keycard before a floor could be selected. The woman pressed the button for the top floor, then stepped back to one side and stood quietly, saying nothing more.
The elevator rose all the way up, the numbers ticking past at a dizzying speed.
Thirty-eight… forty-two… fifty-seven… seventy-one…
Su Qingyuan noticed that this elevator made no stops at all between the ground floor and the top.
A private channel.
The elevator doors opened on the eighty-eighth floor.
Before her stretched a short corridor, at the end of which stood a pair of dark wooden double doors.
"President Lu is just inside, Miss Su." The woman bowed slightly, then turned and retreated into the elevator.
Su Qingyuan walked the corridor alone, the wooden floor giving off a faint sound beneath her steps.
She raised her hand and knocked.
"Come in."
The low, familiar voice came from within.
Su Qingyuan pushed the door open, and her steps faltered for an instant.
This was not the cold, sterile corporate office she had imagined.
The entire floor was one open space, wrapped in floor-to-ceiling glass on all three hundred and sixty degrees. Nancheng's mountains and rivers, its buildings and streets, all lay spread before the eye like an enormous scroll of the city unfurled beneath her feet.
Sunlight poured in from every direction, bathing the whole space in a clear, warm glow.
The work area sat at the very center—a vast desk of dark solid wood, on which documents and a laptop were neatly arranged, with a minimalist metal desk lamp beside them.
But what truly stopped Su Qingyuan in her tracks was the arrangement next to the work area.
A small round table, two armchairs, a white porcelain tea set on the table along with a slim-necked vase holding a few stems of white chamomile.
The sofa area was by the floor-to-ceiling windows—a dark gray fabric couch draped with a light beige throw, and a bookshelf beside it holding several design magazines and art albums.
This corner looked nothing like the office of the man who steered a trillion-yuan commercial empire. It looked more like a… home.
"What are you spacing out about?"
Lu Shiyan came around from behind his desk, two cups of coffee in his hands.
He had shed his suit jacket, wearing only a dark gray shirt, the cuffs rolled up to his forearms, baring well-defined wrists and an understated, restrained watch.
This look had a touch more ease than a formal suit—and a touch more… good looks.
Su Qingyuan drew her gaze back and took the coffee. "I thought your office would be very severe."
"It was, originally." Lu Shiyan lifted his chin, gesturing for her to look at the soft furnishings in the sofa area. "Then I had it changed."
"Why?"
He didn't answer directly. Instead he walked toward the floor-to-ceiling windows with his coffee, his voice mild. "Because some places are too cold to stay in—you can't settle. A little change makes it more comfortable."
Su Qingyuan looked at the white chamomile and the light beige throw, and couldn't shake the feeling that this color palette seemed somehow familiar.
It almost seemed… connected to that offhand remark she'd once made about liking "clean, warm colors"?
She didn't ask, afraid she was reading too much into it.

"Sit down and talk." Lu Shiyan settled into the sofa area and pointed to the seat across from him.
Su Qingyuan sat down. The sofa was soft but well-supported—she sank in without feeling like she was collapsing into it. She set the document folder in her hands on the coffee table and took out the collaboration framework Zhou Wanyin had given her.
"The deal she's offering is a fifty-fifty split. I'd be attached as an independent designer, and she'd provide the resources, the channels, and the brand endorsement." Su Qingyuan pushed the documents across. "What do you think?"
Lu Shiyan took them and flipped through quickly, but lingered a few seconds on every page—clearly reading the key clauses.
Su Qingyuan noticed that his expression while reading the contract was utterly different from the one he'd worn at the salon. At the salon he was an onlooker, gentle and self-restrained; reading the contract he was the one in command, his gaze sharp, letting no detail slip past.
"A fifty-fifty split isn't low by industry standards." Lu Shiyan turned to the last page and closed the file. "But given your potential, you could actually negotiate for more."
"More?"
"You have no reputation right now, so a fifty-fifty split feels like a win to you. But what Zhou Wanyin has her eye on isn't the you of today—it's your value three to five years from now." Lu Shiyan lifted his eyes to her. "If your judgment about yourself isn't wrong, then you could push her for forty-sixty, even thirty-seventy."
Su Qingyuan was taken aback. "It never occurred to me that there was room to negotiate."
"There are a lot of things that never occurred to you before." Lu Shiyan's tone was calm—not belittling her, simply stating a fact. "But now you can start thinking about them."
He set the file back on the coffee table and leaned back slightly against the sofa, his posture relaxed yet commanding all the same.
"My advice is this: you can accept the fifty-fifty split, but add a clause—after two years, the profit-sharing ratio is renegotiated based on your market valuation. That way you give Zhou Wanyin enough profit margin to keep her motivated to promote you, while ensuring your own future upside doesn't get locked in."
Su Qingyuan listened intently, silently committing it to memory.
No one had ever taught her any of this before. Lin Haoyu would only say, "Just do your job, don't overthink it." The Su family only ever thought it was "lucky enough she even had a job."
No one had ever told her that her value was something she could negotiate, that her future was something she could plan.
"And another thing," Lu Shiyan went on. "The contract doesn't clearly define ownership of the intellectual property. The most common problem with attachment-style partnerships is squabbling over copyright of the work. You need to nail this down before signing—your original works, the copyright stays yours forever, and any licensed use requires your written consent."
Su Qingyuan nodded, carving every word into her mind.
"Also—" Lu Shiyan suddenly paused, looking at her.
Su Qingyuan waited a few seconds. He didn't go on, and she couldn't help asking, "Also what?"
"Also," he leaned forward slightly, his voice dropping a register, "I'm glad you came to me to analyze the contract."
Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat.
"Not because of the fact that you came to me itself." Lu Shiyan's gaze settled on her face, calm and earnest. "It's because you know who to come to when you need advice."
The words were restrained, deeply restrained, but Su Qingyuan understood them.
He was saying: you've started to trust me.
She lowered her eyes, picked up the coffee and took a sip, using the rim of the cup to hide the slight upward curve at the corner of her mouth.
"Don't flatter yourself," she said softly. "I just happened to be passing."
"Mm. Just passing." Lu Shiyan agreed obligingly enough, but the arc at the corner of his lips gave him away.

After finishing her coffee, Su Qingyuan stayed in the office a while longer.
Lu Shiyan went to deal with an email, and she stood at the floor-to-ceiling windows, taking in the view.
The view from the eighty-eighth floor was simply too vast. The whole city unfurled beneath her feet, and in the distance the surface of the river glittered with broken gold under the sun. She was suddenly struck by a very strange feeling—standing here, it was as though even life itself had become wider, more open.
"What are you looking at?" Lu Shiyan had come to her side without her noticing.
"The view." Su Qingyuan paused, then said honestly, "I've never looked at this city from this angle before."
"You can come often from now on."
Su Qingyuan turned her head to look at him. He was gazing out the window, the line of his profile cold and severe, but in the depths of his eyes there was a very, very faint tenderness.
She was about to say something when the phone in her pocket buzzed.
It was Su Yurou's number.
Su Qingyuan's brow furrowed slightly as she answered.
The voice on the other end wasn't Su Yurou's—it was an unfamiliar man's, thick with drunkenness and urgency:
"Hello? Are you a friend of the person who owns this phone? She's blackout drunk all by herself in our bar, crying the whole time, and refuses to leave. We're about to close—could you come pick her up?"
Su Qingyuan was silent for two seconds.
"Her affairs have nothing to do with me. Call someone else in her contacts."
"But there are only three contacts in her phone—one's switched off, one isn't picking up, and you're the last one—"
Su Qingyuan hung up.
Lu Shiyan turned his head to look at her, but didn't ask who it was.
Su Qingyuan put the phone back in her pocket, her expression placid. "Su Yurou got drunk at a bar and wants me to come get her."
"Are you going?"
"No."
Her answer was crisp and clean, without a trace of hesitation.
Lu Shiyan looked at her, and something hard to define surfaced in his eyes—not surprise, but a kind of "just as I expected" appreciation.
"The old you would definitely have gone," he said.
"The old me is the old me." Su Qingyuan raised her eyes to him. "Before, I owed the Su family. That debt is paid. Now, she owes me."
She paused, her voice low:
"That I'm not kicking her while she's down—that's already the greatest kindness I have in me."
Lu Shiyan said nothing, only let out a soft "Mm."
But Su Qingyuan noticed that the look in his eyes as he regarded her had grown a shade deeper than before.

At dusk, Su Qingyuan left the Lu Group building.
Lu Shiyan saw her to the door without saying much, only handing her a document before she got into the car.
"What's this?"
"Suggested supplementary clauses for that partnership with Zhou Wanyin. I had legal draft them for you. Use them if you want, no problem if you don't."
Su Qingyuan took it—a thick stack, clearly not something tossed off carelessly.
She looked up at him, wanting to say thank you, then felt those two words were far too light.
"Lu Shiyan." For the first time, she didn't call him Mr. Lu.
His brow flickered.
"You've helped me with so much," Su Qingyuan said, looking at him earnestly. "I'll repay it."
Lu Shiyan looked down at her. The light of dusk fell across his shoulders, and his whole figure seemed gilded with a soft golden edge.
"There's no need to repay it," he said, his voice very low. "If you make a good life for yourself, that's the best repayment there is."
Su Qingyuan gripped the document folder tightly, something in her chest squeezed hard.
She turned and got into the car without looking back.
Because she was afraid that if she did, she wouldn't be able to hide the emotion in her eyes.
The taxi pulled out of the financial district and merged into the evening rush-hour traffic.
Su Qingyuan leaned back against the seat, watching the neon lights gradually brighten outside the window, his words replaying over and over in her mind—
"If you make a good life for yourself, that's the best repayment there is."
She closed her eyes, the corners of her mouth curving.
This man—he kept talking about "investment," but the things he did, which one of them was an investment?
It was plainly—
Never mind. Stop thinking about it.

Meanwhile, at a certain bar in the southern part of the city.
Su Yurou slumped over the bar counter, a row of emptied glasses before her.
The bar had already closed, and the staff were doing their final cleanup. Seeing that she showed no sign of leaving, they wore expressions of helplessness.
"Ma'am, we really do have to close now…"
Su Yurou lifted her head, her eyes red and swollen, her makeup a complete ruin.
She looked at the empty bar and suddenly laughed.
The laughter was shrill and desolate, echoing through the empty space, the kind of sound that made one's skin crawl.
"Su Qingyuan, your heart is so cruel." She muttered to herself, her voice hoarse. "You really just won't help me anymore?"
"You weren't like this before… before, all I had to do was cry a little and you'd soften…"
She braced herself against the bar and stood, staggering, steadying herself only by grabbing the back of a chair.
Her phone screen lit up.
It was a message from her agent: 【Yurou, the brand has officially terminated the contract. About the penalty fee—come to the company tomorrow to discuss it.】
Su Yurou stared at the message, and the last tear in her eyes fell.
Then she laughed.
A laugh uglier than weeping.
"Fine… fine then…" She gripped the phone tight. "Su Qingyuan, you think you've stomped me down so now you can rest easy?"
"It won't happen."
"If I'm not getting my peace, don't you dream of getting yours either."
She staggered out of the bar, the night wind pouring down her collar, cold enough to make her shudder.
The nights of Nancheng were never warm.
Least of all for the losers.
