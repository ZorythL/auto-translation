Chapter 13 — The Night Wind Was Gentle, and She Said, "I think I'm starting to like you"

Su Qingyuan said "my treat," but in the end it was Lu Shiyan who paid.
It wasn't that she didn't want to pay. When they finished eating and went to settle the bill, the cashier smiled and said, "This gentleman has already taken care of it."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. Lu Shiyan was standing by the door, head bowed over his phone, his profile traced into soft lines by the restaurant's warm amber light. Sensing her gaze, he lifted his head, his expression all innocence. "What is it?"
"We agreed it was my treat."
"Mm." He slipped his phone into his pocket. "You're treating. I'm paying. No conflict there."
Su Qingyuan looked at his perfectly self-righteous expression, opened her mouth, and found that she had absolutely nothing to say.
This man could even cheat with airtight logic.
They stepped out of the restaurant, and the night wind rushed to meet them, carrying the crisp chill peculiar to late autumn. Su Qingyuan instinctively drew his coat tighter around her shoulders—the one he'd draped over her outside the police station, which she'd kept forgetting to return.
Lu Shiyan glanced at the gesture but didn't remind her to give it back.
"Drive you home?" he asked.
"Let's just walk," Su Qingyuan said. "I ate too much. Need to walk it off."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, then nodded.
The two of them walked side by side through the nighttime streets of Nancheng.
This street was near the creative park, and at this hour there was hardly a soul around. The streetlamps stretched their two shadows long, long, overlapping then parting, parting then overlapping.
Neither of them spoke.
Yet that silence wasn't awkward—on the contrary, it carried a strange sense of ease. As if they'd been together for a long time, with no need to force a topic, no need to fill the quiet, just walking, and that alone was enough.
Passing a flower shop that hadn't yet closed, Su Qingyuan paused.
In the window sat a bouquet of white chamomile, the tiny blossoms huddled together, clean and warm.
Lu Shiyan followed her gaze.
"You like them?"
Su Qingyuan pulled her eyes away and shook her head. "Just looking."
He said nothing, and the two of them kept walking.
After about a dozen steps, Su Qingyuan heard the bell of the flower shop door ring behind her. Assuming they were closing up, she didn't turn around.
Until Lu Shiyan said, "Hold out your hand."
She turned, and a bouquet of white chamomile was held out in front of her.
She froze.
"Didn't you say you were just looking?"
"You said you were just looking," Lu Shiyan's voice sounded especially low in the night wind, "but the way you looked at them—it wasn't just looking."
Su Qingyuan looked at the bouquet, then back at him.
His expression was very calm, with none of that deliberate romance, none of that grand show. As if he'd done something perfectly ordinary, not worth making a fuss over.
But the string in her heart had just been plucked, hard.
"Thank you." She took the flowers, lowered her head, and breathed in their scent, her voice a little muffled.
"You're welcome."
The two of them kept walking.
Su Qingyuan held the chamomile in her arms, and as she walked the little blossoms trembled and trembled, brushing against her chin—ticklish, and sweet.
By the time they reached the foot of her apartment building, the light above the entrance was still on, its dim yellow glow spilling onto the ground like a small, warm lake.
"We're here." Su Qingyuan stopped.
Lu Shiyan stopped too.
The two of them stood facing each other, a bouquet's distance between them.
"About today," Su Qingyuan lifted her head to look at him, "thank you. Not just for the flowers—for the police station too. If it weren't for you, I wouldn't have gotten out of there so fast."
Lu Shiyan lowered his eyes to look at her, his voice very soft. "I told you, you don't need to thank me."
"I know you said that. But I still want to say it."
Su Qingyuan looked into his deep eyes. The night wind tousled her hair, a few loose strands falling against her cheek. She suddenly felt that there were some things which, if she didn't say them tonight, she might never have the courage to say later.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"I think I…" She paused, her fingers tightening slightly around the bouquet, her voice soft as though afraid of startling something, "…am starting to like you."
The night wind suddenly went still.
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his eyes deep, so very deep, like undercurrents hidden beneath the surface of a midnight sea.
He didn't speak right away. He was silent for a few seconds, then bent slightly toward her, narrowing the distance between them.
"Think?" His voice was very low, low enough that it seemed meant for her ears alone.
Su Qingyuan's heart was pounding hard, but she fought to keep her face composed. "It's just 'think.' I'm not sure yet."
"Then tell me once you're sure."
His tone was very calm, but Su Qingyuan noticed that the hand hanging at his side curled slightly at the fingertips.
That tiny movement made the last bit of uncertainty in her heart melt away all at once, like a block of ice meeting hot water.
So he was nervous too.
So she wasn't the only one whose heart was racing.
"Goodnight." Su Qingyuan took a step back.
"Goodnight."
She turned and walked through the entrance, the bouquet of white chamomile in her arms, climbing the stairs one step at a time.
Three floors up, she couldn't resist glancing down through the stairwell window.
Lu Shiyan was still standing there.
Beneath the streetlamp, he stood alone, his suit jacket on her shoulders, leaving him in nothing but his shirt, the night wind lifting its hem ever so slightly.
He was gazing up at the building, though she couldn't tell which window he was looking at.
Su Qingyuan ducked back, her heart pounding like a drum.
She hurried up the stairs, opened her door, stepped inside, set the flowers carefully on the table, then walked to the window and looked down.
He was already gone.
The space beneath the streetlamp was empty, leaving only a patch of dim yellow light spilled across the ground.
Su Qingyuan stood at the window, then slowly crouched down and buried her face against her knees.
It was over.
Completely over.

Su Qingyuan: ……
She stood up, walked to the table, and looked at the bouquet of white chamomile.
The tiny blossoms, their clean and pure color, their faint fragrance.
She remembered what he'd said: "The way you looked at them—it wasn't just looking."
This man—how did he manage to be domineering and tender all at once?
Su Qingyuan found a glass jar, arranged the flowers in it, and set it on the windowsill. Moonlight spilled in through the window, falling on the little white blossoms, quiet and lovely.
She looked at the bouquet and suddenly let out a small laugh.
Think.
Starting to.
Those two words really were holding something back.
But she knew that her real feelings ran far more than "think," weighed far more than "starting to."
She just… wasn't ready yet to say "I like you so much, so very much."
A little longer.
Wait for him to come a little closer, wait for her to be a little more sure.
And then, she'd look him in the eyes and say it, sincerely and in earnest:
Lu Shiyan, I like you. Not "I think," not "starting to."

Early the next morning, Su Qingyuan had barely arrived at the studio when her phone blew up.
Countless messages came flooding in—WeChat, Weibo, industry forums—all buzzing about the same thing.
The design drafts for the "First Light" series had been posted online.
She hadn't posted them.
Su Qingyuan opened the Weibo post, and her pupils shrank.
Nine images, capturing all seven finished drafts of the "First Light" series, down to the pencil shavings scattered across the worktable and a half-finished cup of coffee. The shots were taken from directly above the table, in high resolution—clearly someone had slipped into the studio while she was away to photograph them.
The Weibo caption was dripping with snide insinuation:
"A certain up-and-coming 'genius designer's' 'First Light' series—the inspiration and compositional elements bear a striking resemblance to a foreign designer's portfolio from three years ago. Original work, or 'borrowing'? See for yourselves."
Below it were several comparison images.
Su Qingyuan zoomed in on the comparisons and nearly laughed in fury.
The other side had dug up the portfolio of an obscure foreign designer. There were indeed a few light-and-shadow-themed elements in it, but whether in structure, material, craftsmanship, or core concept, it was completely different from the "First Light" series.
The so-called "striking resemblance" was entirely manufactured—through cropped angles, selective close-ups, and forced associations.
But the average netizen wouldn't look that closely.
They'd only see the word "plagiarism," only see those "similarities" deliberately framed in the comparison images, and then storm into the comment section to fling abuse with gusto.
The comments had already climbed toward ten thousand.
"Another plagiarism freak?"
"Crashed and burned the second she got popular. Hilarious."
"Zhou Wanyin actually signed her? Looks like Sister Wanyin misjudged this one."
"People like this deserve to get blacklisted."
Su Qingyuan's fingers went cold, but her mind was racing.
Who had posted this Weibo?
Su Yurou was in the detention center—it couldn't be her.
Someone from the Su family? Possible. But more likely—Su Yurou had left a contingency plan in place before going in. She'd been scheming this for a while; she just hadn't expected to get arrested first. That Weibo post had been scheduled in advance.
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath, set down her phone, and walked to the window.
The osmanthus tree was still there, its fragrance still there.
But her state of mind was completely different from yesterday.
She closed her eyes, her thoughts spinning at top speed.
She couldn't panic.
She couldn't lose her composure.
This was her first collection, drawn line by line with her own hand. Every line, every structure, every detail of craftsmanship—she could explain the creative logic behind all of it.
The other side couldn't produce any evidence that she'd plagiarized.
But she could produce evidence of her own originality.
From the conceptual sketches to the finished drafts, she had every step on record in her system.
Su Qingyuan opened her eyes and dialed Zhou Wanyin.
"Sister Wanyin, have you seen what's going on online?"
"I've seen it." Zhou Wanyin's voice was steady and composed, without a trace of panic. "I've got the legal team handling it right now. Qingyuan, let me ask you one question, and I want you to answer me honestly—are these designs your original work or not?"
"They are."
"Good." Zhou Wanyin's tone turned firm. "Then I'll handle this matter. Don't post anything, don't respond to anything. Wait for word from me."
The call ended.
Su Qingyuan held her phone, most of the worry settled out of her heart.
But she knew this wouldn't blow over so easily.
The fact that the other side had chosen this particular moment to strike was clearly carefully planned—the "First Light" series hadn't been officially released yet, she hadn't had the chance to establish any public recognition, and slinging mud at a time like this was the easiest way to make it stick.
She had to do something.
She couldn't just wait.
Su Qingyuan walked to the worktable, opened her computer, and logged into her own Weibo account.
She'd registered for Weibo a long time ago but had never posted anything. Her avatar was still the default gray, and she had only a few dozen followers, most of them bots.
She thought for a moment, then pulled up a photo of the very first draft of the "First Light" series from her phone—the first version she'd drawn three days ago, with rough lines and a loose structure, worlds apart from the final draft.
Then she uploaded that photo with a single line of text:
["First Light," day one. In three days, it became what you've seen. I have a record of every step of the process. Just wait.]
After posting, she set the phone face-down on the table and began assembling all the records of her creative process.
From the first rough sketch, to the second version, the third, the fourth… all the way to the seventh and final draft.
From notes on material selection, craft research, and technical breakthroughs.
From the experimental process of the bidirectional moonstone cutting technique, to photographs of all the failed versions.
She organized every bit of it, arranged it along a timeline, and compiled it into a complete creative archive.
If the other side wanted a fight, then a fight they'd get.
A fight to the finish.

Two hours later, Zhou Wanyin called.
"Qingyuan, I tracked down the account that posted that Weibo. It's a marketing account, and behind it is a small PR firm. I had someone ask around, and they said they were commissioned to post it by a man named Su Guoqiang."
Su Guoqiang.
Su Qingyuan's adoptive father.
Su Yurou's biological father.
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, her fingers tightening around the phone.
She wasn't surprised.
Not in the slightest.
Su Yurou had been arrested, and Su Guoqiang had panicked. His daughter's reputation was already ruined, and now she'd lost her personal freedom too—he had to do something to turn the situation around. Or rather, to take revenge.
"Sister Wanyin, I'll handle this one myself."
"Are you sure?"
"I'm sure." Su Qingyuan opened her eyes, her voice frighteningly calm. "There are some accounts that it's time to settle all at once."
She hung up, scrolled through her contacts, and found a number.
It belonged to the lawyer who'd helped her with her lease before—not the top-tier attorney Lu Shiyan had brought, but a middle-aged female lawyer who specialized in family disputes, surnamed Liu, sharp and efficient in her work and upright in character.
"Lawyer Liu, I'd like to consult about something. Concerning the support obligations between adoptive parents and an adopted daughter, and… the matter of prosecuting adoptive parents on suspicion of defamation and copyright infringement."
Lawyer Liu clearly hadn't expected her to ask about this. She was silent for two seconds, then said, "Ms. Su, for something like this, I'd advise you to talk it over with your adoptive parents first. Once you take the legal route, whatever the outcome, the family bond is severed for good."
"It's already severed." Su Qingyuan's tone didn't waver in the slightest. "And besides, they're the ones who drew the knife first."
There was silence on the other end for a few more seconds, then Lawyer Liu said, "All right. I understand. Come to the firm, and we'll talk it through in person."
Su Qingyuan hung up and picked up her bag to head out.
At the door she paused, glancing back at the seven design drafts on the worktable.
Outside the window, the osmanthus tree swayed gently in the wind, its shadow falling across the drafts.
She suddenly remembered what Lu Shiyan had said standing right here yesterday: "This story—I'm in it too."
Su Qingyuan pulled her gaze away and pushed open the door.
This time, she didn't want anyone to take the hit for her.
She would sweep her own battlefield clean—by herself.

On the way to the law firm, her phone buzzed.
Lu Shiyan: [I saw what's happening online. What do you need me to do?]
Su Qingyuan looked at the message, warmth stirring in her chest, but her reply was perfectly composed:
[Nothing. I've got this one myself. He's my adoptive father—this is a fight I should fight on my own. You just stay on the sidelines and watch.]
There was a pause on the other end.
Lu Shiyan: [All right. I'll watch.]
Then, a few seconds later:
Lu Shiyan: [But if you get tired of fighting, tell me anytime. I'll finish the second half for you.]
Su Qingyuan looked at those words—"I'll finish the second half for you"—and her nose suddenly stung.
This man, who would always say "step in when you can't go on anymore," who always handed her the initiative, who never overstepped.
And the more he was like this, the more she wanted him to overstep.

Su Qingyuan put her phone away and looked out the car window.
The taxi was just passing Nancheng's financial center, the tallest tower glittering in the sunlight.
The windows on the eighty-eighth floor reflected a dazzling glare. She couldn't see whether anyone was standing up there, but she knew—there was one person there.
Watching her.
Waiting for her.
Ready, the moment she grew tired of fighting, to step in and finish the second half for her.
Su Qingyuan pulled her gaze back, the corners of her mouth curving up.
Su Guoqiang.
Bring it on.
Old debts and new—today we settle them all.
