Chapter 30 — The Light of Daybreak
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626232.html

The night Su Yurou received the template for the open letter, it rained over Nancheng until dawn.
She sat at the desk in her rented room, the glow of the screen lighting up her gaunt face. The document Gu Changfeng's people had sent her wasn't long—just over three hundred characters—written in a tone of "sincere confession," recounting how she had "taken part" in the early conception of Su Qingyuan's "First Light" collection. "Those late-night discussions, those sparks of inspiration—Qingyuan didn't create those works alone. I contributed my ideas to them too."
Every single word of it was a lie.
Su Yurou stared at the screen, fingers hovering over the keyboard, slow to come down. She thought of Su Qingyuan's back as she sat sketching on the attic floor. Back then their rooms had been separated by a single wall, and she had often heard the sound drifting through from the other side—pencil scraping across paper, a soft rustle, rustle, rustle, like silkworms feeding on mulberry leaves, going on and on into the dead of night. She had never once pushed that door open. Never once asked, "What are you drawing?" And she had certainly never "taken part" in any discussion.
She had only sat on this side of the wall, listening to that rustling sound, going half-mad with jealousy.
Her phone buzzed. A prodding message from Gu Changfeng's people: "Miss Su, is the letter ready? Director Gu would like to have it by tomorrow."
Su Yurou typed a few words, deleted them, typed again, deleted again. In the end she replied with a single line: "I'll have it to you before noon tomorrow."
The reply came: "Good. The money will be in your account within twenty-four hours of the letter going out."
Su Yurou closed the chat window and opened a blank document, her fingers resting on the keyboard for a long while. Then she began to type—but not the open letter. What she typed was a real letter. A letter to Su Qingyuan.
"Qingyuan,
By the time you read this, I should already be gone from Nancheng. Maybe in Australia, maybe somewhere else—anywhere but here.
Gu Changfeng asked me to write an open letter saying I had a hand in the 'First Light' collection. He wanted me to trade that for the money. He said it didn't matter whether it was true or false—as long as someone believed it.
I don't know what his face will look like when he reads this. He'll probably fly into a rage. He might send people after me, or he might not, because in his eyes I'm already a dead piece on the board, not worth the effort.
My flight is this afternoon. This evidence is the one thing I can finally do right before I go.
Qingyuan, in this life I owe you far too much. More than I can ever repay. I won't say 'I'll repay you in the next life,' because in the next life I don't want to meet you again. Not because I hate you—because I can't bear to face you.
Su Yurou"
She printed the letter out, and together with the USB drive—which held every chat record between her and Gu Changfeng, the transfer receipts, and the open-letter template—she slid it all into a kraft-paper envelope and sealed it. On the envelope she wrote three characters: Su Qingyuan.
Then she picked up her phone and booked an afternoon flight to Melbourne.
Gu Changfeng's money—she didn't want it anymore. Australia, she was still going. But not on his money—on the last of her own savings. It wasn't much, enough to last a few months. Once she got there she'd figure something out—a job, a small business, anything. Whatever happened, she couldn't stay here any longer.
She drew back the curtain. At some point the rain outside had stopped. The clouds had split open along a seam, and sunlight spilled down through the gap, falling onto the rooftop of the building across the way, golden and glittering.
Su Yurou looked at that light, and suddenly remembered when she was very small, not long after Su Qingyuan had first come to live with them. One evening they had sat together on the steps in the courtyard, and Su Qingyuan had pointed at the sunset on the horizon and said, "Look—that light is so beautiful." She had answered scornfully, "What's so great about it? It's there every day." Su Qingyuan hadn't argued. She had simply gazed quietly at that swath of light, the colors of the sunset reflected in her eyes.
Now, all at once, she understood—what Su Qingyuan had been looking at then wasn't the sunset. It was the beam of light she had always carried inside her. That light had carried her through every stretch of darkness, all the way to today.
And she, Su Yurou, had never had a light like that. She had only jealousy, resentment, and the stubborn refusal to lose. Those things could keep a person going for a while—but not for a lifetime.
Su Yurou tucked the envelope into her bag, picked up her keys, and went out the door.
It was two in the afternoon when Su Qingyuan received the envelope. She had just come back from Qu Ya's studio, the venue layout plans for the joint exhibition still in her hand. The envelope had been left under the doormat outside her studio door, one corner peeking out. No sender information, no shipping label—clearly placed there by hand.
She bent down and picked it up, recognizing the handwriting of the three characters "Su Qingyuan" on the envelope—crooked and uneven, like a grade-schooler's writing. Something stirred in her chest. She took out her keys, unlocked the door, stepped inside, and tore the envelope open.
The paper was thin, A4, as if pulled straight from a printer. She read the handwritten letter first. Su Yurou's writing was as ugly as ever, but every character was pressed down hard, some of the strokes torn through by the nib, leaving tiny smears of ink.
When she finished, she stayed silent for a long time.
Then she took out the USB drive and plugged it into her computer. The contents were more complete than she'd imagined—the chat records between Su Yurou and Gu Changfeng, from the Hengsheng affair all the way to the open-letter template, every message time-stamped. There were also several audio recordings—phone calls between Su Yurou and Gu Changfeng, their contents enough to make the blood run cold.
Su Qingyuan pulled the drive out and locked it in a drawer. Then she picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Su Yurou is gone. She didn't write the open letter—she left behind all of Gu Changfeng's evidence. The letter says her flight to Australia is this afternoon."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "I just got word—Gu Changfeng's people are looking for her too. Looks like she didn't just refuse to write the letter. She sold Gu Changfeng out."
Su Qingyuan looked at the message and felt a sudden wave of feeling. In this life, Su Yurou had done many wrong things. But this once, she had done one thing right. Not because she had suddenly turned good—because she had finally grown tired, too tired to keep lying to herself. Sometimes a person's change isn't an epiphany. It's exhaustion. Once you've burned away all your malice, your resentment, your obsession, maybe in the ashes that remain there's still a little something clean, something like a conscience.
Su Qingyuan replied to Lu Shiyan: "Is that evidence enough to shut Gu Changfeng up?"
Lu Shiyan: "Not enough. But enough to make him realize he can't control everything."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips, set the phone down on the workbench, picked up her carving knife, and went on polishing the fourth sample in the "Roots" series. The sunlight outside crept out from behind the clouds, falling on her hand, on the carving knife, on the gem taking shape beneath it. Everything was the same as always. And yet everything was different now.
Three days later, Gu Changfeng's people found Su Yurou's rented room. The place was empty, the key still in the door, the room swept clean, as if no one had ever lived there. The only thing left was a note on the desk that read: "Don't bother looking. You won't find me."
When Gu Changfeng saw that note, his expression turned as sour as if he'd swallowed a pound of bitter herbs. The chess piece he'd poured so many resources into cultivating had bolted without a word—and before bolting had copied every piece of evidence over to his opponent. It wasn't the money he grieved over. It was his own judgment. He had thought Su Yurou would stay obedient, would go on hating Su Qingyuan forever, would go on being the good, useful blade in his hand. But he'd forgotten—a blade used long enough rolls at the edge, and a blade with a rolled edge would sooner snap itself than be held again.
He sat in the leather chair in his study, staring out at the gray, overcast sky, and was struck by the sudden feeling that this fight—he might not win it. Not because Lu Shiyan was too strong. Because he himself had chosen the wrong people. Every one he'd picked—Su Yurou, Director Jiang, that media company—had a weakness. He'd used those weaknesses to control them, and forgotten that people with weaknesses can turn and bite back at any moment.
And the person Lu Shiyan had chosen—Su Qingyuan—had no weakness. Or rather, her weakness wasn't the kind that could be exploited. The things she cared about—her work, her bottom line, her principles—none of them could be bought with money or shaken with threats.
Gu Changfeng closed his eyes and leaned back against the chair, exhaling a long breath. The breath fogged into a white cloud in the cold air, then dissolved.
He opened his eyes, picked up his phone, and dialed a number. "That piece we pulled earlier—reformat it. Run it tomorrow."
The voice on the other end hesitated. "Director Gu, if we run it now, with Su Yurou's side—"
"Su Yurou is already gone. Her account, her chat records, every trace—all wiped clean. With no evidence, Su Qingyuan can't touch me."
"But what if—"
"There is no what if." Gu Changfeng's voice was cold as iron. "Run it."
The next morning, the hit piece went out on schedule.
The headline read: "Exclusive Exposé: Biennale Gold Medalist Designer Su Qingyuan and Her 'Unconventional' Relationship with Lu Group President Lu Shiyan." The wording was more "polite" than the first version, but the insinuation ran far stronger. Every "coincidence" was bolded, every overlap in the timeline highlighted in red, and by the end, any ordinary reader would be left with a question mark in their mind—was she genuinely talented, or did she rise through connections?
In the first hour after the piece went out, the comment section was still fairly rational—some questioned its truthfulness, some demanded the evidence be shown. By the second hour, the paid trolls had moved in, and the comments began tilting one-sidedly toward the negative. "I always felt there was something off about her—how could a no-name designer with no background suddenly blow up like that?" "The Cartier collaboration was forced through by pressure from Lu Group too, right? Cartier really has no backbone." "Biennale gold medal, ha—money makes the world go round." By the third hour, Su Qingyuan's Weibo was flooded with abuse. Her DMs overflowed, her comment section fell, and the studio phone rang off the hook.
Su Qingyuan didn't panic. She sat at her workbench, the USB drive Su Yurou had left spread out before her. She hadn't wanted to use this evidence, because using it meant dragging Su Yurou back into the whirlpool. But Su Yurou was already gone, no longer in the whirlpool—and Gu Changfeng still was.
She picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "I'm going to make public the evidence Su Yurou left. What do you think?"
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "Make it public. But not under your own name. Have Zhou Wanyin release it in the studio's name—it carries more credibility than if you do it yourself."
Su Qingyuan replied: "All right."
She organized the key contents from the drive—the chat records of Gu Changfeng telling Su Yurou to write the open letter, the open-letter template, and the letter Su Yurou had written just before leaving. She didn't make all the chat records public, only the most essential pages, enough to prove Gu Changfeng had orchestrated everything from behind the scenes, but without exposing more of Su Yurou's privacy. Then she called Zhou Wanyin and laid the matter out in brief.
When Zhou Wanyin had heard her out, she was quiet for a few seconds. "Qingyuan, are you sure you want to make this letter public? When Su Yurou wrote it, she probably never imagined you'd release it."
"She knew when she wrote it." Su Qingyuan's voice was very calm. "When she left the letter for me, she knew how I'd use it. This isn't a gift she gave me—it's the innocence she gave back to herself. Her innocence may not be worth much, but she wanted to do one right thing at the end."
Zhou Wanyin fell silent again for a few seconds, then said, "All right. I'll release it."
An hour later, the official account of Zhou Wanyin's studio published a long post. The headline read: "A Complete Response Regarding the Rumors Spread About Ms. Su Qingyuan." The article carried no excess sentiment, no emotional accusations—it simply laid out the evidence one piece at a time: screenshots of the chat records between Gu Changfeng and Su Yurou, the open-letter template, and Su Yurou's handwritten letter from just before she left.
The final paragraph read: "All of the above evidence has been submitted to and notarized for the record. Against any individual or organization that continues to fabricate or spread rumors, we reserve the right to pursue legal liability. Ms. Su Qingyuan's talent needs no one's endorsement. Every one of her works was drawn by her own hand, stroke by stroke, and ground out by her own hand, cut by cut. Anyone who doubts her is welcome to go and see the actual pieces from the 'First Light' collection, the 'Light Traces' collection, the 'Roots' series—and judge for themselves whether that craftsmanship is something 'connections' can produce."
In the first hour after the long post went out, public opinion began to loosen. Someone dug up the detailed craftsmanship images from the "First Light" collection and analyzed the layered halo of the Ripple Cut frame by frame, reaching a conclusion: this level of craftsmanship was not something connections could manufacture. By the second hour, Cartier's official account reposted Zhou Wanyin's long post with the caption: "Cartier's collaboration with Ms. Su Qingyuan is based on her design work. There is no other reason." By the third hour, the hashtag #BelieveInSuQingyuan# shot to the top of the trending list.
The comment section turned completely around.
"The chat records are right there—and people are still saying she relied on connections?"
"Who is Gu Changfeng? Why does he want to ruin Su Qingyuan?"
"Chairman of Dingfeng Group. Lu Group's old rival. This is a corporate war, and Su Qingyuan got dragged into it through no fault of her own."
"That letter Su Yurou wrote before she left made me cry. She did so many wrong things, and in the end she finally did one thing right."
"No matter what Su Yurou did in the past, this time she stepped up and told the truth. That deserves some respect."
Su Qingyuan looked at the comments. She didn't cry, didn't smile—she only felt a strange sort of calm settle over her heart. As if a storm that had raged for a very long time had finally stopped. Not because she had won, but because the rain had run its course, and the sky was due to clear.
The studio door was pushed open. Lu Shiyan walked in—no insulated bag in his hands, no flowers, just empty-handed. He crossed to her, looked down at her, was silent for two seconds, then reached out and pulled her into his arms, holding her tight.
Su Qingyuan buried her face against his chest, breathing in the clean, cool scent of him, and felt a sudden heat rise behind her eyes. She didn't cry—she only closed her eyes and listened to his heartbeat. One beat, two, three. Each one steady and strong, like a kind of promise.
"Gu Changfeng has lost this round—completely." Lu Shiyan's voice came from above her head, low and even. "Not in the bid. In the court of public opinion. The moment the open-letter business broke, his Dingfeng Group's stock dropped six points today. The board is already discussing whether to let him stay on as chairman."
Su Qingyuan lifted her head from his arms and looked into his eyes. "Will he step down?"
"Not necessarily. But the next time he wants to come after you, it won't be so easy." Lu Shiyan reached out and gently wiped away the dampness that had welled up at the corner of her eye at some point. "Because everyone knows now what kind of man he is."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips, drew back out of his arms, walked to the window, and pushed it open. Cold air rushed in, sweeping away the heavy mood inside the studio. The sky outside had cleared, sunlight peeking out from behind the clouds, falling on the bare osmanthus tree, on the cyclamen on the windowsill, on her face.
She drew a deep breath and let it out.
"Lu Shiyan, I want hot pot."
"Okay."
"The spicy kind."
"Okay."
"The really, really spicy kind."
"Okay." Lu Shiyan watched her profile lit by the sun, the corner of his mouth lifting slightly. "Are you crying?"
"No."
"Then what are you wiping at?"
"The sun's too bright."
Lu Shiyan didn't call her bluff. He walked over and stood beside her, looking out the window too. The two of them stood side by side at the window, the sunlight falling over them both, casting their shadows on the floor—close together, close enough to almost overlap.
Winter in Nancheng was still going on. But Su Qingyuan knew the coldest part had already passed. Not because Gu Changfeng had lost, but because she had won—won the battle inside her own heart. Before, she'd been afraid of being doubted, afraid of being dismissed, afraid that all her effort could be wiped away by a single line: "She relied on connections." Now she wasn't afraid anymore. Because her work was her evidence, her name was her brand. Whatever anyone said couldn't change that fact.
The hot pot place was the same Hunan restaurant as last time. When the pot of bubbling red-oil broth was set down, Su Qingyuan's eyes were even brighter than the last time.
"You look very happy," Lu Shiyan said, pouring her a glass of ice water.
"Mm." Su Qingyuan picked up a piece of tripe, dipped it up and down seven or eight times, and put it in her mouth, hissing at the heat but unwilling to spit it out. "Happy."
Lu Shiyan watched her lips, flushed red from the spice, his gaze as soft as moonlight. "There won't be anything like this again."
"Anything like what?"
"Someone doubting you."
Su Qingyuan set down her chopsticks and looked at him. "Lu Shiyan, when you say that, you sound exactly like you're making a promise."
"It is a promise."
The earnestness on his face left her a little flustered. She lowered her head and went on eating. As she ate, something suddenly came to mind. "Oh, right—did Su Yurou make it to Australia?"
"She did. Landed in Melbourne last night." Lu Shiyan's tone was very flat. "She posted to her Moments, said 'the sunshine in Melbourne is lovely.' Visible to one person only."
Su Qingyuan paused. "To whom?"
"You."
Su Qingyuan fell silent. When Su Yurou posted that, she probably hadn't known whether Su Qingyuan would even be able to see it. But she'd posted it anyway, as if to confirm—did you get my letter? Did you see it? You know now, don't you? You don't have to forgive me. I just wanted you to know I finally did one thing right.
Su Qingyuan didn't reply to that Moments post, and didn't mention it to anyone. She only opened Su Yurou's WeChat profile picture and glanced at it once—the avatar had been changed to a photo of a Melbourne beach, blue sky and emerald sea, the sunshine lovely. She switched off her phone and went back to eating her hot pot.
The next day, Anna from Cartier called. "Miss Su, regarding the global launch of the 'Light Traces' collection, headquarters has a proposal."
"What proposal?"
"They'd like you to attend the launch wearing a piece of jewelry you designed yourself—not a Cartier piece, but your own work. They said the 'Light Traces' collection is your light, and Cartier is only the vessel that carries the light."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, a scalding emotion welling up inside her. For a century-old brand like Cartier to be willing to let an independent designer wear her own work at its flagship store's global launch—that was no longer just a "collaboration." It was respect. It was placing her on equal footing—not "the brand and the partner designer," but "you and us."
"Anna, thank you."
"Thank me for what? You earned it yourself." Anna laughed. "Oh, and that Mr. Lu of yours—he'll be coming to Paris with you, won't he?"
Su Qingyuan was taken aback. "How did you know?"
"Last time he sent someone to Cartier headquarters to discuss the supply chain, he mentioned it in passing. He said if the 'Light Traces' launch was arranged in Paris, he'd come with you. He hoped we could help arrange the itinerary."
Su Qingyuan held the phone, the tips of her ears flushing faintly red. This man—without her even knowing—had already arranged every detail, from the supply chain to the itinerary, from the coat to the transportation in Paris. Not one thing he did was grand, but strung together they formed an entire dense map, written all over with the words "I'm here."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Anna said when you went to Cartier headquarters to discuss the supply chain, you mentioned the Paris business in passing."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "Mm. In passing."
Su Qingyuan looked at the words "in passing" and smiled. She slipped the phone into her pocket, picked up her carving knife, and went back to work. The sunlight outside was lovely, falling on the workbench, on her hand, on the gem taking shape beneath her tools. All at once she remembered a rainy night long ago, when she had sat on the floor of a hotel room, staring at her empty bank account, certain her whole world had ended.
What she hadn't known then was that the rain that night was the end of the storm—and also the beginning of dawn.
