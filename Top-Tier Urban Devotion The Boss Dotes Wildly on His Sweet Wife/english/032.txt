Paris, the Wall of Love, and Light
As the plane sliced through the clouds, Su Qingyuan fell asleep against Lu Shiyan's shoulder.
She had a very long dream. In it, she was still a little girl, sitting on the attic floor of the old Su family house, a stub of pencil in her hand, drawing on a crumpled sheet of paper. Outside the window was Nancheng's overcast grey sky; downstairs was the voice of her foster mother, Liu Xiulan, nagging at her to cook. She didn't answer. She kept drawing. She was drawing light—the thin, golden light that filtered out from behind the clouds.
That image lasted a long time in the dream. Then it changed abruptly. She was standing before an enormous glass door; inside was a magnificent banquet hall, outside an empty corridor. Through the glass, she saw someone standing inside. He wore a black suit, and he was looking at her. She wanted to see his face clearly, but a film of mist coated the glass, and no matter how she tried, she couldn't make him out.
Then she woke.
The plane was descending. Beyond the cabin window lay the Paris night, ten thousand lights glittering like a golden sea. Lu Shiyan was still holding the exact pose he'd been in when she fell asleep—motionless, afraid of waking her.
"You're awake?" He looked down at her.
"Mm." Su Qingyuan rubbed her eyes and straightened up off his shoulder. "How long did I sleep?"
"Three hours."
"You didn't move the whole time?"
"Didn't want to wake you."
Su Qingyuan looked at his shoulder, creased from where she'd leaned, and something inside her softened. "You could have woken me."
"Couldn't bear to."
Just three words. Su Qingyuan's nose stung. She turned her face toward the window, pretending to watch the Paris night below. The lights drew closer and closer, like countless golden stars scattered across the ground. She suddenly remembered a photograph of Paris she'd seen in a magazine when she was very small—the Eiffel Tower, the Seine, the Louvre. Back then she'd thought Paris was another world, somewhere she could never go in this lifetime. Now she was here. And he was beside her.
The plane landed, taxied, and came to a stop. Su Qingyuan unbuckled her seatbelt, stood, and took her carry-on down from the overhead bin. Inside it were her sketchbook and her carving knives—Lu Shiyan had packed them, wrapped in soft cloth and tucked into the innermost layer of the bag so they wouldn't get knocked about.
"Let's go." Lu Shiyan held out his hand.
Su Qingyuan placed her hand in his palm and let him close his fingers around it. The two of them stepped out of the cabin door, and the night wind rushed to meet them. Paris in winter was colder than Nancheng; the air was dry, carrying a crisp, indescribable scent.
"Cold?" Lu Shiyan unwound his scarf and wrapped it around her neck.
"I'm fine." Su Qingyuan buried her face in the scarf and breathed in the familiar scent of him.
In the airport hall, the staff Cartier had arranged were already waiting. A Frenchman in his thirties, dressed in a navy-blue uniform, held up a sign with her name on it. Seeing them emerge, he came forward with a smile.
"Miss Su? Mr. Lu? I'm Jean-Philippe, from Cartier's concierge service. The car is ready. Please, this way."
A black executive car was parked at the airport exit. Su Qingyuan climbed in and leaned back against the seat, watching the streets of Paris stream past the window. The late-night city was quiet and gentle, the streetlamps casting a warm amber glow over the ancient buildings, each like a painting.
The car stopped in front of a hotel. Su Qingyuan got out and looked up—not one of those gilded, glittering luxury hotels, but a small boutique tucked away in a side street. The frontage was modest, but every detail breathed a restrained French elegance.
"This is the hotel Cartier arranged for you, Miss Su." Jean-Philippe opened the car door. "Your rooms are ready. Tomorrow morning at ten, Madame Anna will come to collect you and take you to the flagship store."
"Thank you." Su Qingyuan took the keycard and wheeled her suitcase into the lobby.
The room was on the fourth floor—not large, but very comfortable. Beyond the floor-to-ceiling window stretched the Paris skyline; in the distance she could see the tip of the Eiffel Tower, glinting gold against the night. Su Qingyuan stood at the window for a while, then turned and found Lu Shiyan standing behind her.
"Where's your room?" she asked.
"Next door."
Su Qingyuan glanced at him and smiled. "You booked it?"
"Mm."
"On purpose?"
"Mm."
Su Qingyuan reached out and gave him a light punch. "Why didn't you just book one room?"
Lu Shiyan caught the fist she'd swung at him and looked down at her. "Because I know you're not ready yet."
Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat. This man—he always left the choice to her. He would never say, "Let's stay together." He would never say, "We're together anyway." He would wait—wait until she was ready, wait until she said "yes" on her own.
"Lu Shiyan, you really are far too patient."
"With you, always."
Su Qingyuan turned her face away and looked out at the Eiffel Tower. "Tomorrow evening, shall we go up to Montmartre?"
"All right."
"See the Wall of Love, then go eat croissants?"
"All right."
Su Qingyuan turned back to look at him. "Why do you say yes to everything?"
"Because whatever you say is fine by me."
Su Qingyuan laughed, rose onto her toes, and kissed him on the lips. "Good night."
"Good night."
Lu Shiyan turned and walked out of the room, drawing the door softly shut behind him. Su Qingyuan leaned against the door panel, listening to his footsteps recede down the corridor, then the door of the next room opened and closed again. She curled her lips into a smile, walked to the bed, and let herself fall, flinging herself into the soft covers.
Paris. She and he, in Paris. Tomorrow, her work would debut at the Cartier flagship store. The day after, they would go up to Montmartre and see the wall covered in "I love you." She rolled over, pulled the covers up to her chin, and closed her eyes.
Outside the window, the lights of the Eiffel Tower blinked on and off, as if waving hello to her.
The next day, Su Qingyuan woke at six. Not jolted awake—she simply woke on her own. She lay in bed watching the morning light play across the ceiling, drifting for a moment, then got up, washed, and dressed. She wore a white turtleneck sweater, black wide-leg trousers, and over them the smoke-grey cashmere coat Lu Shiyan had given her. She didn't pin her hair up; she let it fall loose over her shoulders, and put on only a single pair of stud earrings—samples from the "First Snow" collection, her own work.
The woman in the mirror was clean, sharp, understated, yet every detail was her own choice. She smiled at her reflection, picked up her bag, and stepped out.
In the corridor, Lu Shiyan was already waiting. Today he wore a dark grey coat over a black turtleneck; he looked like he'd stepped out of a magazine. Seeing Su Qingyuan, he gave her a once-over, his gaze lingering for an instant on her earlobes. "Wearing your own work?"
"Mm. 'First Snow.'"
"Beautiful."
"Do you mean the earrings, or me?"
"Both."
Su Qingyuan laughed and slipped her arm through his. "Come on, Anna's probably getting impatient."
Down in the hotel lobby, Anna was already waiting. Today she wore a red wool dress, her presence at full force. Seeing Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan walk out side by side, she arched a brow. "Miss Su, you don't look like a designer today."
"Like what, then?"
"Like someone about to conquer Paris."
Su Qingyuan was a little embarrassed by the words, but she didn't deny them. Because it was the truth—today, she really was going to conquer Paris. Not through connections, not through luck, but through her work.
The Cartier flagship store, 13 Rue de la Paix.
Su Qingyuan stood at the entrance, looking up at that black wrought-iron door, her heart pounding like a drum. For more than a century, this door had welcomed countless royals, celebrities, and artists—but it had never once welcomed the work of a Chinese independent designer. Today was the first time.
"Nervous?" Lu Shiyan stood behind her, his voice low.
"A little."
"No need." He reached out and gently took her hand. "Your work belongs here."
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath and pushed the door open.
The exhibition hall was on the second floor. As Su Qingyuan walked up, the first thing she saw was light. Not a harsh, dazzling light, but a soft, warm one—like the glow of the sky at daybreak. Anna said this was a color temperature Cartier had calibrated specifically for the "Light Traces" collection, so that every piece would be shown in its most perfect state.
Five pieces were displayed in five separate glass cases, each lit until it seemed to glow from within. Su Qingyuan walked slowly past, looking at them one by one. The first was the ring—a diamond of the palest pink, the hairline groove on the inner band casting a faint shadow under the light. The second was the necklace, its white diamonds set half-suspended in midair, as if they might drop at any moment. The third was the pair of drop earrings, two teardrop-shaped morganites, wholeness and brokenness set side by side. The fourth was a brooch, its theme "light trajectory"—platinum shaped into the path of a beam of light, setting out from a single tiny diamond and winding forward, like a road without end. The fifth was a bracelet, its chain made with a special weaving technique, like fibers of light twisted together strand by strand.
Su Qingyuan stood before the fifth piece, looking at the bracelet she had polished over countless days and nights, her eyes growing a little hot. But she didn't cry. Today wasn't a day for crying. Today was a day for smiling.
"Miss Su." Anna came over, two glasses of champagne in her hands. "The people from headquarters have arrived. They'd like to meet you."
Su Qingyuan took the champagne and turned. At the top of the stairs stood three people—two men and a woman, all around fifty, dressed with care, elegant in bearing. The man in the middle—Su Qingyuan recognized him—was Jean-Pierre, Cartier's creative director, the one who had said over the phone, "There's still hope for this industry."
Jean-Pierre walked over, smiling, and extended his hand. "Miss Su, at last we meet."
Su Qingyuan took his hand. "Jean-Pierre, thank you."
"Thank me for what? It was your work that made me say those words." Jean-Pierre let go and looked toward the pieces in their cases. "The 'Light Traces' collection is the first guest collection in my thirty years at Cartier that made me feel this brand still has a future."
The words scorched her. "Jean-Pierre, you're exaggerating."
"I'm not." Jean-Pierre looked at her seriously. "Do you know the greatest difference between 'Light Traces' and Cartier's past guest collections?"
"What?"
"In the past, the guest collections were designers imitating Cartier. 'Light Traces' is Cartier drawing closer to you." Jean-Pierre paused. "From today on, it isn't Cartier that has chosen you. It's you who have chosen Cartier."
The hand holding her champagne glass trembled faintly. Not from nerves, but from being understood. When she made "Light Traces," she had never thought, I want to make a Cartier-style piece. What she'd thought was, I want to make something only Su Qingyuan could make. And she had. Cartier had seen it. Jean-Pierre had seen it.
"Thank you, Jean-Pierre. I'll remember these words for the rest of my life."
"The rest of your life is too long." Jean-Pierre laughed. "Remembering them until our next collaboration is enough."
Su Qingyuan laughed too.
The launch event lasted two hours. Not many people came—they were Cartier's hand-picked guests: a few members of European royalty, top collectors, the most influential critics in the jewelry industry. Each piece had a cluster of people around it, discussing in low voices, peering through loupes, occasionally letting out a gasp of admiration.
Su Qingyuan stood in a corner, watching those people gather before her work, and felt something very strange—unreal. Those people were names she had once been able to see only in magazines. Now they stood before her work, examining through loupes the details she had polished with her own hands.
"What are you thinking about?" Lu Shiyan had come to her side without her noticing.
"I'm thinking—if this is a dream, I don't want to wake up."
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently pinched the back of her hand. "Did that hurt?"
"No."
"Then it's not a dream."
Su Qingyuan laughed and leaned against his shoulder, looking at the case in the distance with the crowd around it. Inside was that half-suspended necklace; under the light, the white diamonds looked like a frozen star. "Lu Shiyan, do you think I'll ever make something better than this?"
"Yes."
"How do you know?"
"Because you've never once stopped moving forward."
Su Qingyuan straightened off his shoulder and looked into his eyes. The light in them was brighter than the lights in the hall. She rose onto her toes and whispered into his ear: "Thank you, for bringing me here."
Lu Shiyan reached up and gently ruffled her hair. "I didn't bring you here. You walked here yourself. I just watched from the side."
After the launch, Anna suggested dinner at a restaurant by the Seine. Su Qingyuan agreed. Today she didn't want to be alone; she wanted to be among people, to talk, to laugh, to celebrate. Because she deserved it.
The restaurant was on the Left Bank of the Seine, small, its warm amber light spilling through the glass windows and falling on the river, shattering into a sheet of golden light. Su Qingyuan sat by the window, looking out at the Seine, a glass of red wine in her hand. Lu Shiyan sat beside her, his arm draped over the back of her chair, not drinking, just quietly keeping her company.
Anna sat across from them, chatting with Jean-Pierre in French about something. Su Qingyuan couldn't understand, but from their expressions she could tell they were talking about her. She didn't mind, because they were saying good things.
"Miss Su." Jean-Pierre suddenly turned to her, speaking in English. "I have a proposal."
"Please."
"Next autumn, Cartier wants to hold a global touring exhibition on the theme of 'Light.' Starting from Paris, on to New York, Tokyo, Shanghai, and finally back to Paris. I'd like to make the 'Light Traces' collection one of the core works of the tour. Would you be willing?"
Su Qingyuan set down her glass, her heart quickening. "I'm willing."
"Then it's settled." Jean-Pierre raised his glass. "Miss Su, congratulations. From today on, your light will illuminate more than just yourself."
Su Qingyuan raised her glass and touched it to his. The red wine swayed faintly in the glass, reflecting the warm amber light overhead.
After dinner, Anna and Jean-Pierre left first. Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan strolled slowly along the Seine, the river shimmering silver-grey under the streetlamps, the distant Eiffel Tower lit gold like an enormous lighthouse.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Tomorrow we'll go up to Montmartre."
"All right."
"And then?"
"And then I'll take you to see something."
"What thing?"
"A secret."
Su Qingyuan tilted her head to look at him. He kept his eyes ahead, his expression perfectly serious, but the curve at the corner of his mouth gave him away. She didn't press. Because when he said "secret," there was a light in his eyes—a light that made her feel that whatever it was, it would be good.
The next morning, Montmartre.
Su Qingyuan stood on the steps before the Sacré-Cœur, looking out over all of Paris. The weather was fine; sunlight broke through from behind the clouds and fell on the grey rooftops, on the hazy horizon in the distance, on her face. She drew a deep breath and let it out. The air was cold but fresh, as if it had rinsed her lungs clean.
"Let's go." Lu Shiyan took her hand. "The Wall of Love isn't far."
The Wall of Love was in a small square in the Montmartre garden, not large, with a blue background, covered in "I love you" written in white lettering—more than three hundred languages, more than three hundred "I love you"s. Su Qingyuan stood before the wall and read slowly, from the French "Je t'aime" to the Japanese "愛してる," from Arabic to Hebrew, from Sanskrit to Latin. Every language had its own handwriting—some neat, some scrawled, some like drawings—but all said the same thing.
She found the line in Chinese. "我愛你," three characters, in black traditional script, sitting quietly on the blue wall, like a secret long kept hidden.
Su Qingyuan looked at those three characters and suddenly felt the urge to cry. Not out of emotion, but because it had suddenly struck her—these three words, Lu Shiyan had never once said to her. Not because he didn't want to say them, but because he was waiting. Waiting for the right moment, the right occasion, waiting for her to say "I'm ready to hear it." Today, he had brought her here.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"You brought me here—is there something you want to say?"
Lu Shiyan was silent for a few seconds. Then he turned to face her and gently took both her hands in his. His palms were warm, completely enclosing her two hands. The sunlight fell on his face; his expression was calm, but Su Qingyuan could see what was hidden in the depths of his eyes—three words that had begun a very, very long time ago and had never once been spoken aloud.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"Je t'aime." In French.
Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat.
"愛してる." In Japanese.
Her eyes grew hot.
"Ich liebe Dich." In German.
Her tears fell.
"Ti amo." In Italian.
She laughed through her tears.
"我愛你." In Chinese.
Su Qingyuan rose onto her toes, wound both arms around his neck, and buried her face in the hollow of his throat. She cried—not the crying of sorrow, but the kind that comes after waiting a long time, finally arriving, never having to wait again.
"Lu Shiyan, you really are infuriating."
"Mm."
"Did you say 'I love you' in so many languages because you were afraid I wouldn't understand?"
"No. Because I was afraid I hadn't said it enough."
Su Qingyuan lifted her head from his shoulder and looked at him through tear-blurred eyes. "It's enough. One is enough. Save the rest to say slowly, later."
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently wiped the tears from her face. "All right. I'll say them slowly, later."
On the Wall of Love, that line of black "我愛你" rested quietly in the sunlight. Not far off, an elderly couple was also looking at the words on the wall. The old woman pointed at the line in Chinese and asked her husband, "What's this?" He shook his head. "I don't know, but it's lovely." The old woman laughed, took his arm, and leaned against his shoulder.
Watching them, Su Qingyuan suddenly felt that this was the future she wanted. Not something grand and sweeping, but something quiet—looking at a wall together, eating together, walking together along the Seine, growing old together.
"Lu Shiyan, will we be like them someday?"
"Like what?"
Su Qingyuan pointed at the old couple. The old woman was using her scarf to wipe a smudge of ice cream from the corner of her husband's mouth, the gesture as natural as if she'd done it countless times.
Lu Shiyan glanced over, then turned back to look at her. "We will."
"How do you know?"
"Because I won't let you get ice cream on the corner of your mouth."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a punch. "I wasn't talking about ice cream!"
"I know." Lu Shiyan caught the fist she'd swung at him. "What I mean is, we'll be together a long time. So long we won't be able to remember when it began."
Su Qingyuan looked at him and smiled. The sunlight fell on her face, into her eyes, on the smile at the corner of her mouth. She suddenly remembered a long time ago—a rainy night, when she'd sat on the floor of a hotel, believing her world had ended. Back then she hadn't known that that rain was the end of the storm, and also the beginning of dawn.
Now she knew.
Coming down from Montmartre, Su Qingyuan's legs ached a little. Lu Shiyan offered to carry her on his back; she wouldn't let him, so he took her hand and walked slowly. The setting sun stretched their two shadows long, long, overlapping into one—like a painting that would never fade.
"Lu Shiyan, you said you'd take me to see something. What is it?"
"We're going to see it now."
Lu Shiyan led her through a few small lanes to the entrance of a tiny shopfront. The frontage was old, made of wood, its paint peeling, but it had been wiped spotlessly clean. In the display window sat croissants of every kind—plain, almond, chocolate-filled, golden, glistening under the light.
"What's this?"
"The oldest croissant shop in Paris. It's been open eighty years." Lu Shiyan pushed open the door, and a bell rang with a tinkle. "You said you wanted croissants, so I looked it up—this one's the best."
Su Qingyuan stepped inside. The shop was tiny, with only three tables. Black-and-white photographs hung on the walls, showing the shop as it had looked decades ago. The owner was a white-haired old man, who said a word of welcome in French when he saw them come in.
Lu Shiyan ordered in French. Su Qingyuan couldn't understand, but from the old man's smile she could tell he was saying good things. The croissants came out still steaming, their golden, flaky crusts layered like petals. Su Qingyuan picked one up and took a bite; the crust shattered between her teeth, the scent of butter filling her mouth—hot, flaky, sweet.
"Good?"
"So good!"
Lu Shiyan watched her eyes shine as she ate, the corners of his mouth lifting. He picked up a croissant, took a bite, then frowned. "Too sweet."
"It's not sweet." Su Qingyuan took another big bite.
Lu Shiyan looked at her and smiled. He pushed the rest of his croissant in front of her. "If you like it, eat more."
Su Qingyuan didn't stand on ceremony; she finished both croissants. When she was done, she took a sip of coffee, leaned back in her chair, and let out a long breath. "Paris is wonderful."
"Is it Paris that's wonderful, or the croissants?"
"Both. But the best thing—"
She paused and looked at Lu Shiyan. "The best thing is that you brought me here."
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently brushed a flake of pastry from the corner of her mouth. "I'll bring you again sometime."
"When is 'sometime'?"
"Whenever you want to come."
Su Qingyuan smiled, her eyes curving. "Then it might be soon. Because I might want to come again tomorrow."
"Then tomorrow it is."
Su Qingyuan looked at his earnest expression, and her heart melted into a hopeless mess.
By the time they'd finished the croissants, the sky had darkened. They walked slowly back to the parking lot through Montmartre's little lanes, the streetlamps flickering on one by one, casting the cobbled path beneath their feet into a soft, hazy glow.
"Lu Shiyan, what shall we do tomorrow?"
"Tomorrow we'll go see an exhibition."
"What exhibition?"
"The Louvre. You once said that as a child you saw a photo of the Mona Lisa in a magazine, and wanted to see her with your own eyes."
Su Qingyuan stopped walking and looked at his back. He didn't turn around; he kept walking forward. But she knew he was waiting for her. She hurried to catch up, took his arm, and leaned against his shoulder. "Lu Shiyan, just how many surprises have you prepared for me?"
"Not many left."
"Really?"
"No."
Su Qingyuan laughed, her eyes curving into crescents. The Paris night wind was chilly, but she wasn't cold at all. Because leaning against his side was the warmest place there was.
Back at the hotel, Su Qingyuan stood at her door without going in right away. Lu Shiyan stood behind her, waiting.
"Lu Shiyan, today at the Wall of Love, you said 'I love you' in so many languages. There was one I didn't hear."
"Which one?"
"The Chinese one."
"I said it."
"Say it again."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze deep as the night. The corridor light fell on his face; his expression was very tender. "Su Qingyuan, I love you."
Not in French, not in Japanese, not in any language that needed translating. In Chinese, her mother tongue, the language she'd been able to hear and speak since the day she was born. These three words she had heard countless times—on television, in books, in other people's stories. But never once had they been spoken to her.
Today, he had made up for that.
Su Qingyuan's tears fell again. She rose onto her toes and kissed him.
The corridor was very quiet, only the sound of their two breaths. The light was bright and warm, falling over them like a beam that would never go out.
At length, Su Qingyuan let go, stepped back, and wiped her tears. "All right. Good night."
"Good night."
She turned, opened the door, went in, and shut it. She leaned against the door panel for a long while, listening to his footsteps recede down the corridor, then the door of the next room opened and closed again.
She curled her lips into a smile, walked to the window, and drew the curtains open. The Eiffel Tower was still there, its golden lights blinking on and off, as if telling her—good night.
Su Qingyuan opened the window a crack, and the cold air poured in, carrying the crisp scent unique to a Paris night. She breathed in deeply and let it out. Then she picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Good night. See you tomorrow."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "See you tomorrow."
Su Qingyuan set her phone on the nightstand and turned off the light. In the darkness, she closed her eyes. Many things spun through her mind—the "I love you" on the Wall of Love, the flaky crust of the croissant, the lights of the Seine, the gold of the Eiffel Tower, the look on his face when he said, "Su Qingyuan, I love you." These things turned for a long while, then slowly settled into quiet.
She rolled over, pulled the covers up to her chin, and sank into a deep sleep.
