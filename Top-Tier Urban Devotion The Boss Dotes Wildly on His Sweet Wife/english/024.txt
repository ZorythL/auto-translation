Light Traces Bloom, and an Old Acquaintance Returns
After the bid, Nancheng's business world went quiet for a few days.
That quiet wasn't normal. It was the kind of suffocating stillness that comes before a storm. Su Qingyuan could feel it. Lu Shiyan still came to pick her up every day, still ate with her, still handled documents on the sofa in her studio—but the frequency of his phone calls had gone up, and his tone had turned flatter than usual. So flat it was almost devoid of any emotion at all, which was, if anything, the dangerous signal.
Su Qingyuan didn't ask. She knew that if he wanted to tell her, he would. And if he didn't, asking would only burden him with an explanation he didn't want to give.
She poured her focus into the "Light Traces" collection.
The freedom Anna had given her exceeded all her expectations—that one line from the creative director, "Let her draw, don't put any limits on her," was like a key that unlocked a door she'd never walked through before. And behind that door, Su Qingyuan found an entirely new version of herself.
The "Light Traces" collection was different from "First Light." "First Light" had been an autobiography—the process of struggling in the dark, breaking out of the cocoon, meeting the light, becoming the light. The emotion ran thick; every detail carried the warmth of her own self. But "Light Traces" was a dialogue—her dialogue with Cartier, with a century of craftsmanship, with that narrow seam between "classic" and "innovation."
She kept the core language of "light," but shifted her expression from "intense" to "restrained."
The first piece was a ring. Its center stone was an extraordinarily rare, ultra-pale pink diamond, so close to colorless that the pink only surfaced—faint, almost imperceptible—under certain light. She crafted a minimalist platinum band, free of any superfluous ornament, carving only a single, razor-fine groove along the inner edge of the band, so that when light passed through, it cast a faint shadow—and that was where the name "Light Traces" came from.
The second was a necklace. Its center stone was a flawlessly cut white diamond, set in a way that was almost "incomplete"—the diamond wasn't fully encased in metal but left half-suspended, as if it might fall at any moment. Yet in the instant of that fall, light would refract out from the bottom of the diamond and settle on the wearer's collarbone.
The third was a pair of drop earrings—two teardrop-shaped morganites, designed asymmetrically. The left ear was a whole, full teardrop; the right was a "cross-section," sliced away by a third to reveal the internal structure. Wholeness and incompleteness side by side, like two ways of telling the same story.
Three pieces, three expressions of light. Not "dazzling" light, but light that simply "existed"—understated, unclamoring, yet you knew it was there.
Su Qingyuan sent the design drafts for all three pieces to Anna, and this time attached no written explanation at all.
Anna's reply came far faster than before—just one line: "Headquarters says we can start production early. Spring launch. Are you ready?"
Su Qingyuan answered: "I'm ready."
She didn't ask whether "headquarters was satisfied." Anna's tone had already given her the answer.
The osmanthus tree outside the window was completely bare now, but the sunlight was still lovely, falling through the stripped branches onto the worktable like gold scattered across the floor.
Su Qingyuan put the design drafts away and went to fetch a glass of water. As she reached the pantry, her phone buzzed.
An unknown number. She picked up, and on the other end was someone she'd never expected.
"Qingyuan, it's me. Lin Haoyu."
Su Qingyuan's fingers tightened slightly, but her voice didn't waver. "What is it?"
"I… I want to see you. There's something I need to say in person. I'm not asking you to take me back—I know that's impossible now. It's something else. Something important. About Su Yurou and Gu Changfeng."
Su Qingyuan was silent for a few seconds.
"Where do you want to meet?"
"You decide."
"The café outside the creative park. Half an hour."
"Okay. Thank you for being willing to see me."
Su Qingyuan hung up and stood in the pantry, looking at the sunlight outside, and took a deep breath.
Lin Haoyu. It had been a long time since that name had crossed her mind. Back in that hotel lobby, she'd told him, "I don't pick up the garbage I've thrown out a second time"—that was the last time she'd spoken to him. Later he'd secretly photographed her and Lu Shiyan's relationship announcement and sold the pictures to a gossip account; Lu Shiyan had sued him, and his company had fired him too. After that, she'd heard nothing more of him.
And now he'd appeared out of nowhere, saying it was "about Su Yurou and Gu Changfeng."
Su Qingyuan picked up her bag and walked out of the studio.
At the café, Lin Haoyu was already waiting.
The moment Su Qingyuan saw him, she froze for a second.
He'd changed a great deal. Not for the better—he'd aged. A man barely in his twenties, looking well into his thirties. His hair was a tangled, untended mess; he wore a pilled gray sweater; his eyes were sunken, his cheekbones jutting—the whole of him like a tree drained dry of every drop of water.
When he saw Su Qingyuan walk in, Lin Haoyu shot to his feet, too abruptly, knocking over the coffee cup in front of him. Dark brown liquid spilled across the table. He scrambled to wipe it up, flustered and clumsy to the point of utter wretchedness.
"It's fine," Su Qingyuan said to the server coming over. "Could you bring him another cup, please."
She sat down and looked at Lin Haoyu. He looked at her too, his gaze laced with too many tangled emotions—regret, envy, self-reproach, and a sliver of lingering attachment he couldn't even admit to himself.
"You've changed a lot," he said, his voice hoarse.
"So have you." Su Qingyuan didn't bother with pleasantries. "You said there was something important. What is it?"
Lin Haoyu drew a deep breath, pulled a USB drive from his pocket, set it on the table, and pushed it toward her.
"On this drive are some chat records between Gu Changfeng and Su Yurou. Not all of them. One time Su Yurou got drunk and logged into her social account on my phone, then forgot to log out. I copied the chat logs."
Su Qingyuan looked at the USB drive but didn't reach for it.
"Why are you giving me this?"
Lin Haoyu lowered his head, staring at the now-dried coffee stain on the table, his voice very low. "Because I want to do one right thing. In this life, I've done too many wrong things to you. I want to make up for it, just once—even if it can't ever be made up."
The café went quiet for a few seconds. Someone at the next table was murmuring, and the coffee machine hissed steam.
"Lin Haoyu, do you know what it means to give me these?" Su Qingyuan's voice was very calm. "It means a complete break with Su Yurou. Once she finds out, she won't let you off."
"She stopped being willing to let me off a long time ago." Lin Haoyu gave a bitter laugh. "After she got out, she came to me once. Not to reconcile—to threaten. She said if I told anyone about her and Gu Changfeng, she'd make sure I couldn't survive in Nancheng. I was scared then. But I'm not scared now. Because I've got nothing left to lose."
Su Qingyuan looked into his eyes and saw something there resembling "relief." Not letting go—accepting. Accepting that he'd lost, accepting that he'd been wrong, accepting that there was no going back.
"I'll take the drive." Su Qingyuan reached out, took the USB, and slipped it into her pocket. "But I don't promise I'll use it. And I don't promise that if I do, I'll cover for where it came from."
"That's all right." Lin Haoyu raised his head and looked at her. "Use it however you want. I'm not asking for your forgiveness, and I'm not asking to atone. I just… want to make myself feel like I'm not a thoroughly rotten person."
Su Qingyuan said nothing. She didn't know whether Lin Haoyu counted as a "rotten person." He'd done many wrong things—betrayal, harm, secret photos, selling her out. But he had also, back when she was at her poorest, bought her a few meals—even if those meals had later become bargaining chips, "I treated you so many times, how are you going to pay me back." Human nature was too complicated to be summed up in two words like "good person" or "rotten person."
"I'm leaving." Su Qingyuan stood up.
"Qingyuan." Lin Haoyu called out to stop her, his voice trembling slightly. "Is he good to you?"
Su Qingyuan stopped walking but didn't turn around.
"Very."
"That's good, then." Lin Haoyu's voice sank lower. "That's good, then."
Su Qingyuan walked out of the café, sunlight settling warm on her shoulders. She touched the USB drive in her pocket and didn't look back. Behind her, Lin Haoyu sat by the café window, watching her figure vanish through the gates of the creative park, then lowered his head and buried his face in both hands.
He didn't cry. He'd long since forfeited the right to.
Su Qingyuan returned to the studio and plugged the USB into her computer.
There were a lot of chat records—hundreds of pages, fragmentary, spanning from the period of Su Yurou's detention to after her release. Su Qingyuan skimmed through quickly, and the more she read, the more her heart turned cold.
Gu Changfeng wasn't "helping" Su Yurou. He was "using" her. Every single thing—from arranging Lawyer Fang, to instructing her to blur the lines of plagiarism with "reference," to teaching her how to go head-to-head with Su Qingyuan at the Biennale—was all Gu Changfeng pulling strings behind the scenes. Su Yurou was just one of his chess pieces, a piece he could discard at any moment.
But the most crucial thing in the chat logs wasn't any of that.
There was one exchange, dated three days before the Biennale's final verdict was announced.
Su Yurou: "If Su Qingyuan takes the gold, what do we do?"
Gu Changfeng: "She won't."
Su Yurou: "How can you be so sure?"
Gu Changfeng: "I had someone put a word in with the judging committee. Not to make her lose—to make her win less cleanly. All it takes is a hint of a rumor that 'Lu Shiyan applied pressure,' and her gold medal gets discounted. People won't remember how good her work was; they'll only remember that 'she got there on a man.'"
Su Yurou: "And if it gets traced back to you?"
Gu Changfeng: "It won't. The person who passed the word has already left the country."
Su Qingyuan stared at this exchange, her fingers slowly tightening.
During the Biennale, there had indeed been a rumor that "Lu Shiyan pressured the judging committee." At the time she'd assumed Su Yurou had made it up herself—she'd never imagined Gu Changfeng was behind it. He wasn't helping Su Yurou win; he was making sure she couldn't win. Whether or not Su Qingyuan took the prize, he meant to splash a layer of filth across her trophy.
Su Qingyuan leaned back in her chair, gazing at the ceiling, and let out a long breath.
This man was darker than she'd imagined.
She picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: [Lin Haoyu just came to see me and gave me this. Sending you a screenshot.]
She sent over the screenshot of that key exchange.
Lu Shiyan's reply came fast—not text, but a call.
"Gu Changfeng started laying his groundwork even earlier than you or I thought," he said, his voice deep. "He had his fallback ready before the Biennale. A plan for winning, a plan for losing. He never lets himself be in a position with 'no cards left to play.'"
"So what do we do?" Su Qingyuan asked.
"Nothing for now." Lu Shiyan's tone returned to its usual steadiness. "These chat records can't be used directly as evidence in court—the source is illegitimate, and Su Yurou's account isn't Lin Haoyu's. But they can be used elsewhere."
"Where?"
"Public opinion," Lu Shiyan said. "What Gu Changfeng fears most isn't the law—it's public opinion. His Dingfeng Group is preparing for an IPO right now. If news that he's linked to a plagiarism case breaks at exactly this juncture, investors will question his integrity. The listing could be shelved."
Su Qingyuan fell silent. She didn't really understand IPOs and investors and all that, but she could hear it—Lu Shiyan already had a plan.
"When are you planning to use these?"
"No rush." A trace of cold edged into Lu Shiyan's voice. "We wait for him to make the first move. Whoever moves first loses."
Su Qingyuan remembered something he'd said before—the rules of the business world weren't like a children's fight, where whoever throws the first punch is in the wrong.
"All right. I'll keep the drive safe."
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm?"
"When Lin Haoyu gave you the drive, did he attach any conditions?"
"No. He said he wanted to do one right thing."
The other end was silent for a moment. Then Lu Shiyan said something that took Su Qingyuan by surprise: "He may genuinely want to change. But whether he changes is his business, and whether you forgive him is yours. Whatever you choose, I'm on your side."
Su Qingyuan held the phone, and a wave of warmth rose in her chest. He wasn't the kind of person who'd say "you should forgive him" or "you shouldn't forgive him." He handed the choice over to her, completely and entirely, and then said—whatever you choose, I'm on your side.
That was the source of the feeling of being held, steady and secure.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Got it. Are you picking me up tonight?"
"I am. Wait for me."
Su Qingyuan hung up, unplugged the USB drive, and locked it in a drawer. Then she walked to the window and pushed it open. Cold air rushed in, dispelling the heavy, stagnant air of the studio. In the distance, the cloud layer over Nancheng's skyline hung thick and grayish-white, as if it were about to snow.
She thought of what she'd said on the mountaintop the night before—"I just want to watch the first snow with you."
Maybe it would be soon.
That evening, when Lu Shiyan came to pick her up, he didn't look well.
Not unwell toward her—it was the kind of weariness that comes right after handling a thorny matter. After getting in the car, Su Qingyuan didn't rush to ask. Instead, she took a thermos out of her bag and handed it to him.
"What's this?"
"Red date and ginger tea. It's cold out—drink something warm."
Lu Shiyan took it, unscrewed the lid, and drank a mouthful. The bite of the ginger and the sweetness of the red dates melted on the tip of his tongue, warming a path from his throat all the way down to his stomach. He turned his head to glance at her, and the weariness in his eyes was replaced by something tender.
"When did you make this?"
"This afternoon. There were tea bags in the studio; I brewed it." Su Qingyuan's tone was offhand, as though she were speaking of something trivial. "You don't drink ginger tea, but your voice was a little hoarse today—you might have caught a chill. Ginger tea drives off the cold."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, and suddenly he smiled. Not the usual restrained, reined-in faint smile, but the kind that wells up from the bottom of the heart after someone has remembered a detail about you—a smile that can't be suppressed no matter how hard you try.
"Su Qingyuan, you're becoming more and more like me."
"Like you how?"
"Like the things I'd do."
Su Qingyuan paused, then understood his meaning—remembering every word the other person said, noticing every detail about them, and then taking care of things before they'd even spoken. Wasn't that exactly what he'd been doing for her all along?
"You rub off on people you're close to," she said, turning her face away, pretending to look out the window.
"You're calling me the good influence again."
"You are."
Lu Shiyan smiled, started the car, and pulled out of the creative park.
After they'd driven for a stretch, Su Qingyuan suddenly said, "Lu Shiyan, what do you think Gu Changfeng will do next?"
Lu Shiyan tapped his fingers lightly twice on the steering wheel—a habit of his when he was thinking.
"He'll go after your weakest point."
"My weakest point?"
"Not you yourself." Lu Shiyan turned his head to glance at her. "Your supply chain. Orders for your 'First Light' collection have already passed eighty million, supported by three suppliers. If one of them runs into trouble, delivery gets delayed. Delays mean unhappy clients, unhappy clients mean damage to the brand. And brand damage means the Cartier partnership takes a hit too."
Su Qingyuan's heart sank.
"You said before that one supplier's boss has a private connection to Gu Changfeng. That one?"
"Mm. Hengsheng Jewelry, the boss's surname is Jiang. He and Gu Changfeng have known each other for over twenty years." Lu Shiyan's tone was very calm, as though discussing a problem he'd already solved. "But I've already prepared a backup plan. If Hengsheng runs into trouble, the alternate supplier can step in within twenty-four hours. Capacity, craftsmanship, delivery cycle—all benchmarked against the originals. There won't be any gap."
Su Qingyuan looked at his profile, and that feeling of being held, steady and secure, surged up in her again.
"When did you put together the backup plan?"
"The day you signed the contract with Zhou Wanyin."
"That day again?" Su Qingyuan couldn't help laughing. "Have you made the day I signed that contract into your start button?"
The corner of Lu Shiyan's mouth lifted slightly. "More or less."
The car stopped outside a restaurant. Not a hotpot place, not a Hunan restaurant, but a Japanese place Su Qingyuan had never seen before, with a very small storefront. Pushing the door open, inside there was only a single long counter, behind which stood a middle-aged man in a white chef's coat. Seeing Lu Shiyan, he gave a slight bow.
"Mr. Lu, your seats are ready."
Lu Shiyan nodded and led Su Qingyuan to sit at the counter. There was no menu; the chef took one look at Lu Shiyan and began quietly preparing the ingredients.
"This is my private haunt," Lu Shiyan said in a low voice. "In all of Nancheng, you're the only person I've ever brought here."
Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat. A private haunt. The only person he'd ever brought.
The first course was sashimi—the medium-fatty belly of bluefin tuna, melting the moment it touched her tongue, the rich fat-aroma bursting on the tip of it. Su Qingyuan's eyes lit up, but she forced herself to hold back, not wanting to seem too unworldly. Watching her work so hard to stay composed, the smile in Lu Shiyan's eyes deepened.
"Is it good?"
"It's all right." Su Qingyuan picked up a second piece, twice as fast as the first.
"All right?"
"Really, it's all right." She picked up a third piece.
Lu Shiyan didn't call out her bluff. He lifted his cup and slowly drank a mouthful of sake. In the lamplight, the lines of his profile were cold and gentle all at once, like a painting composed with great care.
As Su Qingyuan ate, she suddenly set down her chopsticks and looked at him.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Have you ever thought about—if one day everything were over—no Gu Changfeng, no Su Yurou, no bids, no lawsuits—what would you want to do?"
Lu Shiyan set down his cup and gave it serious thought.
"Take you to see the aurora."
Su Qingyuan went still.
"The aurora?"
"You said that when you were little, you saw a photo of the aurora in a book, and you thought it was the most beautiful light in the world." Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze soft as moonlight. "You said, 'If one day I could see the aurora with my own eyes, I'd have no regrets in this life.'"
Su Qingyuan opened her mouth, wanting to say something, but her throat felt as though something were lodged in it.
How long ago had she said that? Ten years? Fifteen? She didn't even remember saying it. But he did. He didn't just remember—he'd placed it into a plan for the future.
"Lu Shiyan, just how many of my memories have you stolen?"
"Not stolen." He poured her a cup of sake. "Collected. Every word you've ever said, I've collected it."
Su Qingyuan lifted her cup and took a big swallow, using the burn of the sake to press down the heat rising in her eyes.
"And you?" she asked. "Is there something you've always wanted to do but never have?"
Lu Shiyan looked at her and stayed silent for a long while.
Then he said, "Yes."
"What?"
"Wait."
"Wait for what?"
"Wait for you."
At last, Su Qingyuan's tears fell.
Not tears of sorrow, but the kind that—when someone loves you in the quietest, most patient, most certain way, loves you so much that you forget the whole world and remember only that single word, "wait"—come welling up from the bottom of the heart, scalding, unstoppable.
"Lu Shiyan, you really are a detestable man."
"Mm."
"You make me cry every single time."
"Mm."
"Can you stop waiting for me? I'm already here."
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently wiped the tears from her face.
"I know you're here." His voice was very low, very soft, like a night wind passing through a pine forest. "But I'll still wait. Wait for when you're tired and lean on my shoulder, wait for when you want to talk and I'm listening, wait for when you look back—and I'm always there."
The chef quietly set the next course on the counter, then withdrew soundlessly to the kitchen at the back.
In the whole space, only the two of them remained.
Su Qingyuan sniffled, swiped roughly at her face with her sleeve, lifted her cup, and clinked it against his.
"Then it's settled. The aurora."
"It's settled."
"No backing out."
"No backing out."
The two of them touched cups, the sake rippling faintly in the glasses, reflecting the warm amber light overhead.
Outside the window, Nancheng's night wind howled past.
The forecast said the temperature would drop tonight.
But Su Qingyuan knew—her winter would not be cold.
