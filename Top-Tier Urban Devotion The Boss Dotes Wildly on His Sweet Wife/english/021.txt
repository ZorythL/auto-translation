Chapter 21 — Cartier's Olive Branch, Gu Changfeng's Probe
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626223.html

Chen Xiaoman's photos had been sitting in the drawer for three days.
Su Qingyuan hadn't told a soul—not even Lu Shiyan. It wasn't that she meant to keep it from him; she just needed to think it through first—when, and in what way, these photos should be brought into the light. A trump card stays a trump card precisely because, while it's still in your hand, no one knows it exists.
Over those three days, her life had been packed wall to wall with work.
Orders for the "First Light" series had already broken eighty million, and word came from the supply chain that the first batch of finished pieces would be delivered in five days. Zhou Wanyin could barely contain her grin over the phone, saying it was the fiercest debut she'd seen from a newcomer in her twenty years in the business.
As for Cartier, Anna had stayed silent.
Su Qingyuan didn't push. She knew that for a brand like Cartier, every decision had to pass through layer upon layer of approval. A forty-sixty split was probably a first for them, too. She was waiting—but not passively. During that wait, she finished the first design draft for her bespoke line.
It was a ring. The client was a woman in her thirties who wanted to give it to her mother, a cancer survivor in recovery. The theme was "rebirth." Su Qingyuan used a single small green tourmaline, ringed with a circle of tiny diamond chips—like the glow that surrounds a new sprout breaking through the soil.
The client cried when she saw the design draft. Su Qingyuan didn't cry, but the moment she hit send, her hand trembled ever so slightly.
This was what design meant to her. Not the money, not the fame—it was these tears. These scalding tears, shed because someone had finally been understood, seen, treasured.
On the morning of the fourth day, Anna's call finally came.
"Ms. Su, headquarters has agreed."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, her heart skipping half a beat, but her voice stayed steady. "Forty-sixty?"
"Forty-sixty." There was a trace of rueful laughter in Anna's tone. "Headquarters says it's the first time they've ever made an exception for a Chinese designer. They hope you won't make them regret it."
"I won't."
"I'll have the contract sent to your inbox. If you have no objections, we'll sign next Wednesday and launch the capsule collection at the same time. No restrictions on theme, no restrictions on timeline—but we'd like it to release in the spring of next year."
"All right. I'll wait for your contract."
Hanging up, Su Qingyuan sat at her worktable, gazing out the window at that bare osmanthus tree, and let out a long, slow breath.
Cartier. Guest artist. A forty-sixty split.
Three months ago, those three phrases strung together had been something she wouldn't have dared to dream of, even in her dreams.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan:
[Congratulations. Anna told me about the Cartier deal.]
Su Qingyuan: [How did she put it to you?]
Lu Shiyan: [She said: "Your girlfriend is the hardest designer I've ever had to negotiate with."]
Su Qingyuan's lips curved. [And then?]
Lu Shiyan: [And then I said: "Thank you. I think so too."]
Su Qingyuan laughed out loud. She could just picture the look on Anna's face hearing that—probably much like her own face that day in the elevator, listening to the System relay Lu Shiyan's inner monologue.
Su Qingyuan: [Dinner's on me tonight. A real treat—the kind where I don't let you pick up the tab.]
Lu Shiyan: [All right. I'll be waiting for you to pay.]
She set down her phone and started tidying up her worktable. Today she'd decided to give herself a half-day off—she'd hardly rested since the Biennale. Her body wasn't tired, but her heart needed room to breathe.
Halfway through tidying, a knock came at the door.
Not Lu Shiyan—he had a key and never knocked.
Su Qingyuan went over and opened it. A man in his fifties stood outside.
A custom dark-gray suit, hair gone gray but his bearing vigorous and sharp, with a kind of arrogance in his brow that came from long years at the top. His features were deeply cut—he must have been handsome in his youth—but the years had carved too many calculating lines into his face, turning that handsomeness into a sharpness that set people on edge.
Su Qingyuan recognized him at a glance.
Gu Changfeng.
She'd seen him in Chen Xiaoman's photos, but in person he carried far more menace. It wasn't an overt ferocity—it was a restrained chill, coiling around you soundlessly, like a snake.
"Ms. Su, forgive the intrusion." Gu Changfeng smiled, a perfectly courteous smile, but there was no warmth behind his eyes. "I'm Gu Changfeng. I wonder if you've heard of me?"
Su Qingyuan leaned against the doorframe, with no intention of inviting him in, her tone flat. "Mr. Gu, I've heard the name. What brings you to me?"
Clearly Gu Changfeng hadn't expected this attitude from her—not warm, not flustered, not even curious. He paused, his smile unchanged. "Ms. Su won't invite me in to sit a while?"
"The studio's small; it's hardly suitable for hosting. There's a coffee shop across the way with a nice atmosphere. If you don't mind, Mr. Gu, we can talk there."
Gu Changfeng looked at her, a flicker of appraisal entering his gaze. After a few seconds, he nodded. "All right."
At the coffee shop, Su Qingyuan ordered an Americano, and Gu Changfeng asked for a Longjing—the fact that a coffee shop served Longjing said plenty about how unusual the place was.
The two of them sat facing each other across a small round table.
Gu Changfeng raised his Longjing, took a slow sip, set the cup down, and got straight to the point. "Ms. Su, I've come today to apologize."
Su Qingyuan said nothing, waiting for him to go on.
"I did help Su Yurou with a few things." Gu Changfeng's tone was so candid it didn't sound like a lie. "But that was out of personal connections. Her mother, Liu Xiulan, and I are distant relatives. When she came to me for help, it was awkward to refuse. But I never imagined she'd use the resources I provided to steal your techniques and plagiarize your work."
Su Qingyuan lifted her Americano, took an unhurried sip, set it down, and looked Gu Changfeng in the eye.
"Mr. Gu, you didn't come here today just to apologize, did you?"
Gu Changfeng smiled, and this time the smile was a shade more genuine than when he'd walked in—but only a shade.
"Ms. Su is a sharp one. Then I'll speak plainly. I want to work with you."
Su Qingyuan arched an eyebrow.
"I know your deal with Cartier is already settled." Gu Changfeng's tone carried an air of "I know everything." "But Cartier is an international brand, and their market channels in this country are limited. The Dingfeng Group has a jewelry company under its umbrella—not as high-end as Cartier, but with strong penetration in the country's second- and third-tier cities. If you're willing to work with us, I can offer you better terms than Cartier—a fifty-fifty split, or even forty-sixty, with sixty going to you and forty to me."
Su Qingyuan looked at him, and suddenly smiled.
"Mr. Gu, you just said you came to apologize. But what you've said since doesn't sound like an apology. It sounds like a poaching pitch."
Gu Changfeng wasn't thrown by the remark—if anything, his smile deepened. "Ms. Su, in business, apology and poaching have never been separate things. I admit I misjudged when I helped Su Yurou before, but now I want to set that mistake right. Working with you is correcting a mistake for me, and one more path forward for you. A win-win—why not?"
Su Qingyuan set down her coffee cup and leaned back slightly against her chair.
"Mr. Gu, let me ask you something."
"Please."
"When you helped Su Yurou, did you know her work was stolen?"
Gu Changfeng's smile froze for an instant.
"Did you know whose work she'd stolen?"
His smile slowly retreated.
Su Qingyuan didn't wait for his answer and pressed on. "You knew. Because you had Lawyer Fang teach her how to blur the lines with 'taking inspiration,' how to skirt the edges and dodge legal risk. You didn't 'misjudge'—you knew full well she was stealing, and you helped her steal."
The coffee shop fell quiet for a moment. The customers at the next table seemed to sense the tension and quietly moved to other seats.
Gu Changfeng looked at Su Qingyuan, his gaze turning fathomless.
"Ms. Su, some things lose all meaning once they're said out loud."
"I never intended to have anything meaningful with you." Su Qingyuan stood, took a fifty-yuan note from her wallet, and laid it on the table. "This coffee's on me. Mr. Gu, you came to me looking to 'work together,' not because I'm so very talented, but because Cartier chose me, and you don't want a competitor to monopolize the advantage. When you helped Su Yurou, you never imagined she'd lose this badly. Now you want to swap in a new pawn—sorry, but I'm no one's pawn."
She picked up her bag and turned to leave.
"Ms. Su." Gu Changfeng's voice came from behind her, not loud, but edged with cold. "You may not know this yet, but anything Lu Shiyan can give you, I can give you just the same. And anything Lu Shiyan can't give you, I can give you too. You're clever—you should know that in business, one more friend is better than one more enemy."
Su Qingyuan stopped, but didn't turn around.
"Mr. Gu, you're right—one more friend is better than one more enemy. But a man like you will never be my friend, or anyone's friend. All you have are pawns and discarded pieces."
She walked out of the coffee shop. The autumn sunlight fell warm across her shoulders.
She didn't look back, but she knew Gu Changfeng was surely watching her go.
A man like that had probably never been called out to his face by a girl in her twenties. He might be angry—or he might, on the contrary, want her all the more. Not to have her as a person, but to have her as a "bargaining chip." Her refusal had only made her more valuable in his eyes.
At that thought, Su Qingyuan couldn't help but let out a soft laugh.
Ridiculous.
These men at the top, forever treating everyone as goods to be priced.
But she wasn't.
She never had been.
That evening, Lu Shiyan came to pick her up.
Once she was in the car, Su Qingyuan told him everything about Gu Changfeng's visit, start to finish. When Lu Shiyan had heard it all, his fingers tapped twice lightly against the steering wheel—his habitual gesture when thinking.
"You turned him down without hesitation," he said, his tone betraying nothing.
"What else? Was I supposed to work with him?"
"No. You were right to refuse." Lu Shiyan glanced at her sideways. "But a man like Gu Changfeng won't let it rest after being turned down. He'll come back, just in a different way."
Su Qingyuan leaned back against her seat, watching the neon flow past the window. "If he comes back, I'll refuse again. He can't very well kidnap me."
Lu Shiyan said nothing, but Su Qingyuan noticed his fingers tighten a fraction on the wheel.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Are you worried about me?"
"It's not worry." Lu Shiyan's voice dropped lower. "I'm thinking about how to make sure he never dares come looking for you again."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. The shifting light from beyond the window played across his face. His expression was calm, but there was a cold glint deep in his eyes—the kind she'd seen in business news, the look Lu Shiyan wore when facing a rival.
"What do you want to do?" she asked.
"Nothing." His tone warmed back to its usual temperature. "At least, nothing for now. He came today to probe—to test your attitude, and to test my reaction. If I struck back immediately, I'd be playing right into his hands. He wants to see whether I'd lose my head over you."
Su Qingyuan nodded. She understood now. When Gu Changfeng came today, the apology was fake, the poaching was fake—the probing was the real point. He wanted to know whether Su Qingyuan was Lu Shiyan's soft spot, and if she was, just how soft that soft spot was.
"So what's your reaction right now?" she asked.
Lu Shiyan reached out and took the hand she'd rested on her knee, lacing his fingers through hers.
"My reaction is—you're my soft spot, but not my weakness."
Su Qingyuan looked at their joined hands, and somewhere inside her was scalded by those words.
Soft spot and weakness. They sounded like synonyms, but from his mouth they took on entirely different meanings. A soft spot meant caring; a weakness meant making mistakes because you cared. He cared about her, but he wouldn't lose his judgment over her.
This was Lu Shiyan.
Forever clear-headed, forever restrained, forever making her feel that—a sense of security wasn't about how much he gave her, but about the fact that he never lost himself.
The car pulled up outside a restaurant. Not some upscale place—just an ordinary Hunan eatery, red lanterns hanging at the door, an ad for "signature chopped-chili fish head" pasted across the glass window.
Su Qingyuan took a look, then turned to Lu Shiyan. "Since when do you eat spicy food?"
"Since the last time you said you wanted chopped-chili fish head." Lu Shiyan undid his seatbelt and pushed open the door to get out. "I looked it up—this is the most authentic place in Nancheng."
Sitting in the passenger seat, Su Qingyuan watched his retreating figure, and suddenly smiled.
She'd mentioned wanting chopped-chili fish head about two weeks ago. It had been an offhand remark; she'd forgotten it the moment she said it. But he'd remembered. Not only had he remembered—he'd found the most authentic Hunan restaurant in Nancheng and made the trip specially to bring her here.
She pushed open the door, got out, and quickened her steps to catch up to him, slipping her arm through his quite naturally.
Lu Shiyan glanced down at her and said nothing, but the corner of his mouth lifted ever so slightly.
The private room in the Hunan restaurant was small but very clean. When the chopped-chili fish head arrived, its red oil bubbling, Su Qingyuan's eyes lit up.
"Is it good?" Lu Shiyan watched her.
Su Qingyuan picked up a piece of fish, put it in her mouth, and hissed in a breath at the heat—but her eyes shone as though someone had switched on a light behind them. "It's delicious! Hurry, try it."
Lu Shiyan picked up his chopsticks, took a small piece, and ate it slowly. His expression didn't change, but Su Qingyuan noticed the tips of his ears had gone faintly red—from the heat of the chili.
"Can't you handle spicy food?" she asked.
"I can."
"Then why are your ears red?"
"It's warm in here."
Su Qingyuan bit back a laugh and didn't call his bluff. She poured him a glass of ice water and pushed it in front of him. He glanced at her, picked it up, and took a big gulp, and the red at the tips of his ears slowly faded.
The two of them ate and chatted. Nothing profound—just little everyday things: the studio's new orders, the terms of the Cartier contract, a project Lu Group had been negotiating lately. In the past, Su Qingyuan would have thought "this has nothing to do with me," but now she listened intently, because this was his life. Just as he listened intently to the story behind every one of her pieces.
After the meal, Su Qingyuan went to settle the bill of her own accord. This time, Lu Shiyan didn't fight her for it. He leaned against the counter and watched her take her credit card from her wallet and hand it to the cashier, a contented light in his eyes—not because she'd paid, but because she'd finally stopped "fighting" him over the tab, and had simply, naturally, taken the task into her own hands.
"All paid." Su Qingyuan tucked the card back into her wallet and turned to him. "Let's go."
The two of them walked out of the Hunan restaurant, the night wind sweeping into their faces. The late-autumn wind had grown cold, and Su Qingyuan hunched her shoulders. Lu Shiyan immediately took off his coat and draped it over her shoulders. This time she didn't say "no need"—she gathered the coat closer, tilted her head to glance at him, and smiled.
"What is it?" he asked.
"Nothing." She buried her face in the coat's collar and said in a muffled voice, "It just feels like I've known you for a very long time."
Lu Shiyan reached out, slipped an arm around her shoulders, and drew her closer to his side.
"Not long," he said, his voice very low. "But there's a whole lifetime ahead of us."
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder, watching the streetlights stretch their two shadows long, so long, overlapping into one—like a painting that would never fade.
A whole lifetime ahead.
Those words sounded so lovely.
Back at the foot of her apartment building, Su Qingyuan didn't go up right away. She stood at the entrance and turned to look at Lu Shiyan.
"Gu Changfeng said something today that I keep turning over in my mind."
"What did he say?"
"He said, 'Anything Lu Shiyan can give you, I can give you just the same. And anything Lu Shiyan can't give you, I can give you too.'" Su Qingyuan looked into his eyes. "And I wanted to tell him right then—what you give me isn't money, isn't resources, isn't opportunities. What you give me is the feeling of being seen. All my life, no one ever truly saw me. You're the first."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, saying nothing. The light of the streetlamp fell into his eyes, like shattered stars.
"Gu Changfeng can't give me this." Su Qingyuan's voice was soft, but earnest. "Because he doesn't see me—he only sees my value. But you see me—not Su Qingyuan the designer, not Su Qingyuan, Lu Shiyan's girlfriend, just Su Qingyuan."
The night wind went still.
Lu Shiyan reached out and gently cupped her face, his thumb tracing slowly across her cheekbone, as though sketching a painting he could never look at enough.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"The first time I saw you wasn't before that rainy night."
Su Qingyuan went blank. "What?"
"Three months ago, at the Nancheng Design Association's annual gala. You didn't have an entry pass; you stood in the corridor outside the venue, watching the exhibition inside through the glass doors." Lu Shiyan's voice was very, very low, like he was telling a secret he'd kept hidden for a long time. "I was standing inside the doors at the time, and I saw you. You were wearing a sweater washed pale with age, holding a sketchbook in your hands, drawing. You drew with such focus, completely unaware that someone inside the glass doors was watching you."
Su Qingyuan's eyes went wide. She didn't remember that scene. Three months ago, she had indeed gone to that gala—not as an invited guest, but to deliver a document to a designer client. While she stood in the corridor waiting for the client to come out, she really had watched the exhibition inside through the glass doors, and she really had sketched a few strokes in her sketchbook.
But she didn't remember anyone inside watching her.
"How long did you watch me?" she asked.
"A long time." Lu Shiyan's thumb stilled on her cheekbone. "Long enough that my assistant thought something was wrong with me. A man who never attended social functions, standing before a glass door, watching for ten minutes."
A sudden sting rose in Su Qingyuan's nose.
"Then why didn't you come find me back then?"
"Because of your eyes." Lu Shiyan's voice dropped even lower. "There was something in your eyes—not longing, not envy, but 'one day, I'll be standing in there too.' That look was too pure. I didn't want to disturb it."
Su Qingyuan's tears finally fell.
So before the rainy night, they had already met.
So when he'd said "tell me once you're certain," it hadn't been politeness—he really had waited for her a long, long time.
From the glass door three months ago, to the headlights of that rainy night, to the helping hand in the hotel lobby, to the silent company at the salon, to the key to the studio, to the coffee on the eighty-eighth floor, to the awards ceremony in Paris, to tonight's chopped-chili fish head—
He had been waiting all along.
Waiting for her to walk out, waiting for her to stand tall, waiting for her to turn around and see that there was someone who, from a very, very long time ago, had already been watching her.
"Lu Shiyan, you really are too much."
"Mm."
"Why didn't you tell me sooner?"
"Because if I'd told you sooner, you'd have thought I was fishing for credit."
Su Qingyuan laughed through her tears. She rose onto her tiptoes, wound both arms around his neck, and buried her face in the crook of his neck.
"Lu Shiyan, thank you for waiting for me."
Lu Shiyan tightened his arms and gathered her whole self into his embrace.
"You're welcome." His voice was low and tender against her ear. "Now that the waiting's over, it was all worth it."
Beneath the streetlight, the shadows of the two of them, locked in an embrace, fell across the ground like one complete circle.
With no gap.
