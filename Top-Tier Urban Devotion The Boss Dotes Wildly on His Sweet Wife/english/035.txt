Chapter 35 — Countdown to the Joint Exhibition, and the Storm Returns
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626237.html

Su Qingyuan hadn't expected the news of the joint exhibition to draw such a wave of attention.
The night Qu Ya Studio's official account posted the exhibition poster, the reshares blew past fifty thousand. The comment section was a uniform chorus of anticipation and curiosity — "A crossover exhibition of photography and jewelry? I've never seen that before!" "Qu Ya's work has always had this 'rootedness' to it; pairing it with Su Qingyuan's 'Roots' series is honestly a match made in heaven." "Su Qingyuan's first big move since coming home — can't wait!" There were a handful of skeptical voices too — "A newcomer who just barely surfaced, and she gets to stand shoulder to shoulder with Qu Ya in a joint show? On what grounds?" "Is Qu Ya short on cash or something, collaborating with just anybody?" But those voices were quickly drowned out by the flood of supportive comments.
Su Qingyuan didn't reply to the doubters. She didn't need to — because a month from now, the work would speak for her.
The venue for the joint exhibition was set in the main hall of the Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art: five hundred square meters, eight-meter ceilings, a floor-to-ceiling glass curtain wall facing Nancheng's skyline. The first time Su Qingyuan walked into the space, she was struck silent by its vastness and its quiet. Her work would stand here, alongside Qu Ya's work, before hundreds of invited guests, before everyone who came to see the show.
"Nervous?" Qu Ya stood beside her, the floor plan in her hands.
"A little," Su Qingyuan admitted honestly. "Before, I was always at other people's shows, looking at other people's work. This time it's my own show. Mine and yours."
"Then be nervous. Nerves mean you care." Qu Ya patted her shoulder. "Come on, let's go figure out exactly how to lay it out."
For the next two weeks, Su Qingyuan's life was crammed full of the exhibition.
Up at six every morning, at the studio by seven, handling the follow-up on the "Light Traces" collection — Cartier had finalized the route for the global touring exhibition: after Paris came New York, Tokyo, Shanghai, and finally back to Paris. She had to provide a different display scheme for each stop, and the workload was no small thing. From nine in the morning until four in the afternoon was Qu Ya's time. Venue measurements, booth design, lighting calibration, traffic-flow planning — every single item required her involvement in the decisions. In the evening she'd return to her own studio and keep refining the last two pieces of the "Roots" series — the fourth piece was entering its finishing stage, the fifth still in rough grinding. At night Lu Shiyan would come to pick her up, the two of them would have dinner together, and she'd lean against his shoulder, sometimes falling asleep mid-sentence.
"You're wearing yourself out like this," Lu Shiyan said more than once.
"I'm not tired," Su Qingyuan answered every time. "Doing what you love doesn't tire you out."
Lu Shiyan looked at the faint shadows under her eyes and didn't press her again. He simply brought a different dinner every day — soup one day, plain congee with little side dishes the next, her favorite malatang the day after. He knew he couldn't talk her out of it, but he could use a bowl of hot soup, a warm meal, to let her feel a little bit of warmth at the end of an exhausting day.
Ten days before the exhibition opened, Su Qingyuan got an unexpected phone call.
It was the secretary-general of the Nancheng Design Association, surnamed Bai, a man in his fifties with an extraordinarily wide network and an extraordinarily heavy voice in the industry. Secretary-General Bai's tone was courteous but distant. "Miss Su, I hear you're putting on a joint exhibition with Teacher Qu Ya? Would you mind coming by the association for a chat? There are a few things I'd like to discuss with you."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, a thousand thoughts turning over in her mind. She'd had little contact with the Design Association before, but she'd heard Secretary-General Bai's name — famously "hard to get in to see, hard to get a smile out of." For him to call and request a meeting himself was unheard of.
"Of course, Secretary-General Bai. When would be convenient for you?"
"Tomorrow afternoon at three, at the association office."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan sent Qu Ya a message: "Secretary-General Bai wants me to come to the association tomorrow. Did he say what it's about?"
Qu Ya's reply came quickly: "He didn't say? I got one too. Tomorrow afternoon at three, the association office. Looks like it's something big."
Su Qingyuan stared at the message, a not-so-good premonition settling in. Not fear — instinct. For Secretary-General Bai to summon both her and Qu Ya at once, it couldn't be good news.
The next afternoon, Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya met downstairs at the association. Qu Ya wore a black wool coat, her expression considerably more serious than usual. "Any guesses what this is about?"
"No idea. But it won't be bad news, and it won't be good news either," Su Qingyuan said plainly. Qu Ya glanced at her. "Come on. We'll know once we're inside."
Secretary-General Bai's office was on the seventh floor — not large, but imposing. Photographs of every past chairman of the Design Association hung on the wall, and the bookshelves were crammed with trophies and certificates of every kind. Secretary-General Bai sat behind his desk, his grizzled hair combed meticulously, wearing a dark gray Zhongshan suit, looking like a retired old professor — if one ignored the shrewdness in his eyes, the kind that came from long years at the top.
"Miss Su, Teacher Qu, please, sit." Secretary-General Bai gestured to the two chairs across from him. "Tea or coffee?"
"Tea," Qu Ya said.
"Coffee, thank you," Su Qingyuan said.
Secretary-General Bai nodded and sent his assistant to prepare it. Then he took a document from a drawer and laid it on the desk without opening it. "I asked you here today because I'd like to discuss something with you."
Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya exchanged a glance.
"The venue for your joint exhibition — the main hall of the Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art — may no longer be available."
Su Qingyuan's heart sank. Qu Ya's brow furrowed. "Secretary-General Bai, what do you mean?"
Secretary-General Bai let out a sigh. "The city has a major diplomatic event come up at the last minute, and they need the museum's main hall. The dates fall right on the week of your exhibition. I tried to fight for you, but it was arranged directly by the city. There's nothing to be done."
Qu Ya's face darkened. "Secretary-General Bai, our exhibition posters are already out, the invitations have already been mailed, the media have already been notified. And you're telling me the venue's no longer available?"
"I know, I know." Secretary-General Bai raised a hand in a placating gesture. "That's why I'm helping you find a way around it. There are several alternative venues in Nancheng — I've made a list. Take a look."
He pushed the document across to them. Qu Ya picked it up to read, and Su Qingyuan leaned over. Several alternative venues — too small, too out of the way, or facilities too outdated. Not a single one was worthy of this exhibition.
Qu Ya set the document down. "Secretary-General Bai, none of these venues will do."
Secretary-General Bai's expression stiffened. "Teacher Qu, I know these venues aren't on par with the museum, but these are special circumstances—"
"Special circumstances aren't our problem." Su Qingyuan took up the thread, her tone calm but resolute. "Secretary-General Bai, our exhibition posters say 'Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art.' The invitations say 'Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art.' The press release the media put out says 'Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art' too. Changing the venue at the last minute isn't as simple as swapping one place for another — it's a disrespect to every invited guest, and a disrespect to our work."
Secretary-General Bai looked at her, a hint of appraisal entering his gaze. Clearly, he hadn't expected Su Qingyuan to speak so directly. He'd probably assumed she was just a "newcomer" tagging along on Qu Ya's exhibition, someone he could fob off with any old excuse.
"Miss Su, I understand how you feel, but the city's arrangement—" "Secretary-General Bai," Su Qingyuan cut him off, "we'll handle the venue ourselves. No need to trouble you." Secretary-General Bai was taken aback. "We'll find our own venue. If we find one, the exhibition goes on as planned. If we don't, we can postpone. But those alternative venues — we won't be using them."
Qu Ya turned to look at Su Qingyuan, an emotion in her eyes that was hard to name — something like surprise, and something like admiration.
Secretary-General Bai was silent for a few seconds, then nodded. "Very well. Miss Su, I respect your decision. If you find a suitable venue and need the association's help, come to me anytime."
Walking out of the association building, Qu Ya caught hold of Su Qingyuan. "Qingyuan, do you have a venue in mind?"
"No."
"Then you—"
"I'll find one." Su Qingyuan looked into Qu Ya's eyes. "Sister Ya, this exhibition isn't just my show, and it isn't just your show. It's 'ours.' I won't let it be held in some place that isn't worthy of it."
Qu Ya looked at her, silent for a few seconds, then laughed. "All right. Find it. I'll look with you."
The two of them stood on the steps of the association building, Nancheng's winter wind howling past, whipping their hair into disarray. But Su Qingyuan didn't hunch into herself. She tilted her head back and looked up at the gray, overcast sky. She would find a venue worthy of "Roots and Light." Not out of stubbornness — out of refusing to settle. Her work didn't settle, Qu Ya's photographs didn't settle, and this exhibition wouldn't settle either.
Over the next three days, Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya scoured every venue in Nancheng that could possibly host an exhibition.
Art museums, art centers, creative spaces converted from old factories, the banquet halls of five-star hotels — even a few private clubs. They'd look at one, reject one. Too small, too dark, too noisy, too many columns, ceilings too low, location too remote. Every venue had a fatal flaw; not a single one was worthy of "Roots and Light."
On the evening of the third day, Su Qingyuan sat in her studio, a map of Nancheng spread out before her, covered in red crosses. Every red cross was a venue that had been rejected. She leaned back in her chair, gazing at the ceiling, and let out a long breath.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "Have you eaten?"
Su Qingyuan: "No. Not hungry."
Lu Shiyan: "I'm at the door."
Su Qingyuan blinked, stood up, walked to the door, and pulled it open. Lu Shiyan stood outside, an insulated bag in his hand, wearing a black coat, his scarf blown crooked by the wind. Looking at him, her nose suddenly stung. "Why are you here?"
"You haven't eaten a proper meal in three days." Lu Shiyan walked in and set the insulated bag on the table. "If I didn't come over, were you planning to starve yourself to death?"
Su Qingyuan said nothing — because she had, in fact, planned to finish looking at the map before eating, and after looking at the map she probably wouldn't have wanted to eat at all.
Lu Shiyan opened the insulated bag: a bowl of steaming little wontons, a bowl of red-bean tangyuan, a small dish of pickles. He pushed the wontons in front of her. "Eat first. Once you're done, I'll help you find a venue."
Su Qingyuan picked up the wontons and took a sip of the broth. The savory chicken soup melted on her tongue, warmth traveling all the way down her throat to her stomach. Her eyes suddenly felt hot. "Lu Shiyan, do you know what kind of venue I'm looking for?"
"No. But tell me, and I'll know."
Su Qingyuan set down the bowl and pushed the map in front of him. "The venue I'm looking for needs ceilings at least six meters high, no columns, good natural light, a location that's not too remote, convenient transport, the ability to display both jewelry and photography at once, and a professional lighting system. None of the ones I've seen will do."
Lu Shiyan looked at the red crosses on the map, silent for a few seconds. "There's one place that meets every single one of your requirements."
"Where?"
"The Lu Group headquarters. The ground-floor lobby."
Su Qingyuan froze.
"Eight-meter ceilings, no columns, an entire floor-to-ceiling glass wall, excellent natural light. It's in the core of the CBD, easy to get to. The Lu showroom has hosted countless high-end exhibitions; the lighting system is professional." Lu Shiyan's tone was even, as if stating a simple fact. "And — no charge."
Su Qingyuan looked at him, opened her mouth to say something, but her throat felt as though something were lodged in it. "You... why didn't you say so sooner?"
"You didn't ask before."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head, looked at the red crosses on the map, and suddenly laughed. She'd searched for three days, crossed all of Nancheng, looked at dozens of venues, and not one would do. And the best venue had been beside her the whole time. It wasn't a case of being "blindest under the lamp" — it was that it had never once occurred to her that his company headquarters could become her exhibition hall.
"Lu Shiyan, are you helping me, or being romantic?"
"Helping you." Lu Shiyan reached out and gently wiped a bit of wonton broth from the corner of her mouth. "The romance is just a bonus."
Su Qingyuan laughed and gave him a light punch.
The next day, Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya went to the Lu Group headquarters. Lu Shiyan didn't accompany them; he had his assistant take them to see the venue while he stayed up in his office. He said, "This is your exhibition. If I go, people will think you got the venue through your connection to me." Su Qingyuan understood what he meant. His connections could be used, but no one could be allowed to think her joint exhibition only came together because of his connections. So she went alone, with Qu Ya, in the identity of "Su Qingyuan" — not "Lu Shiyan's girlfriend."
The ground-floor lobby was larger and brighter than Su Qingyuan remembered. An entire floor-to-ceiling glass curtain wall faced Nancheng's skyline, sunlight pouring in from every direction, lighting the whole space through and warm. The floor was pale gray marble, clean as a mirror, mirroring the silhouettes of those who passed. The ceiling looked to be at least eight meters high, not a single column in sight, the view as open as a giant transparent box.
Qu Ya stood in the center of the lobby, turned a full circle, then turned back to look at Su Qingyuan, her eyes full of light. "Qingyuan, this place... it's perfect."
Su Qingyuan smiled too. She took out her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: "Venue's settled. This is the one. Thank you."
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly: "You're welcome. By the way — the ground-floor lobby's schedule is open, so use it however you like."
Su Qingyuan looked at the words "by the way" and smiled.
With the venue settled, the preparations for the exhibition entered their final sprint. Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya practically lived in the ground-floor lobby of the Lu Group — lighting calibration, booth layout, traffic flow, security plans; every detail had to be confirmed and reconfirmed. Lu Shiyan would come down to take a look every day when he got off work, but he never disturbed them — he'd just stand at a distance and watch for a while, then head back up to keep working.
Sometimes, when Su Qingyuan looked up, she'd see him standing at the entrance of the lobby, hands tucked in his coat pockets, gazing quietly in her direction. She'd wave at him; he'd give a slight nod, then turn and leave. This feeling of being watched but not disturbed gave her a deep sense of steadiness — he was there, but not an interference. He was there, but not in charge. He was simply there.
Three days before the exhibition opened, the unexpected happened.
Su Qingyuan got a call from a number she didn't recognize. When she answered, it was a young woman's voice on the other end, her tone carrying a deliberate politeness. "Miss Su, I'm a reporter with Art and Design magazine. My name is Lin Xiaoxi. We're working on a feature about 'crossover joint exhibitions,' and we'd like to interview you and Teacher Qu Ya. Would that be convenient for you?"
Something stirred in Su Qingyuan's mind. Art and Design was one of the country's top art magazines; a feature on the exhibition there would be a huge boost to publicity. "Reporter Lin, the exhibition's media day is set for the day before the opening. Teacher Qu Ya will be there that day, so you can come interview us then."
"Of course, thank you, Miss Su. There's one more thing — I heard your exhibition venue was changed from the Nancheng Museum of Contemporary Art to the Lu Group headquarters. Was that because the museum's hall was requisitioned by the city? Or is there another reason?"
Su Qingyuan's fingers tightened slightly. She hadn't announced this news publicly yet. Even though the venue had changed, she and Qu Ya had agreed to release the news all at once, only after everything was finalized. How did this reporter know?
"Reporter Lin, you're very well informed. The venue has indeed changed; we'll explain the specific reasons in a press release later."
"Of course, thank you, Miss Su. One more question — there's a rumor going around online that you changed venues because the museum felt your work 'wasn't up to standard' and 'wasn't worthy of the main hall.' Do you have any response to that?"
Su Qingyuan's heart gave a lurch. "Reporter Lin, I won't respond to baseless rumors like that."
"Miss Su, if you don't respond, people will assume you're tacitly admitting it."
Su Qingyuan's grip on the phone tightened further. She took a deep breath, her tone calm. "Reporter Lin, whether my work is up to standard — come see for yourself on opening day. If there's nothing else, I'll hang up now. See you on media day."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan immediately searched online. Sure enough, a few small accounts were spreading the same story — "Su Qingyuan and Qu Ya's joint exhibition turned away by the museum, last-minute venue change, suspected substandard work quality." It hadn't spread widely yet, but it had already sparked a small ripple of discussion within the industry.
Su Qingyuan put down her phone, leaned back in her chair, and closed her eyes. She'd originally planned to announce the venue change in another couple of days. Now it was clear she had to do it ahead of schedule.
She called Qu Ya and laid out the whole thing. After listening, Qu Ya was quiet for a few seconds. "Qingyuan, who do you think is pulling strings behind this?"
"I don't know. But the only ones who could know both that the museum's venue was requisitioned and that we were changing venues are a handful of people. Secretary-General Bai, his assistant, the two of us, and Lu Shiyan."
"It can't be Lu Shiyan."
"I know. It can't be Secretary-General Bai either — he has no motive. His assistant — I don't know that person."
"I'll look into it." Qu Ya's voice turned grave. "On your end, announce the venue change early. Don't let the rumors keep festering."
"All right."
Su Qingyuan hung up, opened her laptop, and started writing the press release. She didn't respond to the rumors in the statement; she simply stated a fact objectively — that due to the original venue being temporarily requisitioned by the city, the exhibition's location had been changed to the ground-floor lobby of the Lu Group headquarters. With thanks to the Lu Group for its support of the arts. The opening time was unchanged, only the location; their understanding was appreciated. Once finished, she sent it to Qu Ya for confirmation, then released it through Qu Ya Studio's official account.
Within an hour of posting, the reshares passed ten thousand. The tone of the comment section shifted from doubt to understanding — "So it was requisitioned by the city, not that they got kicked out." "The Lu Group is sponsoring the venue? Su Qingyuan's got that much clout?" "It's not clout, it's the work. Lu Shiyan is her boyfriend, but the Lu Group sponsoring the exhibition isn't because she's Lu Shiyan's girlfriend — it's because this show is worth it."
Su Qingyuan looked at those comments and let out a breath of relief. But she also knew the people spreading the rumors wouldn't stop there. They'd switch angles, switch tactics, and keep flinging mud at her exhibition.
She wasn't afraid. Because she held the hardest card of all — her work.
Two days until the exhibition opened.
Su Qingyuan was making the final booth adjustments in the Lu Group's ground-floor lobby. All five pieces of the "Roots" series were in place, displayed in five custom-made cases. The lighting angle of each case had been calibrated over and over, to present the gemstones' most perfect luster.
Qu Ya's photographs hung on the walls — twenty of them, each one personally chosen and finalized by her. The two women's works intertwined throughout the space — not "yours over there, mine over here," but "yours here, mine there, meeting in the middle."
Su Qingyuan stood in the center of the lobby, turned a full circle, taking in the works, the photographs, the lighting, and a scalding emotion welled up inside her. Two months. From inspiration to finished piece, from concept to reality, she had taken two months. In those two months she'd stayed up countless nights, ground countless stones, revised the plans dozens of times. Now, at last, they were about to meet the world.
"Beautiful." Lu Shiyan's voice came from behind her. Su Qingyuan turned and saw him standing at the lobby entrance, two cups of coffee in his hands. "When did you come down?"
"Just now. I saw you spacing out, so I didn't call to you." Lu Shiyan walked over and handed her the coffee. "Americano, no sugar."
Su Qingyuan took the coffee and drank a sip, the bitterness blooming on her tongue, and she suddenly realized the taste was very familiar. A long time ago, the first time he invited her up to the eighty-eighth floor for coffee, she'd taken a sip of the Americano and frowned at the bitterness, and he'd immediately had it swapped for a latte. Today he'd bought an Americano again — because he knew she'd grown used to the bitterness.
"Lu Shiyan, do you think this exhibition will be a success?"
"It will."
"How do you know?"
"Because you're in it."
Su Qingyuan looked at him and smiled. "Not because the venue is at your company?"
"The venue doesn't matter. What matters is the work. Your work is worthy of anywhere."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head and looked at her own reflection in the coffee cup. Then she raised her head, looked at the works about to be exhibited, and said, "Tomorrow. It's tomorrow now."
"Mm. Tomorrow."
The two of them stood side by side in the center of the lobby, surrounded by the works about to make their debut. The light fell over them, casting their two shadows on the floor, close together — so close they nearly overlapped.
Su Qingyuan suddenly remembered a long time ago, when she was a little girl, sitting on the attic floor of the old Su family house, drawing on a crumpled sheet of paper. What she drew was light. Back then she didn't know that one day, that light would become real.
Now she knew.
