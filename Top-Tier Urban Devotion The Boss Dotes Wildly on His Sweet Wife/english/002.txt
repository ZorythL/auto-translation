Chapter 2 — A Promise in the Palm of His Hand

[Ding—the All-Around Comeback System has been successfully bound.]
The voice didn't come from her phone, nor from any direction at all. It simply rang inside Su Qingyuan's head, as though something deep in her consciousness had just been switched on.
Su Qingyuan's fingers clenched the bedsheet, her spine drawing taut into a straight line.
"Who's speaking?"
[This unit is an all-around growth-assistance system, serial number 0731. It has detected that the host meets the binding conditions and has automatically established a neural link. The host need not be alarmed; this unit is merely an auxiliary program and possesses no independent consciousness.]
Su Qingyuan was silent for three seconds.
In those three seconds, countless thoughts raced through her mind—was she so exhausted she was hallucinating? Had someone drugged her? Was this…
Outside the window the rain still fell, the faucet still dripped, and her fingers, pinching her own thigh, hurt.
This wasn't a dream.
"Why me?" she asked.
[Host's composite scores: Appearance 87, Intelligence 92, Talent 65, Business 23, Medicine 0, Combat 12. Overall rating: C-minus. Potential assessment: SSS-plus. This unit automatically screens for the individual with the highest potential worldwide who is also in a state of extreme adversity. The host ranks first.]
A string of data unfolded before her eyes, like a translucent screen of light appearing out of nowhere.
Su Qingyuan blinked. The screen was still there.
She reached out to touch it; her fingers passed straight through the emptiness.
[The host cannot currently make physical contact with the system interface. Please operate it by thought.]
Su Qingyuan tried thinking the word "close" in her mind, and the screen really did vanish.
She thought "open" again, and the screen reappeared.
She drew in a deep breath.
Stay calm. Whatever this thing was, it didn't seem hostile, at least for now. And if what it said was true, this might be the greatest turning point of her entire life.
"This starter package you mentioned—what is it?"
The screen lit up again, lines of text floating into view.
[Starter Package Contents:
1. Top-Tier Design Talent (activated)
2. Photographic Memory (activated)
3. Basic Self-Defense (activated)
4. Startup Capital: 100,000 yuan (deposited)]
Her phone buzzed.
Su Qingyuan picked it up and looked: a bank text. A deposit of 100,000.00 yuan had been credited to the account ending in your number; balance 103,247.50 yuan.
She stared at that string of figures for five seconds.
In twenty-three years of living, her bank balance had never once cracked five digits.
[Side quest triggered: The Battle to Clear Your Name.
Quest description: Current public sentiment holds short-term sympathy for the host, but most people still see the host as "the pitiable woman whose work was stolen" rather than "a truly gifted designer." Within 72 hours, please create a top-tier design work, unveil it publicly, and make everyone remember your name—not your misfortune.
Quest reward: Appearance +3, Intelligence +2, unlocks Intermediate Design Talent, unlocks new skill: Piano (Beginner).
Failure penalty: None. (This unit has no penalty mechanism; it merely advises the host to seize the opportunity.)]
Su Qingyuan looked at the quest description, the corner of her mouth curving up faintly.
No penalty mechanism.
This system was rather reasonable.
She glanced at the time on her phone—1:17 a.m. Seventy-two hours, which meant before 1:17 a.m. three days from now.
Three days to create, from scratch, a work that could "make everyone remember her name."
The Su Qingyuan of yesterday would have called it a pipe dream.
But right now, lines, colors, and structures were already surging through her mind—like a flock of birds caged for far too long, suddenly released, all of them scrambling over one another to burst free.
She knew it wasn't a hallucination.
That so-called "top-tier design talent" was real.
Su Qingyuan rolled out of bed, switched on the old desk lamp in the corner, set up her drawing board, and picked up a pencil.
She didn't start sketching right away. Instead, she closed her eyes.
A building took shape in her mind.
Not a building that actually existed—one she had traced over and over in her imagination through countless sleepless nights, a dream. An art museum built by the sea, white from end to end, its curves rising and falling like ocean waves, its glass curtain walls mirroring sky and water until you couldn't tell where the architecture ended and nature began.
She had never been able to draw it before. She could never quite handle those curves, and there was always some structural contradiction she couldn't resolve.
But now, everything was different.
She opened her eyes. The pencil touched the paper.
Shhk, shhk, shhk—
The lines poured out as smoothly as water. She didn't even need to think; her hand simply knew what to draw. Every technical problem that had plagued her for years suddenly had an answer—and not just one answer, but dozens, hundreds, each one exquisite, each one quickening her pulse.
Minute by minute, second by second, time slipped past.
At some point the rain outside had stopped.
The lamplight cast her hunched silhouette against the mottled wall, the pencil flying across the paper—one sheet, two sheets, three…
By four in the morning, she had finished the first draft.
By six, she had drawn rendering views from three angles.
By eight, she had refined it down to the structural joints.
Su Qingyuan set down her pencil, rubbed her aching wrist, and looked at the stack of drawings spread before her, her eyes growing a little hot.
This wasn't the "pretty good" level she used to work at.
This was a height she had never reached. No—not just her. She dared say there weren't more than a handful of people in the entire Capital design scene who could reach this standard.
[Ding—creation progress: 35%. Please be sure to rest, host; excessive fatigue will affect the quality of your work.]
Only then did Su Qingyuan notice that day had broken.
When she stood, her knee gave a sharp click and her head swam a little. She hadn't eaten anything since last night; her stomach had been starving for so long that it had gone past the point of feeling it.
She opened the fridge. Inside there was only half a carton of expired milk and two wilted cucumbers.
She checked the milk's expiration date—five days past.
Without batting an eye, Su Qingyuan put the milk back and picked up her phone to order delivery. But the moment she opened the app, she saw a text from an unknown number, sent at 2 a.m.
"Hello, Miss Su, this is Qin Zhou, Mr. Lu's personal assistant. Mr. Lu asked me to pass along a message: the area in the north of the city has only fair public safety at night, so please take care of yourself. Also, tomorrow at ten in the morning, someone will come to replace your door lock and install security fixtures. You may wait at home at that time, or you may authorize us to handle it directly. If you need anything at all, please feel free to contact this number."
Su Qingyuan paused.
Mr. Lu?
Lu Shiyan?
Into her mind came the image of the man standing at the hotel entrance the night before—holding an umbrella, extraordinarily tall, his gaze very deep.
She looked down at the old wooden door of her rented room—the lock was rusted; one hard kick would open it. She had lived here two years and had never once thought of changing the lock. Not that she hadn't wanted to—she simply hadn't had the money.
This man—how had he thought of even this?
She considered it, then replied: "Thank you, but there's no need to trouble yourselves. I'll be home during the day."
After sending it she felt something was off—why on earth had she told him she'd be home during the day?
Never mind. She was too tired to think about it.
She read the text again and caught a detail: "Mr. Lu asked me to pass along a message"—he had told his assistant to text her at 2 a.m., reminding her to stay safe.
Su Qingyuan set her phone face-down on the table and pressed a hand over her heart.
It was beating a little fast.
Not the fast of fear. Another kind.
She decided not to dwell on it.
At that same moment, in the top-floor office of the tallest building in the Capital's CBD, Lu Shiyan was signing documents.
He had been working for nearly twenty hours straight. After returning from the awards banquet the night before, he had come straight to the company. At one point Qin Zhou had wanted to remind his boss that he ought to rest, but meeting those eyes that betrayed no emotion whatsoever, he swallowed the words back down.
"Mr. Lu," Qin Zhou said, knocking and stepping in, "Miss Su has replied."
Lu Shiyan's pen paused.
"What did she say?"
Qin Zhou glanced at his phone. "She said, 'Thank you, but there's no need to trouble yourselves. I'll be home during the day.'"
The office went quiet for two seconds.
Lu Shiyan set down his pen, leaned back against his chair, and tapped his long fingers twice against the armrest.
"Is she saying not to bother," Qin Zhou ventured, "or is she just being polite?"
Lu Shiyan shot him a look.
Qin Zhou immediately said, "Understood. The security fixtures proceed as planned, the door lock gets swapped for a smart fingerprint lock, a video doorbell goes in, and I've already arranged with property management to replace the motion-sensor lights in the stairwell."
Lu Shiyan said nothing, which counted as tacit approval.
Qin Zhou turned to leave, then was called back.
"That building she lives in—have you looked into the ownership?"
"I have. The building is called No. 6 Xinhua Lane, built in 1992, seven stories, four units per floor, twenty-eight units in all. Ownership belongs to a small company called Hongda Properties, whose actual controller is a local businessman named Liu Jianguo. At present sixty percent of the residents are tenants and forty percent are longtime owner-occupants. The whole building has no elevator; its exterior walls and pipes are severely deteriorated, and over the past three years it's received two condemned-building rectification notices."
"Make an offer. Buy the whole building." Lu Shiyan's tone was the same as if he were saying buy a cup of coffee. "Relocation and resettlement fees for all residents at double the market rate; we'll cover the breach penalties for tenants ending their leases early. Once the acquisition is complete, demolish and rebuild the entire structure to high-end boutique-apartment standards."
Qin Zhou quickly ran the budget for this plan in his head.
Inside the Capital's Second Ring Road, buying a whole old building plus demolition plus reconstruction…
Conservatively, half a billion to start.
"Yes, Mr. Lu." Qin Zhou jotted it down without changing expression. "There's one problem, though. Liu Jianguo isn't an easy man to deal with. A developer once wanted to buy that building of his, and he jacked the price up fivefold on the spot. In the end the deal fell through."
Lu Shiyan picked his pen back up and went on signing.
"Then tell him that if he won't sell, his company will go bankrupt within a month. By then he won't even get that fivefold rock-bottom price."
Qin Zhou: "…"
Yes, boss.
He silently lit a candle for Liu Jianguo in his heart.
In the end Su Qingyuan did order delivery, ate a bowl of congee, then took a shower and slept.
She set an alarm to sleep for only three hours.
When she woke, there were over a dozen missed calls and several dozen messages on her phone. She didn't go through them one by one, just picked out the few that mattered most.
The message from her best friend, Wen Ruan, jumped out the most: "Qingyuan, do you even know you're trending?!! I saw it on set and lost my mind!!! Who is that man?!! Insanely handsome!!! What's your relationship?!! Tell me right now!!!"
Su Qingyuan smiled and replied with two words: "No relationship."
Wen Ruan shot back instantly: "Tell that to someone who'll believe you."
Su Qingyuan didn't reply again and looked at the other messages.
There were sympathies from a few college classmates, feelers from peers in the design circle, and several job offers from headhunters—it seemed someone had already taken note of her name.
She was about to turn off her phone when a new message popped up.
Unknown number. Its contents: a single image.
The image was a scan of a design contract. The client party was "Lu Global Real Estate Group," the project name was "Yunlan Bay Seaside Art Center," and in the line for the design fee was written, in plain sight: eight million yuan.
Below it was a line of text: "Miss Su, Mr. Lu says that if you're interested, this contract can be signed at any time. The project is not on a tight schedule; you may complete it at your own pace. Mr. Lu says, 'She deserves the finest stage.'"
The hand holding her phone trembled slightly.
Eight million.
Not eighty thousand, not eight hundred thousand—eight million.
The most money she had ever seen in her life was the hundred thousand that had landed in her bank account just yesterday.
She knew Lu Shiyan was very rich. At the banquet last night, the way everyone had treated him said it all. But why would he hand her an eight-million-yuan project? Just because she'd drawn one good draft? There were plenty of people in this world who drew well; eight million could hire the most top-tier designers on earth.
Unless…
Su Qingyuan didn't dare let the thought go further.
She put down her phone, took three deep breaths, and let her heartbeat return to normal.
Then she walked to the drawing board and picked up her pencil again.
Whatever that man meant, whether this money was goodwill or a trap, there was only one thing that mattered right now: getting that work onto paper.
Prove herself.
Not to Lu Shiyan, and not to the Su family.
To prove it to herself.
She could do this.
At three in the afternoon, the doorbell rang.
Su Qingyuan lifted her head from her drawings, rubbed her sore, swollen eyes, and went to open the door.
Two men in work uniforms stood outside, one carrying a toolbox, the other holding a cardboard box printed with the logo of a well-known brand.
"Hello, Miss Su, we were sent by Mr. Lu to change your lock and install security fixtures." The one with the toolbox politely presented his work ID.
Su Qingyuan glanced at what was in the cardboard box—a fingerprint lock, a video doorbell, door-and-window sensors, all top brands on the market, some she had never even seen, probably some kind of high-end custom models.
"Come in." She stepped aside.
The two workers moved quickly and worked cleanly; in under an hour they had everything installed and tested. Before they left, they even patched the chunk of wall that had flaked off the door frame with filler, then sanded it smooth, so that you couldn't tell there had been any repair at all unless you looked closely.
Su Qingyuan closed the door and tried out the new fingerprint lock—a light touch of her finger and the door opened, as silky-smooth as running her hand over satin.
She stood at the doorway, stunned for quite a while.
This door, all of a sudden, was completely out of place with the rest of the room.
She looked again at the eight-million contract on her phone, closed her eyes, and drove all those tangled thoughts out of her head.
Draw.
Focus on drawing.
She returned to the drawing board and got back to work.
Minute by minute, second by second, time slipped away.
By six in the evening, she had completed another full set of elevation drawings.
By nine at night, she had finished the section drawings and detailed joints.
At one in the morning, she finally set down her pencil.
Spread before her were forty-two drawings in all. From conceptual sketch to rendering, from floor plan to structural detail, from material selection to lighting design—everything was there, internally consistent, seamlessly whole.
It was an art museum built by the sea.
It had a name—when she finished the last drawing, Su Qingyuan quietly spoke that name in her heart—
"Listening to the Sea."
[Ding—creation progress: 100%. Composite work rating: SSS class. Far exceeds the gold-medal standard of the current top global design competitions.]
The instant the system's chime sounded, the doorbell rang too.
Su Qingyuan started, then walked to the door and glanced at the video doorbell's screen.
A man stood in the stairwell.
Not a worker, not a courier, not a delivery driver.
It was Lu Shiyan.
He wore a dark-gray overcoat, one hand tucked into his pocket, the other just lifted away from the doorbell button. The stairwell's sensor light was on, its dim yellow glow falling over him, plating his whole figure in a layer of warmth.
It was one in the morning.
A light rain was falling outside.
He had come alone—no assistant, no driver—the damp, cold air clinging to him palpable even through the screen.
Su Qingyuan's heart lurched into a faster beat.
She drew in a deep breath and opened the door.
