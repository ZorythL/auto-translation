Chapter 29 — Snow Falls in Silence, the Undercurrent Runs On
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626231.html

After the first snow, the temperature in Nancheng plunged ten degrees overnight.
Su Qingyuan changed into a thick cashmere overcoat—the one Lu Shiyan had had prepared for her, smoke-gray, cleanly tailored, warm without being bulky. When she walked into the studio wearing it, the heating had only just come on, and the room still held the chill it had gathered through the night. She didn't rush to switch on the work lamps; first she watered the moth orchid on the windowsill, then stood at the window looking out at the dull, gray sky.
Su Yurou had said she was leaving.
Five days had passed since that conversation. Su Qingyuan hadn't received a single word from Su Yurou since—no texts, no calls, no figure suddenly appearing at the studio door. It was quiet, the way a single drop of water falls into a deep pool: once the ripples spread and faded, the surface lay so still it was as if nothing had ever happened.
But Su Qingyuan knew that the calm before a storm was often the most unsettling of all.
She wasn't worried that Su Yurou would have a change of heart, nor that Su Yurou would try something more—Su Yurou's fangs and claws had been all but pulled out; even if she wanted to wound someone, she couldn't reach anywhere vital. What worried her was Gu Changfeng. That man was too quiet. Ever since the Hengsheng affair, he hadn't made a single move—like a snake lying dormant in its winter den, neither eating nor drinking, simply waiting for spring to warm the world into bloom.
But it was winter now.
What was he waiting for?
Su Qingyuan drew her gaze back, sat down at her worktable, and opened the production-progress sheet for the "Light Traces" collection. Zhenyi's capacity was running at full tilt; the first batch of finished pieces had been crated yesterday and was expected to reach Paris in three days. Cartier's flagship store was putting the final touches on the exhibition hall for the global debut of "Light Traces," and Anna sent photos from the site every day—from an empty hall to display cases gradually filling up, from white walls to the pale gray she'd specified, from ordinary lighting to a color temperature calibrated specifically for "Light Traces."
Looking at those photos, Su Qingyuan sometimes felt it wasn't quite real.
13 Rue de la Paix. The birthplace of Cartier. Her work would make its debut there, before the whole world.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Zhou Wanyin: [Qingyuan, Qu Ya's team has sent over the preliminary plan for the joint exhibition. When are you free—shall we go through it together?]
Su Qingyuan replied: [This afternoon? My studio.]
Zhou Wanyin: [Sounds good. I'll bring lunch.]
Su Qingyuan set down her phone and went on working. Lately she'd developed a new habit—filling every moment that might otherwise drift into worry with work. It wasn't avoidance; it was channeling her energy into the things she could control. When Gu Changfeng would strike, and how, she couldn't control. But she could control her own work—every line, every cut, every detail.
That afternoon, Zhou Wanyin arrived at the studio right on time.
She wore a red down jacket and carried two big insulated bags, looking less like a famous designer than like a mother dropping off a meal for her daughter. "We're having Japanese bento today—I saw a new place getting good reviews in our group chat yesterday, so I ordered two." She set the insulated bags down on the table in the lounge area, took off her down jacket, and looked the studio over. "Did you change something in here again? Did you swap out the flower on the windowsill?"
"I did. The moth orchid's bloom period was over, so I replaced it with a cyclamen." Su Qingyuan rose from her worktable and rubbed at her aching neck.
Zhou Wanyin caught the faint shadows under her eyes and frowned. "Did you stay up late again?"
"No. I just got up early."
"What time?"
"Six."
"That's not so bad." Zhou Wanyin took the bento out and laid them out bowl by bowl. "But your workload's been too heavy lately—Cartier isn't even done yet and the joint exhibition's already starting. You're not made of iron. Your body is the capital of the revolution; if you collapse, who's going to do the designing?"
Su Qingyuan sat down, picked up the miso soup, and took a sip—warm, the heat traveling down her throat all the way into her stomach. "Sister Wanyin, you're getting naggier and naggier."
"You drove me to it. If you were my daughter, I'd have pinned you down at home to rest ages ago."
Su Qingyuan curved her lips and said nothing. The way Zhou Wanyin said it carried a very natural sort of concern—not politeness, not social nicety, but the kind of unguarded bluntness you only show once you've come to think of someone as your own. After eating for a while, she suddenly asked, "Sister Wanyin, have you heard anything about Gu Changfeng making moves recently?"
Zhou Wanyin's chopsticks paused mid-air. She set them down and looked at her. "Why are you suddenly asking about that?"
"He's too quiet. After the Hengsheng business, he did nothing. That's not like him."
Zhou Wanyin fell silent for a few seconds, then took out her phone, scrolled through a few pages, and handed it over. "I didn't mean to tell you—I was afraid it would distract you. But since you've asked, see for yourself."
Su Qingyuan took it. On the screen was an unpublished press release. The headline read:
[Exclusive Exposé: Biennale Gold Medalist Su Qingyuan and Her "Unconventional" Relationship with Lu Group President Lu Shiyan]
Her fingers tightened a fraction.
The piece was written very "cleverly"—it didn't say outright that she'd plagiarized, didn't say outright that she'd leaned on a man. Instead, in a tone of "objective neutrality," it "laid out" her timeline from nobody to Biennale gold medalist, then gave a light little tap at each key juncture—"Coincidentally, around this very time, Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan's private relationship was heating up."
Not a single word in the whole thing was a lie, but strung together, every word hinted at the same meaning: her success had nothing to do with talent.
When she'd finished reading, Su Qingyuan handed the phone back to Zhou Wanyin. "When is this piece going out?"
"Originally scheduled for yesterday. But for some reason it was pulled at the last minute." Zhou Wanyin put her phone away. "I had someone look into it—it's a media company under Dingfeng Group running the operation. The piece was already laid out, the ad slots all bought, and then it was called off at the eleventh hour."
Su Qingyuan leaned back in her chair and gazed up at the ceiling. Called off. Why call it off? What was Gu Changfeng waiting for?
"Qingyuan, what are you going to do?" Zhou Wanyin asked.
"Nothing." Su Qingyuan sat up straight and took another sip of miso soup. "The piece wasn't published, so I'll act as if I don't know. If it runs, I'll respond as I should. But going and asking about it now would only make me look guilty."
Zhou Wanyin looked at her, an emotion in her eyes hard to name—something like relief, something like heartache. "You're far steadier than before. In the old days, when something like this hit you, you'd cry first, then carry it all alone and never tell a soul."
"In the old days I was alone." Su Qingyuan set down her bowl. "I'm not anymore."
Zhou Wanyin smiled and didn't ask "who is it now." The answer was too obvious.
After seeing Zhou Wanyin off, Su Qingyuan didn't go straight back to her worktable. She stood at the window, looking out at the dull gray sky, her mind turning the whole time. Gu Changfeng had bought the ad space, laid out the piece, lined it up to run yesterday—and then called it off at the last moment. What was he waiting for?
A better opportunity? Was he waiting until her "Roots and Light" joint exhibition was officially announced before publishing, so he could strike at her and Qu Ya at once? Until after the Cartier debut, so Cartier would get dragged into the whirlpool of public opinion?
Or—was he waiting for Su Yurou to leave?
Su Qingyuan picked up her phone and sent Lu Shiyan a message: [Gu Changfeng had a hit piece ready earlier and pulled it at the last minute. Do you know anything about this?]
Lu Shiyan's reply came quickly, but it wasn't text—it was a call.
"I know." His voice was steady, but Su Qingyuan caught the cold edge beneath it. "That piece is still in my hands."
"In your hands?"
"The ad supplier the media company used is one of Lu Group's partners. After the piece was laid out, the supplier's people saw it, felt something was off, and reported it up. I had someone go talk to the media company—no threats, no pressure, just a conversation. They were sensible about it and pulled it on their own."
Su Qingyuan held the phone, silent for a few seconds. "Does Gu Changfeng know it was you?"
"He knows. But he has no proof." Lu Shiyan's voice was light. "So for now he won't make a move. Because he doesn't know which of his moves I'll be there to intercept."
It suddenly struck Su Qingyuan that Gu Changfeng's situation now was like a man walking through the dark of night, dreading at every step that he'd tread on a trap. He couldn't be sure how far Lu Shiyan's reach extended, couldn't be sure which path was safe, couldn't be sure—whether he could still win this fight at all.
That kind of "uncertainty" wore a person down more than any direct blow.
"Lu Shiyan, when did you start laying this groundwork?"
"From the day you signed the contract with Zhou Wanyin."
That day again. Su Qingyuan couldn't help laughing. "Did you make that day your 'Su Qingyuan Protection Plan launch date'?"
A low laugh came from the other end of the line. "More or less."
Su Qingyuan stood at the window, phone in hand. The sky outside was still very gray, but inside her heart it was bright as day.
That evening, when Lu Shiyan came to pick her up, he brought a bag of sugar-roasted chestnuts.
"Saw them on the way and picked them up." He handed them over as casually as if he were remarking that traffic was good today.
Su Qingyuan took the bag. The chestnuts were still warm, their shells glossy with oil, the split openings revealing golden-yellow kernels. She peeled one and popped it into her mouth—soft and sweet, so hot she had to puff out little breaths.
"Delicious."
"Mm."
"These chestnuts you picked up on the way—where'd you buy them?"
Lu Shiyan named a street. Su Qingyuan filed it away in her mind. That street wasn't on any of the usual routes between his company and the creative park; he'd gone out of his way to buy them.
She didn't call him out. She peeled a chestnut and held it up to his lips. "Open up."
Lu Shiyan glanced at her, then opened his mouth and ate it. The chestnut's sweetness melted on his tongue, and the corners of his lips lifted faintly.
The car stopped at a red light. Su Qingyuan watched the world beyond the window and suddenly said, "Lu Shiyan, what do you think Gu Changfeng will do next?"
Lu Shiyan thought it over and said, "He'll wait. Wait for a moment when I'm not around."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "When you're not around?"
"Like when I'm away on business, or have an important commercial event I have to attend and can't stay by your side." Lu Shiyan tapped his fingers lightly once on the steering wheel. "He'll choose a time like that to strike, because that's when I won't have the bandwidth to cover both fronts."
Su Qingyuan was quiet for a moment. "Do you have any business trips coming up?"
"No. I've had my assistant push all of them back."
"Push them back to when?"
"To whenever you feel safe."
Su Qingyuan's nose prickled. He hadn't pushed his trips back to some specific date, but to a condition—"whenever you feel safe." That condition was hers to define; the initiative lay in her hands.
She lowered her head and went on peeling chestnuts, setting each peeled kernel one by one into the paper bag. When she'd gathered five or six, she handed the bag to him.
"For you. I peeled them."
Lu Shiyan glanced down at the perfectly whole kernels in the paper bag, reached out to take it, and said in a low voice, "Su Qingyuan, when you do things like this, I'm going to want to go on business trips less and less."
"Then don't go."
"What about work?"
"Is work more important than me?"
Lu Shiyan looked at her for two seconds, then laughed. Not his usual restrained, reined-in faint smile, but the kind that said "you win"—helpless and willing all at once.
"You're the most important."
Su Qingyuan was scalded by those three words and turned her face away, pretending to look out the window. Beyond the glass, Nancheng's night was descending, the streetlamps lighting up one by one, joining into a warm river of light. Her reflection showed in the car window, the curve of her lips refusing to be pressed flat no matter what.
The car stopped at the foot of her apartment building. Su Qingyuan didn't get out right away; she leaned back against the seat and looked at the streetlamps beyond the windshield.
"Lu Shiyan, do you think Su Yurou will really leave?"
"Whether she leaves depends on whether Gu Changfeng lets her." Lu Shiyan's voice was steady. "Gu Changfeng has poured a lot of resources into her—he won't let her go easily. And even if he does, he'll have her finish one last thing first."
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "One last thing?"
"For instance, stepping forward at some critical moment to say things that hurt you." Lu Shiyan's tone was very calm, like someone analyzing a business case. "Su Yurou is your adoptive sister; in outsiders' eyes, her words carry credibility. If she suddenly appears at the crucial moment of the Cartier debut or the joint exhibition's opening and says some half-true, half-false things to the media, your brand image will take damage."
Su Qingyuan's heart sank. It wasn't that she hadn't thought of this possibility, but coming from Lu Shiyan's mouth, it carried a different weight entirely. He wasn't just guessing—he was telling her: this possibility, he'd already considered, and he'd already prepared a response.
"Do you have a contingency plan?" she asked.
"I do." Lu Shiyan reached out and took her hand. "If Su Yurou steps forward, we'll release everything at once—the court verdict from when she falsely accused you, the chat records Lin Haoyu provided, the photos Chen Xiaoman took. The moment she dares to open her mouth, we'll silence her for good."
Su Qingyuan looked at their two clasped hands and was quiet for a long while. Releasing all that evidence meant tearing open every scar again, letting the whole world look once more. But sometimes, tearing open a scar isn't a show of weakness—it's so people can see that the wound has healed, that it won't hurt anymore.
"All right. Let's do it that way."
She pushed the door open and got out, walked a couple of steps, then turned back. Lu Shiyan was watching her through the car window. "Lu Shiyan, the chestnuts tonight were delicious. But tomorrow you don't need to go out of your way to buy them—it's too far."
Lu Shiyan was taken aback, then laughed. "How did you know I went out of my way?"
"Because that street isn't on any route between your company and my studio. I checked the map."
Lu Shiyan looked at her, his gaze deep as the night. "Su Qingyuan, you're getting harder and harder to fool."
"When have you ever fooled me?"
"Every time I say 'on the way.'"
Su Qingyuan laughed, her eyes crinkling. "Then from now on, don't say 'on the way.' Say 'on purpose.'"
"On purpose."
The two of them looked at each other, and both smiled.
Su Qingyuan turned and went upstairs, her steps so light it was as if she were treading on clouds. At her door she fished out her keys, opened it, and switched on the light. The cyclamen on the windowsill was blooming beautifully under the lamplight, its pink-and-white petals like so many tiny butterflies.
She walked to the window and looked down. His car was still parked there, the window half down, his face tilted up toward her window.
She picked up her phone and sent him a message: [I'm in. Head back now.]
Lu Shiyan's reply: [Mm. Shut the window properly, and don't crank the heating too high—it'll dry you out.]
Su Qingyuan looked at that line, curved her lips, closed the window, and drew the curtains.
She lay down on the bed and shut her eyes.
A great many things were turning in her mind—the "Light Traces" debut, the "Roots and Light" joint exhibition, Gu Changfeng's dormancy, whether Su Yurou would stay or go, the look on his face when he said "you're the most important," that one perfectly peeled chestnut. These things turned for a long time, then slowly quieted down, like a world after a heavy snow, all its clamor buried under white.
She turned over, pulled the quilt up to her chin, and sank into a deep sleep.
At that same moment, in a high-end residential district on the outskirts of Nancheng.
Gu Changfeng stood at the floor-to-ceiling window of his study, a cup of tea gone stone-cold in his hand. Beyond the glass lay the complex's night scene; the artificial lake shimmered with cold white light under the moon, and a few ground lamps lit the pebbled paths into a soft blur.
His phone buzzed. He glanced at the caller ID and answered.
"Director Gu, about Su Yurou… she asked again today about that sum of money."
Gu Changfeng said nothing.
"She says that if she still doesn't get the money by next week, she'll figure something out herself. She says she doesn't mind 'going down in flames and taking everyone with her.'"
Gu Changfeng lifted the teacup and took a sip of the cold tea, bitterness spreading across his tongue. "Tell her the money's ready. She'll get it next week. But first she has to do one last thing."
"What thing?"
Gu Changfeng set the cup down, walked over to his desk, took a USB drive from the drawer, and turned it over in his hand. "Have her write an open letter. Doesn't need to be long—just say that Su Qingyuan's work wasn't completed by her alone. That back in the Su household, Su Yurou took part in the early conception of the 'First Light' collection too."
There was a brief silence on the other end. "Director Gu, that's fake. Su Yurou never took part in any of it."
"True or false doesn't matter. What matters is that people believe it." Gu Changfeng's voice was cold—cold as the moonlight outside the window. "The most valuable thing Su Qingyuan has right now isn't her designs—it's her 'original creator' label. All it takes is one crack in that label and her brand value shrinks. When the brand shrinks, Cartier will reassess the partnership; when Cartier wavers, Lu Shiyan's jewelry division takes a hit; when Lu Shiyan takes a hit, my opening arrives."
"But last time Su Yurou said she didn't want to do this anymore. She wants to take the money and go."
"Then let her choose." Gu Changfeng put the USB drive back in the drawer. "Either she takes the money and writes this letter, or she gets nothing and leaves on her own. If she chooses the latter, then every single thing she's done before, I'll see to it she bears the consequences of it herself. She's not a fool. She knows how to choose."
The other end let out a sigh. "Understood. I'll go tell her."
The call ended. Gu Changfeng walked back to the window and looked out at the night. The moonlight fell across his face; his expression was very calm, but in his eyes burned an obsessive light—a light that wasn't hatred, but unwillingness. Unwilling to have lost the bid, unwilling to have been suppressed by Lu Shiyan for twenty years, unwilling to watch a girl in her early twenties stand unrivaled in the spotlight while he could only stand in the shadows, using methods that couldn't bear the light of day, trying to claw back a single round.
He thought back many years, to when he'd competed with Lu Shiyan's father for the same project. He was still young then, certain he would win. He lost. From that day on, he never once won against anyone from the Lu family.
"This time," he murmured to himself, gazing at the moon outside, "I won't lose again."
He turned off the light and walked out of the study. The whole room sank into darkness, with only the moonlight outside falling coldly across that empty desk.
And across the USB drive in the drawer—the hidden weapon it concealed.
