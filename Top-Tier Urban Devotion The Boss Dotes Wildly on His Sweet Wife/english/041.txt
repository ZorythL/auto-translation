First Light
The year Lu Xi turned three, Su Qingyuan made a decision—to take her to Northern Europe to see the aurora.
It wasn't a whim. For three years she had gone with Lu Shiyan every year, but it had always been just the two of them, with Lu Xi left in Zhou Wanyin's care. This year, Lu Xi said, "Mama, I want to see the dancing lights too." She had started talking earlier than other children her age—full sentences, clear logic, just like her father.
Su Qingyuan talked it over with Lu Shiyan. He thought for a moment. "She's too little. Twenty below zero—she won't be able to take it."
"We can stay in a glass igloo and not go outside. She can watch from indoors."
Lu Shiyan looked at the light in her eyes—a light he had seen before. It appeared every time she had a new creative spark, or whenever there was something she desperately wanted to do. He didn't object again. "All right. We'll go."
On the day they set off, Lu Xi wore a little penguin-shaped backpack and a red down jacket, looking for all the world like a plump, round little tomato. She held Su Qingyuan's hand and looked excitedly around the airport. "Mama, the plane is so big."
"Mm. We're going to ride this big plane to see the aurora."
"Where is the aurora?"
"In the sky."
"Where in the sky?"
Su Qingyuan crouched down and straightened her scarf. "Tonight, when it gets dark, look up and you'll be able to see it."
Lu Xi nodded, half understanding, and turned to look at Lu Shiyan. "Papa, have you seen the aurora?"
"I have."
"With who?"
"With your mama."
"Why didn't you take me?"
Lu Shiyan crouched down to meet her eyes. "Because you hadn't been born yet."
Lu Xi thought about it. "But I'm born now. Can you take me?"
Lu Shiyan smiled and reached out to ruffle the top of her head. "Yes."
On the plane, Lu Xi sat between Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan, buckled into a child's seatbelt, a picture book about the aurora in her hands, flipping through it over and over. Su Qingyuan leaned against Lu Shiyan's shoulder, watching her daughter's earnest profile—round face, big eyes, long curled lashes, like her. But that focused expression was like his. When she read, her little brow would furrow slightly, and she turned the pages so gently, as if afraid of damaging them. Those small details were exactly like him.
"Lu Shiyan, look at the way she turns the pages."
Lu Shiyan glanced down, the corner of his mouth lifting. "Like you."
"Like me? I don't turn pages like that."
"You do when you're flipping through your sketchbook. Very gently, very slowly, as if afraid of disturbing something."
Su Qingyuan looked at him, and something inside her went warm.
They changed planes in Helsinki, flying on to Ivalo. Lu Xi pressed herself against the window, watching the vast white snowfield below, clapping in excitement. "Mama! Snow! So much snow!"
"Mm. When we get to the hotel, you can go play in the snow."
"What about the aurora? Is the aurora in the snow too?"
"The aurora is in the sky."
Lu Xi tilted her head back to look at the sky outside the window. It was very blue, with no aurora. She was a little disappointed, but the snowfield outside quickly drew her attention again.
The glass igloo hotel. It was the first time Lu Xi had stayed in a room like this, and she was so excited she bounced up and down on the bed, kicked off her shoes, and spun in circles in her socks beneath the glass dome. "Mama! The ceiling is glass! You can see the sky!"
"Mm. Tonight, when it gets dark and the aurora comes out, you'll be able to see it just lying in bed."
"Really?"
"Really."
Lu Xi stopped bouncing, lay down obediently, pulled the covers up, and kept her eyes wide open, staring at the sky beyond the glass dome. It wasn't dark yet—a blue-gray, with a few birds flying past.
Su Qingyuan sat on the edge of the bed and tucked her in. "Sleep for a bit. I'll wake you when it gets dark."
"I'm not sleepy."
"Then lie down and rest."
Lu Xi blinked. "Mama, tell me a story."
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment. "What kind of story?"
"The story about you and Papa."
Su Qingyuan glanced at Lu Shiyan. He was standing by the window, a cup of coffee in his hand, and at those words, the corner of his mouth lifted slightly.
"Where should I start?"
"From the very beginning."
Su Qingyuan looked at her daughter's bright, shining eyes and thought for a few seconds. "In the very beginning, Mama didn't know Papa yet. Once, Mama went to see an exhibition but couldn't get in, so she stood outside the glass door looking in. Papa was inside the door, and he saw Mama."
"And then?"
"And then, a long time later, one night it rained. Mama didn't have an umbrella and was crouched at the side of the road. Papa drove by, stopped, and asked if I wanted to get in the car."
"Did you get in?"
"I did."
"Weren't you afraid he was a bad person?"
Su Qingyuan smiled. "No."
"Why?"
"Because of his eyes. Just like yours when you read—very earnest, very gentle."
Lu Xi nodded, half understanding. "And then?"
"And then Papa stayed by Mama's side ever after."
"Ever after?"
"Ever after."
Lu Xi thought about it, then asked, "And me? Where was I?"
"You were still up in the sky."
"Where in the sky?"
Su Qingyuan pointed at the glass dome. "Up in the sky, waiting until Papa and Mama were ready, and then you came down."
Lu Xi followed her finger to the sky. It had grown dark now, a deep blue, and the first star had begun to shine. "Is that star me?"
"Maybe."
Lu Xi smiled, her eyes curving into little crescents. She rolled over and hugged Su Qingyuan's arm. "Mama, I like this story."
"As long as you like it. Go to sleep now."
Lu Xi closed her eyes, her lashes trembling faintly. After a while, her breathing grew even—she had fallen asleep.
Su Qingyuan gently slipped her arm free, tucked her in, and got off the bed. Lu Shiyan stood by the window watching her, the cup of coffee in his hand already gone cold.
"That story you told her—was it true?"
"Most of it was true."
"That part about the rainy night—you said you got in the car because of my eyes?"
Su Qingyuan walked over and stood before him. "Mm. Your eyes. Very earnest, very gentle. Not like a bad person's."
Lu Shiyan looked at her and smiled. "That's not what you said at the time. At the time you said, 'Sorry to trouble you.'"
"That was just being polite."
"And now you're not being polite?"
"Now there's no need to be polite." Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder. "Now you're Lu Xi's papa."
Lu Shiyan reached out and wrapped an arm around her shoulders. "Su Qingyuan, thank you for telling me that story."
"You're welcome."
The aurora appeared in the dead of night. Su Qingyuan was gently shaken awake by Lu Shiyan. "The aurora." She opened her eyes. Across the glass dome, green light flowed—brighter than any time before, sweeping across the whole sky like a luminous river. She turned to look at Lu Xi. The little one had woken at some point and was staring up at the sky with wide eyes, her mouth open in a small O.
"Mama, it's moving."
"Mm. It's dancing."
"How long will it dance?"
"I don't know. Maybe a few minutes, maybe a few hours."
Lu Xi said nothing, and kept watching the flowing light overhead. Green, pink, purple, drifting slowly across the night sky like a living painting.
"Mama."
"Mm."
"The aurora is so pretty."
"Mm."
"Can we come and see it every year from now on?"
Su Qingyuan glanced at Lu Shiyan. He was looking at her too, a smile in his eyes. "Is that all right?" Su Qingyuan asked Lu Shiyan.
"All right."
Lu Xi smiled, reached out her hands, took Su Qingyuan's with her left and Lu Shiyan's with her right. "Then it's settled. We come every year. Pinky promise."
Su Qingyuan and Lu Shiyan exchanged a glance and reached out together to hook pinkies with Lu Xi.
"Pinky swear, cross my heart, no changing for a hundred years." Lu Xi's childish voice echoed through the glass igloo.
The aurora still flowed overhead. Su Qingyuan looked at her daughter's little face lit by its glow, and suddenly remembered, years and years ago, sitting on the floor of the attic in the old Su family house, reading a worn-out travel magazine. In it had been a photograph of the aurora. Back then she had thought: in this lifetime, I must go see it once. Not only had she seen it herself—she had brought her daughter along too. That girl drawing light on the attic floor probably never imagined that one day, her light would shine on so many people.
The next day, they built a snowman on the snowfield. Lu Xi named it "Lumi-Lumi," because it was made of snow, but when the sun shone on it, it sparkled and glowed. Lu Xi wrapped her own scarf around the snowman, stepped back to look, decided she wasn't quite satisfied, then took the scarf off and rewrapped it. "Mama, it doesn't have eyes."
Su Qingyuan pulled two black buttons from her pocket and handed them to Lu Xi. Lu Xi pressed the buttons into the snowman's face, then found a little twig for a nose.
"There. Does it look good?"
"It looks good."
Lu Xi nodded with satisfaction, then turned and ran off toward the snow in the distance, leaving a trail of tiny little footprints behind.
Lu Shiyan stood beside Su Qingyuan, watching their daughter race across the snow. "Su Qingyuan, would you say she's like you, or like me?"
"Like you."
"How is she like me?"
"The way she turns pages is like you. The earnest way she looks is like you. The stubborn way she gets is like you too."
Lu Shiyan turned his head to look at her. "The way she smiles is like you. The light in her eyes is like you. The kind way she is, that's like you too."
Su Qingyuan leaned against his shoulder, watching that little red figure in the distance frolicking through the snow. "Lu Shiyan, we're very lucky."
"Mm."
"It's not luck. We worked for it."
"Mm. We worked for it, and then the luck came."
Lu Xi ran back, a handful of snow cupped in her hands. "Mama, Papa, what are you talking about?"
"About you." Su Qingyuan crouched down and brushed the snow off her.
"What about me?"
"Saying you're like Mama, and like Papa too."
Lu Xi thought about it, then smiled. "Then I'm both of you."
Su Qingyuan looked at her, and her nose suddenly stung. "Mm. You're both of us."
After they returned to Nancheng, Su Qingyuan made a new series—three pieces. The first was a brooch, shaped as two large hands holding one small hand, using three metals—platinum, rose gold, and yellow gold. They represented Papa, Mama, and child. The second was a pair of stud earrings, one large and one small—the larger a moon, the smaller a star. The moon cradled the star, like a mother cradling her daughter. The third was a necklace, its pendant a teardrop-shaped moonstone with a halo of four layers—the same as the one she had worn at her wedding. But this time, the fourth layer was not "Future"—it was "Xi." Her daughter, her light.
She didn't release this series publicly, nor did she exhibit it. She placed the brooch on Lu Shiyan's desk, the earrings in her own jewelry box, and hung the necklace at the head of Lu Xi's bed. Others couldn't see them, but they could.
That was enough.
The days passed one by one. Lu Xi learned to walk, to run, to talk, to write. The first picture she drew was of a family of three—three stick figures holding hands, with a glowing yellow ball above their heads. She said it was the sun, or it could also be the aurora.
Lu Shiyan stuck this drawing on the wall across from his desk, so he could see it every time he looked up. Once, his assistant came in to deliver documents, saw the drawing, and paused. "President Lu, this is...?"
"My daughter drew it."
The assistant looked at the drawing, then at Lu Shiyan's expression, and tactfully asked no more, setting down the documents and leaving.
When Su Qingyuan heard about it, she laughed for a long time. "Your subordinates must think you've changed."
"Have I?"
"You have. Your office wall used to have a strategic map. Now it has a child's drawing."
Lu Shiyan looked at the drawing on the wall, the corner of his mouth lifting. "Strategic maps change every year. This one I never have to change."
Su Qingyuan looked at his earnest expression, and her heart softened.
The year Lu Xi turned four, the final stop of the global tour for Su Qingyuan's "Light Traces" collection came back to Paris. She brought Lu Xi along. It was Lu Xi's first time in Paris, and everything looked new to her. Beneath the Eiffel Tower, she tilted her head back so far that she didn't even notice her hat fall off. "Mama, it's so tall."
"Mm. It's called the Eiffel Tower."
"Why does it have to be so tall?"
"So that people can see it."
"See what?"
"See all of Paris. See where they stand."
Lu Xi nodded, half understanding, then bent down to pick up her hat, brushed off the dust, and put it back on.
Montmartre, the Wall of Love. Su Qingyuan took Lu Xi to see the wall covered with "I love you."
Lu Xi didn't recognize most of the words, but she recognized those three in Chinese. "Mama, what does this say?"
"'I love you.'"
"Who loves who?"
Su Qingyuan crouched down and pointed at the words on the wall. "Everyone loves everyone."
Lu Xi thought about it. "Then I love you."
Su Qingyuan's eyes grew hot. "I love you too."
"Papa loves you too."
"Mm. Papa loves me too."
Lu Shiyan stood behind them, watching this scene, saying nothing. But he took out his phone and snapped a photo, saving it into an album called "Xi." It was full of photos of his daughter—her first smile, first steps, first time calling out "Papa" and "Mama," first snowman, first aurora, first time in Paris.
He rarely posted on his social feed, but that day he posted a photo of the mother and daughter from behind, standing before the Wall of Love. The caption was just two words: "Mine."
The comments section exploded. Zhou Wanyin: "Ahhhh so sweet!!!" Qu Ya: "When are you bringing little Xi to see my exhibition?" Anna: "C'est beau (so beautiful)." Jean-Pierre: "Félicitations (congratulations)."
When Su Qingyuan saw that post, she smiled. She reshared it with the caption: "Mine too."
Beneath her reshare, Lu Shiyan replied with one line: "You're mine too." Su Qingyuan didn't reply, but her ears turned red.
On their last night in Paris, they stayed at the same little hotel from their first visit. Su Qingyuan stood at the window, watching the Eiffel Tower glittering gold against the night.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Do you still remember the first time we came to Paris?"
"I remember. You said, 'Paris is wonderful.'"
Su Qingyuan smiled. "You still remember what I said?"
"I remember. You said, 'The best part is that you brought me here.'"
Su Qingyuan turned to look at him. "How do you remember everything?"
"Because anything to do with you is worth remembering."
Su Qingyuan walked over and leaned against his shoulder. "Lu Shiyan, do you think we'll always be like this?"
"Like what?"
"Traveling together, watching the aurora together, looking at the tower together. With little Xi."
Lu Shiyan lowered his head, his lips against the crown of her hair. "We will. Always."
Outside the window, the lights of the Eiffel Tower flickered on and off, as if telling them—goodnight.
