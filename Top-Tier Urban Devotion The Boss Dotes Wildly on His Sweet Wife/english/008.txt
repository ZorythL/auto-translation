Chapter 8 — The Journey Home
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417510/72626210.html

On the flight home, Su Qingyuan made a decision.
She was going to truly build "Yanyi Culture" into something real.
When she'd agreed to join Lu Shiyan's company earlier, it had been more out of instinct than anything else—she trusted him, trusted that he would never do her harm. But as for what exactly she should do, and how, she hadn't really thought it through. Over these past few days in Milan, she'd met with Philippe, wandered through the design exhibitions, taken in countless brilliant works of design. It was as if someone had thrown open a window in her mind.
She didn't want to be merely a passive "investee."
She wanted to be a true founder.
By the time the plane touched down at the Capital's international airport, it was already two in the morning, Beijing time. Beyond the cabin window everything was pitch black, only the runway lights flickering in the night.
Su Qingyuan unbuckled her seatbelt and stretched. More than ten hours of flying had left her back and waist stiff, but her mind was unusually clear.
Lu Shiyan turned around from the row in front and glanced at her.
"Qin Zhou has arranged a car. It'll take you back to Yunxi first."
"And you?"
"There's something at the company. I need to swing by first."
Su Qingyuan frowned. "Going to the office at two in the morning?"
Lu Shiyan's expression didn't change at all, but his gaze flickered ever so slightly, as if he were weighing whether to tell her the truth.
"There's a problem with an acquisition. The other side suddenly backed out at the last minute. I need to handle it in person." He said it in a tone as flat as if he were remarking that the weather was nice today.
Su Qingyuan looked into his eyes. There was a faint layer of dark shadow beneath them—he hadn't really slept on the flight either, had been poring over documents the whole time.
"How long has it been since you slept?" she asked.
Lu Shiyan paused. "Not long."
Su Qingyuan didn't press further. She knew that asking would get her nowhere. This man would never admit to her that he was tired, never let his guard down in front of her, never let her worry. But the more he was like this, the more she felt some part of her heart being gripped tight—aching, sore, tender.
Once the plane had come to a full stop, the group disembarked. Wen Ruan walked ahead, yawning, Qin Zhou trailing behind with the luggage, while Lu Shiyan and Su Qingyuan brought up the rear.
The pre-dawn airport was utterly deserted, only the sound of their footsteps echoing through the cavernous arrivals hall.
As they reached the exit, Su Qingyuan suddenly stopped.
"Lu Shiyan."
Lu Shiyan turned around.
Su Qingyuan stepped closer, reached out, and gently straightened the collar of his coat. She'd done this once before—in Milan, with a loose thread on his shirt collar that hadn't really been there. But this time, her hand lingered a little longer, her fingertips trailing lightly along the edge of his collar, as if reluctant to leave.
Lu Shiyan's breath caught.
"Get some rest," Su Qingyuan said, her voice very soft. "Don't make me worry."
Lu Shiyan looked down at her.
The pre-dawn airport lights fell across his face, casting his features like an oil painting. His eyes were very deep, and in them was her reflection, along with some emotion she couldn't quite read.
"All right," he said.
Just two words. But Su Qingyuan heard everything hidden inside them—there was a promise, there was tenderness, there was restraint, there was all the earnestness a twenty-eight-year-old man held for a twenty-three-year-old woman.
Su Qingyuan let go and turned toward the car Qin Zhou had arranged.
Lu Shiyan stood where he was, watching her receding figure. She didn't walk fast, but every step was steady. Her back was held perfectly straight, her hair lifting faintly in the night wind.
She didn't look back.
But just before getting into the car, she paused, turned her face slightly, and gave him a glimpse of her profile.
The light struck her from the side, her silhouette as sharp and clear as a paper-cut.
Then she bent down and got into the car.
The sound of the car door closing echoed once through the empty airport hall, then faded into silence.
Qin Zhou came over and said cautiously, "Mr. Lu, the car's ready."
Lu Shiyan didn't move, still watching the spot where the car had vanished toward the exit.
"Mr. Lu?"
"...Let's go."
Qin Zhou followed behind Lu Shiyan, silently making a note to himself: today the boss had watched Miss Su leave for seven seconds longer than last time.
Back at Yunxi, Su Qingyuan didn't go straight to sleep.
She took a shower, changed into something comfortable, then sat down at her desk and opened her laptop.
The piece she would exhibit at her solo show in Milan wouldn't be a single work, but a whole series. She'd already settled on the theme—"The Rebirth Trilogy."
Part One: Fracture. About pain, about breaking, about the marks left behind by the grinding passage of time.
Part Two: Growth. About healing, about struggle, about the new shoots that push up out of the cracks.
Part Three: Bloom. About hope, about the future, about a brand-new self.
The theme was too vast—so vast that no single work could contain it. So she needed three, or even more—a complete series, each piece answering the others, building layer upon layer, like the three movements of a symphony.
In the document she typed the first line: "A fracture is not an ending. It is a beginning."
Then she stared at that line for a long time.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan: "Home yet?"
"Yes. And you? At the office?"
"Mm."
"Have you eaten?"
"Yes."
Su Qingyuan looked at that "Yes" and knew full well he was most likely lying to her. Three in the morning—where would he get food? But she didn't call him out on it. She knew this man had his own rhythm and his own limits; she didn't need to keep an eye on him every second.
"Get back to it, then. I'll draw for a while and then sleep."
"Don't stay up too late."
"You too."
The conversation ended there. Brief, plain, without a single sweet word. But to Su Qingyuan, this very plainness was its own kind of sweetness. As if some unspoken understanding already existed between them—neither of them needed to say much to know what the other was thinking.
She switched off her phone and turned back to the computer screen.
Her fingers tapped at the keyboard, ideas welling up like spring water.
She wrote for two full hours, from three in the morning until five, laying out the complete framework of "The Rebirth Trilogy." The core concept, the form of expression, the choice of materials, the spatial layout of every single piece—all of it now had a clear outline.
When she typed the last word, she leaned back in her chair and let out a long breath.
【Ding—Main quest updated: The Rebirth Trilogy.
Stage One: Complete the overall framework design. Progress: 100%.
Quest reward: Intelligence +2, new skill unlocked: Advanced Project Management.
Stage Two: Complete the first draft of the first work, "Fracture." Time limit: 30 days. Quest reward: Appearance +3, new skill unlocked: Advanced Materials Science.】
Su Qingyuan looked at the quest panel, the corner of her mouth curving up slightly.
Thirty days to finish the first work.
The timeline was tight, but she loved this feeling of being pushed forward by deadlines. For as long as she could remember, her best work had always been done under pressure. Not because she enjoyed pressure, but because pressure left her no time to hesitate, no time to doubt herself, no time to revise over and over. She could only trust her first instinct—and that instinct was usually the best.
She shut down the computer, went back to her bedroom, and lay down on the bed.
Before closing her eyes, she glanced at her phone. Lu Shiyan hadn't sent any new messages, but she noticed his WeChat status had changed to "On a business trip."
A business trip. Three in the morning—not the office, but a business trip.
Su Qingyuan's finger hovered above the screen for a moment, then she typed out a line: "Stay safe. Let me know when you get there."
After sending it, she set the phone down beside her pillow and closed her eyes.
Just before drifting off, she heard the system give a soft little chime.
【Host's heartbeat value: 85→89. Note: Only 6 points away from the "Deep Love" threshold. This system recommends that the Host—】
In her mind, Su Qingyuan said silently, "Recommend that I go to sleep."
【...Understood. Good night, Host.】
The next morning, Su Qingyuan was woken by a call from Qin Zhou.
"Miss Su, Mr. Lu asked me to pass along that he's gone to Shanghai to handle the acquisition. It'll take about three days. I'll be in charge of everything here at Yunxi—if you need anything at all, just reach out to me anytime."
Su Qingyuan rubbed her eyes, her voice still a little hoarse. "Has he arrived?"
"He got in at five this morning. He's already resting at the hotel."
Su Qingyuan glanced at the time—ten in the morning. He'd slept less than five hours.
"Has he had breakfast?"
Qin Zhou was silent for a second.
"Mr. Lu says he isn't hungry."
Su Qingyuan drew in a deep breath.
"Qin Zhou, do something for me."
"Go ahead."
"Find a reliable restaurant near his hotel and order a light breakfast—congee, some small side dishes, eggs. Nothing greasy, nothing spicy. Have them deliver it to his room before eleven. Don't tell him I ordered it."
Qin Zhou was silent for another second, then his voice carried a barely perceptible trace of a smile. "Of course, Miss Su."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan got up, washed, changed her clothes, and went to the study.
She was about to start drawing "Fracture."
It was the first piece of "The Rebirth Trilogy," and also the hardest. Because what it depicted wasn't something beautiful or polished—it was pain itself. She needed to take all the grievance, rage, loneliness, and despair of those twenty-three years and transform every bit of it into line and color, lay every bit of it bare on the page.
This wasn't merely a matter of technique—it was a matter of the psyche.
She had to tear those wounds open all over again.
Su Qingyuan picked up her pencil and closed her eyes.
Into her mind rose the cluttered attic of the Su household. At eight years old, her favorite thing had been to hide up there and draw. The attic's skylight was tiny, and sunlight poured in through that little opening, casting a square patch of light on the floor. Dust danced in the column of light, as if it were alive.
At twelve, Zhao Xiulan told her that drawing was "a waste of money" and threw all her pencils into the trash. She crouched beside the bin and picked them back out one by one. Some were already broken; she wrapped them in tape and kept using them.
At fifteen, Su Yurou took a work she'd spent a month on, submitted it to the school art exhibition, and won first place. When she went to explain the situation to the teacher, the teacher said, "Your sister draws quite well too. Just let her have this one."
At eighteen, her university acceptance letter "got lost," and Su Yurou went to Beijing and attended the school she'd wanted to go to.
At twenty-two, Lin Haoyu cheated on her and got together with her stepsister.
At twenty-three, Su Yurou stole her work and won the gold medal.
Scene after scene, as if someone were playing a film inside her memory.
Su Qingyuan opened her eyes, and her pencil fell to the paper.
Scratch, scratch, scratch—
The lines flowed out from her fingertips, sharper and more violent than ever before. All the emotions she usually hid so well now took shape in the strokes beneath her hand. Not graceful, composed lines, but twisted, broken lines, lines that looked as if they were struggling.
She drew for an entire day.
From ten in the morning until nine at night, with only two glasses of water in between and a single bowl of congee Qin Zhou had sent over. Her eyes were fixed on the page, her hand moving at lightning speed, her whole body as if possessed by something.
At nine that night, she finally set down her pencil.
On the paper was an abstract architectural sketch—a building growing up out of a fracture. Not out of ruins, but out of a crack. And those cracks weren't external, they were internal. As if the building itself had come alive, had wounds, and those wounds were bleeding.
Su Qingyuan looked at the drawing, and suddenly felt a sharp pain in her chest.
Not a physical pain.
It was that old pain from long ago, the one she'd pressed down into the deepest part of her heart, the one she'd thought had healed—suddenly surging back up, drowning her like a rising tide.
Her tears slid down soundlessly.
One drop, two drops, three drops.
She wiped at them with the back of her hand, but couldn't wipe them all away. The tears were like a broken dam—she couldn't stop them no matter what.
Her phone buzzed.
She picked it up to look. It was a message from Lu Shiyan.
"Did you order the breakfast?"
Su Qingyuan sniffled and replied with a single word: "Mm."
The other side's reply paused for a few seconds.
Then came a video call request.
Su Qingyuan looked at the green answer button on the screen and hesitated. The state she was in right now—red eyes, red nose, the not-yet-dry tear tracks still on her face—she didn't want him to see her like this.
But she pressed answer anyway.
The screen lit up. Lu Shiyan's face appeared, the background a business suite at the hotel. He sat at a desk, his shirt collar slightly open, his sleeves rolled up to his forearms.
He glanced at her, and his expression changed at once.
"What's wrong?" His voice was low and urgent.
"Nothing," Su Qingyuan said with a small smile. "I got too absorbed in the drawing. Just a bit emotional."
Lu Shiyan stared into her eyes for several seconds.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm?"
"You're crying."
Su Qingyuan's smile froze.
This man—he could always see right through her.
"While I was drawing 'Fracture,' I remembered some things from the past," she admitted honestly. "It's all right. Crying it out actually helps. I'd been holding it all in, and that was why I couldn't draw it."
Lu Shiyan was silent for a while.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"I'm not by your side. Take good care of yourself."
Su Qingyuan's nose stung again.
This man, in Shanghai, more than a thousand kilometers away, across a screen, hadn't said "don't cry," hadn't said "don't be sad," but "take good care of yourself." Because he knew there were past things he couldn't bear in her place, wounds she had to face on her own.
But he wanted her to know—he cared.
"I will," Su Qingyuan said. "You too. Don't push yourself too hard."
Lu Shiyan looked at the screen, the corner of his mouth curving slightly.
"All right."
The two of them looked at each other across the screen for a few seconds, neither of them speaking. That silence wasn't awkward—it was a very deep, very quiet kind of companionship.
"Go and rest," Lu Shiyan said first. "You've been drawing all day."
"How do you know?"
"Qin Zhou told me."
Su Qingyuan couldn't help laughing. "Is Qin Zhou your man or mine?"
Lu Shiyan thought about it. "Yours."
Su Qingyuan's heart skipped a beat.
That answer had come too fast. Faster than she'd been ready for.
"Good night," she said first.
"Good night."
The video call ended.
Su Qingyuan looked at the words "Call duration 4 min 32 sec" on the screen, pressed the phone against her chest, and closed her eyes.
Three days later, Lu Shiyan came back from Shanghai.
By the time he reached Yunxi, it was already eight in the evening. Su Qingyuan was in the study, working on the second version of the "Fracture" sketch. When the doorbell rang, she assumed it was Qin Zhou delivering dinner and ran over to open the door.
The door opened, and Lu Shiyan stood in the doorway.
He wore a black cashmere coat, a paper bag in his hand—strawberry cake again.
His hair was a little windblown and disheveled, the shadows under his eyes even darker than three days ago, but his eyes were bright, and the moment he saw her, those eyes seemed to ignite.
"You're back," Su Qingyuan said.
"Mm."
"Have you eaten?"
"No."
Su Qingyuan stepped aside to let him in. "I made congee. There's some left. Wait a moment, I'll heat it up."
As Lu Shiyan changed his shoes, his gaze swept across the whole room—the study lights were on, sketches scattered all over the floor, a half-eaten bowl of congee and a cup of gone-cold tea on the dining table. Compared to when he'd left three days ago, the place now had a certain air of being earnestly lived in.
Not tidy—alive.
In the kitchen, Su Qingyuan heated the congee, pots and pans clinking and clattering. Lu Shiyan leaned against the kitchen doorframe, watching her busy figure from behind.
She was wearing a white loungewear set, her hair carelessly tied at the back of her head, her apron knotted crookedly, like a schoolkid.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm." She didn't even turn around.
"Three days, and you've gotten thinner."
Su Qingyuan's hand paused.
"So have you," she said, her voice a little smaller. "You've barely slept, haven't you?"
Lu Shiyan didn't answer.
The congee was heated. Su Qingyuan brought it to the dining table, then stir-fried a light dish of seasonal vegetables and fried a poached egg. Lu Shiyan sat at the table, watching her bring out the food dish by dish, as if he were watching a performance.
"Eat," she said, handing him the chopsticks.
Lu Shiyan took the chopsticks and lowered his head to eat.
He didn't eat fast, but every bite was deliberate, as if he were savoring something precious. Su Qingyuan sat across from him, chin propped in her hands, watching him eat, the corners of her mouth curved.
"You're not eating?" he asked.
"I already ate. This is what I saved for you."
Lu Shiyan lifted his head and glanced at her. In that glance was something very complicated—there was emotion, there was tenderness mixed with ache, there was some soft feeling he wasn't quite willing to admit to.
He lowered his head and kept eating.
After the meal, Lu Shiyan went to wash the dishes. Su Qingyuan leaned against the kitchen doorframe and watched him, just as she had three days ago before she left for Milan.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Did the acquisition get sorted out?"
"It did."
"Did it go smoothly?"
"No. But it's resolved."
Su Qingyuan didn't press further. She knew there was nothing she could do to help with his work, but there was one thing she could do—make sure that while he was here, he was relaxed, comfortable, free of all those troubling matters.
"When you're done washing up, come take a look at my new drawings," she said.
Lu Shiyan set the last bowl in the drying rack, dried his hands, and followed her into the study.
Twenty-odd sketches were scattered across the study floor, all of them studies for "Fracture." Lu Shiyan crouched down and looked at them one by one. He wasn't just glancing—he was truly looking, lingering over each one for a long time, as if reading every detail within the drawing.
Su Qingyuan stood behind him, so nervous her palms were sweating.
Even though they were already that kind of close, even though he knew what she was capable of, every time she showed him new work she still got nervous.
Lu Shiyan finished looking at the last one, stood up, turned around, and looked at her.
"Su Qingyuan."
"Mm."
"In these three days, you've drawn what would take someone else three months."
Su Qingyuan was taken aback.
"And what's more," Lu Shiyan's gaze fell on the sketches, his voice low and earnest, "this isn't a question of technique anymore. It's a question of the heart. You've carved your own heart out and put it on the page."
Su Qingyuan's eyes reddened.
"Does it hurt?" Lu Shiyan asked.
Not "is it good," not "do you feel confident," but "does it hurt?"
Su Qingyuan's tears fell.
"It hurts," she said.
Lu Shiyan reached out and pulled her into his arms.
His embrace was warm, carrying the longing of three days apart, and a very deep, unspoken ache.
Su Qingyuan buried her face against his chest, listening to the beat of his heart, steady and strong, one beat after another.
"It'll be all right," Lu Shiyan's voice came from above her head, low and gentle. "These fractures will become your finest work."
Su Qingyuan closed her eyes, and her tears slid down soundlessly.
But the corners of her mouth were curved.
Because she knew he was right.
Three days later, Su Qingyuan received a call from Su Guoliang.
The number was an unfamiliar one she hadn't blocked. She picked up, and what she heard was Su Guoliang's weary, aged voice.
"Qingyuan, your mother's been hospitalized."
Su Qingyuan gripped the phone, saying nothing.
"Her high blood pressure flared up. The doctor says it's serious. Could you... could you come and see her?"
Su Qingyuan was silent for a long time.
"Su Guoliang."
"Mm?"
"Do you remember? The year I was eighteen, I ran a high fever, forty degrees, and you and your wife took Su Yurou off on a trip. I was home alone, burning up for three days, and in the end I crawled to the hospital by myself."
The line went silent.
"You didn't make a single phone call to me back then," Su Qingyuan said, her voice perfectly calm—so calm it held not a trace of warmth. "Now your wife is in the hospital, and you want me to go see her?"
Su Guoliang's voice trembled a little. "Qingyuan, she is your mother, after all..."
"She is not my mother," Su Qingyuan cut him off. "My mother died when I was three. That woman you married was never my mother."
A stifled sob came through the line.
Su Qingyuan didn't soften.
"Su Guoliang, I won't come to see her, and I won't come to see you either. From this day on, your affairs have nothing to do with me."
She hung up the phone and blocked that number too.
Then she sat at her desk, looking out at the Capital's gray, overcast sky, and lost herself in a daze for a while.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Lu Shiyan.
"What's wrong?"
Su Qingyuan was startled. How did he know? She glanced at the study doorway—no one there. Then she glanced out the window—the Maybach was parked down below, Lu Shiyan leaning against it, looking up at her window.
She hadn't told him, and he'd come again.
Su Qingyuan walked to the window and, across the dozens of meters between them, looked down at the man standing below, his face tilted up toward her.
She picked up her phone and typed a line: "Su Guoliang called. Said Zhao Xiulan was hospitalized and wanted me to go see her. I refused."
Lu Shiyan lowered his head to glance at his phone, then lifted it, his gaze crossing the dozens of meters to land on her.
"Well done," he replied.
Su Qingyuan looked at those two words and smiled.
Not a bitter smile, not a helpless smile, but a smile of release.
Because she knew that in this world, there was at least one person who would always stand on her side.
