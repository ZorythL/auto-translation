The Gathering Storm
On the third day after Su Qingyuan submitted her work, she received a phone call.
It was from the competition's organizing committee, and the voice on the other end was courteous and official. "Hello, Miss Su Qingyuan. We have received a letter of complaint alleging that your competition entry, 'Fracture and Rebirth,' bears suspicious resemblance to an unpublished concept draft created two years ago by the Italian designer Luca Bianchi. Under the rules of the competition, we need you to provide proof of your work's originality. In the meantime, your entry will be temporarily frozen, pending the conclusion of our investigation."
Su Qingyuan gripped her phone, her knuckles turning faintly white.
Plagiarism. Plagiarism again.
Those two words were like a venomous snake she could never shake off, coiled tight around her life. Every time she was about to rise, someone used those two words to press her back down.
"Who filed the complaint?" she asked.
"I'm sorry, the complainant's identity is confidential."
"I understand. What materials do you need for proof of originality?"
The other party sent over a list: a record of the creative process, the original manuscripts, timestamps, an explanation of the source of inspiration, and letters of certification from at least two industry experts.
Su Qingyuan hung up, sat down at her desk, and looked out at the Capital's gray, overcast sky.
She felt no anger, no grievance.
She simply found it laughable.
The complaint claimed she had plagiarized an unpublished work of Luca Bianchi's. Luca Bianchi was a renowned Italian architectural designer, and Su Qingyuan did indeed know him. Not through Lu Shiyan, but through a paper she had written back in her university days. That paper had analyzed three of Bianchi's signature works, and after Bianchi himself read it, he had contacted her by email; the two of them had exchanged thoughts on design philosophy several times.
An unpublished work of Bianchi's? She had never even laid eyes on it.
A complaint like this was either stupid or malicious. Or perhaps both.
[Ding—external threat detected: competition entry maliciously reported. The System recommends the host take the following actions: 1. Compile the timeline evidence of creation; 2. Contact Luca Bianchi personally to issue a statement of clarification; 3. Have Lu Shiyan apply pressure through official channels. This System has automatically backed up the complete record of the creative process, including screen recordings, scanned manuscripts, and revision history. The evidence has been packaged and can be retrieved at any time.]
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath.
She had evidence. She was afraid of nothing.
But she wanted to know who was pulling the strings behind this complaint.
The answer wasn't hard to guess.
Su Qingyuan picked up her phone and dialed a number.
"Wen Ruan, do me a favor."
"Go ahead." It was noisy on Wen Ruan's end—probably a film set.
"Find out for me whether Su Yurou has been in contact with Zhou Wantang lately."
"Zhou Wantang? That designer who came back from Milan? Sure, I'll ask around. But why not have your CEO Lu look into it? Wouldn't he be much faster than me?"
Su Qingyuan was silent for a second.
"He could. But this is something I want to figure out for myself first."
"All right. I'll let you know if I hear anything."
After hanging up, Su Qingyuan began organizing the evidence. The System's backup was meticulously complete—from her very first stroke to the final revision, the entire process had been recorded. Timestamps, IP addresses, device serial numbers, nothing left out. She packaged all the materials and sent them to the competition's organizing committee.
Then she opened her inbox and wrote an email to Luca Bianchi.
She wrote it in fluent Italian—an ability she had only discovered after the System unlocked her advanced language aptitude. It was as though a language had been implanted in her brain, the grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation all as clear and natural as a mother tongue. In concise, courteous language she explained the situation and asked Mr. Bianchi to issue a statement on her behalf, confirming that she had never had access to his unpublished work.
Less than ten minutes after she sent the email, a reply arrived.
Bianchi's reply was brief, but his stance was unmistakable:
"My dear Su, I have never shown you any unpublished work, nor have I ever heard of this 'Fracture and Rebirth' project. On the contrary, I consider you one of the most original young designers I have ever met. If someone questions whether your work plagiarizes mine, that is an insult to me—because your work is better than mine. The statement is attached and ready for use. Also, do you need me to come to China to testify for you? I can fly over at any time."
Su Qingyuan read that last sentence and couldn't help but smile.
This old Italian man had a temper as fiery as her own.
With Bianchi's statement and the System's record of her creative process, the so-called "plagiarism complaint" was like a paper wall—one push and it would collapse.
But Su Qingyuan did not hand over the evidence right away.
She wanted to see just how far this little play could be carried.
The next morning, Qin Zhou came to Yunxi.
His expression was a shade graver than usual.
"Miss Su, we've traced the IP address of the complaint letter. It came from the fixed IP of Zhou Wantang's studio. But Zhou Wantang herself denies being behind it, claiming it may have been the work of someone else at the studio."
Su Qingyuan leaned back on the sofa, a cup of warm water in hand, her expression as calm as if she were listening to someone else's story.
"Of course she'll deny it."
"What Mr. Lu wants to know is, how do you intend to handle this?"
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment. "Don't rush to strike back just yet. Wait until the organizing committee's investigation results come out. If they handle it fairly, I'll cooperate. If they side with the complainant—"
She paused, the corner of her mouth curving into a faint, cold arc.
"—then have Lu Shiyan pull the title sponsorship from the entire competition. He's the title sponsor, isn't he?"
Qin Zhou was taken aback for a moment, then laughed.
"Miss Su, you're becoming more and more like Mr. Lu."
Su Qingyuan raised an eyebrow. "Is that a compliment or an insult?"
"A compliment, of course," Qin Zhou said. "Mr. Lu's principle in everything is—either don't make a move at all, or make sure the other party never gets the chance to retaliate again."
Su Qingyuan lowered her head and took a sip of water.
What she didn't tell Qin Zhou was that the reason she wasn't rushing to humiliate them was that she was waiting for the right moment. The organizing committee's investigation results would be made public, and when that happened, she would throw out all the evidence at once, letting everyone see who was being falsely accused, who was making slanderous accusations, and who was orchestrating it all from behind the scenes.
By then, Zhou Wantang's reputation and Su Yurou's competition eligibility would both be utterly finished.
It wasn't that she didn't seek revenge.
She simply liked to take it at the best moment, in the most ruthless way.
Su Yurou thought victory was already in her grasp.
After the complaint letter was sent, she had been keeping her eye on the competition's official website. The next day, sure enough, she saw the status of Su Qingyuan's entry change to "Under Review—Suspected Dispute."
She was so thrilled she nearly jumped up.
"Mom! Look! Her work's been frozen!" Su Yurou rushed into the living room clutching her phone. Zhao Xiulan was watching a TV drama, and the moment she heard the news she leaned over.
"Really? That wretched girl is finally getting what she deserves?"
"Really! The website says 'suspected plagiarism, under investigation.'" Su Yurou laughed until her eyes narrowed into slits. "I always said it—how could someone who graduated from a second-rate university paint anything good? She must have copied it!"
Zhao Xiulan laughed too, but after a moment her laughter gave way to worry. "But doesn't she have that Lu fellow backing her? What if that Lu fellow helps her…"
"Mom, don't worry." Su Yurou spoke with total confidence. "However powerful Lu Shiyan is, he can't interfere with the results of the competition's investigation, can he? This is an international competition, not something his Lu family runs."
What she didn't know was that, although Lu Shiyan had not directly interfered with the investigation, he had done something far more ruthless—he had someone compile the source of the complaint letter, the IP address, and all associated accounts into a single report, and sent it straight to the email of Philippe Delort, the chairman of the competition's organizing committee.
The body of the email contained just one line: "Mr. Delort, someone is cheating in your competition. Would you like to see the evidence first, or her work first?"
Philippe's reply came quickly too: "Lu, just how many people has this little friend of yours offended?"
Lu Shiyan replied: "It's not that she offended anyone. It's that some people don't know who they're dealing with."
Philippe sent a sighing emoji, then said: "I'll handle it personally."
On the third day, the organizing committee's preliminary investigation results came out.
The results were not made public, but Su Qingyuan received an official notice from the committee: "Upon preliminary examination, the contents of the complaint letter are found to be in serious conflict with the facts. Miss Su Qingyuan's entry, 'Fracture and Rebirth,' is independently and originally created, with no suspicion of plagiarism whatsoever. The freeze on the work has been lifted, and it will proceed to judging as normal. The committee will address the malicious complaint separately."
Su Qingyuan finished reading the email, and the corner of her mouth curved up slightly.
She was not surprised, not even moved. Because she had known all along this would be the result.
But what happened next was the true main event.
That afternoon, an anonymous account posted a set of chat-log screenshots on a social media platform.
The screenshots showed a conversation between Zhou Wantang and Su Yurou spanning two full weeks. The contents were shocking—
Su Yurou: "Senior Zhou, I need you to design a competition entry for me. The theme is 'Rebirth,' and the style needs to be similar to my sister's. How much?"
Zhou Wantang: "Two million. Payment first, draft after."
Su Yurou: "Money's no problem. But I have one more request—my sister is competing too. Is there any way you can get her eliminated?"
Zhou Wantang: "What kind of way?"
Su Yurou: "Report her for plagiarism. Don't you know Luca Bianchi? Just say she plagiarized his unpublished work. No one will be able to prove otherwise anyway."
Zhou Wantang: "I can do that. But that's a separate matter—add another five hundred thousand."
Su Yurou: "Deal."
The moment the chat logs went out, the entire internet exploded.
#InternationalYouthDesignCompetitionComplaintIncident# shot to the top of the trending list, with views surpassing three hundred million in two hours.
Netizens' comments were overwhelmingly condemning Su Yurou and Zhou Wantang:
"Holy crap, this is too disgusting, right? Plagiarizing your own entry by hiring a ghostwriter, and then reporting someone else?"
"Two million to hire a ghostwriter, five hundred thousand to report her own sister—what kind of human garbage is this Su Yurou?"
"Zhou Wantang is shameless too. She's supposed to be a designer who came back from Milan, and yet she does something like this?"
"Su Qingyuan is so pitiful, isn't she? Her work was stolen by her own sister and she still got framed for plagiarism—what kind of hell-mode opening is this?"
#SuQingyuanIsSoPitiful# climbed right behind it to second on the trending list.
Netizens dug up Su Yurou's social media accounts, and the comment sections were nothing but abuse. Setting her account to private didn't help—the screenshots had long since flooded everywhere. Zhao Xiulan's phone number was leaked once again and her phone rang off the hook. The official account of Su Guoliang's company was buried under comments saying, "Your daughter has no shame."
Su Yurou sat in her own room, trembling all over.
"How could this… how could it have been leaked? Those chat logs only existed on my phone…"
What she didn't know was that those chat logs had been leaked by Zhou Wantang, "voluntarily."
Zhou Wantang was no fool. The moment she learned that Lu Shiyan had already intervened in the investigation, she immediately did two things: first, she shoved all the blame onto Su Yurou, claiming that she herself had merely been "tempted by money," while Su Yurou was the true mastermind; second, she voluntarily handed over the chat logs in exchange for not being held legally liable.
She even sent Su Qingyuan a private message: "Miss Su, I'm sorry. I was blinded by Su Yurou's money. I'm willing to apologize publicly and refund all the fees."
Su Qingyuan finished reading the message and replied with just two words: "Too late."
She was no saint; she would not pretend nothing had happened just because the other party "apologized." Zhou Wantang's name already stank throughout the industry. Her studio could not land a single project, and her clients all terminated their contracts. Once a designer's reputation is destroyed, her career is over.
Su Qingyuan would not kick someone when they were down, but neither would she reach out a hand to pull them up.
Everyone had to pay the price for their own choices.
As for Su Yurou—
Three days later, Su Qingyuan received a second email from the competition's organizing committee.
"Upon investigation, Miss Su Yurou's entry, 'Nirvana,' was found to have been ghostwritten by another party, and she herself does not possess the ability to complete the work independently. In accordance with the competition rules, Su Yurou's competition eligibility is hereby revoked, and she is permanently banned from participating in this event. At the same time, her ghostwriter, Ms. Zhou Wantang, will be placed on the industry blacklist and reported to partner institutions worldwide."
Su Qingyuan set down her phone and let out a long breath.
She did not go to see Su Yurou's reaction; she didn't need to. She knew her stepsister must be crying now, throwing a fit, smashing things, cursing everyone.
But all of it was Su Yurou's own choosing.
From the day she stole her work, from the day she hired a ghostwriter, from the day she reported her for plagiarism—every step had been walked by Su Yurou herself.
Su Qingyuan had merely stood there, doing nothing at all.
It was Su Yurou who had pushed herself off the cliff.
That evening, Lu Shiyan came to Yunxi.
He brought dinner, and he brought a piece of news.
"Philippe Delort has finished looking at your work. He asked me to tell you that he hopes to meet you in Milan next week."
Su Qingyuan's hand, in the middle of picking up food with her chopsticks, paused.
"Meet me? Or meet my work?"
"He wants to meet both." Lu Shiyan looked at her. "He said, 'This young person's talent should not be confined by a competition's rankings. She needs a bigger stage.'"
Su Qingyuan's heartbeat quickened.
Philippe Delort, a towering authority in the global design world—his recognition was worth more than any trophy. If he was willing to open that door for her, her life would be utterly transformed.
"I'll go," she said.
Lu Shiyan nodded. "The flight and hotel are already booked. We leave the day after tomorrow."
Su Qingyuan glanced at him and couldn't help but smile. "Did you already know all along that he'd want to meet me?"
Lu Shiyan did not deny it.
"Then why didn't you tell me ahead of time?"
"Because," he looked at her, his gaze calm and candid, "I wanted you to focus on your painting first. If I'd told you in advance that Philippe wanted to meet you, you might have been distracted, you might have started thinking, 'I have to create something he'll like'—but then it wouldn't be your work anymore. Your work should be painted only for yourself."
Su Qingyuan looked into his eyes, and her heart felt as though it were being gently cradled by a pair of warm hands.
This man was always, in places she couldn't see, taking care of everything for her.
"Lu Shiyan."
"Mm."
"Thank you."
Lu Shiyan was slightly startled.
It was the first time Su Qingyuan had ever said thank you to him.
She was not someone who liked to say thank you. In the Su household, she had said too many thank-yous, and in return had only received ever more relentless demands. Later she had learned never to say thank you to anyone, because saying thank you meant owing a debt of gratitude, and a debt owed would, sooner or later, have to be repaid.
But facing Lu Shiyan, for the first time she felt that saying thank you was not owing a debt, but rather that she truly, from the bottom of her heart, wanted to thank him.
Lu Shiyan looked at her, the corner of his mouth curving up slightly.
"No need to thank me," he said. "You deserve it."

Chapter Six: The Light of Milan
Two o'clock in the afternoon, Milan time. Su Qingyuan's plane touched down at Malpensa Airport.
Milan in November—the air was cold and damp. The sky was a pale shade of gray-blue, and the silhouettes of ancient buildings loomed faintly in and out of the thin mist, like an oil painting steeped in the passage of time.
Lu Shiyan had arranged a car in advance, and a deep blue Maserati was already waiting at the exit. The driver was a gray-haired Italian man in a well-tailored black uniform; seeing Lu Shiyan, he bowed slightly and said, in heavily accented English, "Mr. Lu, welcome back to Milan."
Su Qingyuan glanced at Lu Shiyan. "You come here often?"
"At least once a month." Lu Shiyan opened the car door for her. "The Lu Group has investments in Milan."
Su Qingyuan bent down and got into the car without pressing further. She knew Lu Shiyan's business empire spanned the globe, and Milan was merely one stop among many. But what she didn't know was that, in recent years, Lu Shiyan had been coming to Milan frequently not because of investments, but because he had been searching for someone who could complete the Yunlan Bay project for him.
He had sought out many designers. The top-tier, the young, the radical, the conservative—every one of them a celebrated figure in the industry. But every time, he had felt it wasn't right.
Until he saw Su Qingyuan's "Listening to the Sea."
In that moment he knew: it was her.
But he had told Su Qingyuan none of this. Some words, said too early, become pressure; said too late, become a pleasant surprise. He was waiting for the most fitting moment.
The car wove through the streets of Milan and came to a stop before a nineteenth-century building. It was a private design club, open only to the world's top designers; its members included the estate foundation of Zaha Hadid, Rem Koolhaas, and Philippe Delort himself.
The doorman wore a tailcoat, and as he pulled the door open he bowed slightly, his bearing as elegant as if he were performing an ancient court dance.
Su Qingyuan drew a deep breath and stepped through that door.
The corridor was long, and the walls on both sides were hung with design manuscripts—there was a sketch of Mies van der Rohe's Barcelona Pavilion, an elevation of Le Corbusier's Ronchamp Chapel, and several originals by contemporary masters she couldn't identify. Every one was an original; every one was priceless.
Su Qingyuan's steps slowed.
Not out of nervousness, but because the vitality these drawings radiated, vitality that transcended time, filled her with awe.
Lu Shiyan walked at her side, not urging her on.
He understood this feeling.
At the end of the corridor was a heavy wooden door. There was no nameplate on it, only a brass handle, polished bright by countless hands.
Lu Shiyan pushed the door open.
Beyond it was a spacious sitting room. A fire burned in the fireplace, the logs crackling. Several large architectural photographs hung on the walls, and light from outside the window filtered in through white gauze curtains, bathing the entire room in a soft glow.
By the window, a white-haired old man was bent over something, examining it closely.
At the sound of the door, he raised his head.
Philippe Delort. Seventy-three years old, a towering authority in the global fields of architecture and design, chairman of the judging panel of the International Youth Design Competition. His hair had gone completely white, but his eyes were astonishingly bright, like two polished dark gemstones, holding within them half a century of design wisdom.
His gaze slid past Lu Shiyan, then came to rest on Su Qingyuan.
He looked at her for a long time.
So long that Su Qingyuan felt her own heartbeat was about to stop.
Then Philippe stood up.
He was not tall, his back slightly stooped, wearing a dark gray wool sweater, a pair of khaki casual trousers, and a pair of worn brown leather shoes on his feet. He looked less like an emperor of the design world and more like a kindly old grandfather from next door.
He walked up to Su Qingyuan and extended his hand.
"Su," he said. Not Miss Su, not Su Qingyuan, just "Su." A single syllable, clean and crisp, as if they had already known each other a long time.
Su Qingyuan took his hand.
The old man's hand was thin, the knuckles pronounced, but warm and strong.
"Mr. Delort, hello."
Philippe did not let go, but fixed those astonishingly bright eyes upon her.
"Do you know why I wanted to meet you?" he said, in heavily French-accented English.
Su Qingyuan thought for a moment. "Because of my work?"
"No." Philippe released her hand, turned, and took a stack of drawings from the table—her very own "Fracture and Rebirth" series.
He turned to the first one, the overall rendering she had painted. A building growing up out of the ruins, old brick walls and new steel structures interwoven, like a giant rising again from its wound.
"Because this work," Philippe said, "made me cry."
Su Qingyuan was stunned.
"I have lived seventy-three years, looked at design drawings for fifty years, and never once cried over any work." Philippe lifted his head and looked into her eyes. "But your work made me cry. Not because of beauty—I've seen too many beautiful things. It's because there is something in your work called 'truth.' It wasn't learned from books, it wasn't copied from case studies; it grew out of your own life."
Su Qingyuan's eyes reddened.
Philippe pointed at the cracks in the drawing. "These cracks weren't designed. The cracks were already there, inside your heart. You merely painted them out."
Su Qingyuan's tears finally fell.
This French old man, whom she had never met before, had seen straight through her in three minutes. Seen through twenty-three years of grievance, seen through all her scars, seen through the words she had hidden behind those lines and never dared to speak aloud.
"Mr. Delort…" Her voice was a little hoarse.
Philippe patted her shoulder gently, like soothing a child who had been wronged.
"Su, your talent is a gift God gave to the design world. But talent is not for showing off—it is for healing. You must remember this: every stroke you paint today could become someone's light tomorrow."
Su Qingyuan nodded firmly.
Lu Shiyan stood off to one side, watching this scene, his gaze as soft as melting sugar.
He had met Philippe many times, but he had never seen the old man treat a young person so earnestly. Philippe was famous for his severity; his praise was more precious than gold, and his criticism sharper than a knife.
But at this moment, the look in his eyes as he regarded Su Qingyuan was like a grandfather looking at the granddaughter he was proudest of.
"Lu," Philippe suddenly turned his head and looked at Lu Shiyan, "you come over here too."
Lu Shiyan walked over.
Philippe extended his hands and patted both of them on the shoulder at once, like an old pastor blessing a newlywed couple.
"You two—one is the future of the design world, the other is the conscience of the capital world. Together, the two of you can do many remarkable things." The old man smiled, baring a row of neat white teeth. "But there's one thing I want to remind you of, Lu—if you dare to bully her, I'll fly over and beat you up."
Rarely, the corner of Lu Shiyan's mouth curved. "Mr. Delort, you might not be able to beat me."
Philippe burst out laughing, his laughter loud and hearty, enough to make even the fire in the fireplace leap.
Su Qingyuan watched the rare, relaxed look on Lu Shiyan's face, and the corner of her own mouth curved up as well.
Outside the window, Milan's twilight was deepening.
The ancient city was gilded with a layer of gold in the setting sun, and in the distance the spires of the Duomo loomed faintly in and out of the thin mist.
Su Qingyuan stood at the window, looking out at this unfamiliar city, yet her heart felt extraordinarily steady.
Because she knew that no matter where she went, the man standing behind her would always hold up a piece of sky for her.
And in her hand, she grasped the pen that could change the world.
