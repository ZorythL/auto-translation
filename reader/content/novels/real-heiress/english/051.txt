Chapter 51

Apart from the man in glasses and the man who'd been shoved, the other tourists hadn't actually suffered any real harm—the worst of it was a slightly soured mood and an extra trip to the police station. So when they'd heard thirty percent off, they were already quite satisfied. But the moment the words "for free" were spoken, it stirred up the greed in them.

A whole trip out, and they might get it all for free—that would make the journey more than worth it.

So when everyone heard that the man in glasses had wrangled such a big benefit on their behalf, they fell in behind his demand one after another. "Yeah, give it to us free."

Meng Wan's eyes narrowed slightly. She studied the man for a moment, then the corners of her lips curved up. "All right then. In that case, let's first head to the hospital and get an official assessment of the injuries. Once the results come back, I'll keep them on file, and then we can talk about compensation."

If they wanted it free, then let's drag it out and see who could outlast whom.

The man in glasses knit his brows. "How long do the assessment results take?"

They had to go home tomorrow.

Meng Wan made something up off the top of her head. "Two or three days at the least, a little over a week at most."

"That won't do. We're leaving tomorrow—we've already bought our train tickets. We haven't got time to sit around waiting on some assessment report of yours."

A tourist behind him chimed in, "Right, and I have to be back at work the day after tomorrow."

"My eldest sister's getting married three days from now. I absolutely have to be back tomorrow."

Meng Wan shrugged and said helplessly, "Then there's nothing to be done. I'm only going by the rules—in cases like this, everything has to be kept on file. The thirty-percent discount wouldn't need filing, but I'd still have to write up a detailed account of the situation, submit it to my superior for a signature, and only then could I get the funds approved."

Hearing how complicated Meng Wan made it sound, the man in glasses frowned. "We only settled the tour fee this morning. Why don't you just refund it to us directly? Why does it have to be so much trouble?"

"That won't work. Once that money's paid, it counts as having entered our travel agency's books—it's public funds now, and withdrawing public funds of course requires authorization. Otherwise wouldn't I be embezzling public money for my own use? You know how it is, going back to work yourselves—I can't very well lose my own rice bowl."

"And how long does this authorization of yours take?"

The man in glasses ran the numbers in his head. The tour fee was five hundred fifty a head, ten people in all, so five thousand five hundred total. Thirty percent off came to one thousand six hundred fifty—a hundred sixty-five back per person.

But if they waited on that so-called assessment report, even reckoning just two days, food and lodging alone would run at least seventy or eighty per person per day, on top of the delay to their schedule. He had an important meeting the day after tomorrow that bore on his promotion—he couldn't afford to lose the time.

Thirty percent off, then. Better than nothing. And after all, he'd only suffered a minor scratch, nothing serious.

Hearing the shift in his tone, Meng Wan answered, "Authorization also takes two or three days—"

Before she'd even finished, the man in glasses anxiously cut her off. "That takes that long too?"

"Don't get worked up—let me finish." Meng Wan soothed his temper. "It's true the authorization takes two or three days. But, considering everyone's circumstances, I'll put the money up myself first—I'll front it to the travel agency. That way I can guarantee you all get home on time, with no work missed and no wedding missed."

"It's not my wedding, it's my eldest sister's."

"Yes, yes, yes—no missing your eldest sister's wedding." Meng Wan played up her plight. "Now, I may be the head of Luo Zhigang's travel agency, but I'm only a small fish—there are bigger bosses above me. I made this thirty-percent call today on my own, without clearing it with my superior, so how much will actually get approved is honestly anyone's guess. I'll shoulder that risk on everyone's behalf. And to be frank, fronting this money out of my own pocket is no small sum either. If you're all agreeable, I'll go home right now to get the cash, then come by the hotel later and refund each of you one by one. How does that sound?"

Luo Zhigang watched, dumbstruck, as Meng Wan spun her earnest nonsense. Wasn't she the highest authority at the travel agency? Where was there a bigger boss above her? And since when did their agency have all these rules? The tour fee was right here in his pocket—it hadn't even been booked yet. They could just hand it straight back. Why on earth did she need to put up her own money?

But he knew this whole mess was his own doing, and everything Meng Wan was doing was meant to reduce the compensation he'd cost them. So he couldn't go butting into her negotiation.

Hearing Meng Wan put it that way, the man in glasses lost his appetite for haggling. Forget it, this would do. He was a grown man—he could hardly bully a young girl into coughing up more than five thousand.

The others fell silent too. Everyone out earning a living had their own hardships; since nothing too serious had come of it, the decent thing was to let it go. Besides, this young girl was willing to take on the risk herself and put up the money for them—that was sincerity enough. Push any harder and she might not even agree to the thirty percent anymore. As things stood, this was already a good outcome.

Meng Wan's little ploy of playing the victim worked well enough, and the matter was finally resolved peacefully. The two sides reached a settlement and signed an agreement drawn up by the officer. Meng Wan had Luo Zhigang take the tourists back to the tour bus first—she still had something to take care of.

The jade shop, as the fuse that had set off this whole brawl, had naturally been brought to the police station too. Seeing that Meng Wan had settled everything, the shop's owner, Jin Muhai, tried to slip away on the sly. He had no intention of footing any bill.

But Meng Wan wasn't about to let him off so easily. "Boss Jin, please hold on a moment!"

Jin Muhai's heart skipped. The foot he'd just lifted to leave shrank sheepishly back. He knew there was no dodging this reckoning with Meng Wan.

He turned around, beaming at her. "Does Guide Meng have some further instruction?"

He'd only learned today that Meng Wan had become the head of the travel agency Luo Zhigang worked for. The two of them had been cooperating for a long time, and that agency had never had a boss before.

"Boss Jin, what your shop did today wasn't very decent." Meng Wan didn't mince words. "You know perfectly well what the quality of your goods is. Most tourists may not know how to appraise jade, but you're bound to run into the odd expert like today's. Are you going to pin the blame on us guides every single time?"

Jin Muhai gave a sly chuckle and shrugged it off. "Our shop does bear part of the responsibility for today's incident. It was the new shop assistant—she's inexperienced, doesn't know how to smooth things over. I've already given her a severe scolding!"

Jin Muhai didn't take this travel-agency head the least bit seriously. Wasn't she just a girl barely out of her teens? He could fob her off with any old excuse.

Meng Wan let out a soft scoff. "When it comes to commissions, would a new hire really know that much? Without your instruction, would she dare go blurting out trade secrets like that?"

Seeing Meng Wan press her point so relentlessly, refusing to give ground, Jin Muhai realized she was no pushover. He switched tactics, and his form of address along with it. "Manager Meng, now you're misjudging me. I've run this jade shop for how many years—you think I don't understand a simple thing like this? Besides, we'll be working together again in the future. I'm counting on you to bring me plenty more tourists."

"There won't be a future." Meng Wan said gravely. "On behalf of our travel agency, I'm terminating our partnership. From now on, the groups our agency brings will no longer visit your jade shop. Are you satisfied with that?"

"That won't do!" Jin Muhai panicked. "Manager Meng, how about this—my shop will cover the losses we caused this time, but our cooperation has to continue."

The reason Jin Muhai had put his shop assistant up to saying what she'd said was that he knew Luo Zhigang was an honest, simple sort—he could make an honest man swallow the wrong in silence and cover up the problem with his jade. That had been his calculation.

But he hadn't reckoned on the travel agency now having a boss like Meng Wan, who counted every grain of rice. Making people eat losses on his behalf wouldn't be so easy anymore.

"This money should have been yours to pay from the start. But for old times' sake, I won't stoop to being as shabby as you. People can grab an advantage once, but no one gets to grab it every single time. Walk by the river often enough and you're bound to get your shoes wet."

"You—!" Jin Muhai's face went red with held-in fury. He'd lived half his life and now he was being lectured by a young girl—it was more than his pride could bear.

"I wish your business nothing but growth—and may every cent you earn be honest money."

With that, Meng Wan boarded the tour bus without looking back.

She'd long since wanted to end the arrangement with this jade shop, and this was the perfect chance to spell it out. Since Jin Muhai was so shameless, there was no need for her to spare him any face either.

After dropping everyone off at the hotel, the man in glasses spoke up again. "Manager Meng, are you still giving us those jade pendants you mentioned?"

"I am." Meng Wan smiled. "Tonight I'll bring the money and the pendants over together. But don't get your hopes up too high—they're just ordinary jade pendants, not worth much, but they're a token of our goodwill. If you like them, you can even take them home as gifts for friends."

At that the man in glasses nodded. "Then we'll wait for you at the hotel. And mind you, we know your travel agency's phone number and address."

The implication being: if Meng Wan didn't show, they'd come find her in person.

Meng Wan smiled. "Rest assured, everyone. I, Meng Wan, am not a woman who goes back on her word. If I say I'll bring them, I'll bring them."

By the time she left the hotel, the sun was nearly down. Meng Wan hadn't even had time for lunch, and now that the matter was settled and her taut nerves had loosened, her stomach started to act up.

Pedaling along, she scanned the roadside for a restaurant. Rounding a corner, she spotted a noodle shop with the rich aroma of beef-bone broth wafting out. This was the place.

She'd just parked her bicycle when, out of the corner of her eye, she caught Luo Zhigang riding up after her. Seeing Meng Wan stop, he squeezed his brakes and put a foot down too.

Meng Wan had started out with a belly full of anger, but after a whole afternoon of wrangling, her mouth was parched and her stomach was rumbling and grumbling—she no longer had the heart to be angry with him.

"Come have a bowl of noodles with me."

He'd been running around all day too; he was surely hungry.

Luo Zhigang, for once, was the picture of obedience, and followed Meng Wan inside with his head hanging and his face downcast.

Meng Wan glanced at the handwritten menu on the wall and ordered two bowls of beef noodles and a plate of cold appetizers.

When it came time to pay, Luo Zhigang scrambled to cover it, and Meng Wan didn't stand on ceremony with him. Maybe footing the bill for these two bowls of noodles would make him feel a little better.

It wasn't quite mealtime yet, so the shop wasn't crowded. Meng Wan picked a seat by the window and sat down. Luo Zhigang waited for the noodles to be ready, then carried them over.

The moment the noodles arrived, Meng Wan dug in red-faced and ravenous. Noodles alone didn't quite satisfy her, so she peeled two cloves of garlic and handed one to Luo Zhigang.

Luo Zhigang had his head down, wolfing down his noodles. When he saw the clove of garlic he paused, dumbfounded, then took it and bit off a big chunk with his front teeth, his cheeks bulging, chewing noodles and garlic together with gusto.

Noodles without garlic lose half their flavor—and these noodles were wonderfully chewy, the broth fresh and rich. Who'd have thought there was a noodle shop this good tucked away in this corner of town?

The two of them ate, a mouthful of noodles followed by a bite of garlic. The bowls, big as faces, hit bottom at almost the same moment. Meng Wan picked up the last bite of the cold dish and set down her chopsticks, satisfied.

"Let's have a talk."

Luo Zhigang immediately laid his chopsticks across his bowl, swallowed the last mouthful of noodles, and sat ramrod-straight like a schoolboy waiting for his teacher to deliver a scolding.

Meng Wan put on her boss's demeanor and said sternly, "Do you understand where you went wrong?"

Luo Zhigang spoke up dispiritedly. "I shouldn't have come to blows with a tourist. But I really didn't mean to—someone shoved me, and I hurt him by accident."

"That's not what I'm talking about."

So he still didn't know where the real problem lay.

Apart from the fight, Luo Zhigang felt he'd genuinely done nothing wrong—everything else had been done exactly by the book.

Meng Wan didn't want to dance around it with him. "Didn't I warn you before? I asked you not to take tourists to spend money of your own initiative."

"Now that you mention it, maybe you did." At the time it had gone in one ear and out the other; he hadn't taken it to heart at all. "But every other guide does it too. Aren't you a fool if you turn down money that's right there for the taking?"

He honestly didn't understand why Meng Wan kept harping on a thing everyone tacitly agreed was fine.

"I never said you couldn't earn it. I only told you not to take tourists to spend money of your own accord, or pressure them into buying. And don't pretend you haven't noticed—the forced-spending racket among Yuyang's guides has gotten very bad. I think the higher-ups will crack down on it sooner or later, and I don't want a guide from our agency getting made an example of."

"Well, you can rest easy on that score." Luo Zhigang puffed out his chest. "I've never once forced a tourist to spend."

Meng Wan knew he hadn't. The time she'd run into him at the jade shop, she'd seen that this fellow, for all his eagerness to earn money, still respected the customers' wishes.

"I believe you."

Hearing Meng Wan say that, Luo Zhigang felt a tightness swell in his chest, and a rush of guilt came over him. He'd held a prejudice against her before, thinking she wasn't fit to run the travel agency—that if he'd had that kind of money, he could've done it too.

But today, Meng Wan had both settled his dispute with the tourists and put that two-faced scoundrel Boss Jin in his place. The "softness" she'd shown the tourists and the "hardness" she'd turned on the scoundrel—she'd switched between the two with effortless ease.

In his heart, Luo Zhigang gave Meng Wan his stamp of approval: she was made of boss material.

"I'm sorry for how I treated you before. And as for the compensation money this time—dock it from my wages. If you want to fire me, I won't hold any grudge. Once I find a new job, I'll pay back a portion every month."

Meng Wan crossed her arms over her chest. She wanted to laugh but had to keep up her dignified bearing as the boss, so she feigned sternness. "That money is rightly yours to pay. So starting tomorrow, you won't have any time off for the next two months. I'll trouble you to take on a couple of extra groups and earn it back."

Luo Zhigang asked uncertainly, "You mean—you're still letting me keep working at the agency?"

"Of course. You're a key member of our travel agency. Summer's almost here, so you'd better pull yourself together. By the time this summer's over, I want us to have doubled the agency's earnings."

On the ride over following Meng Wan, Luo Zhigang had run through every possibility in his head, and the likeliest one, he'd figured, was getting fired. He and Meng Wan weren't on good terms; maybe she'd long wanted to let him go and had simply lacked an excuse—and now he'd handed her one, perfectly justified.

But here was Meng Wan calling him a "key member," not only keeping him on but lining up plenty of work for him. He'd been measuring a gentleman's heart with a small man's yardstick.

"I'm sorry." Luo Zhigang bent at the waist and bowed his head low.

Meng Wan read many layers of meaning into that single apology. Let everything that had come before this day vanish like smoke along with that one "I'm sorry."

"Are you willing to work with me to make our travel agency better and better?"

Meng Wan felt these words were more heartening than a simple "It's all right."

Tears brimming in his eyes, Luo Zhigang nodded hard. The emotion had welled up and he was just about to swear his loyalty when a loud "crash" sounded behind him, giving his liver such a fright it trembled.

When he turned to look, he saw a cook in an apron, furious, hurling a basin to the ground with all his might.
