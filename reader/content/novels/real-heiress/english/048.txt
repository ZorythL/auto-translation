Chapter 48

Meng Wan was the sort of person who, once she decided to do something, did it without delay. Early the next morning she rode her bike to the hotel where James was staying.

At the hotel, she'd meant to go straight up, knock on James's door, hand over the gift, and explain herself clearly. But once she reached his door, she dithered, unable to bring herself to knock after all.

She wasn't as brave as she'd imagined.

Meng Wan was terribly afraid the one to open the door would be that woman. If the woman was in James's room this early in the morning, it would confirm that things between the two of them had already grown very intimate.

She wavered for a good long while, but in the end she didn't have the courage to face that possibility, so she simply went back down to the hotel lobby to wait. James would have to come out sooner or later, she figured.

Wait for him she did—but to her surprise, James didn't come out from inside. He came in from outside.

He'd spent the night elsewhere.

The moment James stepped into the lobby he caught sight of Meng Wan, a little surprised that she'd turn up here at this hour.

"Meng Wan, what brings you here?" he asked, walking toward her with a smile.

Meng Wan forced up a smile and explained, "I came to give you your birthday present. The other day, by the time I got there you'd already left. It was all my fault—I let myself get so busy I forgot. You must have waited a long while."

"As long as you're all right." James looked at her, smiling. "I didn't wait long that day, actually—something urgent came up afterward and I left early. The next day I went to Lin Dong's place looking for you, and Lin Dong said you'd already moved back to your own home."

So he had come looking for her, then.

"Oh, right!" James said brightly. "I heard you've taken over the travel agency—you're the manager now."

"Manager?" Meng Wan turned the slightly unfamiliar word over. "Honestly, I'm not even sure what my position is. By seniority I'm the agency's youngest hand, and by age I'm the youngest too. We don't have clearly defined ranks either. In everyone's eyes, I suppose I'm just the one who runs things."

James was about to say something, then realized he had too much to say—and that saying it here in the middle of the lobby didn't seem quite right.

"Let me treat you to a coffee. A new café just opened on Nancheng Street—we can talk over a cup."

Meng Wan had meant only to explain herself and then leave, but a few words from him swayed her, and now she didn't want to go after all.

"All right, I'm in your hands, then."

What sort of bewitching potion had this man slipped her?

Meng Wan was someone who loved trying new things. Whenever a new shop or entertainment spot opened anywhere in Yuyang City, she was always among the first to go.

But this life around, she'd poured herself heart and soul into work and rather lost touch with all that. The café James mentioned, she'd been to in her past life. She didn't care for the taste of coffee, but she loved the smell of it.

So every time she went to a café, she'd order just one cup to breathe in, and in the end barely drink a few sips. She loved this café's décor and atmosphere—it was a fine place to talk.

"What will you have?" James said, flipping through the menu.

"A pour-over, I think."

Coffee these days wasn't like in later years with so many varieties—there were only a few plain, basic options.

James ordered one pour-over and one Blue Mountain, and got Meng Wan a slice of chocolate cake besides.

"What were you about to say just now?" Meng Wan brought up the topic herself.

James straightened up and said in earnest, "I wanted to talk to you about company management."

Meng Wan blinked. She'd run through all sorts of things he might say in her mind—and never once imagined what he wanted to talk about was actual "business."

"We're a travel agency about as small as they come—can that even be called a 'company'?"

In Meng Wan's mind, a company was the sort of place set in tall buildings, full of people in crisp suits. Her shabby little travel agency didn't come anywhere near the word "company"—it was a work unit at best.

James gave a helpless laugh. "As long as this travel agency is privately held by you and registered on the books, then it's a company. You're the company's legal representative, and that means you're responsible for managing it."

Meng Wan repeated his words under her breath. "I'm the company's legal representative. I'm responsible for managing the company."

"Exactly. The leader of a company is responsible for the whole of its personnel and organizational management, financial management, operational management, and risk management. In a large company there'd be dedicated departments for each of these, but at the scale your agency is now, you'll probably need to handle all of it hands-on yourself."

Meng Wan tried to sort what he'd said into categories. "So—I assign tasks to the guides, I have Accountant Bi square the books, I get new furniture for the agency. That's all this management you're talking about?"

"Exactly."

To Meng Wan these had felt like petty, fiddly chores, yet James had given each of them a professional name, and all at once her work seemed lofty and impressive.

This was just how people got talked into working hard.

"I'm not worried about the rest. What worries me most is the personnel and organizational management." James put his finger right on the heart of his concern. "Before, you and the others were all colleagues, so you were on an equal footing. Now you've become the leader, which makes you their superior—and that sudden shift is something you yourself surely haven't adjusted to. I'm sure your subordinates don't yet see you as a leader with authority over them either. In a situation like that, you'll get employees who won't accept your management and may even do things that go against your instructions."

His words made Meng Wan think of Luo Zhigang. She'd noticed long ago that, ever since her status changed, the one most resistant to it was Luo Zhigang. He'd never said so outright, but whenever she assigned him a task, he was always cold and indifferent, barely deigning to respond.

Before, Meng Wan had simply put it down to his personality—so long as he did his work well, it wasn't much of a problem. But hearing James put it this way today, it seemed Luo Zhigang did have a grievance about her leading him.

He didn't want to submit to her management.

Meng Wan asked humbly, "So what should I do?"

This was something she had no experience with at all. In her past life all she'd known was eating and drinking and having a good time; it had never once occurred to her that she might one day lead other people.

Taking Meng Wan's temperament into account, James offered his advice. "First, you have to establish a clear concept of the superior-subordinate relationship, and you start with how people address you. However unwilling they are in their hearts to acknowledge you as their leader, if they say it often enough, the fact that you're the leader gets internalized in their subconscious, and they'll start factoring in your wishes as the boss before they make any decision."

Forms of address, Meng Wan murmured to herself. So from now on she'd have the staff call her "Manager Meng."

"Sir, miss, here are your coffees and dessert."

James nodded, signaling the waiter to set them down.

He went on. "Second, you can't go on getting along with everyone like a friend the way you used to. A leader is a leader. Don't let your subordinates think you're a soft touch, or they'll go over your head and make decisions on their own. To put it in your terms—you have to carry yourself with a leader's authority."

This one was a bit hard for Meng Wan. She wasn't the type to stand on ceremony to begin with; she was easygoing and casual with everyone.

"Last, you need to set up paths for advancement and a system of rewards and penalties for your employees, so they get the benefits and the sense of self-worth they're due from their work. That way they'll do their jobs conscientiously and bring the company the greatest returns."

This one Meng Wan had thought of too. Right now her agency had only a handful of guides, so some issues hadn't yet surfaced—things like how to assign tour groups, what the commission rates should be, and so on.

She'd have to lay a solid foundation first, so that there'd be rules to follow down the line.

Every point James made struck right at her weak spots. So being a leader was a discipline in itself, with all these subtleties and intricacies. She'd thought becoming a leader just meant ordering other people around and reaping the rewards while she sat back. It seemed being a leader was no easy job after all.

"Thank you, James—you've taught me so much today." Meng Wan felt as though she'd just sat through a master class, and a free one at that.

"Any time you have questions, feel free to talk it over with me. We can compare notes on what it takes to be a leader."

Meng Wan waved a hand. "I couldn't compare myself to you. How big is my travel agency? You've got far more people to manage than I do."

"The principles are all the same." James raised another point. "Once you have a lot of employees, you have to learn to delegate authority. Just keep the number of people you directly manage within a count you can handle, build a complete organizational framework, and manage downward level by level. At that point, what you'll need to consider is the selection of talent—what kind of person suits what kind of post. That makes being the leader a great deal easier on you."

Meng Wan nodded and quietly committed everything James said to memory.

"All right, I've said everything I came to say. Now hand it over."

With that, James raised an eyebrow at her and held out his palm.

Meng Wan scratched her head, puzzled. Was he asking for tuition?

She mulled it over, then fished a wad of cash from her pocket, hesitating, unsure how much she ought to give.

Her befuddlement startled a laugh out of James, and he gave the table a light pat with his outstretched hand. "Not money—my birthday present!"

"Oh no, how could I forget about that!"

Meng Wan was tickled by her own foolishness. The very first thing she'd said when they met was that she'd come to give him his birthday present, and then she'd gotten so caught up in the lesson that she'd forgotten all about it, even making him hold out his hand for it. What a scatterbrain.

She quickly rummaged through her bag, drew out a square wooden box, and placed it in James's palms with both hands.

James cupped the box and looked it over carefully, then, full of anticipation, lifted the lid—and on the base, a small dog, exquisitely carved and translucent through and through, met his eyes.

"Wow!" James shook his head in admiration. "This is absolutely the favorite birthday present I've ever gotten!"

Meng Wan breathed a sigh of relief. "As long as you like it."

When a gift you've prepared delights its recipient so much, the giver is all the happier for it.

James drew the little dog closer and studied it intently. "It's even grinning with its mouth all crooked. The carving's so fine, and so full of character—you really went to a lot of trouble. Thank you."

The bottom of the dog was even carved with his name—clearly something Meng Wan had had custom-made.

"It wasn't much trouble, really. The craftsman's skill deserves all the credit." A little shy, Meng Wan picked at her fingers.

James carefully replaced the lid, his voice light and cheerful. "Once my new place is done being renovated, I'll set it on my desk."

"Your new place?" Meng Wan asked. "You're not staying at the hotel anymore?"

"I bought an apartment in a new development downtown. Once it's renovated, I'll move in."

A thought stirred quietly in Meng Wan's mind. Around Yuyang, people usually only moved into a new place when they were about to marry. Could it be that James and that woman had already reached the point of talking marriage?

Her chest tightened, and Meng Wan drooped, listless and downcast. "Then... I wish the two of you every happiness."
