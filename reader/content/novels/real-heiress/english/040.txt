Chapter 40

Meng Wan gave Xiaohai a few instructions, asking him to help look after the Zhou family, and then rushed straight off to the travel agency without a moment's pause.

When she arrived, the office was ablaze with light—for the first time, every last person was present in the room together.

She'd met Luo Zhigang before; the other, a man named Liu Fei, she was seeing for the first time. The group exchanged somewhat stiff nods by way of greeting.

Accountant Bi had assumed Meng Wan still hadn't returned, and was mildly surprised to see her. "Didn't Xiaohai say you'd gone away on a trip?"

"I just got off the train. Xiaohai told me what was happening here, so I wanted to come understand the situation first and see if I could help."

"Sit, and we'll talk." Accountant Bi sighed. "A notice came down from above yesterday. Because our Youth Travel Agency isn't turning enough of a profit, it's to be sold off."

Meng Wan pulled over a stool and sat to one side. Looking at Accountant Bi's care-worn face, she asked, puzzled, "Our agency isn't turning a profit?"

As far as she knew, their little office's earning power wasn't bad at all. How could it not be profitable?

Accountant Bi relayed the notice from above to her:

"The Youth Travel Agency under your unit, owing to operating difficulties and poor returns, is to complete its restructuring within three months. If no party takes it over, its business license will be canceled, its premises reclaimed, and its personnel left to make their own arrangements."

The office they were in had originally been the "Youth Employment Office" under the Xiguan Subdistrict Administration. Later, as Yuyang's tourism industry grew, the government had granted staffing quotas and startup funds, and this "Youth Travel Agency" had been set up.

On paper the agency was collectively owned, but in practice it was attached under the subdistrict office—counting as a small labor-service enterprise. This little room was the subdistrict office's property, rent-free.

That was precisely what put the agency in such an awkward spot: it was neither purely state-owned nor wholly private, but a "creature of the cracks" of its era.

Owing to a shift in policy, the higher-ups had recently received a directive: governments at every level were to promptly clear out attached enterprises and poorly run small collectives. The subdistrict office's marching orders were unambiguous—"Either restructure, or close down."

Accountant Bi heaved a long sigh. "It makes money, sure, but there are so few of us. Only a handful can actually bring in income, and even if you all worked month after month without a single day off, it'd still be no match for the big agencies. So the higher-ups just grabbed any excuse to draw a clean line between us and them."

"Then why not hire a few more guides?"

It was a question she'd long wanted to ask. With more people, there'd be no more situations where there were too many tour groups and not enough hands to lead them.

"It's not as simple as you think." Accountant Bi took a slurp of tea and explained. "The staffing quotas here are limited—one radish, one hole. New guides don't draw a base salary; they live purely on commission. One year tourism was in a slump, and a guide led just a single group in several months. The agency had no way to pay him a wage. So nowadays, apart from purely affiliated guides like you who don't take a staffing slot, everyone's scrambling to jump ship to the big agencies."

And strictly speaking, purely affiliated guides like Meng Wan weren't supposed to exist either—they'd been exploiting a loophole in the policy. So Accountant Bi didn't dare take on too many, keeping the number to one or two.

"Can't you just apply to the subdistrict office for more staffing slots?"

"It's not that I haven't tried. But our agency runs on its own profits and losses. Honestly, even if we did go private, the subdistrict office still has its own favored offices—why would they hand such precious staffing slots to us?"

Meng Wan caught the gist: they were neither doted on by Dad nor loved by Mom.

Accountant Bi poured out more of his troubles. "Lately the subdistrict office got new leadership, and the new chief sees this 'Youth Employment Office' of ours as a historical burden to be shed. And it just so happens policy now requires separating government from enterprise—the subdistrict office can no longer run businesses directly. So we're the ones they've taken the knife to. 'Poor returns' is just a pretext."

Meng Wan glanced at the other two guides in the room and asked, "So there's no room at all to turn this around?"

"Same thing I said. Either restructure, or close down."

Restructuring meant, in essence, converting from state ownership to private ownership—and what stood in the middle was the sum of money needed to buy the agency out. Buying the agency itself wasn't expensive; what was expensive was the "Youth Travel Agency" operating license. It was precisely because she couldn't obtain such a license herself that Meng Wan had chosen to operate as an affiliate.

But now the license was ready-made—far simpler than going through the whole process herself. As long as she had the money, she could take it over.

The trouble was, she couldn't produce that sum right now, and no one at the agency could either. She'd originally assumed the restructuring would come next year, in which case she might have managed to scrape and patch the money together by then. She just hadn't expected the opportunity to arrive so abruptly, catching her unprepared.

"Accountant Bi." Meng Wan tapped her toe lightly and asked, her expression grave, "Did the higher-ups say how much money it would take to buy this agency?"

"'Fair competition.'" Accountant Bi sounded utterly dispirited. "Which really just means highest bidder takes priority. From what I know, a boss has already surfaced who's willing to put up a hundred thousand."

Meng Wan wasn't surprised. Their agency might be small, but its earning power was anything but poor. The only reason it hadn't grown was that it was hamstrung by the subdistrict office. Once the market economy took off down the road, this agency would have enormous potential.

That person, she thought, must have his eye on that very potential.

Then she heard Accountant Bi go on. "He means to buy us out and open a billiards hall!"

The few people present exchanged blank looks. Didn't that mean all of them, every last guide, would be out of a job?

That simply wouldn't do!

Luo Zhigang flared up on the spot. "Then what's to become of us?"

It wasn't as if Accountant Bi weren't anxious himself. But with no money to put up and no one to take over, anxiety changed nothing.

"He said if you want to stay on and help run the billiards hall, he'll pay you a wage. If you don't want to stay, you resign of your own accord and find your own way out."

"And the subdistrict office is just going to wash its hands of us?"

By the rules, a restructuring like this was supposed to protect the basic rights of the existing staff.

"They've made provisions." Accountant Bi produced a document and handed it to Luo Zhigang. "It says right here: 'After restructuring, all original employees must be retained; there shall be no dismissal without cause within three years.'"

Meng Wan's brow knit slightly. She looked at old Bi. "They can't lay anyone off—but resigning voluntarily doesn't count."

Old Bi gave an "Aye" and slapped his thigh. "That's exactly it."

Liu Fei, who'd been listening quietly off to one side, now spoke up. "Unless we can find someone willing to take it over and keep running it as a travel agency, we'll all be facing unemployment any day now."

"Precisely."

But that was just the hard part—finding someone willing to run a travel agency who could also put up that large a sum. And even if such a person were found, they'd have to accept his leadership; if he didn't know how to run an agency, sooner or later it would fold anyway.

So the problem was thoroughly thorny.

"Old Bi." Luo Zhigang stamped his foot in his anxiety. "Help everyone think of something, will you? You're about to retire—but Old Liu and I both have whole families to feed."

In Luo Zhigang's mind, old Bi had after all eaten the public payroll his whole life; over all these years he must have struck up acquaintance with a cadre or two. These days everything got done through connections.

"I have thought, how could I not have thought." Old Bi sat down in a huff. "Am I the kind of man who only cares about his own comfort? Every matter in this agency, big or small—which one of them haven't I seen to?"

Liu Fei stepped in just in time to smooth their tempers. "All right, Zhigang. If old Bi had a way, would he be fretting himself into this state?"

Then, turning to a gentler tone, he went on persuading. "Old Bi, don't take offense at Zhigang. That's just how he is—hot-tempered, speaks without thinking. He means no harm."

The mood stalled for a moment, every one of them with a bellyful of frustration.

Meng Wan's mind raced. They certainly couldn't produce the money—but compared with that billiards hall boss, they had advantages of their own.

After a moment's thought she said, "We can't come up with that much money—but we can work from other angles."

"Such as?" Luo Zhigang listened, mystified, and raked his hair back a couple of agitated times.

Liu Fei caught a glimmer of Meng Wan's meaning and tried to spell it out. "Such as the proper resettlement of personnel? And the operating plan after restructuring?"

The document said highest bidder gets priority, but the leadership also had to give weight to public sentiment, and to the longer-term development plan—these were important considerations in their thinking as well.

The whole city was watching this restructuring work. The moment something went wrong, it became a cautionary case others would hold up as a "model failure," and no one wanted to be that "model."

"That's exactly it." Meng Wan looked at Liu Fei and nodded at him, secretly applauding. Here was a clever man.

Luo Zhigang watched the two of them play off each other and found it hard to keep up with their thinking.

"Old Bi." Meng Wan asked, her expression serious. "Back when this place was converted into a travel agency, did the higher-ups ever issue any official red-letterhead document?"

By normal procedure, repurposing a government office for another use had to receive a formal documented approval.

Old Bi put his hands on his hips, stared at the ceiling, and thought for a couple of seconds. "There was. I'll go dig it out for you."

He turned and walked over to the filing cabinet, took the keys hanging at his waist, and opened the door. Stack by stack he set the neatly piled documents out on the table beside it, rummaging through everything for a good long while.

"Found it!" Old Bi spun around excitedly and waved a sheaf of papers at the group. Dust from inside drifted up on the air and into his throat, setting off a fit of coughing.

Liu Fei hurried to pour old Bi a cup of water, while Meng Wan took the documents from his hands and began poring over them carefully.

Luo Zhigang didn't understand why Meng Wan wanted to read these ancient, mothballed papers. Their eyebrows were practically on fire—what use was reading this? But since she'd said she had a plan, all he could do was go along with her.

The office fell quiet for a time, with only the swift "shf-shf" of Meng Wan turning pages scratching at their nerves; they were dying to know just what she was selling in that gourd of hers.

After a long while, just as they were on the verge of running out of patience, they suddenly heard Meng Wan cry out loud, "Found it!"
