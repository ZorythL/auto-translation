Chapter 52

For a moment, Meng Wan and Luo Zhigang forgot all about the conversation they'd just been having. Only a short while ago the two of them had been the ones on display for an audience; now, in the blink of an eye, they'd become the spectators.

The cook who'd smashed the basin was a man of about fifty, his hair half gone gray, while the man who'd taken Meng Wan's order at the front counter earlier was somewhat younger.

There weren't many people in the shop just then—aside from Meng Wan there was only one other table, and the rest were the shop's own staff. Since it was a small place, the staff consisted of the boss and one middle-aged woman.

At that moment everyone was stunned by the cook's sudden outburst, no one quite able to react.

His face dark, the cook fumed, "What do you mean by cutting my wages again?"

The boss first glanced over at the customers eating their noodles, feeling his pride a little pricked, then stepped forward and said in a low, coaxing voice, "Don't get so worked up. Let's go talk in the kitchen."

The cook didn't budge an inch. "I'm not going anywhere—we'll talk right here! Since you can pull a shameless stunt like this, I don't need to spare you any face either!"

"Master Hai, you mustn't say it like that." Seeing the cook refuse to move, the boss instantly put on airs. "Before, you came begging me—you said you didn't care how low the wage was, you just needed a job. I saw you were getting on in years, laid off and unable to find work, and out of pity I took you on. You can't go biting the hand that feeds you."

"Before, when I cooked the canteen pot at the bearing factory, I made forty yuan a month. You said you could only pay me thirty. I knew I was getting old, so I took the thirty without a word of complaint. But now you're cutting me down to twenty-five—tell me, isn't that just rotten to the core!"

The boss gave a cold snort. "Over at Old Li's noodle shop their cook only makes twenty a month. Twenty-five from me is plenty generous."

"That's because his cooking's no good!"

"You only studied under the old master at Yuxiang Tower for a few days—and there's nothing to show you ever learned anything from it. You used to cook the canteen pot, now you cook bowls of noodles. If your cooking were any good, why aren't you at Yuxiang Tower?"

"You—!" Master Hai was so angry he couldn't get a word out, his deeply wrinkled face flushing crimson.

Anyone who knew Master Hai knew that failing to land a place at Yuxiang Tower was a sore spot he'd carried for years—so the boss was rubbing salt right into the wound.

"What's all this 'you' business? If you want to work, work; if you don't, then get lost. Two-legged turtles may be hard to come by, but two-legged cooks are a dime a dozen. This noodle shop of mine doesn't depend on you."

"Fine." Master Hai had seen this man for what he truly was. "Then I quit, here and now. Go find yourself some more brilliant talent!"

With that, Master Hai tore off his apron and flung it forcefully into the boss's face, then strode out the door without a backward glance.

Meng Wan had listened for a good while and caught the crucial detail: Master Hai had studied his craft under the master chef of Yuxiang Tower. Putting that together with the taste of today's noodles, his cooking had to be excellent. The man was a real talent—what a shame this boss had eyes only for money, letting a figure as skilled as Master Hai walk out the door just to save five measly yuan in wages.

Meng Wan shot a glance in the direction Master Hai had gone, anxious to catch up to him. Grabbing her bag, she said to Luo Zhigang, "Is the money on you?"

Luo Zhigang blinked, then realized she meant the tour fee. "It's here."

He pulled a thick envelope out of his bag and handed it to Meng Wan.

Meng Wan's eyes had long since drifted to the window. Distractedly she instructed him, "You head back to the agency first. I'll take care of the rest."

With that she snatched up her bag and rushed out the door. Luo Zhigang turned to watch her go, scratched the back of his head in bewilderment, and gave up trying to fathom what Meng Wan was thinking.

Out of the noodle shop, Meng Wan hopped on her bike and chased after Master Hai. Luckily he wasn't riding, so she caught up with him just as he reached the bend in the road.

"Master Hai." She called him by name too. "Please wait a moment."

Catching up, Meng Wan immediately got off her bike and trotted alongside, pushing it, to keep pace with him.

Master Hai turned at the sound and shot her a glance. Seeing an unfamiliar face, he kept walking without slowing. "Do I know you?"

Pushing her bike, Meng Wan said, "I was just having noodles in your shop. I heard the whole conversation between you and that boss."

"What, did you come to laugh at this old man?" Master Hai's face was grim, hands clasped behind his back as he marched on.

"Of course not. I just ate the noodles you made, and without the slightest exaggeration, they were the best noodles I've had in all of Yuyang. Your skill is truly second to none."

Master Hai's expression softened. He turned his head to glance at Meng Wan, and his pace gradually slowed. "You chased me out here just to pay me a few compliments?"

Meng Wan said with a smile, "Of course not. Mainly I wanted to express how much I loved your noodles. And secondly, I wanted to ask—besides noodle dishes, do you have any other specialties?"

Touched on the subject of his expertise, Master Hai lifted his chin a little and began enumerating them for her. "Oh, the dishes I can make are many. There isn't a single dish you've eaten at any restaurant in Yuyang that I can't make. My best are beef soup and assorted hotpot."

A spark of delight flickered across Meng Wan's brow—she knew she'd found the right person. "I wonder if you have time on the tenth? If you do, I'd like to ask you to cook a banquet for me. I'll pay you the going market rate, plus an extra fee for your trouble."

Tomorrow Lin Xi would sit the college entrance exam—three days in all, finishing on the tenth. Meng Wan was going to host a celebration banquet for her, and at the same time get a look at this Master Hai's cooking.

Out of a job, Master Hai naturally had time to spare, and since there was extra money to be made, why not? He agreed readily. "Write me out a menu. I'll buy the ingredients ahead of time and come cook them on the spot."

Meng Wan parked her bike and took a notebook and pen from her bag and began writing.

Master Hai stopped too, turning to watch her write, nodding as he looked on. It seemed Meng Wan knew her food—every single dish was a Yuyang classic.

Meng Wan finished a long string of dish names, then wrote the Lin family's address at the bottom, tore off the sheet, and handed it over along with two hundred yuan from her bag. "This is for the ingredients. See if it's enough—if not, I'll give you more."

Master Hai glanced at the money and joked, "You trust me just like that? Aren't you afraid I'll take the cash and vanish?"

Meng Wan said firmly, "Trust the one you employ; don't employ the one you don't trust."

The corner of Master Hai's eye twitched faintly. Nodding, he accepted the menu and the two hundred yuan. "See you on the tenth. I guarantee you'll be pleased with this banquet."

By the time she reached the jade workshop, the sun had already slanted to the west. Meng Wan had pedaled hard the whole way; great beads of sweat kept oozing out, and her freshly washed hair was sticking to her again. Summer had truly arrived.

Old Bai had already hired a few people to start making the souvenirs. When she arrived, everyone was gathering their things to knock off for the day, and with Old Bai's introduction Meng Wan greeted them all.

Now that there were more workers, Old Bai's single original workshop was no longer enough, so he'd cleared out a main room to serve as a second workshop. The courtyard was piled with jade stone he'd purchased—not yet polished, stacked neatly piece by piece against the wall.

Meng Wan asked Old Bai a few questions about recent production, then inspected the finished souvenirs on the display rack. The pieces the workers had made were nearly indistinguishable from Old Bai's own, which set her mind at ease considerably.

Looking over the dazzling array of souvenirs, Meng Wan was struck by an idea. She decided to take some to the hotel to test the waters and see whether the tourists would like them—and earn a little extra besides.

She'd originally meant to buy ten little rabbit jade pendants from Old Bai, but he flatly refused to take any money, so Meng Wan had no choice but to accept his kindness.

By the time she got back to the hotel, the sky had gone dark. Meng Wan knocked on each door one by one, returning the money along with a jade pendant to every tourist, and showed them the souvenirs she'd brought. As she and Old Bai had agreed, she offered them for sale at eight yuan eighty a piece.

On seeing the novel little souvenirs, almost everyone bought at least one. In the end demand ran so high that supply couldn't keep up—Meng Wan had brought fifteen pieces in all, and they sold out completely.

This gave Meng Wan a great deal of confidence. It seemed souvenir production needed to be ramped up; at the current rate of output there was no way to meet demand.

Leaving the hotel, Meng Wan hurried home without a moment's pause. She'd just ridden to within a short distance of the residential compound when she spotted, from afar, a dark figure standing beneath a tree, a car parked beside it.

Pedaling on, Meng Wan squinted curiously, trying to make out who it was—until the figure spoke, and she recognized the voice.

"Why are you back so late?"

James stepped out from under the tree to meet her.

"What are you doing here?" Delighted, Meng Wan braked to a stop. "We've only been apart half a day and you already miss me."

James curved his lips up, his eyes brimming with tenderness. "I do. Every second apart from you feels like a year."

"That idiom rolled right off your tongue. Looks like you've been studying hard—worthy of praise."

"Thank you for the praise, Manager. I'll keep working at it. I won't drag my manager down."

The banter over, Meng Wan looked into James's eyes and asked, "What brings you to my house this late? Is something the matter?"

If there weren't, he wouldn't have come to find her so late—it wasn't like the gentlemanly James.

James turned and walked over to the car, took out a dark object, and handed it to her. "A present for you."

Puzzled, Meng Wan took the heavy gift, brought it close to her eyes, and examined it carefully. It was a stone nameplate about two hands long, with four characters carved into it: "Manager: Meng Wan."

"What's this?"

"It's a desk nameplate. Set it on your office desk—anyone who comes looking for you will be able to see it, and it'll remind them of your position."

After finishing her tutoring session that morning, James had tracked down a stonemason who did interior decoration and had him find a good piece of stone, then had Meng Wan's name engraved on it. He'd originally meant to deliver it to her travel agency, but he didn't have the address, so he'd simply come to her house and waited for her.

Deeply moved, Meng Wan hugged the nameplate to her chest, revealing a rare softer, girlish side. In a sweet, coaxing voice she said, "Thank you, my darling boyfriend, for giving me this present. I just love it."

In Meng Wan's experience, when a girl said something like that, the boy would usually be charmed into delight, the two of them would trade a few more sweet nothings, and then there'd be a hug. But she'd forgotten that James was a foreigner.

Meng Wan smiled sweetly, waiting for the hug to come—only to hear James ask, in complete earnest, "Who is 'I'?"
