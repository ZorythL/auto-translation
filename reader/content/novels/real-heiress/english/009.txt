Chapter 9

The moment James got in the car, he set his camera down right between himself and Meng Wan, closed his eyes, and dozed off.

Even though he was staying at the best hotel in the city, there were still plenty of problems. He could put up with the rest, but the soundproofing was so poor it gave him a headache.

The night before, he'd washed up and gone to bed early, but before long the room next door had filled with the moaning and groaning of two people getting intimate—so clear it was as if he were lying right under their bed.

Out of respect for strangers' sex lives, James first turned on the TV and watched for a while. When he sensed the noise next door gradually dying down, he settled in for a second attempt at sleep—only for it to start up again before long, the shrieks even more piercing than the first time.

All trace of sleepiness gone, James climbed out of bed in a fit of pique and wrote three pages of Chinese characters.

It wasn't until nearly eleven that the room next door finally quieted down. James, by then too drowsy to keep his eyes open, had barely drifted off when the noise returned.

He could bear it no longer and called the front desk. The manager came up at once to reason with the couple, hoping they could keep it down, but the people next door, furious at having their fun interrupted, simply started shouting at the manager.

Listening to the crude cursing, James threw on a robe and went out to confront them himself. Seeing him come out, the man's bluster toward the manager subsided a little, and with an overbearing air he asked whether James was the one who'd complained.

James held out his wrist and let him see the time—almost half past eleven. If he didn't stop, then James would camp out at his door and chat with the on-duty staff all night. He didn't believe the man could carry on like that.

His mother, Ms. Ye, had taught him that to deal with an unreasonable bully, you fought unreasonableness with unreasonableness.

The man, fuming, demanded that James switch rooms. James of course refused. Why should the victim be the one to compromise? His attitude was rock-hard: the man could either change rooms or stop making noise.

The man still wouldn't back down, but the woman with him, afraid the matter would blow up, murmured a few words in his ear, and only then did the man very grudgingly agree to switch rooms.

Finally, at midnight, James was able to sleep in peace—though he spent the whole night dreaming of quarreling endlessly with the man next door, and the quality of his sleep took a serious hit.

When Meng Wan saw him fall asleep the moment he got in the car, she too breathed a sigh of relief—otherwise she really wouldn't have known what to talk to him about. She wasn't someone who liked an awkward silence; in any crowd she felt duty-bound to keep the atmosphere lively.

Just now there was only the camera between her and James, and the man's profile was laid bare before her eyes. That nose was so high-bridged it was impossible to ignore; if you set a tiny ant on his face, that nose would be a mountain the ant could never hope to cross.

Meng Wan's attention was drawn to the little freckles on James's cheeks, and with nothing better to do, she started counting them. One, two, three...

The farther along she counted, the smaller the freckles got, and Meng Wan had to lean her body forward, her upper half unwittingly crossing past the camera. The more absorbed she got, the closer she leaned, until her face was only centimeters from James's, her breath fanning against his without her realizing it at all.

James had meant to catch a nap in the car, but before sleep could take hold, he suddenly felt a warm puff of breath against his cheek, tickling it, followed by the jasmine scent of Meng Wan. His heart gave an involuntary lurch.

Could it be she wanted to steal a kiss while he was asleep? So beneath that gentle exterior beat such a bold and forward heart.

The kiss was slow in coming, but the breath kept skating across his cheek, from top to bottom. He had no idea what Meng Wan was actually doing, and he was caught between whether to open his eyes and tell her he wasn't asleep yet.

But that would surely throw this young lady into an awkward spot, so James decided to endure a little longer.

It wasn't until the next moment, when her breath fell against his neck, that James could no longer hold back. His eyes flew open—only to find a head of jet-black hair right in front of him.

"What are you doing?"

His throat dry, his voice rasping, James instinctively blurted it out in his mother tongue.

Meng Wan, intent on counting the freckles along James's lower cheek, was so startled by the sound suddenly coming from atop her own head that she sprang backward.

She stared in horror, eyes wide, her heart bouncing wildly as if on a spring. A thousand thoughts flashed through her mind at once; her throat seemed glued shut, unable to get out a single word. After much stammering, all she managed was, "...I... I..."

Lin Dong had been keeping an eye on things in the back through the rearview mirror, and the instant he noticed Meng Wan leaning so close to Mr. Chen, he hastily snatched his gaze away. See no evil, see no evil. Could it be these two weren't just guide and tourist after all?

His imagination stopped there. Whatever the two of them were to each other, he only needed to earn his money—best not to meddle in other people's business.

He just hadn't expected this Miss Meng to be so forward, declaring her affection so brazenly as if he weren't even there. Tsk!

"It's not what you think!" Meng Wan explained loudly. "I was counting your freckles, and as I counted I just leaned closer and closer without meaning to. I really didn't have any improper intentions!"

Absurd as the explanation was, James believed it—it was exactly the sort of thing Meng Wan would do. They'd met only twice since yesterday, but he had her personality figured out seven or eight parts of the way already, because she was so candid, her feelings written all over her face, and her temperament so impulsive—nothing like the demure image she presented on the surface.

To spare her further embarrassment, James changed the subject, asking with genuine curiosity, "So how many were there altogether?"

Meng Wan hadn't expected his mind to jump so fast. She sat dazed for a few seconds, quickly trying to recall where she'd gotten to, then said meekly, "Twenty-seven, twenty-eight?"

"Then there must be at least fifty on this one face of mine," James said in surprise. "Looks like I'll have to be diligent about sunscreen from now on."

Turning the wheel, Lin Dong picked his moment to chime in. "What do freckles have to do with sunscreen?"

Before driving a cab, Lin Dong had spent three years in the army, leaving and switching to civilian work when his mother's health declined. When he'd first enlisted, he'd trained under the blazing sun every day; after a couple of weeks he'd gotten a good deal darker, but he'd never sprouted any freckles.

James sat up straight and earnestly explained it for Lin Dong. White people's skin contains less eumelanin, the kind that blocks sunlight efficiently, and a relatively higher proportion of pheomelanin, which offers poor protection. Under intense sun, their skin can't protect itself by tanning, so instead it gets damaged directly, triggering inflammation and pigment deposits that form spots.

Lin Dong nodded, half understanding. So he got darker in the sun, and white people sprouted freckles.

Hearing the subject successfully steered away, Meng Wan let out a breath of relief. Thank goodness James hadn't gotten the wrong idea—otherwise she'd never have lived it down.

The rest of the way passed without a word. Lin Dong drove very smoothly, and Meng Wan's eyelids grew heavier and heavier until she too drifted off to sleep.

*

When they reached their destination, Lin Dong woke the two of them. Meng Wan got out of the car and stretched, then suggested Lin Dong come in with them—he'd only be sitting idle in the car otherwise.

Lin Dong was grateful, but he still politely declined. He'd been to these places as a child, back when they didn't charge admission and you could go in whenever you liked. Besides, there was nothing in there but a few dead people's coffins—nothing worth seeing.

Meng Wan had thought that if Lin Dong came along, he could be a sort of buffer between her and James, but since he didn't want to, she couldn't force him.

The first place Meng Wan brought James to was the Tomb of the True and False Princesses.

Princess Qionglin was the emperor's most beloved daughter. When she married, he bestowed on her Yuyang—at the time the most prosperous city in the realm—as her fief, and her consort was her childhood sweetheart. The two were deeply in love, and their union was hailed by all.

Then, half a year after the princess's wedding, a truth quietly came to light: she was not the emperor's natural daughter.

It happened that around the time the empress gave birth to the princess, there was a great drought. To pray for rain from heaven, the emperor and empress went in person to the Linze Temple. The court physicians had judged the birth to be more than a month off, and everyone urged the empress to stay safely in the palace and pray by copying scriptures instead—but the empress was wholehearted in her devotion; for the people's sake she could bear this small hardship.

So on the very day of the rain prayer, the empress accidentally went into early labor. As it happened, a female devotee lodging at the temple was also at full term. Originally the emperor and empress's visit meant all unconnected persons should have been cleared from the temple, but the abbot, seeing how hard things were for the heavily pregnant woman, had quietly arranged for her to stay in a side chamber.

This devotee, bleeding ceaselessly from a difficult labor, was carried at the empress's order into her own meditation chamber so the midwives could deliver them together.

And so, on that same day, two daughters were born at the Linze Temple at nearly the same moment.

Not long after the princess was born, a wild gale suddenly arose, thunder and lightning crashed together, and torrential rain came down like a deluge from heaven. While everyone in the courtyard was reveling in the blessing of the rain, inside the chamber—soundlessly, in secret—a switch of the babies was carried out.

Eighteen years later, the true princess learned her real identity by chance from her mother's lips, and after great effort she finally reached the emperor's presence to plead her case.

Looking at the young woman who bore an eighty-percent resemblance to herself, the empress concluded that she spoke the truth, and so the true princess was at last allowed to return to the palace and live the life of splendor and luxury that had always been hers by right.

As for the false princess, already married, when she learned she was not of legitimate blood, she first made a great scene, then fell gravely ill. Unable to bear others' strange looks or the gulf between her old life and her new, she eventually shaved her head and took the veil, never again coming down from the mountain, and within not even thirty years she wasted away and died of grief.

Her consort, meanwhile, after she'd taken vows, married the true princess and had two sons and two daughters with her.

After the false princess died, he feigned deep devotion, bringing her body back and building a small tomb for her beside his and the true princess's grave, even inscribing the headstone with his own hand.

And so came to be what is now the Tomb of the True and False Princesses.

"How foolish," James said, hearing Meng Wan out.

The story Meng Wan told was someone else's, but wasn't it also her own life? Her eyes brimming with sorrow, she sighed, "You don't understand. She must have suffered a tremendous blow to do something like that. There was no one left in the world to love her."

Wasn't that exactly how she herself had felt back then—watching one after another of the people who'd once fawned over her drift away? The pain of being cast off was carved into her bones.

James didn't agree. "Other people's love is conditional in the end. How can you punish yourself over their conditional love? That's a failure to respect yourself."

Meng Wan lowered her eyes and thought for a moment, then asked, "If you were the false princess, what would you do?"

"Create a condition."

"Create a condition?"

James crossed his arms over his chest and paced around the grave. "The condition for being loved can be bloodline, wealth, status, or beauty. If you lose one condition, then create another one. People who value that condition will naturally come flocking to offer you their love. By then I'd only need to keep a firm grip on that condition, and anyone at all would be mine to use."

James's words were an awakening to Meng Wan—only, he put it so bluntly, so nakedly, that she found it a little hard to accept. Was there really no such thing as unconditional love in this world?

Watching James say all this with such utter assurance, Meng Wan badly wanted to ask him: for what condition would he ever offer up his own love?

But in the end she didn't ask. She was afraid the answer would shatter the image of James she held in her heart.
