Chapter 1

Meng Wan sat in front of her dressing mirror, staring at her own face—fair, smooth skin without a single fine line. A whole night had gone by, and she still couldn't believe she was back in 1995.

She was twenty-two this year. She'd been lucky enough to be born into a well-off family, the kind of young miss everyone else envied.

Her father, Meng Qingyan, was a university professor whose best students had gone on to work in government and business. Her mother, Cheng Xuehua, was deputy director of the city's biggest hospital and had treated more patients than anyone could count.

Her older brother, Meng Xingyang, was still young but had already made section chief at the surveying bureau. Her sister-in-law, Sun Jing, was a teacher.

With a family like that, theirs was clearly an educated, well-off household. Meng Wan had never gone without food, clothes, or pocket money. Everywhere she went, people spoiled her, and with her good looks on top of it all, she was the very image of a pampered heiress.

Everyone assumed a child raised in a family like that couldn't possibly turn out badly. But Meng Wan was the exception.

When she was little, she'd gone through some hard years with her parents when they were sent to the countryside. Later, when things settled down and the Mengs got their old jobs back, they did everything they could to give their daughter the best, hoping to make up for what she'd suffered.

And so they raised Meng Wan into a lazy, arrogant, willful girl whose ambitions far outran her abilities.

By the age when she should have been working, Meng Wan was instead running around with a pack of useless friends, with no drive to make anything of herself.

Her father tried to nudge her gently. "There's an opening in the logistics office at my school. It would be perfect for you."

Meng Wan curled her lip. "What's so great about logistics? Hard work and no credit for it. I'm not going."

Her mother pleaded with her. "Sweetheart, listen to me—go study medicine. Doctors get so much respect."

Meng Wan waved it off. "Respect, sure, and you work yourself half to death every single day."

Her brother talked himself hoarse. "Little sister, I've got a friend in business. Go work for him and the general manager's chair is yours."

Meng Wan raised an eyebrow. "Isn't that just selling things? Boring."

In the end, after everyone gave in, Meng Wan took a job as a librarian at the city cultural center—sorting newspapers and magazines, checking out books for the public, even taking tickets at the door when they were short-handed. She only had a high-school education and had gotten in through connections, so she had no permanent position.

The truth was, Meng Wan could have done better. But it was hard to find another job this easy that also paid her to read books and magazines all day. So even without a permanent post, she took the one job she didn't really mind.

Meng Wan had always loved novels and history. Her grades were bad, but her Chinese and history were decent and her English was passable—it was math, physics, and the other sciences that sank her. On the college entrance exam, she'd scored just twenty-six in math.

When her father heard, he nearly fainted from anger. An economics professor's daughter, scoring twenty-six in math! For days after the results came out, he avoided his colleagues, terrified someone would ask about her marks and have a laugh at his expense.

He'd hoped she might at least scrape into a vocational college and come away with some kind of diploma, which would make it easier to find her a job later. But her scores fell miserably short even of that.

The nineties were full of opportunity, and Meng Wan missed every one. No university, no government post, never even a foot in the business world. She'd been dealt a winning hand and thrown it away—food spooned right to her mouth, and she couldn't be bothered to chew.

The old Meng Wan had assumed she'd coast through her whole life on her parents' and brother's success, living off everything they gave her, drifting along however she pleased.

But when she was thirty, the real heiress came knocking. Once the truth came out, everything Meng Wan had by blood was wiped to zero—and only then, far too late, did she realize she'd never actually held anything of her own.

Overnight, she became a fake who had stolen someone else's life.

After that she lost all her spirit. She dragged herself to forty, then withered away in despair.

On her deathbed she was full of regret. If she ever got the chance to live again, her life would absolutely not turn out like this.

Maybe heaven took pity on her, because it really did give her a second chance—sending her back to the time before the real heiress returned, when everything, every last thing, was still within her reach.

Right then and there, Meng Wan made up her mind: before that day came, she would learn how to stand on her own two feet and hold her future firmly in her own hands. Better to rely on yourself than on anyone else.

Bang, bang, bang. A heavy knock landed on her wooden door, scattering her thoughts and pulling her back to the present.

"Coming." Meng Wan grinned at the mirror, a wide, bright smile. Her life was finally starting.

*

Meng Wan had barely finished psyching herself up when, a moment later, a bucket of cold water hit her in the face.

She opened the door to find Meng Xingyang outside, looking anxious. "Xiao Wan, Dad wants you in the living room."

The joy of seeing her brother again—back from before everything fell apart—didn't even have time to rise before his nervous, hovering manner left her completely confused. Drawing on a lifetime of hard-won experience, she quickly dug through her memory for whatever was waiting for her.

But too much time had passed, and with Meng Xingyang rushing her, her mind went blank. All she could do was follow him out and take it one step at a time. As far as she could remember, the only thing that had ever truly enraged her parents was when she'd registered her marriage to her ex-husband without telling them a word. Everything else had been a lot of noise over nothing—a little sweet-talking and it always blew over.

The Mengs lived in a public rental flat the school had given her father: three bedrooms, a living room, a kitchen, and a bathroom. With six people packed in, it was a little tight. Meng Wan's room was the farthest one down the hall.

Meng Xingyang's work unit had assigned him a flat two years back, but he'd generously handed that two-bedroom over to a colleague whose family of five was crammed into two rooms. He'd figured another flat would come along soon enough, but everything that turned up was either a single room or shared housing, none of which suited him. So he'd dragged it out until now, still living at home.

The hallway was short; a few steps brought her to the living room. On the right side of the red sofa sat a man with a crew cut, in a stiff white shirt gone yellow, the collar already fraying. Next to him sat a girl with her head down, shrinking into herself.

Meng Wan recognized her at once—it was her coworker from the cultural center, Xu Qiuyun.

The memories hit her all at once. She remembered now: this man was Xu Qiuyun's brother, Xu Hongtian.

A few days earlier, the department store had brought in a new shipment of Hong Kong–style clothes. But Meng Wan had already blown through that month's salary, and on top of that she'd asked her family for four thousand yuan to buy a Motorola pager—a huge expense already—so she couldn't bring herself to ask for clothing money too.

She figured she'd just wait. The moment next month's pay came in, she'd rush to the department store and grab every dress she liked.

That was the plan, anyway. But her legs had other ideas. Every day after work she took the long way past the department store to stare at the dresses in the window, picturing how each one would look on her. Then one day a salesgirl told her that her favorite—a pink high-waisted chiffon dress—was down to the last one.

That sent Meng Wan into a panic. She was on good terms with the salesgirl, so she asked her to set the dress aside and promised to pull the money together the next day and take her little dress home.

Desperate, Meng Wan turned to her coworkers, and Xu Qiuyun was one of the ones who lent to her—lent her the most, in fact: a full four hundred yuan.

Meng Wan said she didn't need that much, but Xu Qiuyun said that if she didn't lend it out, the money wouldn't get spent on herself anyway, so Meng Wan might as well use it to buy a dress. It was a strange thing to say, and Meng Wan couldn't quite make sense of it. But Xu Qiuyun was willing to lend and she fully meant to pay it back, so a loan was a loan—and now she could pick up the other pieces she liked too.

Worried Xu Qiuyun might fret, Meng Wan even offered to write an IOU, but Xu Qiuyun said there was no need; she knew what kind of person Meng Wan was. That made Meng Wan even more grateful, and she quietly promised herself she'd pay it all back the moment next month's salary landed—plus a box of Liufuzhai cloud cakes as interest.

But who'd have guessed that before that month's pay even came through, Xu Qiuyun's brother would drag her to the door to collect the debt.

Only then did Meng Wan learn that Xu Qiuyun had lost her father young. There were three Xu children, and she was the middle one, with an older brother and a younger brother. Their mother had no job and had raised all three by folding paper boxes and selling popsicles. Luckily Xu Qiuyun had done well, getting into a vocational college and slipping in just as the old job-assignment policy was ending, which landed her a post at the cultural center.

Her brother, Xu Hongtian, had been buying furniture and appliances for his wedding, and most of that money came from Xu Qiuyun's salary. This month she'd handed over nearly half her usual pay, which left him short for a television. Furious, he'd ordered her to get the loaned money back at once.

Xu Qiuyun refused, of course. She'd lent it out precisely so he couldn't take it all, and even knowing he'd blow up, she didn't want to just give in anymore. For once, she wanted to push back.

She'd thought a few slaps and curses would be the end of it. But she'd underestimated how obsessed her brother was with money. Somehow he'd dug up Meng Wan's home address and actually marched her over to demand the cash.

Before they went up, once she realized this was Meng Wan's home, Xu Qiuyun flat-out refused to go in. How was she supposed to face Meng Wan after this? She'd been the one to offer the loan, and now here they were collecting on it—what kind of thing was that to do?

But the moment she turned to leave, Xu Hongtian grabbed her wrist and hauled her up the stairs by force. The instant the door opened, she knew it was over: her friendship with Meng Wan was finished.

Having people show up at her door demanding money had left the proud Meng Wan feeling deeply wronged. At the time she'd been baffled. She and Xu Qiuyun had agreed she'd pay it back the moment her salary came—so why had Xu Qiuyun come to her home with her brother to collect? If they didn't trust her, they needn't have lent it at all.

Xu Hongtian's sneering words cut Meng Wan's pride to the bone. It was the first time that sheltered girl had ever had cruel words thrown at her, so much so that afterward she refused to hear a single word of Xu Qiuyun's explanation. All she cared about was her own hurt, and she blamed Xu Qiuyun for every bit of it. Little by little they drifted apart, and right up until Meng Wan married and quit her job, she never spoke to her again.

Later she heard that Xu Qiuyun had married not long after she did. Her husband was a man more than ten years older, on his second marriage; supposedly he'd paid the Xu family a hefty bride price, and that money went toward her younger brother's college tuition.

Now, seeing Xu Qiuyun again after all the ups and downs she herself had lived through, Meng Wan's old resentment had long since faded. Back then she'd thought she was the biggest victim, never once stopping to consider how desperate Xu Qiuyun's situation had been.

Looking at the meek, helpless girl in front of her, Meng Wan wanted so badly to tell her, "I'm sorry, Qiuyun. Back then, you must have been so sad."
