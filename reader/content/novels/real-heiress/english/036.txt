Chapter 36

Dongping Village made its living chiefly from growing corn, so all along the way, on either side of the rough, rutted dirt road, the corn seedlings had already grown knee-high.

Meng Wan sat across from James, watching him fold his long legs up to sit, looking more than a little cramped. She smiled and said, "First time on a horse cart, isn't it?"

James nodded. "It counts as a new experience. Life's all about experiences, after all."

Meng Wan said no more. Her gaze turned to the road ahead, her heart drumming the whole time, tension spreading through her body. Even her stomach felt awful—she wanted to throw up but couldn't.

It was an old affliction of hers: the moment she got nervous, she was prone to dry heaving.

"Whoa—" Qin Yizhong pulled the reins and brought the cart to a steady stop in front of a house.

In her past life, when Meng Wan had come, the house had already been renovated into brick and tile. This was the first time she'd seen it as the mud-walled dwelling it was now. The courtyard wall didn't even reach a person's height, so the whole state of the yard could be taken in at a glance from outside.

She climbed down from the cart slowly, both fists clenched and trembling. She stood at the gate but simply couldn't make her feet carry her in.

The front gate stood open. Call it a gate, but it was really just a half-height wooden board.

Qin Yizhong tied the horse to a tree not far off and came up behind Meng Wan in a few strides, urging her on. "Why aren't you going in?"

He genuinely wanted to know just what this young girl and that foreign fellow were really about. Did his sister have some other secret she was keeping from him?

James laid a hand on Meng Wan's shoulder and leaned close to murmur in her ear, "If you're not ready, we can come back another day."

"No." Meng Wan drew a deep breath and let it out. "It has to be today!"

Stalling and running away solved nothing. Facing things head-on and settling them quickly—that was who Meng Wan was meant to be.

Ding Chunling had already taken the children inside to say hello. The window was open, and the sound of talk and laughter from within drifted out on the breeze to Meng Wan's ears.

It was Qin Yi'e's voice.

Breathing in shallow little breaths, Meng Wan shifted her feet and had only just reached the middle of the yard when she saw Ding Chunling already leading someone out.

Qin Yi'e had a few fewer strands of white hair than the last time they'd met in her past life, but her complexion was still terribly sallow, her eyes sunken—she'd aged a great deal.

"This is the girl," Ding Chunling said with a smile, pointing Meng Wan out to Qin Yi'e. "She says she knows you, so I brought her along."

Qin Yi'e followed her pointing finger curiously and saw a beautifully dressed, lovely-faced young girl, accompanied by a tall young man with a foreign look she'd never seen before.

A faint, uneasy premonition stirred in her heart. Half smiling, half puzzled, she said, "You're...here to see me?"

Just then Zhou Nian came out of the house too, standing behind Qin Yi'e, timidly sizing up the two novel strangers before her.

Meng Wan's birth father, Zhou Da, had been crippled by an injury to his leg and couldn't get out of bed. Hearing the voices, he turned on the heated brick bed to look out the window.

Mother and daughter met each other's eyes. It was as though a bolt of lightning streaked across Meng Wan's mind, leaving it blank, her eyes welling unbidden. She blinked, fighting hard to push the tears back down.

Her voice thick, she said, "Might we have a word somewhere private?"

"Whatever it is, just say it here," Qin Yizhong said, displeased with all of Meng Wan's hedging and concealing. What was there that he couldn't hear?

Ding Chunling, though dying of curiosity herself, could see something was clearly off with Meng Wan. She quietly shot her husband a look, telling him to hold his tongue.

For some reason, Qin Yi'e felt the girl carried an air of the strangely familiar. She wanted to know what she'd come for, too.

"You all go inside," she said, turning to her family. "I'll go see what this is about."

Hearing her agree, Meng Wan turned and all but fled the Zhou house, head down, walking straight on, until she reached a stretch of field path. From behind, Qin Yi'e called her to a halt.

"Young lady, what is it you really want with me?" Qin Yi'e was getting on in years, and keeping pace with Meng Wan had left her winded. She stood where she was, hands on her hips, gulping in air.

Meng Wan stopped, looked around her, and slowly turned. She saw James standing not far off, making no move to come closer.

Meng Wan fixed her eyes on Qin Yi'e, a bellyful of words circling her lips with no idea which to say first. After turning it over and over, she simply came to the point: "What I want with you is to claim my family. I've come to be reunited."

For a moment Qin Yi'e didn't follow. Baffled, she said, "What family are we to each other? Among all my relations, I don't think there's anyone like you."

"The closest kind."

The closest kind of what?

Flesh and blood?

The thought struck home, and Qin Yi'e's legs went weak. Her whole body pitched backward, nearly falling. James had been keeping watch on this side the whole time; in a few strides he was there to steady her.

Trembling all over, Qin Yi'e stared in disbelief, her eyes wide enough to split. She raised a finger toward Meng Wan, stammering, "...You—you're..."

A picture of composure, Meng Wan said evenly, "I'm the daughter you swapped away twenty-two years ago."

In an instant Qin Yi'e's memory was dragged back more than two decades.

It was the era of sending youth down to the countryside. A good number of young people from the cities had come to their village, along with intellectuals like Cheng Xuehua and Meng Qingyan.

Back then the villagers assumed they'd committed some wrong and been sent to undergo labor reform, so no one was willing to go near them.

Cheng Xuehua's grandfather had been a wealthy merchant, and she'd grown up in plenty, never having suffered hardship. Dropped all at once into a poor and remote backcountry, she had no idea how to get by.

At that time Meng Qingyan, unable to accept the fact that he'd been sent down, had fallen ill and taken to his bed, leaving the entire family of three to Cheng Xuehua's care alone.

Hauling water, lighting the stove, cooking, washing clothes—every task fell to her. The rest she could manage, but the water-hauling was the worst of it. Dongping Village had only one well, at the east end of the village, while Cheng Xuehua's household lived at the west end. The round trip was several li, and where other families had carts, she had only a carrying pole.

After failing at it a few times, she swallowed her pride and asked a villager to lend her a cart—only for the villager to refuse her outright, which mortified her no end.

After that, she carried it herself, half a bucket at a time, walking a stretch, resting a stretch—painfully slow. She had to go out to the fields during the day, so she could only fetch water at night, and sometimes when it was too dark she'd miss her footing and take a fall.

It was while hauling water that Qin Yi'e crossed paths with Cheng Xuehua. Having grown up in the countryside from a young age, Qin Yi'e had done every kind of farm work and housework there was, and had built up real strength for it.

Seeing a fair, delicate young woman like Cheng Xuehua huffing and straining under two only half-full buckets—it was a pitiful, laborious sight—Qin Yi'e simply hauled a full cart of water and delivered it to her home.

From that first gift of water, one thing led to another, and the two grew close, until in time there was nothing they couldn't talk about.

More fated still, the two of them were found to be pregnant at almost the same time, and went into labor on the very same day.

That day Qin Yi'e had gone to bring Cheng Xuehua some brown sugar. Partway through, Cheng Xuehua's water broke. Meng Qingyan was out at the fields and not home, so Qin Yi'e had no choice but to rush off to fetch the village midwife.

Rural medical resources were scarce in those days; babies were all delivered at home. By the time Qin Yi'e had hurriedly brought the midwife back to the Meng house, her own labor had been brought on, and she cried out, doubled over in pain.

The midwife steadied her first, then went next door to call over a few of the older women. Working together, everyone helped bring both mothers' babies safely into the world.

It was around that time, too, that Qin Yi'e's husband had broken his leg and been left crippled, sitting at home all day doing nothing but drinking or cursing. She had no way to properly care for the child—and on top of that the baby was a girl, who would be all the less welcome.

So as she looked at the two newborns swaddled in their blankets, a wicked idea took root: to switch the children. Cheng Xuehua's family might be poor now too, but they were different—husband and wife were both educated people, and they were sure to raise a child well.

And so she did exactly that. Cheng Xuehua had been left very weak after the birth and lay unconscious throughout. While the midwife had stepped out, Qin Yi'e, in a moment of reckless impulse, swapped the two babies.

Just like that, the real heiress became Zhou Nian, and her own daughter became Meng Wan.

When Cheng Xuehua and Meng Qingyan returned to the city with their two children, she had even thought, with cruel satisfaction, that this was perhaps the most right decision she would make in her whole life—and that was the last she would ever see of her own daughter.

Day after day, month after month, year after year, all these years long, Qin Yi'e had repented every single day for the sin she'd committed. Yet she also prayed this secret would forever be hers alone to bear—that if Heaven meant to punish anyone, let it punish her, and never visit the reckoning upon the child.

Countless times she'd imagined what her daughter might look like grown, longing to catch even a distant glimpse of her. Now the girl stood right before her, and she could scarcely believe it.

Tears trembling at the corners of her eyes, her lips quivering uncontrollably, Meng Wan asked in a choked voice, "After all these years, have you ever regretted the choice you made back then?"

Qin Yi'e's eyes had already brimmed over, her breath coming ragged and uneven. What was done was done—what use was regret now?

"Did they find out?" Qin Yi'e fretted that her daughter might have been made to suffer. "This is none of your doing—it's all my fault, I'm the one who deserves to die. I lost my head back then. I only wanted you to have a good place to grow up, didn't want you to repeat my own ruined life. If they want to beat me or curse me, let them come at me. I'll get down on my knees and apologize to them, I'll kowtow to them!"

Meng Wan let out a soft, bitter laugh and wiped the tears from her eyes. If only it were that simple.

"They don't know yet. Only I know."

"How did you find out?"

"Never mind that." Meng Wan sniffed back her stuffed-up, tear-clogged nose. "Things are as they are now, and nothing you do will undo any of it. There's one thing I need you to promise me first. Everything else, we'll talk about later."

"Tell me." Qin Yi'e couldn't stop her sobbing. "Anything I'm able to do, I'll agree to."

"I need you to promise me—call off Zhou Nian's wedding."

Qin Yi'e was slightly stunned, at a loss as to why Meng Wan would make such a demand. Nian Nian's wedding was this very Saturday. To cancel it now—what would people think? And besides, the groom's family would surely never agree.

"This..." Qin Yi'e gathered her wits a little and stepped out of James's hold. "This probably won't do."

"Didn't you just say you'd agree to anything I asked? How is this suddenly not possible?" Meng Wan took a step toward her.

"I did...but..." Qin Yi'e knit her brows, deeply troubled, the thumb of her right hand unconsciously picking at the thumb of her left.

Before she could finish, Meng Wan cut her off: "No buts. This wedding cannot go ahead!"

"Why can't it?" With no reason at all, how was she supposed to call it off? "This marriage was settled long ago. If it's to be canceled, there has to be a reason."

A reason, a reason.

Meng Wan's mind raced furiously, but for the moment she really couldn't come up with a fitting one. She could hardly say she'd been reborn and witnessed with her own eyes the wretched end the two of them came to.

"If I can give you a reason, can you stop them from marrying?"

Though Qin Yi'e didn't understand why Meng Wan was so set on Nian Nian calling off the wedding, seeing how grave her expression was, she gathered the girl must know something.

"Can you tell me—has something happened with Jianxing?"

Sun Jianxing was Zhou Nian's fiancé.

"He drinks—badly." Meng Wan wasn't entirely sure whether it had started yet at this point. "For Zhou Nian to marry him is to leap into a pit of fire. No good will ever come of it."

Qin Yi'e had thought it would be something far worse. "Isn't it just a bit of drinking? There's not a single man in all of Dongping Village who doesn't drink. Your pa—"

The words stopped there. Qin Yi'e looked at Meng Wan in embarrassment and apologized, "I'm sorry, I..."

Meng Wan brushed aside the ripple that word stirred in her, but Qin Yi'e's ignorance had genuinely vexed her, and she stamped her foot in frustration. "Drinking too much is not the same as having a drinking problem!"

Afraid she'd lose her temper, James stepped forward at once to explain: "Auntie, alcoholism means drinking to excess over a long period, to the point where it's already done clear harm to a person's physical health and mental state. It's no longer ordinary drinking—in medicine and psychology it's classified as a form of addiction. If it isn't treated in time, it can very well lead to violent behavior."

James rattled off a whole string of it, but the only part Qin Yi'e caught was "violent behavior" at the end.

"You mean he'd hit people?" Qin Yi'e fell to weighing it. If he'd hit people, then Nian Nian certainly couldn't be married off to him. There were men in their village who beat their wives too, and the sight of those women with their faces all bruised black and blue had set her shaking with anger, half wanting to go thrash the men herself!

Meng Wan picked up her thread: "That's right—he'd hit people. People with a drinking problem are very unstable emotionally. The slightest displeasure, and they take it out on their family. Just think—if Zhou Nian marries him, she'll be the one lying closest to him every night, facing that danger at every moment. Even if she isn't your own flesh and blood, you couldn't bear to see her married to a man like that, could you?"

Meng Wan could only overstate it a little, and borrow on Qin Yi'e's guilt toward Zhou Nian, in the hope that she'd help put a stop to the wedding.

Qin Yi'e stood rooted where she was, thinking it over in a daze. Then she lifted her head, her tear-streaked face turning grave as she looked at Meng Wan, and said in a steady voice, "I'll do it."
