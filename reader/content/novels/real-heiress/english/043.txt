Chapter 43

By the time she had everything arranged, evening had already fallen, yet Meng Wan didn't feel the least bit tired. This was probably the telltale sign of someone bitten by the "power bug."

In high spirits, she told everyone she'd see them tomorrow and left the travel agency. As she walked, her mind ran through all the important matters of the past little while. There was so much to keep track of, big and small, that for a moment she could hardly manage it all.

She had the nagging feeling she'd forgotten something, but for the life of her she couldn't remember what. It seemed fairly important, too. Frowning, she traced the days backward one by one, recalling everything that had happened the past week.

Working her way back, she remembered she still owed James three thousand yuan.

Wait a moment—today was James's birthday!

The day they'd parted, the two of them had agreed to meet tonight at the Yuxiang Restaurant to celebrate it!

Meng Wan glanced quickly at the watch on her wrist. It was already nearly seven, and James's birthday present was still over at Old Bai's place.

At that thought, she decided at once to swing by Old Bai's first to pick up the gift, then rush straight to the Yuxiang to celebrate with James. She should still make it in time.

What she hadn't counted on was how scarce taxis would be out on the streets at this hour. She walked briskly and tried to flag one down the whole way, and only managed to catch one after about half an hour.

By the time she reached Old Bai's and had the gift in hand, it was nearly eight o'clock. Old Bai had been waiting for her all this while. He'd made up his mind to make tourist souvenirs together with Meng Wan, and over the past few days he'd even finished the design sketches. He handed her a thick stack of drawings and, pulling her aside, began sharing his design philosophy.

Meng Wan was pressed for time. She humored him with a few vague "uh-huhs" and said she'd take the sketches home to study properly and give him her answer tomorrow.

By the time she finally arrived at the Yuxiang, it was nearly nine. Only a scattered handful of diners were still finishing their meals—and in that familiar spot, there was no sign of James.

Had he never come? Or had he given up waiting and left first?

Crestfallen, Meng Wan asked a waiter. The waiter said James had indeed been there. He hadn't ordered anything—he'd seemed to be waiting for someone—and after drinking two pots of tea and seeing that no one came, he'd left.

He hadn't been gone long.

Anxious now, Meng Wan flagged down another cab and hurried to the hotel where James was staying.

As luck would have it, the moment she stepped out of the car she saw James coming out the hotel's main doors.

Delighted, every fiber of her wanted to fly to his side, to apologize for standing him up. She was sure James would understand, and then she could celebrate his birthday and give him his present.

But just as her feet were about to leave the ground, the next instant she froze where she stood, unable to move.

A woman had stepped out of the car ahead of him. It was too dark for Meng Wan to make out her face; she could only tell the woman was dressed very fashionably.

The moment she got out, the woman rushed toward James, slipped her arm through his, and the two of them walked into the hotel together, laughing and talking.

The smile glowing on James's face was one Meng Wan had never seen before—relaxed, full of intimacy and ease.

That smile, reserved for someone else, stung her. The smile on her own face drew back bit by bit, the joy in her eyes slowly faded, and a wave of bitterness welled up in her chest.

On the way home, Meng Wan couldn't help spinning wild theories. That day, James hadn't accepted her confession outright but had asked to wait until today to answer. Could it be that he already had someone he loved, and, unable to bring himself to reject her flatly, he'd wanted to put it off a few days to prepare some explanation she could accept?

Could that really be it?

Her chest tightened. Remembering how, on impulse, she had confessed to him that day, she curled her toes in embarrassment. Hadn't he probably been laughing at her in his heart all along?

She carried the gloomy weather of her mood home. Meng Xingyang, too caught up in his own excitement to notice her spirits, called out to her eagerly and poured out everything he'd learned at work that day.

After his meeting that day, he'd gone straight to the colleague in charge of arranging the inspection delegation and asked about how the delegation's tour guide was chosen.

The colleague told him their unit would soon be receiving a delegation from the neighboring province. The guide was still being selected, but in all likelihood it would be the same as in past years—they'd pick a guide whose schedule fit from among the travel agencies they knew.

When Meng Xingyang mentioned that his own sister was a tour guide, the colleague immediately caught his meaning and was more than happy to hand the delegation over to Meng Wan. After all, they had to pick someone regardless, and this way he could do their section chief a favor in the bargain, at no cost to himself.

Having rattled off all of this, Meng Xingyang fished for praise. "Well? Isn't your big brother reliable? I said I'd ask around for you, and I went and locked it down outright."

Meng Wan's mind wasn't on him at all. She'd half-heartedly caught a few key words, enough to know the matter of the delegation was settled.

By rights she should have thanked him properly, but her heart was simply too tangled just then, and she couldn't bring herself to say anything nice. She managed only a few perfunctory words of thanks before pleading tiredness and saying she wanted to go to her room to rest.

Sun Jing, knitting a sweater off to the side, had been listening to the exchange between brother and sister. She'd clearly noticed that her sister-in-law's mood had been off from the moment she walked in. Seeing her own husband starting to bristle at the cold treatment, Sun Jing hurried over and quietly talked him down.

"Xiao Wan's probably in a bad mood today. Look how weighed down she is—let her rest the night. Once she comes around, she'll thank you properly, I'm sure of it."

Watching Meng Wan's despondent, lost figure retreat, Meng Xingyang forced the words he'd meant to say back down his throat. Truly, the little princess of the household!

------

Men—out with the old, in with the new.

Meng Wan comforted herself over and over. She was at exactly the age to throw herself into building her career. Once she made something of herself, finding whatever sort of man she wanted would be no trouble at all.

Since he already had someone he loved, then she absolutely couldn't go on tangled up with him. She would scrape together the money to repay him at once, then draw a clear line between them and have as little contact as possible from now on.

Make money!

Driven by the voice in her heart, Meng Wan sprang up from the bed at once, got down, switched on the desk lamp, and spread out the design sketches she'd gotten from Old Bai, studying them in earnest.

Since they were tourist souvenirs, they had to carry local flavor—the kind of thing a visitor would see and want to buy to take home and keep.

What were Yuyang's distinctive features?

Its historical landmarks, surely. The souvenir for Langya Pavilion, for instance, ought to have something to do with calligraphy—they could carve the two characters "Langya" as a keepsake.

For Qingyun Pagoda, they could carve a miniature version of the tower.

For the Buddhist grotto, a small jade Buddha statue.

As long as the carving was exquisite and the price cheap, there were sure to be tourists willing to buy.

Old Bai hadn't disappointed her either. He'd drawn up a design for nearly every named scenic spot in Yuyang City, and the contents matched her own ideas to an uncanny degree—he'd chosen the signature view of each site almost without exception.

The souvenir for the Peony Garden in particular, that peony bloom rendered in distinct, layered petals, made even Meng Wan want one for herself.

Her thoughts were carried away by all the work Old Bai had put into these designs, and the talk of love and romance was thrown clean out of her mind.

Making money was still the most important thing!

Early the next morning, Meng Wan arrived bright and early at Old Bai's jade workshop, the design drafts she'd revised past midnight in hand.

Old Bai watched as she went through his life's work sheet by sheet, marking each one and writing her own thoughts on them, and a feeling of being valued welled up in him.

Among today's young people, few had both ideas and the drive to carry them out. Meng Wan was one of that rare few.

"Old Bai." Meng Wan had changed her form of address, too, from "Master Bai" to "Old Bai." She felt it made their relationship feel a touch closer. "All of your designs are practically perfect. Some of them can be done exactly as you envisioned, no problem at all. For others I've written down a few thoughts of my own, and I hope you'll take them on board. Once you've looked through these drawings, you can start on mass production. It would be best to carve at least twenty pieces of each type to begin with."

"Twenty?" Old Bai sat at his workbench leafing through the drafts. "There are twenty designs here in all. Twenty pieces of each comes to four hundred. I've only got one pair of hands—even if I went without food or sleep, I could carve six a day at most. That'd take till the cows come home."

Meng Wan had thought of this problem too. One person clearly couldn't manage it. To turn a profit, you first had to be willing to invest the capital.

"One person definitely isn't enough. You hire three more craftsmen, and the four of you working together should be able to finish before summer break."

There was about a month to go until the school holidays. Summer was sure to be a peak travel season, and she meant to seize the chance to make their agency's specialty known.

Now that was more like it.

Old Bai did the math and figured the timing would work. His eyes rolled toward her. "Hiring people's easy enough to say. It's the money to hire them that..."

He could hardly be expected to pay out of his own pocket. And besides, there was the cost of the jade, the cost of the machinery—every single thing was an expense, and without money you couldn't take a step.

Meng Wan had come prepared. She took a thousand yuan from her bag and set it on his workbench. "Take this and use it first. Try to get a receipt for every expense. For anything you really can't get a receipt for, just record it in the account book. Pay the workers' wages a month at a time, and when it runs short next month, come ask me for more."

Handing over all the money to Old Bai outright was an enormous show of trust—after all, every link in this chain offered an opening for graft.

Old Bai hadn't expected Meng Wan to be so frank. Never mind that he'd had no such intentions to begin with—even if he had, he couldn't have done anything to betray her, not when she trusted him this much.

"Don't you worry." Old Bai looked at her, his expression grave. "I'll make sure every cent is spent where it counts. I'll keep the accounts crystal clear, and I guarantee not a single fen will be wasted."

With matters here settled smoothly, Meng Wan rode her bike on to the cultural center.

She'd originally meant to find Xu Qiuyun and persuade her to quit her job and come work for her. But just as she rounded the corner into the alley, she spotted two familiar figures at the cultural center's entrance, trading tender, lingering words.

Astonished, Meng Wan stared at Xu Qiuyun and Lin Dong. When had these two gotten together behind her back?

Well, well—falling in love and hiding it from her, too. Did they even count her as a friend?
