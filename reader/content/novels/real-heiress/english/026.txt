Chapter 26

Early the next morning, Lin Dong drove the bus to pick up Meng Wan, and the two of them turned up at the hotel right on time.

Xiaohai had been waiting in the lobby for a while already. Back when he was doing the cleaning, he'd shown up even earlier than this.

On duty today was Chen Hui. The moment she saw Meng Wan she handed her a cup of warm water.

"A cup of warm water in the morning is good for you."

"Thanks, Sister Hui." Meng Wan took it with a smile. "Xiaohai's done well these past few days. Points him where you want him, and he listens."

That was exactly the subject Chen Hui cared about most.

She shot a glance at her nephew. When Xiaohai had told her the same thing earlier, she'd had her doubts—afraid he was fibbing. But now that Meng Wan said it too, she could finally set her mind at ease. She knew Meng Wan was a straight-talker; if Xiaohai had done something wrong, she would surely have told her to her face.

When Li Jianshu came down, the three of them were still holding yesterday's formation, with Chen Hui now standing alongside.

Chen Hui had no idea what had passed between the two of them. Smiling, she introduced them. "Jianshu's an old neighbor of mine. Back then it was all iron rice bowls and eating from the big communal pot, everyone just getting by. But Jianshu here had big ambitions—he packed up his wife and kids and went straight south, said he was going to carve out a place for himself. And sure enough, look at him now, a big boss. The rest of us are still here, never budged from our own little patch of dirt."

Listening to Chen Hui's praise, Li Jianshu instinctively glanced at Meng Wan, the corner of his mouth tugging up despite himself. See how impressive I am now? A little tour guide who dared not do as he said—well, he wasn't the Li Jianshu of years ago. These days money had straightened his spine right up. He was big boss Li Jianshu now.

Li Jianshu patted his round belly and said modestly, "Boss this, boss that—I'm still the same Jianshu from Daqian Street. I've just made a bit more money, that's all. Compared to the real bosses who open department stores and hotels, I'm nowhere close."

Nowhere close indeed. Meng Wan bit her lip to keep from laughing. Such clumsy acting—absurd, just absurd. She couldn't help thinking of James again. Truly, the male of the species came in marvelous variety.

"Oh, right." Chen Hui suddenly remembered something. "How's Huaiyu doing? I haven't seen her in years."

Li Jianshu's wife, Zhou Huaiyu, had been the best needlewoman in their part of town. Back when Chen Hui was busy with work, Huaiyu had patched her younger son's trousers for her several times—a good woman who knew how to keep house.

At the sound of that name, Li Jianshu's eyes dimmed and his expression turned heavy. He let out a deep sigh. "Huaiyu passed away the year before last."

Hearing this awful news, Chen Hui's eyes went wide with shock. Zhou Huaiyu's voice and smile seemed as vivid as yesterday. Such a fine person—how could she just be gone?

The dead couldn't be brought back. All Chen Hui could do was offer a few earnest words of comfort. "My condolences, Brother Jianshu. Huaiyu was such a good soul—she's sure to be reborn into a good life."

"Aah," Li Jianshu sighed heavily. "It was her hard luck, really. Just when things were about to get good for the two of us, she up and left."

Inwardly Meng Wan rolled her eyes. More likely you worked the poor woman into an early grave, and here you are calling it her hard luck. The man had no conscience.

"And the children?"

Chen Hui recalled they'd had two sons—the elder should be older than Xiaohai, and the younger had been just a month old when they left.

"The eldest is already working. The younger one's still in school."

"With you this busy, who looks after the children?"

Li Jianshu sighed. "Exactly. That's why I've come back to Yuyang this time—I want to find someone trustworthy, someone who knows where she comes from, to look after them."

Wasn't that just a fancy way of saying he wanted to find himself a new wife? Dressing it up as "finding someone to look after the children." If it were really about the children, he could simply hire a nanny.

Chen Hui caught his meaning too. Even knowing the ways of men, all she could do was play along. "Yes, the children do need someone to care for them."

——

Thankfully, over the next few days Li Jianshu behaved himself and did nothing out of line. Only occasionally Meng Wan would catch him sneaking glances at her. She pretended not to notice—it was only a few days, she could grit her teeth and get through it. No one was going to keep her from making her money.

By convention, a tour guide would take the clients shopping at certain stores on the last day, and in return collect a kickback. It was an arrangement everyone understood without saying. But Meng Wan didn't want to earn that particular money. She felt it wasn't money she ought to take.

Every profession had its principles, and Meng Wan's profession was service. Every principle had to start from one premise: the customer's satisfaction.

So Meng Wan had set herself three basic principles:

First, always greet every customer with the fullest enthusiasm, and never let personal feelings color the way she treated them.

Second, there were no picky customers, only guides who hadn't done well enough. Always start from the customer's interests.

Third, making money was the main goal but not the foremost one. Every yuan she earned had to sit right with her conscience—earned through her own ability and wits, every last cent.

Maybe some would think Meng Wan was being precious about it—who cared how the money was made, so long as it broke no laws it was lawful income. But Meng Wan believed that to do something big, you couldn't fix your eyes only on the gains right in front of you.

Especially on the path of business. If you started out with no principles, forever wriggling through the loopholes in policy, you could make a bit of money, but you'd find it hard to grow into anything big. Meng Wan's ambitions had never been as simple as just being a tour guide. So from the very start she had to lay a solid foundation. Only the person who held to principle could go further.

Meng Wan didn't want to lead the tour group into a spending spree, but there was no resisting someone who came begging to go.

"I hear our Yuyang's Yiyuan jade is very efficacious. Take us to have a look, Tour Guide Meng," proposed one man, on the short side, speaking in heavily accented Mandarin.

Businesspeople were great believers in such things. Nearly every one of their households kept a shrine to whatever deity they worshipped, praying for daily profit and a flourishing trade.

Meng Wan hesitated. Her instinct was not to take them, but since they were asking like this she couldn't well refuse—only she hadn't arranged anything with a shop in advance, so they were sure to quote a steep price.

"Tour Guide Meng, do you know a jade shop?" the man pressed on.

"I do." Meng Wan had no choice but to agree. "Let's head over now."

This was the customer walking into the net of his own accord, not something she'd pushed for, so it wasn't on her. Worst case, she'd help haggle the price down later, and count whatever she shaved off as her kickback.

In one of the counties under Yuyang City there was a mountain called Manqu, and it was on this mountain that the heart of Yiyuan jade production lay.

"Manqu" came from the "Classic of the Central Mountains" in the Classic of Mountains and Seas: "Another hundred and fifty li to the west lies Manqu Mountain, rich in gold and jade above." That was how the mountain got its name.

The jade pendant made from Yiyuan jade had as its main form three clouds of differing sizes stacked one atop another—the lowest the largest, growing smaller toward the top. The natural veining of the Yiyuan jade glimmered faintly between the clouds, like a mountain ridgeline hidden in mist.

There was a saying behind the pendant's three-tiered structure, passed down by the elders. It was called the Three-Tiered Cloud. The bottom layer was the "Root"—wherever you go, don't lose your true self. The middle was "Home"—however far you wander, someone is waiting for you. The top was the "Road"—however hard the trouble, once the clouds part the sky brightens.

The Three-Tiered Cloud pendant had been handed down to this day and could almost serve as a symbol of Yuyang itself, which made it a guide's top choice when taking tourists shopping.

Most people couldn't judge the price of a piece of jade from its appearance; they bought and sold purely on its meaning. If they thought it dear, they'd only assume there was a reason it was dear, and wouldn't haggle much. As a result, many jade shops passed off flawed, second-rate goods as the genuine article to wring out the largest profit.

The jade shop Meng Wan was taking the tourists to had been recommended to her by Accountant Bi. All the guides at their travel agency worked with this shop. This was Meng Wan's first time there, and she had no idea what the quality was like.

It was an old storefront facing the street, its wooden door already a little weathered, a faded sign hanging above. The display window held a few ordinary trinkets.

Stepping inside, you felt a few wisps of coolness. The jade glowed faintly in the glass cases. A back door led to the workshop, where a jade carver was at work; you could just make out the droning hum of the carving machine.

When they arrived, there happened to be another tour group in the shop, listening to a clerk explain the various jades. Standing nearby was a man in an army-green vest holding a yellow flag—presumably their guide. The flag was rolled up, so Meng Wan couldn't see the lettering. She assumed all guides used flags of the same color and thought nothing of it.

Meng Wan led her own group to the other side of the shop. The moment a clerk saw so many people come in, she hurried over, brimming with eagerness.

"Come in, come in." The saleswoman warmly ushered them through.

"You're new, aren't you?" she asked, sidling up to Meng Wan.

Meng Wan nodded.

The saleswoman lowered her voice with a conspiratorial air. "Don't you worry. Leave it to me in a bit."

And with that she shot Meng Wan a smug little look, as if to say: watch how I work.

At once Meng Wan caught the saleswoman by the arm and pulled her aside, murmuring, "Sister, I don't want the commission on this sale. Give them a better price—it isn't easy for folks to make a trip out here."

The saleswoman couldn't believe her ears. Someone actually turning down cash in hand? "Little miss, that won't do. Everything in our shop is marked at a fixed price. If I give you a discount and the other guides find out, what then?"

As she spoke she cut a glance at the male guide nearby.

Meng Wan understood her meaning. If the shop gave her a discount, the other guides would surely think the shop had cut her a special deal—not that the guide herself had given up money she could have made.

And there was the biggest problem of all: customers who got the same item at different prices were bound to be unhappy. Nobody wanted to pay more than someone else for the very same thing.

This really was a fix. What rotten luck, running into another tour group. Meng Wan put her hands on her hips, her foot tapping out a restless beat on the floor. Making money was hard—but who'd have thought not wanting to make money could be this hard too.

It was no skin off her own back not to earn, but the way she was going about it would cut into the other guides' earnings. The trouble was, she wasn't doing anything wrong—it was just that, with everyone in the business operating this way, she stood out as utterly out of step.

And she remembered too: it was precisely because such hard-sell practices had grown so rampant that, to protect the image of Yuyang's tourism, the authorities had eventually issued the Regulations on the Management of Tour Guides, spelling out plainly that guides must not accept kickbacks or pocket commissions, and certainly must not take people shopping at unauthorized establishments.

"How about this, then." Meng Wan tried to offer a solution. "Do you have any modestly priced little items I could give my customers as a free gift? I'll pay for it—you just say the shop's throwing it in."

It was the best win-win she could come up with.

The saleswoman studied the counter and thought it over. Small items they did have—only she'd have to check with the shop manager, since there'd never been a precedent for this sort of thing before.

A few minutes later the saleswoman came striding back, beaming. "The manager says we'll do it just the way you said."

What the manager had actually said was: since there's a sucker willing to spend extra, what reason do we have to refuse?

With Meng Wan's problem solved, the saleswoman launched into her pitch for the tourists—the jade's origins, its meanings, its craftsmanship, its quality.

"The Yiyuan jade from inside Manqu Mountain, our own local Yuyang jade—it's not the see-through kind. It's got a bit of cotton to it, but where it wins is the oily richness. You'll know the moment it's in your hand—warm to the touch, most comfortable worn right against the skin."

As she spoke, the saleswoman brought out several pieces of jade for everyone to try on, building a connection between the tourists and the jade. This was a standard sales tactic.

"How much for this Three-Tiered Cloud pendant?" someone asked, curious.

"The price is secondary. What matters most, big brother, is that you like it—that it speaks to you. The one that suits you is the best one of all."

Instead of telling you the price outright, she let you form an attachment to the product first. By the time the price came up, however high it was, you'd grit your teeth and buy it anyway.

The big brother put on the Three-Tiered Cloud pendant. Against the skin of his chest it was indeed warm, without a trace of chill, and the pendant fell at just the right length to cover the scar across his chest.

The sharp-eyed saleswoman kept coaxing. "Our jade chooses its owner, you know. One look at you wearing this and anyone can tell it's yours—it's bound to bless you with health and long life."

"I'll take this one!"

The man stroked the jade, gladly accepting all its lovely promises. What was a piece of jade worth, after all—if it could bring him health, that was beyond any price.

Only then did the saleswoman name the pendant's price: a hundred and fifty yuan apiece, near enough to half a month's wages for an ordinary worker.

When the others learned the price, they too found it a touch steep. But there was no resisting the flowery web of words the saleswoman spun around this little pendant, enchanting it, lifting the price of the jade up onto the plane of meaning—until what it actually cost was something no one cared about anymore.

That was the gift of selling: you had to know how to tell a story, and make the customer believe your story. Then he'd be glad to pay for it.

Every single person in Meng Wan's group ended up with a Three-Tiered Cloud pendant of their own. The shop hadn't rung up a sale this big in ages.

The manager couldn't keep the grin off his face. He pulled every last clerk over to serve this side, leaving the other group looking rather neglected.

The guide next door was watching the spectacle over here with naked envy. Meng Wan turned, their eyes met, and the two of them gave each other a friendly nod.

He came striding straight over to strike up a conversation. "You're new, right? Don't recognize your face."

Everyone in this line of work was a local; sooner or later you'd cross paths at the sights and form some impression of one another. A fresh face like Meng Wan's—a young woman, no less—was plainly new to the trade.

Meng Wan nodded. "Started not long ago."

"Then you've got some luck—landing a group this big right off the bat." The man's eyes were full of envy. What a kickback that must be.

Meng Wan just smiled and said nothing. However big the kickback, it wasn't hers.

"Which agency are you with?" the man went on.

"Youth Travel."

"What are the odds?" The man looked astonished. "So am I!"
