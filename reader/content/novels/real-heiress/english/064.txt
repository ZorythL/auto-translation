Chapter 64

Receiving James's family went very smoothly. Meng Wan presented everyone with the jade ornaments she'd prepared as welcome gifts, earning unanimous praise.

Because James had no time to spare, he only accompanied them for half a day—introducing Meng Wan to his family and translating for her through the morning.

Since the commentary had to be entirely in English, that task fell to Lan Xiuli. Though she stumbled now and then, on the whole it flowed fairly well; the foreign visitors could basically understand her, and even raised a few questions.

Meng Wan answered the questions and Yu Fei helped translate them across. The three of them worked together quite seamlessly, and by the end of the day they had the whole flow down. Yu Fei's confidence soared too—leading a group didn't seem nearly as hard as she'd imagined.

Madam Ye Xinzhu joined the tour group the second day, touring her own hometown once again with Meng Wan as guide.

James's father was named Kevin, and you could tell that Madam Ye and he were very much in love—when they were together they were practically inseparable.

Kevin was a gentleman even more refined-looking than James, exuding a scholar's air from head to toe. His straight, high-bridged nose and deep brown eyes were striking, and when he spoke he always looked at you with a kindly smile. Though the marks of time—gray hair and wrinkles—had wrapped themselves around him, it wasn't hard to see that in his youth he must have been a strikingly handsome young man.

Growing up in a family like this, James's childhood must have been a very happy one.

In the few days she spent with James's family, though each day left Meng Wan a little tired, she was also very happy. His family were all good people; every time she spoke, they'd respond even when they couldn't understand a word.

On the last evening, Meng Wan invited James along and treated everyone to a meal at the restaurant they were about to open. True, it hadn't opened yet, but Meng Wan still wanted everyone to sample Master Hai's cooking—after all, it wasn't easy for them to make this trip, and they really ought to taste some of Yuyang's specialties before they left.

That night, Meng Wan saw everyone back to the hotel and bid each of them farewell. They said they'd surely come again.

Meng Wan heard one of them say something that sounded like "wan-ding," and then James started laughing. A little bewildered, she went along with him and laughed too.

On the way out, Meng Wan asked James what they'd just been saying. James told her the word meant "wedding"—they'd said that when Meng Wan and he got married, they'd definitely come back for it.

Meng Wan started calculating in her head. "That many people—the gift money should add up to quite a bit, no?"

James tapped her forehead lightly, his voice doting. "Little money-grubber, our customs aren't the same as Yuyang's—we don't give gift money at weddings."

Meng Wan turned serious at once. "What a backward custom! How can you not give gift money at a wedding? Then what do you give? Surely you don't come empty-handed?"

James lifted the corner of his mouth, opening the car door to let her in, then bent his own legs and slid into the driver's seat.

"We give things. For example, you can write up a list of whatever appliances or other gifts you want, and everyone chips in together to buy them for you."

This was the first time Meng Wan had heard of such a custom. She secretly ran through what James's new home was still lacking, knitting her brows and pondering for a good while—only to find he seemed to have already furnished it with everything.

She had the feeling of a gift slipping right out of her grasp, and couldn't help sighing. "Ah, well, it doesn't matter—as long as the people come, that's enough. I'm not one to care about gifts."

James started the car to take her home, working the wheel as he said, "Don't worry—I'll have them give you a big-ticket item."

"What big-ticket item?" Meng Wan's eyes went wide and bright as she turned to look at James.

James put on an air of mystery. "It's a secret. You'll find out when the time comes."

"Hmph." Meng Wan turned back, miffed. "And when exactly is 'when the time comes'?"

Though she'd pictured the day she and James would marry, she always felt that day was still far off—she wasn't ready yet.

"Whenever you say. I'll go along with it anytime."

Meng Wan really did think it over seriously. "Then let's wait until our agency expands. By then I'll have a bumper harvest in both career and family."

James sighed, a touch sour. "Looks like in my girlfriend's heart, work still comes before me."

——

The next day, just as the sky was growing faintly light, the pager suddenly went off, jolting Meng Wan awake from her sleep.

Very reluctantly, she fumbled at the bedside for the buzzing little gadget and pried her eyes open, heavy with drowsiness, to find a message from James telling her to call back immediately.

Meng Wan was instantly far more awake. She bolted upright in bed and rushed for the living room without even bothering to put on her shoes, dialing the number on the pager.

While she waited for the call to connect, ten thousand thoughts flashed through her mind. Had something happened to James's family? Had a flight been delayed?

"Hello!"

It was James's voice—but not his usual bright, gentle tone. Instead it sounded as if it had been rubbed raw with sandpaper, hoarse and worn out.

Meng Wan's heart lurched. Something must have happened. "Calling this early—what's wrong?"

On the other end, James said wearily, "Xiao Wan, my father's been hospitalized."

After hanging up, Meng Wan sat frozen on the sofa, stunned for a moment. On the phone, James had said Kevin and a few of the other uncles had suddenly come down with vomiting and diarrhea in the middle of the night. The doctor said it was acute gastroenteritis—they must have eaten something unclean.

After dinner that evening at her restaurant, Meng Wan had seen everyone back to the hotel and watched them go into their rooms, so the only possibility was that something had gone wrong with that dinner.

A chill ran down Meng Wan's spine. Could it be that her restaurant had fallen before it had even taken the field—dying before achieving victory?

There was no time to dwell on it. By the time Meng Wan rushed to the hospital, the sun was already halfway up the sky, and all the patients had finished their IV drips and were resting in their hospital beds.

"James, how is everyone?" Meng Wan came hurrying up at a jog, out of breath. They were staying at the hospital where Cheng Xuehua worked, so Meng Wan found the place easily, knowing it well.

James stepped forward to steady her and said gently, "They're not awake yet, but they're out of danger now. The doctor says one more round of IV fluids and they can be discharged."

Only then did Meng Wan let out a breath, the worry that had been hanging over her finally settling. "As long as they're all right—that's what matters."

Seeing how disheveled her hair was, James guessed she'd rushed over the moment she woke without time to tidy herself. He used both hands as a comb to smooth her hair. "I'm sorry, I shouldn't have disturbed you so early. But we really were short-handed, and none of the others speak Chinese—even if they came, they'd be no help."

Meng Wan shot him a look. "What do you mean, disturbed? This is what I should be doing. Never mind that they're your family—I'm also the guide responsible for them. This happened after they ate at the restaurant I took them to, so I can't wash my hands of it either."

Meng Wan was utterly shaken now. The one blessing amid the misfortune was that this had happened to their own people. Had it been other tourists, she couldn't even imagine what she'd be facing.

James comforted her with a few words. They'd ask once the patients woke up—it was entirely possible they'd eaten something else afterward.

Cheng Xuehua was on shift today, so Meng Wan called the deputy director over directly. Having a knowledgeable elder around at a time like this was a great comfort.

Cheng Xuehua personally examined the several patients again and confirmed it was acute gastroenteritis brought on by food poisoning. Luckily they'd been brought in promptly the night before, so one more IV drip and they could go home to recuperate.

James had already rebooked the plane tickets for the next day, so today they could all get a good rest.

Hearing Cheng Xuehua's words, Ye Xinzhu was much reassured. She took her hands and thanked her over and over. Both of them knew their children were dating each other, so this counted as the in-laws meeting ahead of time. Pleased with each other's child, and finding the other parent courteous and well-mannered, they both relaxed considerably—the match was tacitly settled in both their hearts.

Around midday, Kevin and the other uncles woke one after another. James went over and asked exactly what had caused the food poisoning. He and Meng Wan had eaten Master Hai's cooking the night before too, and neither of them had any trouble—they must have eaten something unclean behind his back.

Sure enough, Kevin said that after they'd returned to the hotel the night before, before long they'd smelled something delicious wafting up to the window. He really couldn't resist and went downstairs to buy five fried meat pies to share with everyone—and after that came the vomiting and diarrhea.

Meng Wan immediately let out a sigh of relief. It seemed the street stall was the real culprit. In these times the street vendors had no licenses, here today and gone tomorrow, with no guarantee whatsoever of food quality—which was exactly why Sun Jing never let Zhizhi buy snacks off the street.

Kevin was deeply apologetic that, on a whim and unable to hold his cravings in check, he'd not only landed the whole family in the hospital but also made his two children fuss over him for so long.

Watching Kevin, a man nearing sixty, apologize to them like a child, all of Meng Wan's morning of fright and exhaustion melted away in an instant. The old turning childlike—wasn't that Kevin to a tee?

She comforted Kevin with a few words, telling him not to dwell on it; resting well was what mattered most. Really nothing had been spoiled—it just meant going home a day later, that was all.

The doctor instructed that they could only eat light liquid food. Meng Wan, who knew where the cafeteria was, took James along to fetch meals for the patients.

She'd brought the lunch boxes from home, and at the serving window asked the auntie for five portions of rice porridge.

James, following her directions, went to the water room to get hot water. Meng Wan, lunch boxes in hand, had barely turned around when a familiar face suddenly came into view right in front of her.

"Manager Qian?"

Qian Zhenyu was holding a lunch box too—it seemed he was also here to fetch food for a patient.

"Meng Wan?" Qian Zhenyu was a bit taken aback to run into an acquaintance here—an acquaintance he didn't even like, at that. But since she'd greeted him, the necessary pleasantries still had to be exchanged. "You here looking after a patient too?"

Meng Wan glanced at the lunch boxes in her hands and answered with a smile. "Something like that. Just helping fetch some food."

Qian Zhenyu, with his mind on his mother back in the ward, exchanged a few words and then took his leave. "I'm a bit busy, so I'll be off."

"Take care."

Meng Wan stepped aside to clear a path for him. She'd run into Qian Zhenyu several times, and every time he'd worn a haughty, overbearing air. The ordinary manner he showed today, by contrast, was a rare sight.

James had finished getting the hot water and was waving her over. Meng Wan strode forward.

Watching Meng Wan's steady, retreating figure walk over to join the foreigners—the same ones from outside the Tourism Bureau the other day—Qian Zhenyu felt a stir of curiosity, and his feet couldn't help following along.

If he could catch some bit of leverage on Meng Wan, that would be nothing short of perfect.
