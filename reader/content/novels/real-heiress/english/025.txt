Chapter 25

Lin Dong didn't disappoint. The moment his pager went off, he called the little shop at the mouth of the hutong.

When he learned that Meng Wan needed his help, he didn't even ask what it was about—he simply agreed.

Over the phone, Meng Wan arranged to meet him at the bus station. After hanging up, she chatted a while longer with Hao Xiangping and learned that her condition had improved a good deal. The college entrance exams were less than a month away now, and Lin Xi was run off her feet. To save up for his sister's tuition and living expenses, Lin Dong was out hauling fares from dawn till dusk.

In short, the whole family had pulled together like strands twisted into a single rope, all of them slowly working toward something better.

Hao Xiangping took Meng Wan's hand and thanked her again for covering her hospital bills earlier, and for the money she'd given Lin Xi for study materials.

Beyond thanking her, she didn't know what else to say. Words were no longer enough to express her gratitude to Meng Wan, yet she couldn't scrape together the money to repay her now. This debt of kindness, she feared, would hang over her for a long time.

Meng Wan reassured her. A debt of kindness could always be repaid sooner or later, but chances to get well and to study didn't come often. Watching the two of them slowly get back on their feet made her happy. She and Lin Dong were friends, and Lin Dong's family was her family too.

Hao Xiangping genuinely adored this girl. Though she still had a bellyful of motherly things she wanted to say to her, she didn't want to keep her from her business. She went back inside, fetched a lunch tin holding a few scallion pancakes she'd made that morning, and pressed it into Meng Wan's hands, telling her to take them along to eat.

After leaving the Lin household, Xiaohai drove Meng Wan back to the bus station.

Lin Dong was already there waiting, and the instant he spotted Meng Wan he waved at her, brimming with excitement.

His goofy manner made Meng Wan laugh. How could anyone's looks be so at odds with his personality?

Before she could even introduce them, Lin Dong's gaze slid to Xiaohai standing beside her. He asked warily, "And who's this?"

Meng Wan introduced the two of them—Lin Dong was a friend, Xiaohai her apprentice.

Hearing how Xiaohai related to Meng Wan, Lin Dong couldn't help marveling, "Not bad at all. A few days out of sight and you've already taken on an apprentice."

Meng Wan didn't bother with modesty. "Couldn't help it—his talent's just that outstanding."

Xiaohai, meanwhile, stood blankly off to the side, quietly listening to the two of them talk.

The three of them stood in the station. Meng Wan handed one pancake to Xiaohai and ate two herself, then returned the lunch tin to Lin Dong.

With the driver problem solved, Meng Wan took Xiaohai off to buy twenty-odd red wrist cords.

Each cord cost her fifty cents. Looking them over and over, Meng Wan couldn't see how they were worth that much—by her estimate, the cost couldn't be more than a few cents apiece. She'd have to think about how to cut that expense down.

Everything was ready. Meng Wan met up with Xiaohai and Lin Dong at the train station.

She was just about to raise the flag, practiced as ever, when Xiaohai spoke up of his own accord. "Let me do it, Master."

Meng Wan looked him over. He had a point—at over a meter eighty, it'd be a waste not to put that height to use holding the flag.

She handed the flag to him and silently noted her apprentice's second virtue: he was tall, so he could hold the flag up high where everyone would spot it at a glance.

Sure enough, without Meng Wan having to go searching, someone caught sight of the flag and came looking for her.

"Hello—are you Tour Guide Meng?"

Meng Wan turned her head and saw a heavyset middle-aged man. By his accent he didn't sound like a southerner; he sounded more like he was from Yuyang. She figured this must be Li Jianshu, Chen Hui's friend who'd left Yuyang to make his fortune in the south.

Meng Wan smiled and introduced herself. "Hello, I'm Meng Wan. You must be Sister Chen Hui's friend."

"That's me. I never expected our tour guide to be such a beauty." The man eyed Meng Wan with some surprise, his gaze fixed dreamily on her face. "I'm Li Jianshu. You can call me Brother Dashu."

His staring made Meng Wan uncomfortable. She glanced past him. "Where's the rest of our group?"

"Ah!" Only then did Li Jianshu snap out of it. "I got so caught up talking to you I forgot all about them. I'll go bring them over right now."

Watching his anxious figure hurry off, Meng Wan found herself frowning. The way he'd stared at her just now had given her the creeps, and for some reason a bad feeling welled up. But she quickly tamped it down. It should be fine—he knew she was an acquaintance of Chen Hui's, so he shouldn't try anything out of line. Besides, she had Xiaohai with her now, more than enough to keep her safe.

Once she'd gathered up the tour group, Meng Wan introduced herself and handed out the wrist cords. A large portion of these southern small-business owners were men, but there were also five or six women bosses dressed in very fashionable styles.

The moment they saw the tour guide was a pretty young woman, they couldn't help warming to her. These days equality between men and women was the slogan, but in most lines of work it was still men who outnumbered women.

The way these women saw it, a woman was born to do any kind of work at all—the more a field was dominated by men, the more reason to go into it. There was no such thing as work that suited men better; that was just a lie people told to keep women from getting a foot in the door.

It was the same when they did business. There was always someone telling them that going out every day, putting themselves on display, dealing with all sorts of people, was no work for a girl—that making money was something best left to men.

Why should it be?

Why should a good thing like making money fall only into men's hands?

Women had all the more reason to chase a good thing like that, to fight the men for the chance to earn—that way the men would finally see just how capable women could be.

So now, seeing Meng Wan, they felt an extra closeness to her, and the moment they boarded the bus they pulled her into conversation.

From them, Meng Wan learned that the most talkative one was named Xia Qiu, and the young-looking one beside her was her younger sister, Xia Chun.

Xia Qiu was in the clothing trade down south. At first she'd done the buying-and-reselling on her own, but as the business slowly grew she'd opened a shop of her own. When she ran short of hands, she'd called her sister, still back home, to come help. By now their clothing shop had a big storefront, and they were planning to open a second one.

The other few were the three Guan sisters—Guan Feifei, Guan Tingting, and Guan Jingjing. They were in the restaurant trade. They'd started out carrying tofu through the streets and alleys on a shoulder pole, and now they had a restaurant of their own, run by the three of them together.

Listening to them tell their stories, Meng Wan couldn't help but admire them. They spoke of it lightly enough, but she could imagine how hard it must have been, how much bitterness they'd swallowed.

No matter the era, when a woman went out to make her own way, what she faced wasn't just the work itself but society's prejudice, endless mockery and contempt. That these women could stand here now among these men, chatting away with Meng Wan as equals, was proof enough of how remarkable they were.

——

When they arrived, Meng Wan first took everyone to dinner. At the table she and Xiaohai sat with the group of women, deliberately keeping clear of Li Jianshu.

She couldn't shake the feeling that something was off in the way that man looked at her. Best to keep her distance. Fortunately they were only here for five days, and after that there'd be no more dealing with him.

After dinner, Meng Wan saw everyone to the hotel, sorted out the room assignments, and reminded them all to gather in the lobby at seven the next morning and please not be late. Last of all she made a point of bidding the five women good night.

Once everyone had dispersed, Meng Wan packed her things into her bag and reminded Lin Dong he absolutely could not be late the next morning.

She'd barely gotten a few words out when she heard someone call her name. Meng Wan turned, and there was the very person she'd been hoping not to see.

"Tour Guide Meng." Li Jianshu pulled a smile across his greasy face. "You really worked hard today."

Meng Wan forced down her discomfort and kept the smile on her face. "Is there something else you needed, Mr. Li?"

Li Jianshu glanced left and right at the two guardians standing behind Meng Wan, then lowered his voice. "Tour Guide Meng, might it be convenient to have a word in private?"

He wanted to be alone with her? Not a chance.

Meng Wan declined politely, "You can just say it here. They're with me."

The implication was that whatever he had to say, they could hear it too. Meng Wan saw no need to step aside at all.

A shrewd man like Li Jianshu of course caught her meaning. He fished a banknote out of his pocket between his fingers, turned slightly to flash half of the red bill at Meng Wan, and said, "Can this matter be discussed in front of them too?"

Li Jianshu simply didn't believe Meng Wan would let a driver and a tagalong know how much money she made.

Yet the very next second he heard Meng Wan say, frank and unbothered, "Of course it can. I told you, they're my own people. They can be in on anything."

Trying to threaten her with money—he really had underestimated her. As for Lin Dong and Xiaohai, even if Meng Wan sold the two of them out, the pair would probably help her count the cash. As if he could drive a wedge between them with money. Even if those two knew she stood to make ten thousand off this one job, they wouldn't get any greedy ideas. That much trust Meng Wan had in them.

Seeing she wouldn't take the bait, Li Jianshu frowned with faint displeasure. "Before we came, I collected a hundred yuan from each person as the tour fee. I wanted to hand it over to you first."

This genuinely helped Meng Wan a great deal. In this era everything was pay-after-the-fact. For the group she'd taken before, the travel agency had advanced her the money, but this time she'd have to front it all herself—and with a crowd this size, that was no small sum. Just yesterday she'd been calculating whether the little money she had would stretch far enough.

Noticing the shift in Meng Wan's expression, Li Jianshu pressed his temptation further. "I'll collect the rest before we leave and hand it to you too. And if you ever run short in the meantime, just tell Brother Dashu—whatever it takes, I won't let little Meng be left in a bind."

A flicker of disdain crossed Meng Wan's eyes. Hearing how he'd promoted her from "Tour Guide Meng" to "little Meng," she silently rolled her eyes. Little Meng, my foot.

But she couldn't refuse money this close at hand. Meng Wan reached out to take the envelope and gave it a light tug—it didn't budge. She looked up. The man was watching her with a smug grin, no sign of letting go.

His expression seemed to say: weren't you full of backbone just now? Wouldn't even step aside when I asked. And here you are, holding out your hand to ask me for money after all.

Someone else might have felt their dignity challenged, but Li Jianshu didn't know that Meng Wan had lived through two lifetimes. He thought a petty little trick like this could rattle her?

Meng Wan curled the corner of her mouth, reached out with her other hand, gripped the envelope with both, and yanked with all her strength. The envelope whipped free into her hand.

Li Jianshu had not seen Meng Wan's sudden pull coming at all. It caught him completely off guard—his feet lost their footing and his whole body pitched forward.

Meng Wan stepped neatly aside to clear the way for him, and her two guardians behind her obligingly opened up a wide, clear road as well.

Borrowing on the sheer heft of his bulk, Li Jianshu stumbled forward several steps like a great flailing moth before he finally caught himself.

Meng Wan shot him a contemptuous look, took the envelope, and without so much as a backward glance swaggered out of the hotel.

The nerve of him—thinking a little money made him such a big deal. Your auntie Meng Wan was not about to indulge the likes of you!
