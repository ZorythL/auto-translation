Chapter 14

In her past life, Meng Wan had quit her job the moment she married, having worked it for a little over two years in total. After resigning, she devoted herself to being Xu Tian's good little homemaker—which was just a fancy way of saying housewife.

So now, as someone who'd been through it all, she sincerely hoped that married women wouldn't give up their jobs. Even if the wages were low, the money was still their own.

After taking several days of leave, Meng Wan reported to the director's office the moment she arrived at the cultural center.

Her superior, Wu Lin, was a man about the same age as Meng Qingyan—the father of one of Meng Qingyan's students. It was through that connection that she'd gotten the position in the first place.

Xu Qiuyun had told them Meng Wan was out with gastroenteritis, so when she went in, Meng Wan kept clutching her stomach and spoke in a deliberately weak, listless voice.

Wu Lin even showed warm concern, asking whether she needed to take a few more days off—the cultural center hardly noticed one more or one fewer person anyway.

Meng Wan thanked him but politely declined, insisting she was already better and perfectly able to come back to work.

Back in the library where she worked, Xu Qiuyun was tallying up the book-lending records. When she saw Meng Wan at the door, her eyes suddenly lit up, and she waved excitedly. "You're back!"

Meng Wan noticed she was wearing the blue jumpsuit dress Meng Wan had once put together for her, and her hairstyle had changed to a chin-length bob. Her whole face was glowing—she looked like a different person.

In just these few days she'd undergone such a dramatic transformation. It amazed Meng Wan, and it made her happy for her too.

"You look lovely today," Meng Wan praised without holding back.

A blush instantly spread across Xu Qiuyun's cheeks. She set down what she was doing and, bashful, pulled Meng Wan into the break room, beaming. "When you called me yesterday and asked me to take one more day off for you, I thought you wouldn't be back until tomorrow."

"Everything's sorted out, and since I had nothing to do, I came in." Meng Wan squeezed her hand and said gratefully, "Thank you for covering my shifts these past few days, so I could go take care of my own business."

"It was nothing. I'm just glad I could help you."

Seeing how bright and lively she looked, Meng Wan hesitated a few seconds before asking, "...Your family... didn't give you a hard time, did they?"

Xu Qiuyun's expression dimmed for a moment. She pulled Meng Wan down to sit and told her the whole story. "After I got home from your place that day, I had a huge fight with them. I told them I'd never hand over my wages again, and if they wouldn't agree, I'd shave my head, cut my own face, and go up the mountain to become a nun."

So that was why she'd cut her hair short. Meng Wan stroked the ends of it, her heart aching a little.

Xu Qiuyun went on, "It was the first time they'd ever seen me so resolute. They were scared out of their wits, terrified I'd slip the scissors and stab my own neck. They agreed to everything. I only sacrificed a bit of hair, that's all."

Meng Wan gave Xu Qiuyun a big thumbs-up. "They definitely won't dare bully you again."

"Now in their eyes I'm a madwoman."

"Madwoman is good," Meng Wan said. "Women hold things in too much. Sooner or later we're all bound to crack."

Xu Qiuyun was tickled by this. "If we all did, wouldn't everything fall to pieces?"

Whose pieces, exactly?

Meng Wan didn't pursue the thought. She raised an eyebrow. "Qiuyun, do you know whether our library has any books on being a tour guide?"

Xu Qiuyun had been at the cultural center longer than her and worked more meticulously. She knew where nearly every type of book was shelved.

She lowered her gaze and thought it over. It seemed a batch of new books on tour-guiding had been shelved not long ago. "Wait here. I'll go find them for you right now."

Before long, Xu Qiuyun came back with a tall stack of books, new and old mixed together, and set it on the table, slightly out of breath. "Take a look—they're all here."

Meng Wan flipped through them one by one. The new ones were the latest materials, published just last year: Foundations of National Tour-Guide Knowledge, Comprehensive Tour-Guide Knowledge, Tour-Guide Practice. They were exactly what she could use.

Seeing how intently she was reading, Xu Qiuyun asked curiously, "Are you going to take the tour-guide certification exam?"

"I want to give it a try," Meng Wan admitted frankly.

"Will your family agree to it?"

Giving up an iron rice bowl to become a tour guide—running around outdoors all day, out in the wind and rain—was something no parent would ever agree to, let alone for a daughter.

"I don't plan on telling them," Meng Wan said, closing the book. "I'll tell them once I'm on solid footing. By then it'll be a done deal, rice already cooked, and they won't be able to stop me even if they want to."

Hearing this, Xu Qiuyun sat down, looking a little crestfallen. "Then does that mean we won't see each other anymore?"

She had finally made a friend, and now that friend was going to leave.

Meng Wan studied her thoughtfully for a moment. "Maybe we could see each other every day."

Xu Qiuyun didn't understand. There didn't seem to be any travel agency near the cultural center.

"I remember you're very good at math."

Whenever new books were shelved, the work of counting and verifying quantities had always been hers, and she'd never once made a mistake.

Hearing the compliment, Xu Qiuyun smiled shyly. "Not that good, just a bit above average."

Meng Wan was full of sudden ideas. "Why don't you go get an accounting certificate?"

"An accounting certificate?" Xu Qiuyun was puzzled. "What use is that?"

Meng Wan's enthusiasm flared. "Plenty of use. One more skill, one more way out."

If one day she had a travel agency of her own, Xu Qiuyun would be her first choice to handle the money. If she got the accounting certificate now, she could step right into the role then.

Xu Qiuyun knitted her brow and gave it serious thought. She really had no use for an accounting certificate right now, but since Meng Wan said it would be useful, she'd take it.

"I'll do as you say. I'll study together with you."

"Oh, right." Meng Wan dug out the money she'd barely had time to warm. "Here's your two hundred yuan. I'm paying you back."

Xu Qiuyun didn't take it. "They don't dare ask me for money now. You keep it and use it."

Meng Wan pressed the money into Xu Qiuyun's hand. "Take it. Your big sister has money now."

Ever since she'd returned to 1995, she seemed to be constantly playing the part of a big spender. She'd better hurry up and work hard so she could become a real one!

*

The very next day, Lin Dong took Auntie Hao to the hospital. Cheng Xuehua first arranged for an eye examination, then conveniently a full physical as well. Apart from the eye problem being rather serious, there was nothing else seriously wrong—only some malnutrition.

There was still hope for the eyes. Based on the Lin family's circumstances and what they could afford, the doctor scheduled Auntie Hao for an extracapsular extraction. After the surgery, though she wouldn't see as clearly as a normal person, she'd be able to make out most things.

Cheng Xuehua first admitted Auntie Hao to the hospital to wait for the operation.

Lin Dong thanked Cheng Xuehua for a long time, and the moment he left the hospital he paged Meng Wan—but no one answered. He asked Cheng Xuehua for the address of the cultural center where Meng Wan worked, intending to go find her in person.

It was almost the end of the workday. Meng Wan and Xu Qiuyun, arms linked, were just walking out the front gate, chatting and laughing.

Lin Dong rolled down his car window and waved, calling out, "Sister Wan, over here!"

The shout drew Meng Wan's gaze, and she waved back at Lin Dong, pleasantly surprised. Pulling Xu Qiuyun along, she went over. Lin Dong got out of the car, and Meng Wan smiled as she introduced the two.

It was Xu Qiuyun's first time meeting Lin Dong. Seeing how tall and broad he was, with his wide brow and heavy eyebrows, he looked rather fierce, and she didn't quite dare get close.

The two of them gave each other a distant nod, which counted as a greeting.

"How is Auntie Hao?" Meng Wan said, smoothing over the awkwardness.

"That's exactly what I came to tell you." Lin Dong drew his gaze back from Xu Qiuyun. "I took her to the hospital for a checkup today. The doctor said there's still a chance for her eyes to recover."

"Really?" Meng Wan was thrilled for him.

"As long as she has an operation. She won't recover fully like us, but she'll be able to see things, even if a bit blurry."

"That's wonderful all the same." Meng Wan had been worried there'd be no recovery at all.

"I came today because I wanted to treat you to a meal. You've done me such a huge favor—I really don't know how to thank you."

Meng Wan glanced at the time. "I'll pass on the meal. I've been very busy lately. You go take good care of Auntie Hao, and once I'm past this stretch I'll come visit her at the hospital."

Seeing her decline, Lin Dong pressed on, "Then let me drive you home."

"No need, I rode my bike here." Meng Wan turned to look at Xu Qiuyun. "Drive Qiuyun home—she lives far away."

Lin Dong rubbed his hands together, his eyes flicking toward Xu Qiuyun. "Then... then all right."

Driving someone home the first time they'd met, alone with her—it made him a little awkward.

Seeing him hem and haw, Xu Qiuyun assumed he was unwilling. "It's fine, I can get home on my own. No need to trouble you."

"No trouble at all, no trouble," Lin Dong said, waving his hands in a fluster. "A friend of Sister Wan's is a friend of mine."

"Go on, then." Meng Wan gave Xu Qiuyun a push toward the car. "Why pass up a free ride when there's one on offer?"

Xu Qiuyun clutched her bag, all bashful. Lin Dong opened the passenger door for her and helped her in.

Watching the car disappear in a puff of dust around the corner of the alley, Meng Wan started pedaling home on her bike.

The tour-guide certification exam was just a month away. That didn't leave Meng Wan much time to prepare, so she didn't dare waste a single moment. Miss this round, and she'd have to wait until next year.

Even though she'd guided James without a certificate this time and had pulled off the trip beautifully, that was only because James trusted her—others wouldn't necessarily do the same. She was aiming to build something big and strong, and she couldn't afford to be tripped up by one little tour-guide certificate.

During the workday, she got her application materials ready, and over the lunch break she went to the tourism bureau to submit them. The staff also gave her a copy of the National Tour-Guide Qualification Examination Administrative Regulations, which laid out the exam subjects and the eligibility requirements.

The exam was divided into a written portion and an interview. The written subjects were grouped into professional tour-guide knowledge—covering policies and regulations, tour-guide foundations, and tour-guide practice—plus local tour-guide foundations.

The interview assessed skills in guided commentary, standardized service, and handling special situations and contingencies.

She was confident she could do well in the interview. The most daunting part was the written exam—there was so much to memorize, and she hadn't had to commit anything to memory in years. She'd have to put in the work, aiming to pass on the first try.

That afternoon, Meng Wan studied the handbook and drew up a study plan for herself.

Her plan was to get up at six every morning and memorize the local tour-guide foundations—since that was her strong suit, she wouldn't need to spend much time on it. At eight she'd set out for work, using the long stretches of morning and afternoon to memorize and study the three weighty tomes of professional tour-guide knowledge, and at night she'd go over the day's material once more at home.

Fortunately she still had a twenty-year-old brain, which ran nice and smoothly. Had a forty-year-old tried to memorize all this, she'd have had the will but not the way.

With the plan set, she got to work, sprinting flat out for one month. Meng Wan buried herself in study with grim determination. Every day she stopped bothering to put together outfits, didn't have time to so much as dab on lipstick, washed her hair only every three or four days, and gulped down her meals in a few hasty bites. Apart from eating and sleeping, her mind was wholly on the material.

She even dreamed of exam scenarios. Once she dreamed the exam was about to end and she still had half the questions unanswered. She was so frantic she broke out in a cold sweat, her hands trembling too hard to write. When she woke and found it was only a dream, the heaviness lingered with her the whole day.

Her family could all tell something was off about her recent diligence. Meng Wan didn't dare say she was studying for a tour-guide certificate, claiming instead that the cultural center was holding a professional assessment and she had to memorize a great deal of local cultural knowledge.

Seeing their daughter putting in such effort, the old couple were overjoyed. At night they not only turned the television volume down to its lowest, but specially bought her milk and walnuts to build up her strength. Even when little Zhizhi wanted to come play with her aunt, Cheng Xuehua held her back—the whole family was careful not to disturb her.

And so, after a full month of giving it everything she had, Meng Wan finally faced the first exam in her life that she truly cared about and had truly prepared for.
