Chapter 19

Meng Wan felt as if she'd boarded a pirate ship, but the contract was already signed and the money already paid—there was no getting off now. All she could do was grit her teeth and see it through.

Based on the size of the tour group, Meng Wan and Accountant Bi drew an advance against the group's expenses. Accountant Bi reminded her again and again to make sure she collected proper receipts; otherwise the books wouldn't balance, the costs couldn't be reimbursed, and she'd be paying out of her own pocket.

It was the first time Meng Wan had ever carried such a large sum on her person. She took the thick white envelope and tucked it into the inner breast pocket of her jacket.

Bi Guisheng also handed her the ticket vouchers, the tour group's roster, and the contact information for the group leader.

The weather lately had been the fine, mild kind that came with early spring, and several tour groups had booked to arrive in Yuyang over the next few days. But the agency's handful of guides each already had their own groups in hand—there was no one to spare. Bi Guisheng had originally planned to hire a few temporary workers to take up the slack, and Meng Wan's arrival had relieved him of that pressing worry just in time.

Bi Guisheng then went on to fill Meng Wan in on a few things about how the agency worked.

The travel agency had started life as the "Tertiary Industry" office set up by a government work unit to find jobs for its employees' children—its whole purpose had been to solve the employment problem for the neighborhood's young people.

Later, as Yuyang's tourism industry grew, it had evolved into a travel agency.

Bi Guisheng was the most senior member of the agency. On top of that, he'd transferred in from a state-run guesthouse as a veteran accountant, and he held the agency's books and cash in his own hands. Everyone treated him with the utmost deference and didn't dare cross him.

When the guides were out on the road drumming up business, he took on the dual roles of manager and accountant. Youth Travel Agency had no dedicated manager, so Accountant Bi was the one who really held the power.

Of course, Accountant Bi hadn't told her any of that—she'd worked it out for herself.

Bi Guisheng also told Meng Wan which hotel the agency partnered with, instructing her to bring the guests there. The hotel and the agency were deeply tied together, with commissions changing hands: the hotel paid the agency a per-head fee for every guest, and in the same way the hotel would steer its own guests to the agency, which then paid the hotel the same per-head fee in return. The arrangement benefited both sides. These commissions were honestly recorded in the books and proper receipts were issued—a gray area that hadn't yet come under any regulation.

The same went for transportation—they had their regular drivers, and a cut was taken there too—along with the restaurants, the shops, and so on.

Listening to it all, Meng Wan realized there were far too many ways to make money in this business. If she could one day run the whole circuit herself—her own travel agency, hotel, restaurant, fleet of cars—the commissions alone would multiply her earnings many times over.

The only pity was that she had none of that capacity right now. James, perhaps, would have had the means; she, for the moment, could only start small, as a junior guide, and work her way up.

Taking on her first job, Meng Wan placed a call to the group leader and learned that he'd gotten the agency's number from a longtime client—which was why he'd chosen them.

Advertising wasn't widespread in those days, and it was outrageously expensive, so it simply wasn't worth it. Most agencies found their clients through the scramble for customers at the train station exits, the tourism desks in hotel lobbies, the pooling of scattered tourists at scenic-spot gates, and, in a few cases, advance bookings—say, when a work unit had an agency it regularly used, or when an acquaintance made an introduction.

Meng Wan asked about the dates they had planned, the makeup of the group, the sights they hoped to see, and any allergies, so that based on his answers she could draw up a detailed itinerary.

From what the leader told her, Meng Wan learned that the group consisted of retired cadres from a work unit in the capital—an officially funded trip organized by the unit, with each cadre allowed to bring one family member. Ten men and ten women, twenty in all, plus the leader made twenty-one. It was no small number, and right off the bat Meng Wan felt the pressure double.

Twenty-one people would need four of the little van-bodied taxis like Lin Dong's, which meant paying four drivers—hardly worth it. Meng Wan decided instead to start with the coach the agency recommended; everyone could ride in one vehicle at once.

She got in touch with the driver and confirmed tomorrow's departure time, then went in person to the Yunlai Hotel that Bi Guisheng had mentioned and booked eleven standard twin rooms.

She also paid out of her own pocket to prepare a small gift for all twenty-one guests—a red cord bracelet—partly as a gesture of goodwill, and partly to make the group easier to manage.

With the ticket vouchers ready and the guide's license, guide flag, pickup sign, receipt book, and itinerary notebook all in order, Meng Wan set out bright and early the next morning. She met the driver at the train station and brought him breakfast to warm up the relationship.

The driver was a man in his early forties named Wang Hongqiang, lean and sturdy, with a kindly face—clearly someone easy to get along with.

There's no biting the hand that feeds you. Meng Wan was the first guide ever to bring him breakfast unprompted, and a young girl at that, so Wang Hongqiang was extremely friendly toward her—answering every question, granting every request.

That day Meng Wan wore a white blouse with brown dress trousers and a pair of white sneakers. A fluffy braid hung down one side, pinned with a red clip, and she'd done a light touch of makeup with red lips. The bag slung across her chest held her itinerary notebook and all her documents and materials, and she held the pickup sign up in front of her, standing at the station exit looking thoroughly spirited. She wanted hers to be the first face everyone saw the moment they stepped out.

The other guides waiting nearby looked at Meng Wan with great curiosity, whispering about her. They all agreed they'd never seen her before—she had to be a new guide. She looked young, with smooth, tender skin, the sort who'd clearly never known hardship; chances were she'd give up and quit after a few days. None of them took her seriously. In this line of work you had to be on your feet all day, out in the wind and sun, so there were almost no women in it. And a guide didn't just need to use her head—she had to keep up physically too. Plenty of the men couldn't take it and started grumbling about quitting after only a few days, let alone a young girl.

The train pulled into Yuyang station right on time. A din rose up as the disembarking passengers came surging out like waves, one after another. The guides poaching private clients had long since worked their way up against the current, singling out those who hadn't joined a group—people traveling in twos and threes—to pitch themselves to. Organized, disciplined lines were obviously tour groups, and groups like that usually had their guides booked in advance. Meng Wan's was among them.

Meng Wan held her red sign up high above her head, her eyes searching back and forth for the line that matched her group's description.

As the crowd slowly engulfed the exit, all she could see in every direction was a sea of faces. Meng Wan wasn't quite tall enough; she rose onto her toes and back down, peering in. Suddenly she felt someone tap her on the shoulder. She turned to look—it was a young man.

"Hello—Youth Travel Agency?"

The voice sounded very familiar. "You're the group leader I spoke with on the phone yesterday?"

"That's me." He answered with a smile.

Meng Wan lowered her aching arm and shook his hand. "Hello. I'm Meng Wan, the guide for this trip."

"Hello." The man looked her up and down, then reached out and clasped her hand. "I'm Yang Zhiming, the leader for this group of retired cadres."

The one who'd handled this assignment last year had been a colleague of Yang Zhiming's, who'd recommended this agency; the guide who'd received them then had been a man named Liu Fei. Yang Zhiming had assumed it would be that same man this time, and hadn't expected to phone yesterday and reach a woman.

It was the first time he'd ever encountered a female guide. He could tell from her voice that she was young, and meeting her in person only confirmed it. Yuyang was an ancient city, and its most notable sights were steeped in deep historical and cultural significance. He wasn't sure a girl as young as Meng Wan could do justice to the commentary work he cared about most.

The people coming were retired cadres, well-read and cultured. If they weren't satisfied, he, as the leader, would hardly be free of blame.

But he couldn't disregard the young woman's dignity either—the young deserved their chances. He decided to assess her for a day before deciding; there'd still be time to swap her out if it came to that.

Meng Wan had no idea how many thoughts had flashed through this man's mind in those few short minutes. Having exchanged greetings, she looked past him and saw no line of cadres. "Aren't our elders out yet?"

"There are too many people. I had them wait inside for now. Once I'd found the guide and the crowd had mostly cleared, I figured I'd go back in and bring them out."

How thoughtful, Meng Wan thought, quietly impressed. With him around, she'd surely be spared a good deal of worry.

Meng Wan pointed to the coach across the street and said, "That's our vehicle. In a bit we'll take it straight to the hotel."

Yang Zhiming glanced in the direction she indicated and nodded. "All right, I'll go in and bring them out now."

"I'll come with you." Meng Wan tugged at her bag strap and got a firm grip on the flag in one hand.

By now there were only a scattered few people left in the station. Around the platform stood a cluster of plainly dressed elders, discussing where they wanted to go.

"Everyone, may I have your attention for a moment." Yang Zhiming raised his voice. "Let me introduce you—this is the guide for our trip, Comrade Meng Wan."

The whole group turned their eyes on Meng Wan in unison, and in those gazes were doubt, scrutiny, and curiosity.

Meng Wan introduced herself with a smile. "Hello, everyone. My name is Meng Wan, and I'm the guide for our Yuyang trip. I'll be fully in charge of your food, lodging, and travel, so if any of you have questions, come to me. Whether it's about meals, accommodations, transportation, or the history and culture of Yuyang, I'll do my best to answer."

"You look pretty young, miss. How many years have you been doing this work?" The question came from a fashionable auntie with permed curls and gold earrings.

"I'm twenty-two this year. This is my first time leading a group." Meng Wan answered steadily.

"Your first time?" The permed auntie clicked her tongue. "It's no easy thing for us old folks to get out and travel. For your unit to send just one young girl—isn't that taking us a bit lightly?"

The others began murmuring among themselves. It was true—they were all getting on in years, and every outing was one fewer they had left. If the experience was poor, they might as well not have come.

Meng Wan had known someone would question her. As it happened, she had every confidence she could win them over, so a few remarks like these wouldn't shake her.

She was about to speak up and explain when, from the other side, a dignified, plainly dressed auntie spoke up in her defense. "Who doesn't start from nothing? If we old folks won't give the young ones a chance, how are they ever supposed to grow? Besides, since the agency sent Guide Meng here, it means she's got the ability. You haven't even given it a try—where do all these snide remarks come from? Aren't you just knocking the confidence right out of a young person trying to better herself?"

She had a point, and the others chimed in their agreement.

The permed auntie turned her face away and rolled her eyes, muttering under her breath with a curl of her lip, "Tch. Acting all kindhearted."

Meng Wan didn't want a quarrel breaking out before they'd even set off, so she stepped in at once to smooth things over. "I completely understand everyone's concern about my ability, and I'm grateful to you for speaking up for me. Today is your first day in Yuyang, and I hope it can be a pleasant memory for all of you. Whatever you may think of me, just give today a try first. If I don't meet your expectations, I'll ask to step down myself."

Hearing Meng Wan's declaration, Yang Zhiming felt reassured too, and came forward to smooth things over. "Since Guide Meng has put it that way, let's give her a chance. We're all out here to enjoy ourselves—best to keep things friendly."

"That's right," the others agreed.

Seeing the mood settle, Meng Wan handed out the red cords from her bag. "These are Yuyang's special butterfly cords. You can tie them around your wrists—they're pretty, and they make it easier for me to keep the group together and keep anyone from getting lost. Two birds with one stone."

Yang Zhiming helped pass them out. His first impression of Meng Wan was decent enough; he hoped she wouldn't disappoint him going forward.

Once the cords were distributed, Meng Wan led everyone toward the coach. Yang Zhiming murmured beside her, "That permed Auntie Bai and Auntie Luo have been old adversaries for years. The two of them have never gotten along. Don't take it to heart."

No wonder she'd snapped at her right to her face—so they were old sparring partners.

Meng Wan let out a quiet sigh inwardly. The road ahead was long indeed.
