Chapter 35

Meng Wan's mind felt as if someone had hit the pause button. All her thoughts scattered like a flock of startled birds, leaving behind a blank, bewildered emptiness.

Beaming, the woman introduced herself to Meng Wan. Her name was Ding Chunling, and her husband was Qin Yizhong, Qin Yi'e's younger brother. Qin Yi'e was her sister-in-law.

That such a coincidence could exist in the world—Meng Wan could hardly believe it. Her lips moved, but the words kept circling in her mouth and refused to come out.

James could scarcely believe it either, but after all he wasn't the one at the heart of the matter, so however astonished he was, he stayed a bit calmer than Meng Wan. "And how is this Ms. Qin Yi'e doing these days?"

He figured this was what Meng Wan wanted to ask too.

Ding Chunling looked at the pair of them and paused, her eyes hesitating for a long moment, as though there were something hard to put into words. "...It wouldn't be right for me to go gossiping behind people's backs..."

From the looks of it, she wasn't doing well.

A note of urgency in her voice, Meng Wan asked, "What's wrong with her? Is she ill?"

That couldn't be right—it was still too early for her illness to set in. Or had she been bullied by the Zhou family?

"No, it's not that." Ding Chunling smoothed her cuff. "It's her husband—my brother-in-law. There's something wrong with him."

She put it very delicately.

"Did he hit her?" Meng Wan guessed at once.

"No, no." Ding Chunling waved her hands quickly to set the record straight. "He's crippled. My sister-in-law has carried that whole family of five on her own shoulders—you've no idea how hard it's been."

Meng Wan knew very little about this father of hers. By the time she'd gone back in her past life, he'd already been dead for several years.

"Doesn't she also have a daughter who's getting married soon?"

"You even know that?" Ding Chunling said. "The moment I'm back, I've got to go over to their place and help with the preparations." She paused, then asked, "Did you come specially to congratulate them—did you hear that Nian Nian was getting married?"

It was the most reasonable explanation she could think of. Otherwise, what business could a fashionable young girl traveling with a foreigner possibly have with the Zhou family or the Qin family?

Meng Wan gave a vague hum, thinking to herself: you needn't trouble yourself—this wedding isn't going to happen.

James cut in at just the right moment to change the subject. "Big sister, once you get off the train, how will you all get home?"

"My husband borrowed a vehicle to come pick us up." Ding Chunling answered breezily. "You can just follow me when we get off—I'll see to it you reach your destination."

"Then we're much obliged."

With someone they knew, they wouldn't have to scramble about back and forth.

Ding Chunling actually wanted to ask a few more questions—just what was the relationship between Meng Wan and her sister-in-law—but Meng Wan really didn't know how to answer, so she simply closed her eyes to rest.

The clatter of wheels on the rails rang in Meng Wan's ears, much like her own thoughts—every bit as impossible to quiet. She couldn't even say what she was feeling right now. If she had to liken it to cooking, it was as though every method—frying, stir-frying, boiling, deep-frying—had been thrown in at once, a dish that resembled nothing at all.

Only the steady warmth coming from her hand could ease her heart a little. Thank goodness James was here.

"We're almost at Qiyang Station. Anyone sleeping, wake up. Passengers getting off, gather your luggage, keep hold of your children, mind all your belongings. It's a busy stop—watch out for pickpockets."

The conductor called it out up and down the car, glancing left and right as she went.

At her words, the people in the carriage began sorting out their things, getting ready to disembark.

The train stopped at Qiyang Station. James held Meng Wan's hand with one hand and carried the luggage with the other, inching out with the crowd bit by bit.

Seeing Ding Chunling juggling two big bags while leading two children, Meng Wan asked for one of them and carried it for her.

The whole group wriggled along like a squirming caterpillar, finally working their way out of the station. Ding Chunling looked around for her husband, her gaze settling on a freight truck.

"Found him!" Ding Chunling said, pointing the way for everyone.

Meng Wan looked where she pointed and frowned faintly. She'd assumed it would be a minivan; she hadn't expected a cargo truck, with only the passenger seat up front free—the rest of them would have to ride in the back. She herself didn't mind, but she worried James might be fussy about cleanliness.

She turned to him and murmured by his ear, "If you don't want to ride in it, we can stay a night and take a cab over tomorrow."

James shook his head. "It's fine with me."

"Let's go, then." Ding Chunling turned back to call to them, hitched the bag on her shoulder up higher, and took her two daughters by the hand to lead the way.

By reflex, James moved to take Meng Wan's hand too, but the next moment he realized the crowd had thinned and holding hands no longer seemed appropriate. His hand hung stiffly in midair. Just as he was about to draw it back, Meng Wan caught hold of it. Lips curving, she said to him, "Come on."

With that she took his hand and started after Ding Chunling. James gazed at the back she'd turned to him, doting, a smile tugging unbidden at the corner of his mouth.

Seeing people approaching, the man in the truck opened the door and hopped down.

"Papa!" the little sister cried happily, and flung herself straight into the man's arms.

The sight of his daughter set the man beaming. He scooped her up and bounced her a little. "Looks like you ate well at Grandma's—you've gotten quite a bit heavier."

The little sister answered happily, "Grandma's cooking is so good. My big sister and I both ate lots and lots."

Hearing her mention the older daughter, the man finally looked over, and at once spotted Meng Wan and James behind the older sister.

A foreigner, here?

He shot Ding Chunling a questioning look, asking what was going on.

Ding Chunling smiled and made the introductions for both sides: "This is my husband, Qin Yizhong."

"These two I met on the train—what a coincidence, too. One question and I found out they've come to see your sister, to attend Nian Nian's wedding feast."

"Hello, I'm Meng Wan, and this is James."

As Meng Wan made the introductions, her eyes took the man's measure. He had a broad forehead and a square face, and something around the brow and eyes did resemble Qin Yi'e. So this was her uncle, Qin Yizhong.

In her past life the two of them had never met; this was the first time.

His face full of confusion, Qin Yizhong set the child down and walked forward a few steps. The sky had darkened by now, and he had to get closer to see clearly.

His face guarded, he asked, "You're here to see my sister?"

No one in their Qin family had ever set foot beyond Dongping Village before. He was the first to move to the county town—how could he not have known they had such a stylish relative?

Meng Wan nodded. "Auntie Qin...she knows me. Once we meet face to face tomorrow, it'll all become clear."

Qin Yizhong mulled it over dubiously for a couple of seconds. By the look of them, they didn't seem like bad sorts. Take them to his sister's place tomorrow and all would be revealed.

"Let's go, then." Qin Yizhong waved them on. "Sorry to make you ride in the back."

"It's no hardship," James said graciously.

"Oh ho." Qin Yizhong gave a start. "So this foreigner can speak Mandarin."

Meng Wan couldn't help frowning—Qin Yizhong's term for foreigners was a relic of the last century. But the next moment she remembered that it really was still the last century. Never mind, then; let him be.

Ding Chunling climbed into the passenger seat holding the little daughter, while the older daughter rode in the open back with Meng Wan and James.

The back of the truck was a jumble of all sorts of things—mud-caked farm tools, a bamboo basket holding poultry, and a paint-chipped iron pail.

Worried James would find it filthy, Meng Wan was just about to pull out a handkerchief to lay down for him when she saw him toss his bag onto the truck bed without a care and settle down at his ease.

Today he wore a brown leather jacket over a white shirt and a pair of light-blue jeans—the very picture of a cultured young man from the city. Set against the present scene, it made for a striking contrast all its own.

Meng Wan handed the handkerchief to the older sister instead and sat down on her own bag.

"What's your name?" she asked.

"Qin Shanshan."

"And your little sister?"

"Qin Keke."

Meng Wan gently fished for information from the child: "Do you go to your aunt's house often?"

Shanshan shook her head. "When I was little, Auntie's family was too poor, so Papa wouldn't let us go too often—he didn't want Auntie spending money on us. Now that we've moved away, we go even less."

When children visited an elder's home, there was always at least a meal to be had. But for Qin Yi'e, even that meal had been scraped out from between her own teeth.

Hearing this left a bitter taste in Meng Wan's heart, a sour ache flooding her nose. Such a cruel, sinful business, all of it.

James had been watching Meng Wan's mood the whole while. Earlier he'd thought she was only a little out of sorts over some unpleasantness with her family, and had meant to take her out for a change of scenery to let off some steam. But faced with a situation like this, there was nothing for it but for Meng Wan to bear it through herself; all he could do was be there at her side.

Qin Yizhong drove the truck back to his home in the county town—a single-story house on the town's edge. Because his older daughter attended junior high in the county town, they'd rented a little courtyard here; and as it happened Qin Yizhong did renovation work in town, so the whole family had moved out of Dongping Village together.

The lanes here were too narrow for the truck to drive in, so Qin Yizhong dropped them off at the mouth of the alley and went to return the vehicle, while Ding Chunling warmly led everyone toward the house.

The courtyard had three main rooms, with the kitchen on the east side and the toilet on the west.

Originally, Qin Yizhong and Ding Chunling had one room, the two younger girls another, and the third served as the sitting room.

With guests in the house today, Ding Chunling wasn't sure of the pair's relationship—whether to put them in one room or separate the men from the women.

She stole a glance at James, pulled Meng Wan aside, and asked in a low, secretive voice, "Are you two married?"

Meng Wan was taken aback, then quickly grasped why she was asking, and hurried to explain, "We're just friends."

Ding Chunling pursed her lips, plainly not believing a word of it, but since they weren't married, they couldn't share a room. She rapidly reshuffled the arrangements in her head.

After a moment's thought she raised her voice: "Tonight you'll sleep with Shanshan—the child's a quiet sleeper—and I'll sleep with Keke, the four of us in one room." At this her eyes landed on James. "Which means we'll have to trouble you to make do with my husband for one night."

Qin Yizhong's snoring fairly shook the rafters, and his sleeping posture was rather...well. Calling it "a trouble" was hardly an overstatement.

Wholly unaware of the danger bearing down on him, James kept up his gentle smile. "I'm sorry to put you to all this bother."

"No bother, no bother at all."

Ding Chunling thought privately: just don't you go finding it a bother.

Once everything was set down, Ding Chunling started getting dinner ready for the whole household. She'd been at her mother's for half a month, and the kitchen had next to nothing left in it—anything edible had been scavenged clean by Qin Yizhong—leaving only some noodles and eggs sitting forlorn on the stove.

She and the children could make do with that, but for guests who'd come all this way, serving egg-drop noodle soup as their first meal in the house would be a poor show of hospitality. And if tomorrow at her sister-in-law's place someone asked what they'd been fed, the answer would only invite ridicule.

So Ding Chunling went next door and borrowed half a catty of pork belly and a few tomatoes from Auntie Wang, planning to make shredded-pork noodles and tomato-fried eggs.

By the time Qin Yizhong returned, the meal was ready. The dinner table, meant for a family of four, looked crowded with two extra people at it.

Meng Wan ate this meal absentmindedly. With her birth mother almost within reach now, she didn't know whether she ought to be glad or grieved.

Early the next morning, Qin Yizhong hitched up a horse cart, and the whole party set off in grand procession for Dongping Village.
