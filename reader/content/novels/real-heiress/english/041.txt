Chapter 41

Meng Wan had reasoned that since this office had originally served as a "Youth Employment Office," the government was bound to have policies in place to support it—and sure enough, she'd found exactly that.

She pointed at a line in the document and waved the others over excitedly. "Come look at this!"

Liu Fei was nearest. With a puzzled expression he picked up the yellowed page and read it aloud for the other two: "This travel agency, in undertaking the reception of visiting internship and inspection groups from other regions to Yuyang, shall be entitled to a special subsidy."

It went on to spell out the specific subsidy requirements and amounts.

The bulk of a tour's income came from getting tourists to spend, but these "internship groups" and "inspection groups" were organized by work units, made up of leaders and cadres, so a guide was generally only responsible for reception and commentary, earning a modest fee for the effort. That was why the big agencies usually wouldn't bother with such groups—but they were different. They could claim a subsidy, and that was no small sum. After all, even a small fly is still meat.

"A special subsidy?" Luo Zhigang stared blankly at old Bi. "What special subsidy? How come I never knew about it?"

Old Bi was just as bewildered. He fished his reading glasses out of the drawer, put them on, and read it over carefully.

"Aiya. Aiyaya. Aiyayaya." Old Bi cried out, all excited, as though he'd discovered a new continent. "How did it never occur to me to dig through these old documents? No wonder they say there's a house of gold to be found in books."

He'd come on late—transferred over only after the agency was already set up—so he knew nothing of the earlier documents' contents, and had never bothered to leaf through things that had long been buried at the bottom of the box.

Luo Zhigang shared in the delight for a second, then turned glum again. "What good is knowing the policy? We've got no pull with those departments. Even with a subsidy, if we can't land the groups, it's all for nothing."

It was as though a bucket of cold water had been poured over old Bi's head; his fire went out in an instant. He'd been knocking about the grassroots all these years, and the biggest official he knew was the director of their former parent unit—who by now had risen to be a [redacted]. It wasn't right to trouble someone like that over a thing like this, and besides, he didn't carry enough weight to get the man to step in.

"This part's easy." Meng Wan said, beaming with spirit. "My father is a university teacher. He has plenty of students working in the city's government offices. I'll ask them to put in a few good words for us—getting hold of these resources won't be hard at all."

A flicker of surprise crossed Liu Fei's eyes. He hadn't expected Meng Wan to come from a scholarly family. It seemed their little agency really did harbor hidden talents.

Luo Zhigang and old Bi looked astonished too. They'd assumed Meng Wan was just a child from an ordinary household.

After all this listening, Luo Zhigang still didn't get it. The agency was about to be lost—what use was a subsidy? The subdistrict office wasn't going to let them keep operating just because of some subsidy.

Before he could even get the question out, he heard Meng Wan lay out her plan with full confidence: "If we can get this channel open, then we'll have a sure-fire, never-losing line of business. Add to that the resources already in our hands, and the agency's prospects won't be bad at all. What's more, we won't have any personnel turnover, so there'll be no management problems either. If we take over and make the agency thrive, that reflects well on the leadership too. Compared with money, I'm certain they value face more."

Everything was ready; all they lacked was the final push. Meng Wan made up her mind: tomorrow she'd take the proposal and the documents to report to the higher-up leadership, and she'd talk her lips raw if she had to in order to convince them to hand the agency over to them.

"Old Luo." Meng Wan addressed Luo Zhigang the way Liu Fei did, hoping the closer form of address would make him feel the camaraderie of comrades in hard times, and put his heart into the task she gave him.

Sure enough, Luo Zhigang straightened his back and said gravely, "Go ahead."

Meng Wan instructed him, "Starting tomorrow, you keep taking on groups in the agency's name as usual. Whether or not our end goes through, you don't put your work down. We need a steady flow of capital coming through the agency."

Listening to the task Meng Wan assigned him, Luo Zhigang felt for a moment that something was off, though he couldn't put his finger on what. He glanced blankly at Liu Fei, then at Accountant Bi, and seeing that neither of them said a word, gave a dazed little nod.

Meng Wan went on with her arrangements. "Liu Fei, you come with me to the subdistrict office tomorrow. If there's anything I say that doesn't sit right, you can help fill it in."

Liu Fei nodded; that was just what he'd been thinking.

"And old Bi."

"Here."

"You go on just as before—keep guarding the agency. Don't let any outsiders in. Anyone who comes at a time like this is either waiting to watch us make fools of ourselves, or fishing for word from you, looking to buy the agency."

Old Bi nodded heavily, as though he'd received a grave mission to defend the motherland.

He was about to retire, and the agency's future would soon have nothing to do with him—but he had, after all, spent a good many years here, and he did have feelings for the place. Besides, he'd watched both Liu Fei and Luo Zhigang grow up; he couldn't bear to see them lose their jobs.

With everything arranged, Meng Wan and Liu Fei agreed to meet at the subdistrict office the next day. Seeing it was already deep night outside, Liu Fei offered to walk Meng Wan home. She'd been running about all day and was rather tired, eager to get back and rest, so she agreed.

After the two of them left, Luo Zhigang stood rooted to the spot a while longer. Then, as if he'd suddenly seen through something, he shouted, "I know what's wrong now!"

Old Bi gave him a sidelong glance, ignored him, and went on tucking his glasses back into the drawer.

Right by his ear, Luo Zhigang bellowed, "On what grounds are we all supposed to take orders from her?"

------

As the most junior member of the Youth Travel Agency, Meng Wan carried herself with the authority of the most senior. Early the next morning she set out from Lin Dong's place, riding along with him to the subdistrict office.

The subdistrict office was housed in the administrative building in the western district, not far from their agency.

It was exactly the start of the workday, and people were filing in through the entrance one after another. Meng Wan and Liu Fei mingled into the crowd and made their way in. Inside the building, Meng Wan stopped a gentle-looking young woman to ask where the subdistrict office was, and the two of them went up the stairs.

According to old Bi, this newly arrived chief of the subdistrict office was a young man, no more than thirty or so, who'd previously worked at the city tourism bureau.

Meng Wan drew a deep breath and gave the door a light knock.

"Come in!"

Meng Wan rested her hand on the door handle and shot Liu Fei a bracing look—steadying her own nerves as much as his.

Liu Fei nodded back and gave her an encouraging smile.

Meng Wan pushed the door open and stepped into the office.

The furnishings inside were no different from most offices: a desk and chairs, plus a sofa and tea table.

A faint fragrance of tea rode the steam from the hot water into Meng Wan's nose. The man across the room was bent over his cup, blowing on the hot tea.

"Director Zhang, hello." Meng Wan walked a few steps forward. "We're from the Youth Travel Agency. We'd like to report to you about the restructuring."

According to Accountant Bi yesterday, this newly arrived chief was named Zhang Yuqing.

The man addressed as Director Zhang raised his head to glance at them and said, "Have a seat first."

With that he set the thermos in his hand back in its place, covered the teacup again, and carried it over to sit on one side of the sofa.

"You're here about the transfer, I take it." His eyes appraised the two of them. "I've already made everything quite clear to Comrade Bi Guisheng. Do you still have questions?"

Meng Wan began, unhurried. "It's like this, Director Zhang. We'd like to take over the agency ourselves—we don't want to see it sold off."

"Oh?" Zhang Yuqing was taken aback. "Can the two of you produce that large a sum of money?"

Meng Wan gave a radiant smile. "We truly can't."

Zhang Yuqing said nothing, fiddling impatiently with the lid of his teacup, beginning to feel Meng Wan was wasting his time.

The next second he heard Meng Wan continue, "But we do have a document here. Take a look first, Director Zhang."

With that Meng Wan drew the document from her bag, turned to the page she'd found the day before, stood, and presented it to him respectfully with both hands.

"This is the official red-letterhead document from when our office was converted into a travel agency." Meng Wan pointed it out for him. "Have a look at this line."

Following her finger, Director Zhang read the two lines carefully, then asked, puzzled, "What does this have to do with your restructuring?"

Meng Wan squeezed out a great big smile. "When our agency was founded, it had policy support behind it. And we've been running for quite a while now, so we've built up a certain amount of experience and resources. So we'd like you to give us a chance—to trade the ability to turn a profit and the stable resettlement of our personnel for the agency's operating rights."

Zhang Yuqing caught her drift: she wanted to get something for nothing.

"On what grounds do you think I'd give up a clear hundred thousand yuan for an empty promise like yours?"

Ambition in a young person was a fine thing, but it couldn't be this divorced from reality.

"Director Zhang." Meng Wan brought out a different line of argument. "I hear you transferred over from the tourism bureau."

"And what of it?"

"Then you of all people should understand—the top priority for our city's economic development right now is tourism. The higher-ups place great importance on growing the tourism industry. If you can do us this favor, we'll work hard to contribute to our city's economy and make our western district a pioneer in tourism development. By then, that'll reflect well on you too, won't it?"

Listening to Meng Wan reel off this flowery string of talk, Zhang Yuqing couldn't help shaking his head. "You're right that our city is indeed putting major effort into tourism. But to ask me to do you this favor on nothing more than a few imagined words of yours—isn't that a bit too shaky to stand on?"

Meng Wan had come prepared. At once she pulled out the agency's future development plan she'd drawn up beforehand and handed it to Zhang Yuqing for review.

In this plan, Meng Wan had laid out in detail the various problems currently facing Yuyang's tourism industry and the corresponding solutions for each. She'd also written up how to combine the development of tourism projects and products to create distinctive, locally flavored cultural tours, and had even mapped out different tour routes according to the division of scenic areas.

By any measure, it was a plan with real promise.

Sure enough, Zhang Yuqing had spent three years at the tourism bureau and knew nearly inside out the problems besetting Yuyang's tourism. As for the solutions, his views happened to align with what Meng Wan had written—it was a report of genuine reference value.

And after all, he was an official; he'd never worked on the front lines of tourism, so there were some issues even he wasn't aware of, all of which Meng Wan had set out plainly—such as the various channels through which kickbacks were taken.

This wasn't merely a future-development report for one travel agency; it read more like a set of recommendations for the development of Yuyang's tourism as a whole.

If Meng Wan could really run the agency according to this plan, then what she'd said a moment ago was no idle boast. Give her half a year, and the agency could grow to at least double its current size.

"What's your name?" Zhang Yuqing closed the report in his hands and studied the young woman before him in earnest.

Meng Wan knew this meant there was hope. She answered with a brilliant smile, "Director Zhang, my name is Meng Wan."
