Chapter 18

Meng Wan, who had been brimming with confidence and joyfully convinced she'd reached the first spring of her career, hadn't even had time to savor the excitement before her own ignorance gave her a cold, hard lesson.

In 1995, the land of China was at a critical juncture in its transition from a planned economy to a socialist market economy. That year, the coexistence of the two systems was strikingly evident, and the focus of reform had shifted to state-owned enterprises, with the goal of establishing a modern enterprise system suited to the market.

At this time, travel agencies were all state-owned. It wouldn't be until around 1996 that they gradually transformed into limited-liability companies, which made obtaining an operating license as a private individual extraordinarily difficult.

Meng Wan's thinking was still stuck in her past life, when limited-liability companies were everywhere—she'd long since forgotten that travel agencies were still run as state enterprises.

She first went to the tourism bureau to look into things, wanting to confirm what materials she'd need to open a travel agency and roughly how much money it would take.

To her surprise, the clerk at the tourism bureau told her that for a private individual to open a travel agency and obtain a license of one's own, one had to pay a quality-assurance deposit of one hundred thousand yuan, plus a registered capital of one hundred thousand yuan, both of which had to be deposited in the bank in a single lump sum, followed by a capital-verification report.

That meant she'd need at least two hundred thousand yuan in liquid funds locked away—but where on earth was she going to find two hundred thousand?

What's more, in 1995, a tour guide had to be affiliated with a travel agency in order to take on work; private individuals weren't permitted to operate independently.

So Meng Wan couldn't open her own travel agency. Her only option was to join a state-run one. If she took on tours privately and got caught, it would be treated as illegal operation, and she'd face fines or even confiscation of all her earnings.

Dejected, Meng Wan left the tourism bureau, her plan to open her own travel agency dashed completely.

It was a real blow, but she soon adjusted her mindset. The spring winds of reform would soon sweep into Yuyang. For now she'd treat this period as a chance to accumulate experience and save up hard, and once the policy loosened, her opportunity would come.

Having talked herself through it, she quickly rallied and pressed forward with her plan. Over the next several days, she made the rounds of every one of the city's sixteen hotels and inns, large and small, listing their prices, strengths, and weaknesses all in a table. She personally sampled the flavors of the restaurants too, marking them all on a map.

The three things that mattered most on a trip were the experience of food, lodging, and transportation. If even one of them fell short, it would dramatically lower a tourist's satisfaction. As for things like scenic-spot commentary, that was where Meng Wan held the competitive edge—as long as she shored up the other three weak points, she was confident she could do this well.

Before all this, she'd also tallied up the money she had on hand: nearly six thousand nine hundred yuan, every cent of it earned by herself. That gave her tremendous confidence. The ability to make money mattered more than anything.

In 1995, the domestic tourism market was just getting off the ground, and tour guides' income models were in a transitional period between the old and the new. Some could still reap the rewards of the old ways, while others had already begun to face the pressures of the market.

The biggest earners were of course the foreign-language guides, who at the time mainly received inbound tourists from Europe, America, and Japan. On top of base salary and tour subsidies, they earned tips and shopping commissions, and earning over ten thousand a month was common—three to five thousand on a single tour was nothing unusual. In those days it counted as a very high-paying profession.

Moreover, the job represented "state-funded travel, high income, seeing the world"—one of the most fashionable and ideal careers in the eyes of young people.

At this same time, mass domestic tourism was also just beginning, and market competition was steadily intensifying, which drove down tour fees and made guides' incomes unstable.

These guides typically had no base salary; their tour subsidies were often only a few dozen yuan a day, with the bulk of their income coming from tourists' shopping commissions, which fluctuated enormously.

There was also a large group of guides affiliated with the big agencies—CITS, China Youth Travel Service, and the like. They not only received tour subsidies but also a fixed monthly salary; by ordinary-enterprise standards that might be only a few hundred yuan, but the upside was its stability and security.

Following the per-head fee that travel agencies paid guides, Meng Wan drew up a pricing table for herself as a guide. Agencies generally paid guides a tour subsidy of fifty yuan a day; for an affiliated guide like her, if she didn't take her client base from the agency but brought in clients independently, she could double that amount or more—though the bulk of it would still come from tourists' shopping commissions.

With everything ready, Meng Wan decided to first head to the train station to scope things out. That was where guides gathered; she'd gather some intelligence first before deciding which agency to join.

This day happened to be a Saturday, and there were many tourists arriving from the neighboring city, most of them on short trips, hoping to spend a weekend in Yuyang.

A fair number of guides and drivers were staking out the station, nearly all of them men, with Meng Wan the only young woman squeezed in among them.

One of them—a middle-aged man reeking of cigarette smoke, with a head of glossy, oil-slicked black hair—took the initiative to strike up a conversation. "Little miss, here to pick someone up?"

Meng Wan held her breath and edged away, answering offhandedly, "Something like that."

The man's interest perked up at once. "Want to ride in my car? I'll give you a discount, miss."

Meng Wan waved him off. "No thanks, I can't afford a car ride."

The man looked her up and down. Dressed that fashionably and claiming she couldn't afford a ride? Suit yourself, then. Spotting a gap opening up ahead, he immediately shoved his way forward, deliberately jostling Meng Wan to the side as he went.

Meng Wan was light, and that solid shove threw her off balance. She lost her footing and toppled sideways.

"Careful!"

The pain she'd braced for never came—someone caught her and held her steady.

"Are you all right?" The man helped Meng Wan upright, asking with concern.

Meng Wan had nearly jumped out of her skin. Pressing a hand to her chest, she gulped down little breaths. If she hadn't had business to attend to today, she'd have given that old man a piece of her mind.

"Thank you."

Only then did she lift her gaze to the man who'd caught her. He looked young—a shirt with jeans and canvas shoes, a sunny boy-next-door face. He seemed like a student.

But there was a travel agency's yellow flag in his hand, so it appeared he was a guide too.

The cheerful young man flashed her a bright smile. "Glad you're not hurt."

Meng Wan began probing for information around the edges. "You're a guide?"

"How'd you know?" Following her line of sight to the flag in his hand, the young man chuckled. "Yeah, I'm a guide. I'm here to pick up a tour group coming over from the neighboring city to visit."

"I thought you were a student."

"How could you tell?" After the initial surprise, the young man glanced around, then lowered his voice and confided to Meng Wan in a conspiratorial tone, "Actually, I do this on the side. I rented a tour-guide certificate and take tours privately."

You could do that? Meng Wan was quite taken aback. This naive kid really was a guileless college student—blurting everything out, not the least bit afraid she might report him.

Meng Wan kept the thread going to draw out more. "So with a rented certificate, do you have to split the earnings with the agency when you take private jobs?"

The young man confessed just as candidly. "Of course not. I only have to pay the rental fee. These days plenty of guides just affiliate with an agency—you only pay a management fee, and all the money you make from tours is yours to keep. Unless you're one of the iron-rice-bowl types on the state payroll, in which case you do have to split with the agency."

So that was how it worked. In that case, couldn't she also affiliate with an agency and then operate on her own under the agency's name?

"Do you know any reliable agencies you'd recommend?"

The young man assumed Meng Wan was looking for a guide, and promptly recommended himself. "My agency's plenty reliable, and I'm a very professional guide. Don't let the rented certificate fool you—that's only because I'm still a student and can't afford the three or four hundred a month in management fees. I've been doing this for nearly a year now."

Meng Wan glanced at his flag. "Youth Travel Agency." It rang a bell—she must have heard of it somewhere.

"If I need someone, I'll come find you."

*

Leaving the train station, Meng Wan hailed a cab to go find Youth Travel Agency. The driver had only heard the name and couldn't recall its exact location, vaguely remembering it was somewhere in the West City area.

The driver took Meng Wan up and down the streets and alleys of West City, circling around the vicinity for ages, until at last, tucked away in some out-of-the-way nook, they spotted a battered old sign: "Youth Travel Agency."

Meng Wan was deeply skeptical. Was this place even legitimate?

Doubts and all, she decided to go in and have a look anyway—she'd come all this way; she couldn't very well leave with nothing to show for it.

She pushed the door open, and the aroma of freshly brewed tea rushed into her nose. Two men were playing Chinese chess on a low table set up on the floor.

"Hello, is this Youth Travel Agency?" Meng Wan asked, closing the door behind her and taking stock of the room.

The two chess players didn't so much as glance her way, still absorbed in the game. One of them, a white-haired old man, asked smoothly, "Looking for a guide, or wanting to be one?"

People who came here were generally after one of those two things.

"To be one," Meng Wan answered.

"There's a form on the table. Fill it out yourself."

"Checkmate—I win!"

The white-haired old man let out an anguished cry. "Aiya, no, no, that's wrong, I made a wrong move."

Meng Wan couldn't help but curl her lip. So he was a sore loser, this old man.

While the two old men bickered away behind her, Meng Wan focused on looking over the form.

The management fee was actually four hundred yuan a month—nearly half her old salary. She hadn't earned a single cent yet and was already going to be paying out of pocket just to work.

"Sir," Meng Wan turned around. "Let's hold off on the squabbling—I've got a few questions to ask."

All she got in reply was air.

"Even if I let you take the move back, you still can't beat me," the winning old man said smugly.

"That's not so certain." The white-haired old man bristled, glaring.

"Then go ahead and try."

"Try I will!"

The two of them launched back into studying the board. Meng Wan set down her pen and walked straight over to watch the game.

She'd learned chess from Meng Qingyan and had swept the board against every middle-aged and elderly man in the residential compound—her record was a matter of public knowledge.

Meng Wan observed from the side for a moment, then crouched down and murmured in the white-haired old man's ear, "Cannon to the center, over his head."

The white-haired old man first shot her a startled look, as if he hadn't expected this little slip of a girl to know chess. Then he turned back to the board and mulled over her words for a moment—and his brows suddenly unknotted, the corners of his mouth lifting irrepressibly. He gently slid the bottom-rank cannon over and set it square above his opponent's general.

Seeing himself at a disadvantage, the old man across from him cried out in indignation, "You cheated!"

The wheel of fortune turns. The white-haired old man crowed shamelessly, "I won anyway."

"Lousy chess hack—I'm not playing with you anymore!"

With that, the old man across the board, fuming and glaring, swept the pieces into disarray, gave a loud "Hmph," clasped his hands behind his back, and stalked off in a huff.

The white-haired old man was so pleased he couldn't stop grinning. He reset the board his opponent had scattered and carefully studied the move Meng Wan had taught him.

"Sir?" Seizing on his good mood, Meng Wan immediately brought it up. "Isn't this management fee a bit steep? Is there any room to talk it down?"

"Hm?" The old man was still lost in the chessboard. "What did you say?"

"The management fee." Meng Wan crouched and shuffled over across from him, settling onto a little stool.

"That's the price. Take it or leave it."

Well, this old man. She'd just helped him win a round of chess, and he'd turned on her in a heartbeat.

Meng Wan kept trying to bargain with him. "Give it some more thought. If I go with a bigger agency instead, you won't see a single cent."

"Hmph, threatening me." Only now did the white-haired old man look up at Meng Wan. "You're new, aren't you? Go ask around—my management fee is the lowest in all of Yuyang."

He was telling the truth. The big agencies, CITS and China Youth Travel, charged five hundred or even six hundred.

Seeing he wouldn't budge, and not wanting to waste time she could be earning money, Meng Wan made up her mind. Four hundred it was—she'd just work hard to earn it back.

"Deal. Once I pay, can I start working right away?"

"You can." The white-haired old man slurped a mouthful of hot tea. "But let me lay out the unpleasant parts first."

"Please, go ahead." Meng Wan pricked up her ears and listened carefully.

"First: you may take tours using the agency's contract seal and invoices. To the outside world you're a guide of Youth Travel Agency, but in reality you bear your own profits and losses."

This she knew.

"Second: you have to find your own clients and bear all the costs of running your tours yourself. If anything goes wrong, you take full personal responsibility—it has nothing to do with the agency. You carry the risk yourself; the agency bears none."

How cold-blooded, Meng Wan thought to herself.

"Third: each month's management fee must be paid by the third of the month, with a grace period of three days at most. If you can't pay on time, the contract is terminated on the spot."

Meng Wan's mind raced through these tyrannical terms. Every single one was perfectly calibrated to needle her, yet she had no choice but to agree. When you're under someone's roof, you have to bow your head.

"However," the old man said, his tone softening, "seeing as you helped me beat that old codger Hu, I can split a tour off to you to lead—only the money has to be divided down the middle. And going forward, if you ever want the agency's resources, that's fine too—forty for you, sixty for the agency."

He had a conscience after all. Meng Wan signed the contract and paid the management fee, receiving a yellow flag just like the sunny young man's—complete with a big footprint on it—along with a red pickup sign.

A little disdainfully, Meng Wan rolled up the guide flag and made small talk. "Sir, what should I call you?"

"My name's Bi Guisheng. Just call me Accountant Bi, like everyone else."

So he was the one who handled the money—no wonder his tone was so high-handed.

"Everyone?" Meng Wan asked, curious. "How many guides do we have here in total?"

Bi Guisheng stuffed the contract into a drawer and locked it. "Counting you, three."

"Only three?" Meng Wan was a little surprised. "And are they all freelancers paying the management fee like me?"

Bi Guisheng said flatly, "You're the only one paying a management fee."
