Chapter 62

The "warming of the home" had truly turned into a warm home—a single bed left steaming hot by the restless pair.

While James went to shower, Meng Wan smoothed out the bedsheet and wiped the beads of sweat from her forehead. Luckily she wasn't one to sweat much, so the rest of her was still fairly dry.

She picked the apron up off the floor, returned to the kitchen, and got to work on the food. In her past life she hadn't known how to cook, but after her divorce, with no choice but to fend for herself, she'd learned a few basic dishes. For today's first lighting of the stove, she'd even gone to Master Hai the day before to learn how to make a crispy-pork hotpot, and today she wanted to try her hand at it.

As she was washing the vegetables, the trickle of running water filling her ears, a voice suddenly came from behind her.

"Don't you want to take a shower too?"

James had already changed into a short-sleeved shirt and shorts, his hair only half-dried, a white towel draped around his neck, damp strands still dripping water.

Meng Wan kept carefully washing the green beans in her hands, not turning around. "I'll wash up after the cooking's done, or I'll just end up smelling of cooking oil all over again."

"Is there anything I can do to help?"

James peered in, his hands itching mischievously to slide around Meng Wan's waist.

"No," Meng Wan refused sternly. "Help me any more and it'll be dark before we ever get to eat."

If what had just happened happened again—dry kindling and a blazing flame—she couldn't guarantee she'd be able to control herself.

James pressed his lips into a smile, withdrew his arms from the air, and obediently backed out of the kitchen.

Today really couldn't get out of hand again; otherwise the one who'd suffer would be him.

The sounds around her gradually quieted, and Meng Wan threw herself wholeheartedly into the cooking.

She planned to make three dishes and a soup, but since she hadn't cooked in a long while, her technique was a bit rusty—she couldn't prep and stir-fry at the same time, so it took an age. The sun was already sinking in the west by the time she finished the last dish.

She mocked herself inwardly: a single meal had really taken her a whole afternoon.

She carried the dishes out to the living room and only then realized that James was nowhere to be found. He wasn't anywhere in the apartment—she had no idea when he'd slipped out.

By the time she brought out the soup, the man finally came back, laden with bags large and small.

"Where did you go?"

James set down everything in his hands and changed into his slippers. "I went and bought you a change of clothes, plus some toiletries for women, so next time you come over you can just use them."

Meng Wan set down the soup bowl and muttered smugly under her breath, "Who says I'll always be coming to your place?"

"All right," James said, playing along. "I'll use them myself, then. That work for you?"

"Hurry up and wash your hands, dinner's ready," Meng Wan urged. "This is the fruit of a whole afternoon's labor—my back's gone stiff from standing."

"Then I'll rub it for you later."

"On second thought, never mind."

Better not have him rub it right back into bed. He'd managed to hold himself in check once; asking him to hold back again would be more than she had the heart for.

James washed his hands and came to the living room. Looking at the spread—not exactly refined, but with each dish having its own character—he said earnestly, "Thank you for cooking yourself. I'll savor every bit of this meal."

Brimming with anticipation, Meng Wan watched him taste each dish one by one, then asked with starry eyes, "Well? How is it?"

His expression solemn, James didn't stint on the praise. "Delicious. It has the taste of home."

"The taste of home?"

"When I was little, the food my grandmother made for me tasted just like this. This flavor reminds me of the childhood days I spent with her."

His description set Meng Wan's heart fluttering. Could her cooking really be that good?

She picked up her chopsticks and tasted it herself. After eating in silence for a good while, she couldn't help but ask, "This 'taste of home' you're talking about—it wouldn't happen to be the taste of a scorched pot, would it?"

James couldn't help the corners of his mouth curving up. "My grandmother cooked over too high a flame and was always burning the food, so in my memory that's the flavor that belongs to home and home alone."

And here Meng Wan had thought all that praise meant her cooking was actually that good—turns out it was just the taste of a scorched pot. Pouting, she relentlessly piled dish after dish into his bowl. "If you like it, then eat up—have plenty of that taste of home."

James, eyes full of laughter, kept shoveling food into his mouth. "I'll finish every last bite."

——

After dinner, Meng Wan took a shower and changed into the new clothes James had bought her. She had to admit, they fit her quite well.

When she got home, the whole Meng family except Cheng Xuehua was gathered together watching television. Cheng Xuehua was on the night shift, so she wasn't home, and little Zhizhi had long since drifted off to dreamland.

The moment Meng Wan was back, she settled in to watch a TV program with everyone, something she hadn't done in ages.

Sun Jing cut a plate of watermelon and brought it out for everyone, slicing a separate half just for Meng Wan and telling her to scoop it out with a spoon.

Eyeing the big half in Meng Wan's hands, Meng Xingyang complained, "I want to eat mine with a spoon too."

Sun Jing gave him a shove to make him scoot over to the side, then sat down next to Meng Wan herself. "Always making a fuss. You should count yourself lucky there's anything to eat."

Meng Xingyang pouted and wriggled off to the side, picking up a wedge of watermelon and eating it sullenly.

Meng Wan, meanwhile, cradled her watermelon and ate away cheerfully, waiting for whatever Sun Jing had to say next.

"Xiao Wan, your sister-in-law wants to ask you something."

"Go ahead, Sister-in-law." Meng Wan swallowed the watermelon juice in her mouth.

Sun Jing didn't quite know how to begin, and spent a long while marshaling her words. "The tour guides at your travel agency—they make pretty good money, don't they?"

That day at Meng Wan's agency, she'd watched everyone bustling about, full of spirit and drive, and had happened to overhear Luo Zhigang and Jiang Ke talking about landing some big tour group—each booking earned at least a thousand-odd yuan, and even after the agency's cut, a guide could take home at least four hundred.

Hearing that, Sun Jing's heart had stirred. After all, her own monthly salary was under six hundred, and the daily grind of teaching, meetings, and lesson prep was no less exhausting than a guide's work—if anything, more so. These were the days when everyone was "diving into the sea" of business; just the other day, a senior teacher from her office had resigned to go start a business down south.

Watching others make money, she was deeply envious. She just hadn't had any opportunity, and didn't know what she could do. But now that exactly such a chance was right in front of her, she hesitated. Teaching was tiring, but it had the advantage of stability and respect, and she found it hard to choose between the two.

So she wanted to hear Meng Wan's opinion.

In an instant Meng Wan grasped the deeper meaning behind Sun Jing's words. She answered honestly: "It does pay more than an ordinary job, but it's also grueling. Zhizhi is still little—I wouldn't recommend it."

Not long ago, when Meng Wan had been working as a guide herself, she'd been up before dawn every day and home in the dead of night, fretting over this and worrying about that the whole day long. Sometimes she didn't even have time for the toilet, so she didn't dare drink much water. At Zhizhi's age a child couldn't be apart from her mother. Even though the family would all help pick Zhizhi up and drop her off at school, if the child went too long without seeing her mother, she'd surely start acting up before long.

The little flame kindled in Sun Jing's eyes was doused by that one remark of Meng Wan's, and she lowered her head, looking a bit deflated.

Meng Xingyang put down his watermelon and wiped his mouth twice with the back of his hand. "You want to go be a tour guide? I won't allow it!"

This was exactly when their child needed her mother. If she went off to be a guide, who'd look after the home? Besides, being a People's Teacher was such an honorable profession—how could she just up and resign on a whim?

"I didn't say I was going to," Sun Jing explained. "I was only asking."

Meng Xingyang unfolded his crossed legs and turned to face her. "If you're asking, doesn't that mean you've got the idea in your head? Teaching is such a good job—other people would claw their way in—and you want to quit? You only see other people making money. Have you stopped to think whether you can handle that kind of hardship?"

Sun Jing kept her head down and said nothing, picking at the calluses on her fingers, worn there over years of writing.

As the head of the household, Meng Qingyan bore an unshakable responsibility for keeping the family in harmony. His son had a point, true enough, but he needed to mind his tone and his manner of saying it.

He switched off the television and stepped in to smooth things over. "It's a good thing that Sun Jing has ideas of her own. Xingyang, don't be so blunt. Nothing has even happened yet—there's nothing wrong with her just wanting to look into it. The times are always changing. Right now there's this fever for going into business, and watching others rake in more and more money, it's only natural to start having ideas. But first you have to be clear about what it is you really want. Stability and risk—you can't have both."

Meng Wan agreed wholeheartedly with what Meng Qingyan said, and she went on: "Brother, it's perfectly natural for Sister-in-law to have this idea. What she wants to do is her right—you can't hold her hostage by playing the 'mother' card. The child belongs to both of you. The reason I brought up Zhizhi is that I was speaking from Sister-in-law's perspective: she'd surely never have the heart to neglect Zhizhi for the sake of a job, which is why I don't recommend she take this work. But if she's truly resolved to do it, then I won't stand in her way either."

Hearing first one and then another of them lecturing him, Meng Xingyang huffed and turned his head to the side.

"Sister-in-law." Meng Wan set down her watermelon and took Sun Jing's hand. "Can you tell me why this idea came to you? Is it the money? Or are you unhappy with your current work?"

Sun Jing's eyes grew hot, and she choked out the words with a touch of grievance. "Your brother's work unit has already done away with allocated housing. Zhizhi is getting bigger and bigger. For now she can make do sharing a room with us, but once she's in primary school and heading toward middle school, she's going to need a room of her own. The things I never had in this life—my daughter can't go without them."

On her and Meng Xingyang's salaries, buying a commercial apartment would take at least ten years even if they didn't eat or drink. Were they to make Zhizhi share a room with them the whole while until then? Sun Jing couldn't accept that.

Meng Wan immediately put herself in Sun Jing's shoes, her heart going out to this mother. "So you want to earn more money, buy a place of your own, and move in there with Zhizhi."

Sun Jing nodded, a hot tear falling straight onto the back of her hand.

Meng Xingyang stood up, somewhat indignant. "Why would you keep all this from me? If you'd said something, we could have figured it out together!"

"And what good would it do to say something?" Sun Jing said heatedly. "There was that chance at allocated housing before—and without so much as discussing it with me, you nobly handed it over to a colleague. I won't even bring that up now. Now there's a chance to go into business, but there's no way you'll quit, so all I can do is find a way through on my own. Otherwise, what would you have me do?"

Meng Qingyan wanted to speak but couldn't get a single word out, and could only sigh quietly to himself. His son had indeed handled this badly. Once you'd started a family, everything ought to be discussed together—all the more so for something as major as a home.

"All right, you won't argue your way to an answer." Meng Wan didn't want them to keep fighting; dragging up old scores was the surest way to wound feelings. "Solving the problem comes first. I have a suggestion—do you two want to hear it?"

Sun Jing wiped her tears and looked earnestly at Meng Wan. "Xiao Wan, go ahead."

Meng Xingyang, too, put his hands on his hips and turned to look over with a grave expression, waiting for her to speak.

"Sister-in-law, if you want to make money, how about coming to work at the restaurant I'm opening?"
