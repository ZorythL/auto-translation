Chapter 32

The next day was Sunday. Meng Wan hadn't forgotten the time the manager of the jade shop had mentioned to her, and she went over early, catching a ride with Lin Dong.

She arrived just in time to run into the craftsman who carved the little rabbit jade pendants.

He had come to restock the shop at his usual hour, and learning that his rabbit pendants had sold clean out left him in high spirits. Sales the past few times hadn't been encouraging, and he hadn't held out much hope—selling three or four a week was enough to leave him content.

The shopgirl spotted Meng Wan behind him and waved at her eagerly. "It was this little Tour Guide Meng who bought up all your rabbit pendants."

The craftsman turned at her words. Meng Wan gave him a polite smile and introduced herself. "Hello, sir. My name is Meng Wan—I'm a tour guide. Your rabbit pendants are exquisitely made. Not only did my tour group buy them, I picked some up for my family and friends too."

"Tour Guide Meng." The craftsman stood hunched, his deep-set eyes lingering on her face. "I'm hardly anyone to call 'sir.' I'm just an old codger who knows a bit of carving. Call me Old Bai."

Meng Wan smiled and switched the order around. "Master Bai, you're too modest. Your little rabbits are carved so lifelike—those two touches of red on the eyes bring them to life. In all of Yuyang you'd be hard-pressed to find a handful of craftsmen with skill like yours."

There was never any harm in flattering people, and besides, his craftsmanship truly was beyond reproach.

"You've got a good eye, young lady." Old Bai didn't bother being modest this time. He extended Meng Wan an invitation. "Care to come see my workshop?"

Hearing the offer come unprompted, Meng Wan nodded again and again, as eager as a pecking chicken. This was easier than she'd dared hope.

Before she left, the manager called Meng Wan aside and pressed an envelope of commission money into her hand.

Each pendant sold for one hundred fifty yuan, and the rule was a ten percent cut per customer. Because Meng Wan's clients had bought so many, she was given fifteen percent—twenty-two yuan fifty per pendant, four hundred fifty in all for twenty of them.

That day Meng Wan had paid twenty yuan apiece for the rabbit pendants, so by this reckoning she still came out a little ahead.

Money in hand, Meng Wan followed Old Bai to his workshop.

He called it a workshop, but really it was just one room of his home.

Old Bai's full name was Bai Mingyuan. He lived alone in a small walled courtyard on the edge of the old town, set off by itself. The yard was piled with stones large and small, some covered with woven sacks, others left out in the open. Against the wall stood a water trough, and in it soaked several pieces of already-cut material, bluish-white, giving off a soft, gentle glow in the water.

The workshop was in the west wing. Curious, Meng Wan stepped inside, her eyes roaming over this treasure trove worn by the years.

By the window stood a wooden table with a desk lamp on it, the shade dusted gray-white with jade powder. Beneath the lamp sat a machine she'd never seen before: a handle about the size of a fist connected to a flexible shaft, the other end joined to a motor. The tip of the handle gripped a thin metal rod, its head glinting a dull gold.

A few finished jade pendants hung on the wall, threaded on red cord, swaying gently in the draft. There wasn't a single superfluous thing in the whole room—it was simply a home: one table, one chair, one lamp.

Against the farthest wall stood a wooden rack with three shelves, displaying all manner of fine little carvings—a cat, a rabbit, a sheep, a snake—but the most impressive of all was a long dragon covered in intricate scales.

Seeing Meng Wan gaze at his work with such reverence, Old Bai couldn't help a faint swell of pride.

"Like one? Take your pick."

These he'd carved to pass idle time, all from scrap material, so they weren't worth much.

Meng Wan's eyes were held captive by the dazzling array of pieces, unable to pull away. It was a good while before she turned back and said in earnest, "Master Bai, may I pay you to make a custom piece for me?"

"What jade?" Bai Mingyuan settled leisurely onto his work stool and began wiping down the machine.

Different jades carried different prices and called for different techniques. Tough jade could be finely and minutely worked; high-hardness but brittle jade leaned more on showcasing its clarity and color.

"Yiyuan jade."

"What shape?"

Meng Wan lowered her eyes and thought. James was three years older than her—by the Chinese zodiac he was born in the Year of the Dog. "A little dog, then."

That was no challenge. Bai Mingyuan asked, "Any special requirements?"

Next weekend was James's birthday, and Meng Wan had been fretting over what gift to give him. This piece might not be anything costly, but its strength lay in the thought behind it. She believed he would like it.

"None. It's just a birthday present for a friend."

"A boyfriend."

Bai Mingyuan had lived half a lifetime; one sniff and he knew exactly what was going on between a man and a woman.

Meng Wan started, color rushing up her cheeks at once. She said bashfully, "...Not yet."

"Then it won't be long." Bai Mingyuan delivered his verdict. "What's the young man's surname? I'll carve the first letters of your two names on the back of the pendant."

"That won't be necessary." Meng Wan thought it over. "Just write his own name."

She pulled a notebook from her bag, wrote out James's English name in neat letters, tore off the page, and handed it to Bai Mingyuan.

Bai Mingyuan glanced at the English name on the paper—quite the foreign flair, a whole foreign name.

With that weight off her mind, Meng Wan dragged over a stool, patted the jade dust off the seat, sat down, and got to the real business at hand.

She had come today to discuss the possibility of future cooperation with Bai Mingyuan.

Meng Wan wanted to create a tourist souvenir that would be a brand all her own. The souvenir didn't need to be expensive—just something exquisite, something that when people saw it would make them think of Yuyang, and of her, the tour guide who sold it.

It was like the way people of later generations would buy fridge magnets bearing the local flavor of the places they traveled, a kind of collecting—gathering up every place they'd been as a keepsake.

The Sandieyun jade pendants, famous as Yuyang's jade was, held no price advantage. So Meng Wan wanted Bai Mingyuan to make a souvenir that was small, refined, and—most important of all—cheap.

She planned to have Bai Mingyuan come up with a few designs first, produce a small batch, and ease it onto the market. Once it caught on, the volume needed was bound to grow, and at that point Bai Mingyuan alone would never be enough. They could open a studio in his name, dedicated to producing this brand of souvenir. There would be no shortage of business.

Meng Wan began painting Bai Mingyuan a grand picture, hoping he would take the matter seriously. And it wasn't really just empty talk—by her reckoning, in the not-too-distant future, all of this was sure to come true.

Listening to her ideas, Bai Mingyuan knit his brows and considered for a moment. "It's a good idea."

The Bai family had carved jade for generations, and their ancestors had once run a jade shop—a name people in Yuyang could recognize. But this hereditary way of passing things down was bound to have its flaws. By the time the business reached his father's generation, it had begun its slow decline.

As the only son, Bai Mingyuan's father had grown up coddled, raised in the lap of luxury, and could never stand the bitter toil of learning a craft. He had no head for business either. Bai Mingyuan's grandfather had made every arrangement, leaving behind capable assistants to support him, hoping his grandson—Bai Mingyuan himself—might rise to the occasion and one day take the jade shop over from his father.

But no sooner had Bai Mingyuan's grandfather passed away than his father fell to gambling. In those turbulent times, the shop's business plummeted, its earnings nowhere near fast enough to keep pace with how quickly his father squandered the family fortune.

In the end, when Bai Mingyuan was twelve, the Bai family's jade shop was forced to be handed over to a competitor. A storied old shop changed owners overnight; the Bai Jade Workshop was gone for good. It was around that time that Bai Mingyuan's grandmother was driven to death by sheer rage.

With the jade shop gone, the Bai family scattered—some drifting away, some leaving outright—and was never again the lively household it had been. Bai Mingyuan was still young then and understood only that the shop his grandfather had left was no more, and that his father was the one to blame.

From then on he settled in and took up the craft of carving, dreaming that one day he might reopen the jade shop through his own hands. But born into chaotic times, when simply staying alive was hard enough, how could he ever afford to open a shop?

By now his spirit had long since worn away. Being able to scrape a living from this one skill already counted as great fortune, and he dared not hope for anything more.

But just now, listening to Meng Wan's impassioned speech, the long-dead little sprout of his old wish seemed to stir somewhere deep within him—like a few drops of water poured onto parched soil, which it drank in greedily.

Yet the next moment he shrank back. He hadn't done any high-intensity work in many years; he'd been drifting along, getting by. Could he really shoulder this burden?

He was fifty-six now, not sixteen or twenty-six. The old drive was long gone.

The souvenir Meng Wan spoke of—from design to material selection, prototyping, finalizing the form, the carving itself—he'd have to take part in every step personally. That was a massive undertaking.

Meng Wan caught the hesitation in his eyes. So he'd grown too comfortable sitting still; she'd have to give him a hard push.

"Master Bai." Standing in his shoes, Meng Wan tried to persuade him with weight and sincerity. "I know you have many worries, but everything is hardest at the start. Let's just begin—we'll solve problems as they come. We can't let the difficulties we imagine scare us off before we've even tried."

"As the heir to the Bai family's jade-carving craft, don't you want to see such a precious skill carried on and made to flourish? If your grandfather's spirit watches over you and learns that carvings made by the Bai family are spread all across the country, how proud he would be."

That day, after seeing off her tour group, Meng Wan had made a special trip back to the jade shop and asked the manager about Bai Mingyuan. She'd never guessed the old man's family carried such a weathered, sorrowful history behind it.

The corner of Bai Mingyuan's eye twitched faintly. His hand stilled mid-wipe, his gaze locking onto the scroll of calligraphy on the opposite wall: "A great craftsman does not hack."

He had once taken a wife, but not long after, she fell ill with a strange disease. The family was dirt-poor then and simply couldn't afford the treatment. Dragging on and on, she lost her battle with the illness in the end and passed away. He had never remarried.

So the Bai line had, in his generation, come to an end.

It wasn't that he hadn't thought of taking an apprentice, but none of it ever worked out—some found it too grueling, some couldn't sit still, some had no talent.

In the dead of night his dreams would carry him back to his ancestors, his grandfather asking how the jade shop's business fared. He couldn't answer a word. The shop was gone, and now this craft, too, was about to follow him into the coffin. He truly had failed the ancestors of the Bai family.

"I've heard everything you've said. Give me a little more time to think it over."

Seeing he wasn't himself, Meng Wan stopped pressing. "Then mull it over. When I come back next week for the carving, I hope I'll hear good news."

Walking out of the alley, Meng Wan's heart drummed with uncertainty. She couldn't tell whether Bai Mingyuan's noncommittal attitude meant yes or no. It wasn't that he was the only option—but from the very first moment she'd laid eyes on that little rabbit, she'd felt this was the person she was looking for. So as long as it was at all possible, Bai Mingyuan remained her first choice.
