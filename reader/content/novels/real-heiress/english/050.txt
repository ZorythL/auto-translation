Chapter 50

Meng Wan had imagined plenty of reasons. Lan Xiuli had talked herself around, or she'd buckled under pressure from her family.

But when Lan Xiuli said she'd seen Sun Jing, Meng Wan couldn't help a small flutter of panic.

"You laid it all out for my sister-in-law?"

Lan Xiuli gave a self-mocking smile. "That was what I'd meant to do—to beg your sister-in-law to give your brother up to me. But when I saw her holding their child, the three of them laughing and talking together as a family, I knew there was no future for your brother and me."

It was the first time Lan Xiuli had ever seen Meng Xingyang wear such a heartfelt smile—and that smile belonged solely to his wife and child. She, on the other hand, would always be the outsider looking in.

Their family was so happy. Was she really going to wreck the happiness of three people for the sake of her own selfish love?

Lan Xiuli liked to think she was still a person with limits. Everything that had come before, she chalked up to having let her head be turned. Mercifully, she hadn't done anything truly unforgivable. It wasn't too late to turn back.

Reason had made its decision—but a person can't just set feelings down the moment they decide to. So after thinking it over for a good long while, she'd decided to take Meng Wan up on her offer: to leave the Surveying and Mapping Bureau and come work at the travel agency.

Meng Wan let out a breath of relief and immediately praised her. "That's exactly the right way to think about it. One day you'll thank yourself for making this decision."

"Whether I'll thank myself someday, I don't know. But for now, you'd better sort out this job for me properly."

"Don't worry. In a few days I'll sign a formal labor contract with you, hiring you on as an official tour guide at our travel agency. For now, just get your affairs at your old unit in order—make a clean break with the people and things there."

Lan Xiuli nodded. "But I've never worked as a tour guide. Do you really think I'm suited to it?"

Her English was genuinely excellent, but holding a direct conversation with a foreigner might be another matter. As she and her classmates used to joke about themselves, the English they'd learned was the mute kind—good enough to read it and answer test questions, and that was all.

Meng Wan countered, "Then do you think I'm suited to being the manager of this travel agency?"

Lan Xiuli said nothing. On the surface of it, she would never in a hundred years have connected Meng Wan with a manager's post. In her understanding, a manager ought to be someone of a certain age, someone who looked steady and seasoned. Meng Wan was far too young.

Knowing what was on her mind, Meng Wan went on. "See? You think I'm not suited either. But I went and sat down in this very seat anyway. Should I throw away an opportunity this enormous just because I myself feel I'm not suited? If you can't do something, you can learn—who doesn't start from zero and work their way up? Nobody is born knowing how to be a manager. We all learn together, we all improve together—that's exactly what our travel agency stands for."

Looking at how young Meng Wan was, Lan Xiuli hadn't expected her to come out with such a clear-sighted speech. She really was a child of the Meng family.

"All right. I'll go buy some books this afternoon and start studying."

Meng Wan took a printed copy of her own commentary script out of the drawer. "This is the commentary I wrote for every scenic spot. If you think anything's off, feel free to change it—mine is just a template for you to work from. In the end you'll have to internalize it and tell it in your own words. But the hardest part is translating it all into English and delivering it that way. I'm not exaggerating—your job is absolutely the most difficult and most challenging in the whole agency, which is exactly why your rewards will be the greatest, too."

Lan Xiuli took the script and read through a few pages carefully. Most of the vocabulary was fairly everyday, and that part wouldn't be hard to translate. But some of the obscure terms and idioms would be trickier. Still, she was still in touch with her old teacher, so she could go and ask for advice.

"Don't worry. Anything I agree to do, I'll see it through properly."

------

After seeing Lan Xiuli off, a great weight lifted from Meng Wan's heart. In this life, her brother and sister-in-law's marriage would finally be spared that one crack, and the three of them could live happily as a family.

She really was something!

She hadn't been pleased with herself for long, though, when the ring of the telephone shattered the peace of the office.

The caller said that Luo Zhigang had been hauled off to the police station for getting into a fight with a tourist, and they wanted Meng Wan to come bail him out.

Meng Wan wanted to weep but had no tears. Being the boss was just too hard!

The moment she hung up, Meng Wan grabbed some money and rushed off to the police station in a flurry.

The station wasn't large, and it was packed at the moment with a crowd of worked-up people, with bursts of arguing erupting one after another like cannon fire. Meng Wan listened from outside the door for a couple of minutes and couldn't make heads or tails of it, so she just walked straight in.

Squeezing through the gaps in the crowd, she pushed her way to the very back, where the officer was. He was wearing a stern expression, trying to persuade both sides to settle things amicably—but the two parties were trading words back and forth, hopelessly tangled in their quarrel, and no amount of persuading could move them.

Blood seeped from the corner of Luo Zhigang's mouth as he argued his case, spit flying everywhere. His hair had been yanked into something resembling a bird's nest, sticking up and lying flat in a tangled mess.

Meng Wan shot him a look, then tugged at the officer's sleeve. Only then did he notice the person who'd popped up beside him.

Smiling, Meng Wan introduced herself loudly. "Hello, comrade. I'm the head of Luo Zhigang's travel agency—I'm the one who just took your call."

The officer, with a splitting headache from all the shouting, barked sharply at the crowd, "Quiet down, all of you! Be quiet!"

In an instant, the room fell silent.

Then the officer turned back to Meng Wan and asked, "What did you just say?"

Meng Wan repeated what she'd said, this time in front of everyone, and shot a glance at Luo Zhigang. He guiltily looked away, not daring to meet her eyes.

"We've got a rough idea of the situation, but both parties think they're in the right and that it's the other side's fault, so they've been going at it nonstop."

As the officer spoke, he laid out the whole story for Meng Wan.

It had started when Luo Zhigang took a group of tourists to a jade shop to spend money. One tourist had taken a liking to a jade Buddha in the shop, but on learning the price, found it far too expensive. This tourist happened to know something about jade, and judging from the quality of the stone, he reckoned the price of the Buddha was wildly out of proportion to its actual quality. He decided the shop was fleecing its customers—charging them outrageously precisely because they were out-of-town tourists.

At first the tourist argued it out with the shop assistant. Then the assistant, momentarily flustered, blurted out, "The reason it costs this much is because your guide takes a cut. If you can get him to waive his commission, I'll give it to you cheaper."

With that one careless, throwaway line, the jade shop sold Luo Zhigang down the river. The tourist rounded on him next and started laying into him. Luo Zhigang, for his part, maintained that he hadn't demanded any exorbitant commission—the commission rate was fixed, not something he set on a whim, sizing up each customer to see how much he could squeeze out of them. And besides, what tour guide didn't take a commission? It wasn't just him.

So Luo Zhigang started defending himself to the tourist. His voice was loud to begin with, and when he got agitated he couldn't control it at all. Add to that his coarse, rough-hewn looks, and to the tourist's ears it came across as outright provocation. The two of them were soon shouting at each other.

After that, someone—no one knew who—shoved Luo Zhigang, and the two sides went straight at each other in a brawl.

Meng Wan listened, then took a moment to digest it. Luo Zhigang had certainly handled this very badly, but the jade shop's responsibility was clearly the greater.

The matter of a tour guide taking a commission was wrong, yes, but Luo Zhigang hadn't invented the practice, nor was he the only one doing it, and the rate had been set in advance. Now the customer was saying the jade's quality didn't match its price—and even accounting for the guide's commission, Meng Wan was certain the lion's share of the profit went to the jade shop. The shop didn't think its own markup was excessive; instead it deflected the blame onto the guide, manufacturing a dispute between tourist and guide to muddy the real reason behind the murky pricing.

As for Luo Zhigang—the guide—even after Meng Wan had already warned him, he'd still taken his tourists to a place set up to fleece them. And when the trouble started, he hadn't immediately pointed out where the jade shop was at fault; instead, egged on by them, he'd gotten into a shouting match with his own tourist, come to blows, and dragged the whole thing to the police station. His failings weren't small either.

A medium-height man among them, wearing glasses, fixed Meng Wan with a displeased look and said in an unfriendly tone, "Since you're the one in charge of this travel agency, why don't you tell us how this is going to be handled."

This was the first time Meng Wan had run into a dispute like this, and for a moment she genuinely couldn't decide what to do. But they were in the service business. The exact plan for soothing the customer could be worked out gradually—what mattered first was a warm, friendly attitude. After all, you don't strike a smiling face.

"Brothers, sisters, aunties, uncles." Meng Wan smiled around at them all. "Whatever else can be said about today, the fault lies with us, and our travel agency will absolutely make things right with compensation. Everyone please calm down. You're all out here to enjoy yourselves—don't let one little hiccup spoil the mood."

The man in glasses saw that this young girl had a far gentler manner than Luo Zhigang, and she was a good deal easier on the eyes too. His expression eased a little, and his voice dropped. Tugging at his collar to show her, he said, "This is from that guide of yours. Take a look and tell me how you're going to pay for it."

"Well, your fist landed on the corner of my mouth," Luo Zhigang said, gingerly touching the corner of his lip with a finger. "I'm bleeding. You've got nothing but a bit of broken skin and you've still got the nerve to demand compensation!"

The anger that had just gone out of the man in glasses flared right back up. He was about to start arguing with Luo Zhigang again when Meng Wan cut him off with a shout.

"You shut your mouth!" Meng Wan roared fiercely at Luo Zhigang. "I'll deal with your problems later!"

Meng Wan looked at the three red scratches on the man's neck. It was just as Luo Zhigang had said—the skin was broken, but there was no blood. "Is anyone else hurt?" she asked.

"He shoved me, too," called a tall, thin man standing behind the man in glasses, rubbing at his chest. "It still aches right now. It's bound to bruise black-and-blue by tomorrow."

So in this whole brawl, the one who'd come off worst was, in fact, Luo Zhigang.

Now that she understood the extent of the injuries, Meng Wan had a rough idea of how big a concession she could afford to make. "How about this? I'll give you thirty percent off the group's tour fee and refund you the difference, and on top of that I'll give each of you a jade pendant for free. Does that settle it to your satisfaction?"

Thirty percent off meant the group would basically turn no profit at all—she'd treat it as money spent buying a lesson. And their injuries really weren't serious; besides, the day's itinerary was already over, so no part of the actual tour had been disrupted.

"Thirty percent off?" The man in glasses, however, wasn't satisfied. "You kick up all this fuss and you offer me thirty percent off? You're fobbing me off like a beggar."

Meng Wan asked patiently, "Then why don't you tell me what you want, and I'll see whether I can accept it."

"For free!"
