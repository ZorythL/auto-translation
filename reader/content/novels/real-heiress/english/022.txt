Chapter 22

Xiaohai was still putting up one last struggle. "Didn't you already search me? There's nothing on me."

Afraid he'd bolt again, Meng Wan grabbed his arm first, then gave a hard kick to his right shin. "Take that shoe off."

Watching his back just now, the more she'd looked, the more wrong it had seemed: the kid walked with one leg stiff. Though he'd done his best to walk like normal, the slight lean of his body to the left had given him away.

Xiaohai hissed in pain, his right leg jerking up after the kick. "Fine, I'll take it off—what'd you kick me for?"

Meng Wan's face was cold as she threatened, "Take it off, quick, and no tricks. The officers haven't gone far. If you don't want to go back inside, hand the thing over and I'll let you off this once."

Xiaohai, so carefree and devil-may-care a moment ago, turned instantly meek at her words. That stretch of time in prison was the worst ordeal of his life so far; to this day, when he dreamed of being back inside, he'd still wake up in terror. The day he got out, he'd sworn he would never go back.

So Meng Wan's threat worked like a charm. Xiaohai, who'd been on the verge of one last gambit, obediently took off his shoe instead. Chen Hui spotted the gold earring tucked into the sole at a single glance.

She picked up the shoe, took out the earring, and looked at Meng Wan with a face full of guilt as she handed it over. After her search had turned up nothing, she'd even thought Meng Wan had wronged her nephew.

Meng Wan took the earring and clutched it in her hand, her heart finally settling back where it belonged. This boy had nearly ruined her very first group assignment.

She was still steadying her frayed nerves when, over to the side, Chen Hui had already started whacking Xiaohai with the shoe sole.

"I'll teach you to steal!"

Xiaohai hopped about dodging her. "I didn't steal it, I found it."

"If you find something, you give it back." Chen Hui ground out through clenched teeth, landing another smack with the sole. "Finding something and not returning it is stealing!"

Xiaohai took the blow square on, grimacing in pain. "I was wrong, Auntie, I was wrong—isn't that enough?"

Worried Chen Hui's commotion would draw the police back, Meng Wan stepped in to stop her. "That's enough, Manager Chen. Save the beating and scolding for home—it won't do to lure the officers back over here."

As it happened, Chen Hui had worn herself out hitting him too. She stood there gasping for breath and said viciously to Xiaohai, "Just wait till we get home—I'll deal with you then!"

To think that only moments ago she'd felt a flash of guilt for having brought up his time in prison. The ungrateful wretch.

Though Meng Wan hadn't handed him over to the police, that didn't mean she'd let him off so easily. The earring wasn't hers—she had no standing to decide Xiaohai's fate.

"This isn't over. Tonight I'll bring the earring's owner back to the hotel, and how it's finally handled will be entirely up to her."

Chen Hui, who'd assumed Meng Wan would let Xiaohai off, felt her heart, only just set at ease, lurch up again. "Xiao Meng, do him a kindness, won't you—don't let word of this get out? He's already been inside once. If he goes in a second time, how is he supposed to face the world ever again?"

"Manager Chen." Meng Wan said gravely, "Since he's already been inside once, he ought to know there are some mistakes you can't make twice. But not only did he fail to reflect on it, he was about to make off with the earring. I won't cover for behavior like that, no matter what. If I let him off today, then in the future some other Li Wan or Zhang Wan will be the one to teach him a lesson. This isn't about whether I let him off—it's about him recognizing his own mistake and never repeating it. That's what matters most."

"Auntie." Xiaohai let his brows droop. "You don't need to waste your breath on my account. I'll go apologize and own up. Whether she wants to call the police or wants compensation, I'll agree to it. This is my own doing, and I'll bear it myself."

The mere thought of her nephew possibly going back inside had Chen Hui weeping uncontrollably. "Brother, Sister-in-law, I've let you down—I didn't look after Xiaohai well. I've failed the trust you placed in me."

No wonder she'd never once seen Xiaohai's parents—it was because they were both gone. Meng Wan felt a sting in her nose, and suddenly half regretted having spoken so harshly. The truth was, she knew Auntie Bai was the sort with a sharp tongue but a soft heart; she would surely never do anything terrible to Xiaohai—at most she'd lecture him a few words, which would serve as a lesson in itself.

Leaving Xiaohai's home, Meng Wan rushed without pause to the first scenic spot. The whole busload had long been waiting outside the gate, and Auntie Bai, in no mood for sightseeing, sat in the coach quietly sniffling.

Seeing Meng Wan return, Auntie Luo was the first to rush over and ask how it went. Learning the earring had been recovered, she breathed an instant sigh of relief.

Meng Wan hurried onto the coach and placed the earring in Auntie Bai's hands. Auntie Bai had assumed it was already lost for good, her heart long since gone cold; now, seeing the earring again, she was too overjoyed to speak. She cupped both earrings in her hands and let the tears spill freely.

Meng Wan comforted her a while, and once she'd calmed down, told her the whole story and put in a few good words for Xiaohai, hoping Auntie Bai might give him a chance.

Auntie Bai didn't answer Meng Wan. She said nothing at all—just calmly tucked both earrings away into her bag, with no other reaction.

This wasn't what Meng Wan had expected. She couldn't quite read Auntie Bai's mind now, and her heart went out anxiously to Xiaohai. Surely she hadn't made a mistake?

Though there were usually little squabbles among them, today everyone was especially solicitous toward Auntie Bai, and they were all glad for her that the earring had been found.

Luckily Meng Wan had gotten back fairly early and hadn't lost too much time—dinner was only pushed back an hour. By the time they returned to the hotel, it was already past eight.

Chen Hui had long since brought Xiaohai to the hotel; aunt and nephew waited, trembling, for the unknown judgment to come.

That whole day Meng Wan had not only been giving tours and leading the group but also keeping a constant watch on Auntie Bai's mood, hoping she might cheer up—so that come evening, perhaps, she'd show Xiaohai a kinder face.

Back at the guesthouse, Meng Wan counted heads and watched everyone return to their rooms. Auntie Bai had already spotted Xiaohai sitting with his head bowed, and she walked straight over and sat down across from him, her husband taking the seat to one side.

Meng Wan stood guard nearby, hardly daring to breathe.

Just as everyone sat in tense, braced silence, Auntie Bai finally deigned to speak.

"So you're the one who picked up my earring?"

Though Auntie Bai usually wore a smiling, genial face, all Meng Wan could see on it now was an authority that commanded respect without any show of anger.

Xiaohai laced his fingers together and gave a small nod of his lowered head.

Auntie Bai went on, "Guide Meng tells me that when she found you, you were packing your bags, about to run away?"

"I wasn't trying to run away." Xiaohai finally spoke. "I just wanted to find somewhere no one knew me and start over."

But he hadn't a cent to his name, and so he'd taken to that crooked idea.

Hearing her nephew's thinking, Chen Hui secretly brushed away the tears at the corners of her eyes—nothing but heartache, more heartache.

"What was it you went in for last time?"

Xiaohai seemed deeply reluctant to bring up the past. He lowered his head and mumbled, "Stealing."

Afraid everyone would misunderstand Xiaohai, Chen Hui jumped in to explain at once. "Xiaohai was tricked. He wasn't the one who stole the goods—he was just called in to help, and then got caught red-handed by the police while the mastermind got away. Our Xiaohai became the scapegoat."

The spring of the year before last, a hooligan friend of Xiaohai's who worked at a cannery claimed there was a batch of canned goods that had failed quality inspection and were to be disposed of. The friend was the driver assigned to haul them off for disposal, and seeing all those cans about to be thrown out, he couldn't bear it and decided to flip the batch and resell them himself.

He couldn't handle it alone, so he roped in a few friends to help, Xiaohai among them.

He promised that once the cans sold, he'd split the money with everyone. He'd already lined up a buyer; all they had to do was haul the goods over and the job would be done.

He even forged a goods-release form and a vehicle exit pass, and one Friday in the dead of night drove a truck loaded with cans out of the cannery.

He set off down the road with Xiaohai and a few others riding along to guard the cargo. They'd barely started out when the police came after them. The fellow was sharp—seeing the situation go bad, he leapt straight out of the truck, leaving behind the friends who'd come to help him.

When Xiaohai and the others realized things had gone wrong, they pulled the truck over of their own accord, and from the police they learned that these were brand-new cans with no quality problems at all.

Xiaohai and the rest explained that they'd been tricked into it and had no idea the cans were stolen.

But they couldn't account for why they'd had no suspicions about hauling goods in the dead of night and receiving such a hefty payment. By their own assumption that the goods were fine, they should have chosen a daytime hour to move them openly and aboveboard. And with pay that generous, why would the man not have done the job with his own family instead—how could such a cushy windfall ever have fallen to them?

Xiaohai couldn't explain it. In truth he'd had his doubts too, but the lure of money had won out over any pursuit of the truth, and he'd chosen to bury his head and believe it really was a truckload of defective cans. So now, facing the police's questioning, he couldn't claim with a wholly clear conscience that he'd known nothing at all.

Once the police understood the situation—though the men were first-time offenders who'd acted in ignorance—it happened to be during a crackdown, and in the end each was sentenced to two years.

Yuyang was a small place, and the old Taiping Street district where Xiaohai lived was even thicker with people who knew one another. The instant the trouble broke, word spread through the whole neighborhood in no time. People didn't understand the circumstances—they only knew Xiaohai had gone to prison—and then the rumors started.

Some said he'd robbed someone. Others said he'd beaten someone up. Still others spread it around that he'd killed a man.

Chen Hui explained it again and again, and no one believed her at all. People only believe what they want to believe, with no regard for the truth.

The root of it was that they all thought Xiaohai a wild, fatherless and motherless child, raised without a parent's guidance, idling away his days with the neighborhood's good-for-nothing youths—so sooner or later he was bound to go down the road of crime.

After Xiaohai got out, it wasn't hard to tell from the length of his sentence that he hadn't done anything serious. But he was one of the few people in that whole area who'd done prison time, and everyone gave him a wide berth, whispering behind his back. He became the cautionary tale every household used to scold its children.

"If you don't study hard, you'll end up just like Chen Xiaohai—idling around with bad sorts and getting hauled off to prison."

"Keep misbehaving and I'll send you over to Chen Xiaohai's, let him give you a beating."

"You stay far away from that Chen Xiaohai from now on—don't let him lead you astray."

Words like these were beyond counting. Xiaohai had once held an apprentice job at the bearing factory; the day after the unit found out he'd gone to prison, they fired him. And after he got out, no one anywhere was willing to take on a man with a record.

Hunting everywhere for work and finding none, Chen Hui watched her nephew waste away at home, dispirited day after day, and arranged a cleaning job for him at the guesthouse—so he could earn his own keep, and stay under her eye where she could keep watch over him.

Now that she knew the whole story from start to finish, Meng Wan's earlier doubts were finally laid to rest. No wonder a young, strapping fellow like this had ended up doing cleaning work.

A flicker of feeling at last showed in Auntie Bai's eyes. "So you wanted to take the earring and go live somewhere no one knew you?"

Xiaohai nodded dumbly.

"And have you thought about this—the moment you sold that earring off, you'd have truly broken the seal on theft. Can you guarantee that if your life still didn't go the way you wanted afterward, the same idea wouldn't come over you again?"

Once you cross that line, it's very hard to go on living by it again. You'll think the first time turned out fine, so the next won't be a problem either—and then comes a second time, a third—until one day it's wholly out of control, and by then there's no turning back.

No one said a word, as if they'd already foreseen the ending Auntie Bai's words spelled out for Xiaohai. Xiaohai pressed his lips tight, his chin trembling without stop. Red-eyed, he lifted his face toward Auntie Bai, and finally, unable to hold it back any longer, fear tangled with grievance, he burst into loud sobs.

His crying came mixed with garbled apologies, broken by gasps and sniffles. "I'm sorry, I was wrong, I'll never do it again."

Meng Wan was deeply struck, and inwardly heaped admiration on Auntie Bai. With just a few simple words she'd led Xiaohai to recognize the gravity of what he'd done himself. Meng Wan had worried Auntie Bai might lecture him with endless nagging, which would only breed defiance in him and backfire in the end.

Sure enough, the aunties who could make it to retirement in a government work unit all had a few tricks up their sleeves.

The matter was resolved to everyone's satisfaction: Auntie Bai got her earring back, Xiaohai recognized his own wrongdoing, and Meng Wan, too, brought her very first group assignment to a smooth close.

She'd thought the whole thing was settled, and that she'd have nothing more to do with Xiaohai—after all, the two of them were about as unconnected as could be.

But on the very day she sent the tour group off without a hitch, Chen Hui came looking for her.
