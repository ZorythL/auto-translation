Chapter 11

Over the next several days, the three of them worked in easy harmony to complete the travel plan Meng Wan had laid out, and by unspoken agreement no one brought up the little episode from that evening.

On the day James left, Lin Dong and Meng Wan saw him off to the airport together. Meng Wan wasn't one for goodbyes, but at the thought that she might never see James again in this lifetime, she still wanted to bid this man she'd spent such a brief time with a proper farewell.

"This is far enough." James halted, pulling his suitcase to a stop. "Thank you both for all your help—you've given me a truly unforgettable trip."

Meng Wan forced up a smile, trying her best to seem lighthearted. "We didn't have much time together, but I learned a great deal from you too. Thank you for hiring me as your guide. Actually..."

Since he was leaving anyway, Meng Wan didn't want to keep anything from him. "Actually, I'm not a guide. That day I just happened to run into you, and seeing how good-looking you were, I wanted to get to know you. I never told you all these days because I was afraid you'd think I was unprofessional and replace me. I'm sorry—I lied to you."

"Wow." James looked mildly surprised. "But you did wonderfully. You're the finest guide I've ever had."

What surprised him wasn't that Meng Wan wasn't a guide, but that, not being one, she could do the job so flawlessly—her commentary so detailed that even many genuine guides couldn't match it.

"Thank you." Seeing he wasn't angry, Meng Wan let out a breath, and having said it aloud she felt a good deal lighter herself.

Afraid Meng Wan would feel guilty, James steered the subject onto himself. "Actually, there's something I've been hiding from the two of you as well."

Meng Wan and Lin Dong exchanged a glance, then looked at James, puzzled.

James gave a hearty laugh. "The truth is, I understand Yuyang dialect. My grandmother and my mother are both from Yuyang."

Meng Wan's expression froze. She remembered she'd grumbled to Lin Dong that James was finicky and full of fussy quirks—so didn't that mean he'd understood every word at the time?

Lin Dong gave a couple of awkward, sheepish laughs too. Thank goodness he wasn't a talker and hadn't said much.

Watching the parade of expressions cross their faces, James apologized of his own accord. "It was wrong of me. I should have told you sooner. I kept quiet only because I didn't want you to feel too guarded around me."

Meng Wan managed a sheepish smile. "Then I suppose that makes us even."

"Goodbye, my friends."

"Goodbye."

"Goodbye."

Watching James disappear through the boarding gate, Meng Wan felt, for reasons she couldn't quite name, a tangle of emotions she couldn't put into words—disappointment, wistfulness, reluctance to part; probably all of them.

Though she hadn't known James long, she'd already come to think of him as a friend, and parting from a friend was always hard—all the more so when this was the last time they'd ever meet.

*

On the drive back, Lin Dong saw that Meng Wan stayed sunk in low spirits, and he knew she was sad over James's departure.

The truth was, he felt some reluctance too. James was without question a fine man—gentlemanly and courteous, mild-mannered toward everyone, never once losing his temper or uttering a single coarse word, generous with his money and thorough in everything he did, and respectful toward the people who worked alongside him.

But his feelings went no further than reluctance. He didn't know whether Meng Wan felt the same as he did—though by the look of her, something deeper seemed to be lingering in her heart.

To cheer her up, Lin Dong took out the cloth-wrapped lunch box from beside his seat and handed it to her. "These scallion pancakes came fresh out of the pan this morning—they're still warm. Hurry and try one."

He knew Meng Wan loved them, so this morning he'd specially asked his mother to make a few extra.

Meng Wan had left in a hurry that morning and only managed a quick gulp of soy milk. Now, with noon approaching, her stomach had long been growling, and in an instant her tangle of emotions vanished, her whole attention diverted to the food.

"They really are best when they're warm. If they were straight out of the pan, sizzling with oil, that scallion flavor would be even more fragrant on the tongue." Meng Wan imagined it as she ate.

Lin Dong drove with his eyes on the road, glancing at the cracked-faced watch on his wrist. "It's almost lunchtime—where do you want to eat?"

Today Meng Wan had again had Xu Qiuyun put in a day's leave for her. Her family still thought she was at work, so for now she could only find a restaurant outside for lunch.

"Just drop me anywhere in the city center. I'll find a little place and grab a bite."

Lin Dong hesitated a couple of seconds, tapping his index finger lightly on the wheel. "How about... you come eat at my place?"

"Hm?"

Meng Wan, chewing the pancake in her mouth, stared at him wide-eyed.

Lin Dong explained in a fluster, "Didn't you want fresh scallion pancakes straight from the pan? It just so happens that's what we're having for lunch at my house."

How much could one family love scallion pancakes, Meng Wan thought—pancakes in the morning and pancakes again at noon.

But how could Meng Wan pass up a free meal of scallion pancakes? First she made a show of being polite. "Won't that be too much trouble?"

"What trouble? It's just setting out one more bowl and a pair of chopsticks. It's nothing fancy, though—I'm just worried you'll turn your nose up at it."

When he'd dropped Meng Wan home before, the whole area where she lived was public housing—you had to work for a state work unit to live there. Coming from a family like that, her circumstances had to be good, and judging by the way she dressed every day, she never wore the same outfit twice—clearly a child of a well-off family.

"How could I? If I really turned my nose up at it I'd have just said no."

Lin Dong was tickled by her bluntness. It was exactly the sort of thing she'd say.

*

Lin Dong's family lived on East Avenue in the old quarter of Yuyang.

Lin Dong parked the car at the mouth of the alley, and Meng Wan, lunch box in hand, followed him out. It was her first time on East Avenue, and she looked around as she walked.

Rows of old dwellings lined the street, mostly single-story brick-and-timber houses with gray tiles and gray walls. Some had stone blocks and steps before their doors. The houses were old, and now and then you could spot exquisite wood carvings on the beams and pillars.

Going in through the street-front gates, most opened onto deep-set courtyard compounds or large shared courtyards, several households living in each, with leftover honeycomb coal briquettes from the winter piled in the corners.

It was right at mealtime, and the neighbors were at their doors picking over vegetables and chatting, while children chased and tussled in the lanes.

Seeing Lin Dong come back with an unfamiliar girl, they all turned their gaze on Meng Wan.

What Meng Wan dreaded most were the appraising stares of the aunties by the roadside. If she ran into anyone she knew, they could ferret out the color of the underwear she had on that day.

"Brother Dong, it's bad—your uncle and aunt are at your place again, and they brought a man nobody recognizes."

A young boy came pelting out of the lane, panting as he spoke to Lin Dong. Hearing this, Lin Dong rushed off toward the lane at once.

Bewildered, Meng Wan stopped where she was, watching the two of them dash away. Something must have happened at his home, she thought, weighing whether she ought to follow.

An ordinary visit from relatives surely wouldn't have Lin Dong so panicked that he'd run off without so much as a word to her, so there must be some conflict. If she, an outsider, just barged in and happened to witness some family scandal, Lin Dong would inevitably be embarrassed.

But if she simply slipped away without a word, wouldn't that be even ruder?

Cradling the lunch box, Meng Wan stood torn for a good while, then decided to go in and see how things stood. Maybe she was overthinking it and it really was just relatives stopping by; and even if it wasn't, she could at least say a word to Lin Dong before leaving.

So thinking, Meng Wan set off in the direction Lin Dong had gone.

There was only one courtyard at the end of this lane—the Lin family lived in this deep compound. Meng Wan had barely reached the gate when she heard the racket of arguing inside, and her heart sank. It seemed she'd guessed right.

She didn't go straight in but stood outside the gate listening for a while, and before long she'd pieced together the gist of it.

Lin Dong's younger sister was in her final year of high school, his mother had cataracts, and his father had died young. The whole family's living expenses came from what Lin Dong earned driving his cab. Six months earlier his mother's condition had worsened, her eyes nearly unable to see, and Lin Dong had spent every cent of the family's savings treating his mother, Hao Xiangping—leaving nothing for his sister's tuition.

With no other choice, Lin Dong had borrowed a sum from his uncle and his aunt apiece, using it for medicine and tuition, promising at the time to repay it within three months. But business had been poor that stretch, so it had dragged on a good deal longer.

Lin Dong's uncle and aunt, afraid he wouldn't pay them back, would drop by from time to time to press for the money. Today, though, they hadn't come to collect a debt—they'd come to set Lin Dong's sister, Lin Xi, up with a match.

They wanted to push the marriage through. The other party would put up a handsome bride-price, which would clear the debt owed to them and also allow Hao Xiangping to be treated at the best hospital in the city.

A move that solved several problems at once—any sensible person would agree. But to their surprise, Lin Xi resisted fiercely. She said she didn't want to marry; she wanted to take the college entrance exam.

Meng Wan had just gotten the whole story straight in her mind when the woman's wheedling voice drifted out again. "Xiao Xi, listen to your aunt—you can't go wrong. Look at how things are at home: your mother can see less and less, your brother's out from dawn to dusk and can't even find himself a wife, and the family's buried in debt just to scrape your tuition together. So tell me, what's the point of you going to school? What good does all that schooling do a girl, when she just ends up marrying anyway? You might as well marry now—at least then your mother and brother can have it a little easier."

The more Meng Wan listened, the higher her temper rose. This was naked emotional blackmail!

The man took up the woman's thread, striking while the iron was hot. "That's right, Xiao Xi. Your aunt and I would never do you harm. I brought Little Li along today so you could meet him in person—he's a fine-looking young man, his parents are both doctors, he works at the tax bureau, he'll be assigned an apartment the moment he marries, and he'll put up a generous bride-price to treat your mother's eyes. You couldn't find a match like that if you went searching with a lantern."

No way, Meng Wan muttered to herself. There had to be something wrong with this man.

The crowd of onlookers outside the gate had swelled, and Meng Wan was jostled right up beside the doorway. Leaning forward, she could see the scene inside.

"Xiao Xi, it's all Ma's fault, letting you down." Hao Xiangping was weeping, her head of grizzled white hair making her look all the more careworn.

Lin Xi, supporting her mother, choked back tears, her eyes red and swollen. "Ma, don't say that. It's just my own bad luck."

Following the voice, Meng Wan finally got a clear look at Lin Xi—an oval face with a faint honeyed flush, a pair of apricot eyes clear in black and white, a glossy black ponytail, wisps of stray hair blown across her face by the wind and dampened by her tears. The sight of her was enough to make anyone's heart ache.

"It's all because I'm a useless brother." Lin Dong's eyes were bloodshot, both fists clenched, and he slammed one hard against the courtyard wall.

Seeing blood run down the wall, Lin Xi wept harder still, great teardrops sliding down her cheeks to her chin and dropping to the ground. Sobbing beyond words, she got out, "Auntie, Uncle, I... I... I a—"

Before the word "agree" could leave her lips, Meng Wan, sensing she was about to give in, was so anxious she forgot she was a mere outsider. She strode straight in through the gate and, in front of everyone, shouted at the top of her voice:

"I don't agree!"
