Chapter 39

Having won Qin Yi'e over, the family quickly set about packing. Meng Wan asked Qin Yizhong and Ding Chunling to handle the aftermath—to notify the relatives who'd been coming for the Saturday feast that the wedding was off, so they wouldn't make the trip for nothing.

Although Qin Yi'e hadn't invited all that many people, going house to house was still no small task. Meng Wan gave each of them a hundred yuan as compensation, and the two assured her she could leave with an easy mind and let them take care of the rest.

Meng Wan was afraid Sun Jianxing would come looking, so she could hardly wait to leave that very night. Sure enough, before nightfall, in the early dusk, Sun Jianxing—having gotten the news once classes let out—came hurrying over with his mother.

When they arrived, Meng Wan was in the middle of describing to Zhou Nian the styles fashionable in the city these days, even promising that once they were back she'd take her to the department store and buy her a few of the most stylish dresses of the moment.

Zhou Nian listened, utterly enchanted, her whole heart already set on the life that awaited her in Yuyang.

As Sun Jianxing came through the door, the two of them were laughing and chatting away. Putting it together with his mother's account, he judged this to be the girl who'd paid the bride-price back out of her own pocket.

"Zhou Nian."

The two of them turned at the voice. Seeing Sun Jianxing, the smile on Zhou Nian's lips froze. She stood up, a little flustered. "What are you doing here?"

"Zhou Nian." Sun Jianxing took a step forward. "I've come to explain about the bride-price. I knew nothing about any of it from start to finish—it was all my sister-in-law's own doing. Can't you give me one more chance?"

A marriage he'd thought was as good as nailed down had flown off like a roast duck out of his very mouth, and it wounded his pride as a grown man deeply.

Zhou Nian lowered her eyes, a little guilty. "I'm sorry. I can't marry you anymore."

"Why?" Sun Jianxing raised his voice, edging toward agitation. "Just because of that half of the bride-price? I can make it up to you!"

"It's not that, Jianxing!" Zhou Nian raised her voice in turn. "It's that I don't want to marry. I want to go to school. I want to sit the college entrance exam. I want to go see the big city. I think there are all kinds of possibilities for my future—marriage isn't my only choice."

Before today, marriage truly had been the best option for a country girl with no background and no education. But things were different now. After hearing Meng Wan describe the big city, she found every part of it more stirring than the two little bunches of flowers Sun Jianxing had brought her.

Sun Jianxing clearly hadn't expected this to be her reason. He stared at Zhou Nian, dumbfounded, then turned his eyes to Meng Wan beside her. "It's you. You're the one filling Nian'er's head with this—you're deceiving her. You must have some other motive. She's already twenty-two; she didn't even finish high school. What school would ever take her?"

The words carried a bone-deep contempt. Stung by them, Zhou Nian buried her head lower.

"What's wrong with twenty-two?" Meng Wan caught Zhou Nian's hand and rallied her. "Twenty-two is the best age there is. If she wants to study, she can study; if she wants to work, she can work; whatever she wants to do, it isn't too late. And Nian'er is so clever—she's sure to get into college!"

Meng Wan's tone was utterly firm, and it kindled a flicker of warmth in the heart of the just-wounded Zhou Nian.

"And what are you to her?" Sun Jianxing pressed on. "Do you have any idea what their family's circumstances are? You make it sound so easy. Doesn't studying cost money? Even if she got into college, could her family ever afford the tuition?"

He knew the Zhou family's situation all too well. Her father's yearly medical bills alone were enough to drain them dry; the money to marry off the son had been scraped out a mouthful at a time. Where would they ever find the money to send her to school?

"That's no concern of yours. I'll pay her tuition. Even if she does nothing at all, I'll provide for her for the rest of her life. As long as I can earn money, that's enough."

Even doing all this, what Meng Wan owed Zhou Nian could never be repaid in this lifetime.

Sun Jianxing looked at this Cheng Yaojin who had ambushed him out of nowhere and asked through gritted teeth, "Just who exactly are you?"

How could anyone be this good to Zhou Nian for no reason at all? There was no such person in the family.

In truth Zhou Nian was full of questions herself. This relative who'd suddenly surfaced was like the Bodhisattva Guanyin, lavishing such care on her. Was their bond really this close? She was only a distant relation—why would she be willing to spend so much money and so much effort to help their whole family?

Could she really have some ulterior motive?

But given their family's current circumstances, what was there left to be after?

At that thought, Zhou Nian talked herself round: perhaps the woman was simply wealthy, moved by goodwill to rescue the few living relatives she had left—perhaps it gave her some sense of fulfillment.

Meng Wan introduced herself frankly. "I'm Nian'er's kin—the dearest of family, who only wants to see her do better and better."

------

Meng Wan went a few more rounds with Sun Jianxing, until at last, unable to master his temper, his voice rose higher and higher, nearly to a roar. Afraid he might raise a hand against the two girls, James came out from the room and showed him out.

Meng Wan had reason to suspect the man had already taken to drinking; his emotions were far more prone to slipping out of control than an ordinary person's.

Once again it confirmed her course of action: this marriage absolutely could not be allowed to happen.

The Zhou house had two kang. Ordinarily Qin Yi'e and Zhou Da shared one while Zhou Nian slept alone on the other. With guests arrived today, they split up by sex—men on one, women on the other.

For the first time ever, Meng Wan, Zhou Nian, and Qin Yi'e—this tangled trio of mother and daughters—slept side by side.

Each of them carried her own thoughts, each brimming with melancholy over the future.

Early the next morning, before dawn had even broken, Qin Yizhong came to fetch them in a borrowed truck. He'd bought the train tickets the day before as well. Ding Chunling came along to see them off too—after all, once they left, the two sides likely wouldn't meet again for some time, so it counted as a proper farewell.

The couple were glad to see the elder sister's family take a turn for the better. But still—they were all family; surely Meng Wan ought to do something for them as well? In their hearts they couldn't help feeling, to some degree, that things weren't quite even.

The night before last, the moment Meng Wan learned Qin Yizhong did renovation work, she'd asked James whether his place needed any extra hands. James said hiring workers wasn't his responsibility, but that he'd ask around once he was back.

While nothing had come of it yet, Meng Wan didn't want to breathe a word to Qin Yizhong. It would be no good to raise his hopes only to dash them; that would breed all the more resentment, and ill will for nothing.

Even if she brought Qin Yizhong's family to Yuyang as well, they'd still have to eat. So until she'd found him work, she couldn't lightly hold out hope to him. There was no way she could shoulder the livelihoods of two whole households—besides, she herself was still only at the very start of things.

Because Zhou Da couldn't walk normally, James took on the job of pushing him—seeing him through the station and onto the train, the whole family hauling luggage and pushing a man, and only just managing, at the very last moment, to board.

Meng Wan had asked Qin Yizhong to buy six tickets, so they'd have a little more room to sit.

At first Zhou Da was so thrilled he kept tugging at James to ask about this and that, but before long he dozed off, snoring. Soon after, across from him, the mother and daughters slumped against one another and drifted off to sleep as well.

James sat opposite, studying them in earnest.

Qin Yi'e wasn't much older than Meng Wan's own mother, yet she looked a good ten-odd years more aged—especially those thick hands of hers, covered in dark calluses, which told plainly that she'd endured no small measure of hardship.

A storyline like this—a child swapped to make a dragon of another's son—he'd only ever read about in novels. He never imagined that one day such a thing would truly unfold right beside him.

Because he was Meng Wan's good friend, it was only natural that he viewed the matter from her side.

Bringing her family here was only the first step. The questions that followed were the crux of it.

Tell Zhou Nian the truth—how to soothe her feelings? Have Zhou Nian reunite with the Meng family—and what role would Meng Wan play in the middle of it all?

However generous she might be, watching the family she'd once been inseparable from turn and lavish their tenderness on the daughter who shared their blood—that jealousy over kinship, and that guilt over having occupied another's identity, would tear at her without cease. It would force her to keep her own fragility hidden, to feign composure before everyone—just as she was doing right now.

As night fell, the group at last arrived in Yuyang, and Meng Wan took a cab to deliver everyone to Xiaohai's place.

Xiaohai's parents had died early; he lived alone in this little courtyard house. On her last visit she'd noticed the three rooms standing empty and bare, so before leaving she'd had Lin Dong deliver a letter to Xiaohai, with some money enclosed—the letter explained that she wanted to rent his house, and the money was the rent.

When they arrived, Xiaohai wasn't home, but the door was unlocked—likely so they wouldn't be shut out when they came.

The rooms had been cleared out and set in order, transformed beyond recognition from her last visit. The grubby bachelor's funk was entirely swept away, replaced by the faint, clean scent of soap.

The whole place, inside and out, had been gone over; even the curtains had been washed. Xiaohai had also laid in new daily necessities for them.

Meng Wan hadn't realized Xiaohai could be so thoughtful. He deserved praise.

With everyone settled in, and seeing it was getting late, Meng Wan wanted to send James off to rest first; she'd come thank Xiaohai in person in a few days' time.

James knew she must be exhausted, but she looked far more hopeful now than the distracted, fog-headed state she'd been in before they'd left. He knew Meng Wan was sure to do well.

Before he went, James also brought up his birthday the following week of his own accord. They agreed to meet at Yuxiang Tower.

Meng Wan watched James leave, then let out a long breath. She was finally back.

Just as she turned to go inside, she saw a dark shape bearing down fast at the mouth of the alley—it was Xiaohai, come home.

At the sight of Meng Wan, Xiaohai first started in surprise; but instead of the joy at his master's return that she'd expected, his face was urgent as he said to her, "Master, this is bad! The travel agency is about to be sold off!"
