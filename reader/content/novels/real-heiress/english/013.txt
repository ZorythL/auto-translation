Chapter 13

In the end, Meng Wan did get to eat the fresh scallion pancakes straight out of the pan. Maybe it was because the emotional roller coaster had worn her out and her stomach had long been growling with hunger, or maybe it was because the just-fried pancakes filled the air with their aroma, but either way her appetite was excellent, and she ate without even pausing to talk.

Auntie Hao felt deeply guilty. As someone who had lived half a lifetime, she certainly understood the courtesy owed to a guest.

But it had been a long while since the family had bought anything fresh. They were still eating the cabbage they'd salted away over the winter, and there was hardly any meat to speak of—each time, she would render a little oil from a small piece of fatback just to give the vegetables a hint of meaty flavor.

"I really am putting you through it, child." Auntie Hao turned her face toward where Meng Wan sat. "Why don't I have Xiaodong take you out for a proper meal instead?"

Meng Wan swallowed the bite in her mouth and said with a smile, "Auntie, I came here today specifically to eat these pancakes. Nothing out there could ever taste as good as what you make. These are the best scallion pancakes I've ever had."

Lin Dong understood his mother's worry, but Meng Wan genuinely did love these pancakes. He chimed in to reassure her, "Ma, next time, once I've earned some money, I'll take her out to a restaurant. But today she came especially to eat the pancakes you made. You can't very well send a guest off only half full."

Lin Xi joined her brother in trying to put their mother at ease. "That's right, Ma. You can't see it, but Sister Wan's been eating with such gusto—she's already on her second one."

Hearing Lin Xi say her name, Meng Wan looked up and smiled at Auntie Hao, her cheeks puffed out as she chewed, looking so adorable that both Lin siblings burst out laughing.

"Auntie Hao, this is exactly what I love. Your pancakes smell amazing—ten thousand times better than any restaurant's. Today I'm going to polish them all off!"

At last a faint smile touched the corners of Auntie Hao's mouth. "Good child, eat as much as you want. If there's not enough, Auntie will make you more."

It was a simple, humble meal, but all four of them ate with great delight. Afterward, Lin Dong went to wash the dishes while Meng Wan looked over Lin Xi's schoolwork. Although her own grades had never been good, she still had a sharp eye for judging people.

Just from the textbooks crammed with notes and the test papers marked with high scores, Meng Wan could tell that Lin Xi was the real deal—a girl with genuine promise who, with a bit more effort, might well get into a university in the capital.

Lin Xi pulled Meng Wan over to sit on the bed. "Sister Wan, thank you."

If it hadn't been for Meng Wan, she would have agreed to marry today.

"Don't thank me." Meng Wan tucked a stray lock of hair behind Lin Xi's ear. "You should thank yourself. You've held on this far—you can't give up halfway."

Looking at her school uniform, washed pale and thin, Meng Wan sized up her figure. They were about the same build—Lin Xi was even a little thinner. Her clothes should fit. When she got home, she'd sort through her things and bring over some of the clothes she rarely wore.

Lin Xi lowered her head. "If you hadn't shown up, I really wouldn't have known what to do. I'm so useless."

"How old are you?" Meng Wan said. "These were grown-ups' problems to begin with. They never should have been dumped on a child like you. It's the adults who let you down."

Sensing the mood turning heavy, Meng Wan put on a stern face, half teasing. "Still, there is one thing you've done that I'm not happy about."

Lin Xi stared blankly, her clear eyes wide, waiting for what came next.

Meng Wan pursed her lips, pretending to be cross. "How could you score a hundred and forty on your math exam?" She pointed at the mock exam paper on the desk.

She herself had only managed twenty-some points!

When it was time to leave, Meng Wan made a point of taking a few more pancakes with her, and clutching the lunch box, she had Lin Dong drive her straight to the hospital.

She knew this matter wasn't over yet. Now that Li Feng had recognized her, his parents would surely go looking for Cheng Xuehua. She had to get there first and clear the air with Dr. Cheng, or she'd be in for a scolding herself.

Lin Dong was a little worried for her. "Your mother won't be angry with you, will she?"

"Probably," Meng Wan guessed. "But she'll definitely understand."

For as long as she could remember, Meng Wan had done countless reckless, outrageous things, and every time Cheng Xuehua was strict with her words but gentle in her actions, always letting her off lightly. So she wasn't too worried this time either—at most she'd put on a little charm and it would blow over.

Lin Dong didn't know what he could possibly say or do. All he had was gratitude, and more gratitude.

Meng Wan gazed straight ahead. "Don't worry. There'll come a day when you can repay me, and when that time comes, you'd better work hard for me."

After all, wasn't living in this world just a matter of you helping me and me helping you?

Hearing this, Lin Dong looked as if he'd been given a shot of adrenaline. "I'd climb mountains of knives and seas of fire for you—I'd die ten thousand deaths and not refuse!"

Meng Wan pressed a hand to her forehead. He'd clearly read too many martial-arts novels. "...It doesn't need to go quite that far."

*

When Meng Wan was little, she often went to the hospital with Cheng Xuehua. Back then Meng Qingyan was too busy at work to pick her up, so Cheng Xuehua would simply take her along to the hospital, where Meng Wan did her homework in Cheng Xuehua's office and amused herself on her own.

It wasn't until high school, when she decided the hospital's smell was too sharp and stopped wanting to go, that things changed. Li Feng's mother's office had been right next door to Cheng Xuehua's back then, and it was during those years that Meng Wan had crossed paths with Li Feng a few times.

Carrying the lunch box, Meng Wan arrived at Cheng Xuehua's office and knocked, but there was no answer. A doctor dropping off some files told her that Dr. Cheng was seeing a patient and that she should wait in the office for a while.

The moment Meng Wan stepped into the familiar office, memories came flooding back like a tide. After the year 2000, the First Hospital, where Cheng Xuehua worked, would undergo a major overhaul. Its scale kept expanding, and these old offices were all torn down and turned into wards.

Cheng Xuehua came back quickly, and the moment she stepped in and saw her daughter, she was pleasantly surprised.

"How is it you have time to come by?" She glanced at the clock. "Shouldn't you be at work right now?"

Meng Wan steadied Cheng Xuehua into a chair and, smiling sweetly, began to knead her shoulders. "I went to a friend's place for lunch today. The scallion pancakes were incredible, and I thought it wasn't fair for me to be the only one enjoying them. Their place happens to be close to here, so I brought some over for you to try. It's fine if I head back a little later this afternoon."

Cheng Xuehua glanced at the lunch box on the desk, half delighted and half astonished. Since when had her daughter become so thoughtful, even thinking to bring her a meal? She really had grown up.

As it happened, she'd just finished a consultation and hadn't gotten around to lunch. She hadn't felt hungry before, but now, with the aroma wafting over, her stomach suddenly stirred with ripples of hunger.

Seeing her dig in, Meng Wan pulled up a chair beside her and asked attentively, "Well? Good, isn't it?"

It really was good—it would have been even better fresh out of the pan, Cheng Xuehua thought, nodding as she chewed.

"Slow down." Meng Wan poured her a cup of water and set it in front of her. "Have some water so you don't choke."

Watching her daughter's uncharacteristic attentiveness, Cheng Xuehua finally couldn't resist asking, "Are you out of money again?"

"No. I'm earning my own living now—why would I still ask the family for money?"

You ask plenty, Cheng Xuehua thought to herself, and went on guessing. "Then you've gotten into trouble again?"

"Even less so. That was an act of justice on my part!"

Sure enough, Cheng Xuehua wore an "I knew it" expression and kept eating her pancake. "Out with it. Who'd you quarrel with this time?"

"It wasn't a quarrel." Meng Wan told Cheng Xuehua the whole story, deliberately leaving out any mention of Li Feng's parents.

"This is a good thing," Cheng Xuehua said, taking a sip of hot water. "Your money would only get frittered away anyway. Spending it on that girl can actually help her get into university."

"Right, that's exactly what I thought." Seeing that Cheng Xuehua approved of what she'd done, Meng Wan finally dared to reveal the truth. "It's just that the man who wanted to marry the girl... his parents... are also doctors."

"What a coincidence." Cheng Xuehua didn't think much of it. There were three hospitals, large and small, in Yuyang City, with no small number of doctors. What were the odds they'd be at her hospital?

"They're also... at the First Hospital," Meng Wan murmured, fidgeting with her fingers.

"At our hospital?" Cheng Xuehua's voice suddenly shot up, and her face twitched despite her best efforts to control it.

A mute son, with both parents doctors—that could only be the son of Director Xie from the anesthesiology department!

"Did that man recognize you?"

Meng Wan shrank her neck, blinked a couple of times, and gave a faint little nod.

Cheng Xuehua's heart sank halfway. Just yesterday she had asked Director Xie to help with the anesthesia for a critical operation, and now her own daughter had turned right around and wrecked the man's son's marriage prospects. Anesthesiologists were already in short supply—everyone scrambled to get them—and now she'd thoroughly offended one.

But her daughter hadn't done anything wrong. Even though the terms Li Feng had offered were genuinely good, Lin Xi would never have been happy married into that family.

Director Xie was not an easy mother-in-law. She was a domineering woman, and the son she'd raised, being disabled, did whatever she told him—point him east and he wouldn't go west, tell him to beat the dog and he wouldn't so much as touch the chicken. A son like that could never protect his own wife. And if Lin Xi had on top of that accepted a hefty bride price from the family, she'd have had even less dignity, who knew how badly bullied. If it were her own daughter marrying into a household like that, she wouldn't have stood for it either.

"Fine, fine." Cheng Xuehua sighed helplessly. "I'll take the fall for this good deed of yours."

"Thanks, Ma." Meng Wan let out a breath of relief and pressed her luck further. "Then about Lin Dong's mother's illness..."

"Have Lin Dong find a time to bring his mother to the hospital. I'll put in a word with Director Lin in ophthalmology and have her take a good look."

Whatever else was going on, illness still needed prompt treatment. The patient came first.

Meng Wan thanked Cheng Xuehua on behalf of the Lin family, and as a daughter, thanked her mother for her understanding.

Clutching the lunch box, Meng Wan left the hospital and first went home. She carefully packed up the pager along with its original box, warranty card, and instruction manual, gathered a cloth bag of clothes, and carried the things to the communications-equipment street in the old town. She was going to sell the pager.

She couldn't bear to part with her newly bought pager either, but she really was strapped for cash. She'd been putting on airs to do good deeds, and she couldn't ask the family for money anymore—she'd have to figure it out on her own.

Meng Wan went back to the same shop where she'd bought the device. The owner still remembered her—after all, there weren't many young women who'd plunk down four thousand yuan for a pager all at once.

Learning that she now wanted to sell it, the owner was straight with her: even though she'd barely used it, once a thing was bought and unsealed it counted as secondhand, so the price would have to come down. After some back-and-forth haggling, the owner gave Meng Wan three thousand five hundred yuan.

Losing five hundred yuan just like that stung Meng Wan, but considering it was lifesaving money, she figured it was worth it.

Money in hand, she circled back to the Lin household. By this time Lin Xi had already gone to school, leaving only Auntie Hao at home.

Meng Wan found a sheet of paper and wrote down everything she wanted to say, urging Lin Dong to take Auntie Hao to the hospital as soon as possible. Then she placed two thousand yuan under the note, covered it with a book, and left the clothes on Lin Xi's bed.

After saying goodbye to Auntie Hao and seeing that it was still early, Meng Wan decided to go put in some time at work.
