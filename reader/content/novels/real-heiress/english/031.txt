Chapter 31

After hurling those words out with bloodshot eyes, Meng Wan didn't even bother to pack. She snatched up the bag from the sofa and walked straight out of the Meng household.

Meng Xingyang had never imagined things would escalate this far. He moved to get up and stop her, but he was half a beat too slow—by the time he was on his feet, she had already burst out the door and gone.

Afraid that Zhizhi would be frightened, Sun Jing held the little girl tight against her, murmuring to her that everything was fine.

Meng Qingyan's already stern face had darkened until it looked like it had fallen into a cellar, pitch black and radiating waves of cold.

Cheng Xuehua's body trembled uncontrollably, her heart clenching with a sharp, twisting pain.

Driving Meng Wan out had never been her real intention. She had only meant to use it as a threat to make her switch jobs. In her mind, her daughter was a child who could only lean on the family—someone with no independence at all, who would never leave home and live on her own so easily. But she had never expected that the daughter who once had everything handed to her, who never lifted a finger, now had a mind of her own.

The Meng Wan of today felt utterly foreign to Cheng Xuehua. Her daughter had pulled countless reckless stunts over the years, big and small, but all of them had stayed within Cheng Xuehua's control. Today, for the first time, she felt her daughter slip beyond her grasp—going off without a word to earn a tour-guide license and change jobs, signing on with a travel agency, leading her own tours. Every single thing about it was unfamiliar.

When exactly had she changed?

------

After leaving the Meng household, Meng Wan wandered the streets alone. She didn't know where to go, didn't know what she was supposed to do next. This day had come sooner than she'd expected, catching her off guard.

As the sun sank in the west and night slowly closed in around her, Meng Wan knew she had to find somewhere to settle for the time being.

After all, she had lived through this once before. Such things still tugged at her heartstrings, but they could never shake her the way they had in her past life.

She could face everything to do with the Meng family calmly now. She knew this was only the drizzle before the storm—the real tempest was still to come.

She turned over in her mind the places she could go. Thinking it through, the Lin household seemed the most fitting; she could squeeze in with Lin Xi.

Meng Wan flagged down a cab and rode to the Lin home. By the time she arrived, the sky had gone black. Only Hao Xiangping and Lin Xi were home—Lin Dong was still out driving fares.

Because Lin Xi had been coming home late lately, Hao Xiangping had pushed back dinner, doing her best to make sure her daughter got a hot meal. It was one of the few things she, as a mother, could still do for her.

The familiar smell of scallion pancakes hit Meng Wan the moment she stepped through the door, followed close behind by the rich aroma of braised pork.

"Wan, sweetheart!" Hao Xiangping had just dished the meat out of the pan when she looked up and saw Meng Wan come in. Her eyes still couldn't make out faces clearly, but Meng Wan's figure was etched into her memory—she could have picked her out from half a street away by her silhouette alone.

"Auntie Hao." Meng Wan walked closer with a smile.

"Wan, sweetheart, what brings you here?" Hao Xiangping set the plate and the spatula down at the edge of the stove and wiped her hands on her apron, gazing at this unexpected visitor with a face full of delighted surprise.

"Auntie Hao..." Meng Wan paused. "Something's happened at home, so I was hoping I could stay here a few days—if it's not too much trouble?"

"Trouble? Of course it's no trouble!" Hao Xiangping took Meng Wan's hand and led her into the main room, then called toward Lin Xi's room, "Xiaoxi, come see who's here!"

Lin Xi's door was open. Meng Wan could see her at her desk, going over her schoolwork.

The moment she heard Hao Xiangping's voice, she spun around. The serious, focused little face that had been immersed in a sea of knowledge suddenly brightened. She set down her pen at once and came toward Meng Wan.

Lin Xi wore the same look of surprise. "Sister Wan, how come you're here?"

Meng Wan explained again, and Lin Xi declared, thrilled, that she was more than welcome.

"Xiaoxi, help your Sister Wan make up a bed. I'll go dish out the food—we'll all eat together." With that, Hao Xiangping hurried back to the kitchen.

Right on cue, Meng Wan's stomach growled a few times. She'd only managed a few bites earlier, and now that the tension had drained away, her stomach suddenly felt hollow.

Lin Xi excitedly pulled Meng Wan into her room and took a clean set of bedding—quilt with a red cover and a pillow—out of the wardrobe, laying them beside her own neatly folded quilt.

"Sister Wan, sleep next to me. We'll share the bed." She beamed up at Meng Wan with a great big smile.

Meng Wan could see how happy Lin Xi was to have her there, and the smile spread to her own lips, the earlier troubles tossed to the back of her mind.

"Thank you, Xiaoxi." Meng Wan hung her bag on the coat rack and sat down on the edge of the bed. "I just don't want to get in the way of your studying."

The college entrance exam was almost here, and Meng Wan worried that two of them sharing a room would disrupt her review.

Lin Xi dragged over a chair and sat down. "How would you? If you sleeping could throw off my studying, then I'd be hopeless at concentrating."

"Good, then." Meng Wan smiled. "I'll try to keep quiet. Just pretend I'm not here—study however you like, however late you like. Don't mind me."

"I don't stay up that late anyway." Lin Xi glanced at the clock on the wall. "I'm usually in bed by half past nine."

Over the past six months, Lin Xi had worked out a study method that suited her best. She knew some of her classmates studied late into the night every day, but that approach didn't work for her. She needed a good night's sleep to face the next day's lessons with energy, so she made the most of her daytime hours and turned in early.

"How's the review going lately?" Meng Wan glanced at the stacks of notebooks and exam papers on her desk—one look and you could tell she was a good student.

"Pretty well." Lin Xi reported readily, "On the last mock exam I came third in my class, tenth in the whole grade."

"That's amazing!" Meng Wan had known she was a good student, but not this good. "Just keep that frame of mind. As long as you perform like usual when it counts, you're sure to get into the university you've got your heart set on."

Lin Xi scratched her head. Even with decent mock scores, she still worried. For her, the college entrance exam was the one chance to change her life, and facing something that big, it would be a lie to say she wasn't nervous.

To ease her nerves, Meng Wan pulled her up and spun her around in a little circle. "Sister Wan believes in you. You can do this. Don't put so much pressure on yourself—we're all behind you."

Lin Xi nodded obediently. "Thank you, Sister Wan."

"Dinner's ready!" Hao Xiangping called to the two of them from outside.

"Come on, let's go eat." Meng Wan took Lin Xi's hand and they went out together.

"When does Lin Dong usually get back?" Meng Wan sat down and glanced toward the front door.

"Xiaodong won't be back till seven or eight." Hao Xiangping laid a steaming-hot pancake into Meng Wan's bowl. "Let's eat first—no need to wait for him."

Drinking in the aroma, Meng Wan dug in heartily, praising the food between bites: "A pancake like this belongs in heaven—you'd be lucky to taste its like once in a lifetime down here."

Lin Xi laughed at her and picked up a piece of braised pork, marbled with fat and lean, for her bowl. "Sister Wan, try this. My mom's braised pork is delicious too."

The last few times Meng Wan had visited, there hadn't been any meat to go around. But lately, because of Lin Xi's looming exam, Hao Xiangping made meat for her daughter at nearly every meal to keep her strength up.

Meng Wan went on like that—a bite of pancake, a bite of pork—until her stomach was round and full before she finally set down her chopsticks.

Satisfied. No matter how big the world's troubles, a meal was the biggest thing of all; there was nothing a good meal couldn't fix.

Seeing Meng Wan eat with such relish, Hao Xiangping found herself eating a few extra bites too. Today the three of them cleaned every plate: five pancakes, a dish of braised pork, a plate of vinegar-fried cabbage—all polished off, leaving only the bare plates streaked with sauce on display in the middle of the table.

"Oh!" Meng Wan remembered Lin Dong belatedly. "We didn't save any for Lin Dong."

Clearing away the bowls and chopsticks, Hao Xiangping said with a smile, "I set his aside the moment it came out of the pan. No need to worry about him."

Good. She couldn't let her arrival leave Lin Dong with an empty stomach.

Meng Wan headed off Hao Xiangping when she tried to do the dishes, and after much back-and-forth persuaded her to hand the task over. By the time she'd put the whole kitchen in order, Lin Dong was home too.

The same surprise, the same question. Patiently, Meng Wan explained it all again. Lin Dong watched her expression, then added one more thing: "I don't know what happened between you and your family, but whatever you did, I'm on your side. That's just how Meng Wan is." With that, he grinned foolishly and stuck up a thumb for her.

His simple, earnest manner made Meng Wan laugh. "Eat your food while it's hot. Auntie Hao's pork really is good."

Nights at the Lin home were different from the Meng household. Because Lin Xi was studying, Hao Xiangping and Lin Dong did their best to keep things quiet for her—Lin Dong even watched TV with the sound off.

Meng Wan helped him carry his food to the table and sat to one side, watching him eat.

The kid had to be exhausted lately. Just yesterday he'd finished guiding her tour group with not a single day's rest, and today he'd gone right back out to hustle for fares.

The dark circles under his eyes, the stubble sprouting on his chin, the weariness in his gaze—everything about him spoke of his exhaustion.

"Are you busy next week?"

Chewing on his pancake, Lin Dong rolled his eyes upward and thought it over. "Same as always."

Out every day to pick up passengers, home to eat and sleep—that was his daily routine.

Meng Wan tapped her index finger lightly on the tabletop, her eyes fixed on the square, dark-red chunks of pork on the plate. "Come somewhere with me next week. If you've got time tomorrow, go buy two train tickets to Dongshan Province."

It would give him a chance to rest, too.

"Dongshan Province? What for?"

Meng Wan said mysteriously, "We're going to do something big."

The Dragon Boat Festival was almost here, which meant the real heiress, Zhou Nian, would soon be getting married. Meng Wan had to wreck this marriage before then, or Zhou Nian would step straight into a life of misery.

In her past life, the real heiress had married a local primary-school teacher in the next village over, a man around her own age. Later, because he wouldn't stop drinking, the school dismissed him.

After learning Zhou Nian's true identity, Cheng Xuehua had wanted her to divorce and escape that bitter life, but the man clung to Zhou Nian and refused outright. In the end, Cheng Xuehua had to spend a sizable sum of money to get it done.

In short, the whole ordeal had drained everyone involved. Since Meng Wan had been given another life, she couldn't let Zhou Nian walk that road again.

"Aren't you a local—from Yuyang?" Lin Dong scratched his head, chopsticks still in hand.

Meng Wan looked at Lin Dong, the words rising to her lips and then sinking back down. "You'll find out when we get there."

Lin Dong didn't press. Whatever Meng Wan asked him to do, he'd do; whatever she wanted to accomplish, he'd help her with it.
