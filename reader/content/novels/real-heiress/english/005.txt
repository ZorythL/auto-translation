Chapter 5

The Yuyang of 1995 was still in its early stages of development. The economy may have lagged far behind, but the spirit of the people was soaring; in everyone's eyes shone a light of anticipation for the future.

This was an era in which anyone bold enough to strike out and take action could realize their dreams. And precisely because they believed that effort would surely be rewarded, people held an optimistic view of what lay ahead—however hard life was now, everyone trusted that the days would only get better.

Coming out of the Meng home, Meng Wan wandered aimlessly to Qingyun Tower. As a child she'd often come here with her playmates to play hide-and-seek. Back then there'd been no fence ringing it, and you could go in and out of the tower as you pleased. Now, fenced off, it had become a tourist attraction, and visitors came especially to see it.

Hands stuffed in her pockets, she kicked listlessly at a pebble, her head lolling this way and that—until, all at once, her gaze was drawn to a man standing under a tree not far off.

The man stood in profile to Meng Wan, dressed in a black overcoat over black suit trousers. The coat fell to his knees, leaving only half of his long legs visible; Meng Wan figured that if she wore a coat like that, it would reach her ankles.

She shifted her gaze to study the man's profile. Even from some distance she could clearly make out the high, knife-carved bridge of his nose—no exaggeration, it was steep enough to slide down like a playground slide.

At the moment he was fiddling with a map in his hand, comparing it, much perplexed, against the scene before him.

One look and Meng Wan could tell he was a tourist. As it happened, she had nothing to do just then, so she might as well do a good deed and show him around—absolutely not because the man was handsome.

"Hello." Meng Wan bounded over in a few skips and greeted him politely. "Do you need any help?"

The man turned his head at the sound, and the instant that face came into view, Meng Wan's heart skipped a beat.

No wonder she'd felt something was different—he was a foreigner. It was her first time admiring a handsome foreigner up close like this, and it really was a feast for the eyes.

Worried he might not understand Mandarin, Meng Wan rapidly dredged up the English words she remembered. But before she could get a single one out, the man opened his mouth—and out came fluent Mandarin.

"Thank you. I'd like to know, is this the Langya Pavilion? Why doesn't it match the spot marked on the map?"

Meng Wan went up on her toes to peek at the map in his hand. It was a decade-old edition, drawn by Meng Xingyang's work unit. The techniques back then weren't sophisticated, and on top of that, ten years of demolition and construction in the area had long since rendered the map useless.

"This map's out of date. The Langya Pavilion is still a few hundred meters from here."

Understanding dawned on the man. "No wonder."

Meng Wan clasped her hands behind her back and tilted her chin up at him smugly, hinting, "I grew up here. I know these streets better than anyone."

Though she had it in mind to be his guide, what if he didn't need one and just wanted to wander on his own? Then wouldn't she lose face? So she'd say only this much—if he had any sense, he'd know how to respond.

The man's lips curved. His deep eyes fixed thoughtfully on Meng Wan for two full seconds, then he drew a black leather wallet from his coat, pinched out a fifty-yuan note with his long fingers, rested the hand holding the money against the wrist of the hand holding the wallet, and offered the bill to her with both hands.

"Is this enough?"

Meng Wan stared at the money, her expression freezing for a moment. He'd taken her for one of those tout guides looking to pick up clients?

She'd only meant to do a good turn, to show an international friend the warm and hospitable spirit of the Yuyang people—but since he was willing to pay, it would be rude not to accept. She mustn't leave a foreign friend with a bad impression.

Mistaking her hesitation for the money being too little, the man pulled another ten-yuan note from his wallet. "Is this enough?"

Meng Wan pushed the ten back and took only the fifty, then rubbed the crisp new bill between her fingers with a cheerful grin. One look told her it was fresh from the bank.

She asked, beaming, "And what should I call you?"

Seeing her take the money, the man returned the wallet whence it came and introduced himself in standard Mandarin. "My Chinese name is Chen Jinghua, and my English name is James. I'm of mixed parentage—my Mandarin may not be very good, so please forgive me."

Meng Wan prided herself on having met plenty of men, but never one of James's variety.

She used to think her ex-husband, Xu Tian, was the model of a good man. He could always coax her into a dizzy delight with a mouthful of sweet nothings, yielding to her in everything to keep her happy—and yet a man like that had cast her off without a second thought the moment she had no value left.

Then there were men like Song Qingyang—educated, holding office, flawless in every dealing, leaving not a fault to be picked at, the kind of junior the elders adored. But Meng Wan disliked people she couldn't see through.

The man before her had exchanged only a few words with her, yet from his bearing and manner alone you could sense his real cultivation—and his lack of arrogance. Those two qualities were already enough to outclass ninety percent of all men.

Meng Wan blinked, her eyes brimming with mirth, and introduced herself. "Chen—"

She gazed into the man's deep eyes, at that face full of exotic flavor, and simply couldn't bring herself to say the name "Chen Jinghua."

"—James, hello. I'm Meng Wan, a born-and-bred local. Welcome to Yuyang. I hope you have a pleasant travel experience here."

That said, Meng Wan politely extended her hand, wanting to shake hands with the guest who'd come from afar.

Feeling that his fingers were a touch cold, James rubbed his hands together for a moment before placing one in Meng Wan's, the other steadying his own wrist, and the two shook hands amicably.

Since she'd taken his money, Meng Wan's sense of duty swelled. She was no tour guide, but these were places she'd frequented as a child, and every time they went, her father would explain it all to her.

The Langya Pavilion James wanted to see displayed the masterworks of famous calligraphers. Meng Qingyan loved calligraphy; every visit, he would expound on it to Meng Wan with passionate fervor. Having heard it so many times, she'd naturally committed it to memory.

Meng Wan brought James to the Langya Pavilion. The auntie selling tickets recognized her. Meng Wan pointed inside and gave the woman a wheedling, coaxing look, and the auntie simply waved her on, signaling she needn't buy a ticket—just go on in, quick.

Meng Wan bobbed a grateful little bow to the auntie, then, seeing someone coming up behind her, grabbed James and darted inside.

James watched this whole sequence of operations, mystified—and only once he'd been pulled through the gate did he confirm it: he'd just dodged the admission fee?

So this was what his mother meant by Yuyang being a society that ran on personal connections. He liked a society that ran on connections.

There weren't many people inside, just a tour group of a dozen or so following a guide. Meng Wan led James along in chronological, historical order—the same order in which Meng Qingyan had shown her around. She had to follow that exact order to recall the explanations, because her memory ran in sequence, like reciting classical poetry: give her the first line and she could instantly produce the next, but give her the second and it was hard to call up the first.

Mimicking Meng Qingyan's intonation, Meng Wan launched into her warm, spirited account, going in order—dynasty, author, style, school...

From start to finish James wore an expression of attentive listening, now and then breaking into a look of dawning comprehension, and even posing insightful questions based on his own understanding, just like an earnest student in class. Meng Wan found it thoroughly gratifying, as though her labor were being honored.

A wave of smugness rose in her chest, and the more pleased Meng Wan grew, the louder she spoke. When she finished and turned around, she discovered a crowd of tourists standing behind her—the very tour group from before, all of them quietly listening to her commentary.

Meng Wan stood there blankly and glanced at James, not knowing what had happened.

James bent his head to her ear and, still keeping a social distance, murmured softly, "They were all drawn over by your passionate narration."

Meng Wan was overwhelmed by the honor. Her ear tickled, but she didn't dare move. She glanced at the few remaining scrolls of calligraphy in the gallery and wanted to introduce them all for everyone—but right now she was the guide James had paid to hire, so she still had to get his consent.

"James." She turned to look at him and asked under her breath, "Would you mind listening along with everyone?"

"It would be my honor." James smiled and made a courteous "after you" gesture.

With her employer's blessing, Meng Wan spoke even more freely, eager to take every last morsel of knowledge in her belly—fried, stir-fried, deep-fried, every which way—and serve it up for them to taste.

Sure enough, no part of a life ever goes to waste. There were things you never knew when they might come in handy.

By the end of the whole tour, Meng Wan's mouth was parched. The two of them came out of the Langya Pavilion, and Meng Wan kept glancing around, hoping to find a little shop to buy something to quench her thirst, when the man beside her spoke up.

"Wait here for me a moment." With that, James walked off toward the other side.

Meng Wan thought nothing of it, assuming he'd gone to the restroom; she reckoned the public toilet was some distance off. She braced herself against a willow tree by the path, freshly budding with new green, and began to stretch.

In her old age her back had given her trouble, and she'd always needed to stretch now and then, so she'd fallen into the habit of stretching anytime, anywhere. Though her body was young again now, the habit was hard to shake.

With both hands against the trunk, Meng Wan bowed ninety degrees toward the tree, her whole body forming a right angle, her back fully extended.

Bliss, Meng Wan thought, privately content.

"Here you go."

A clear, bright voice suddenly sounded by her ear. Meng Wan's eyes flew open to find a Coca-Cola, straw already in it, materializing right before her.

Meng Wan turned her head. The drink's owner had bent down in imitation of her, holding himself at her same height, that innocent, guileless face smiling at her. Her heart skipped a beat from the emptiness of it, and because her breathing had gone ragged, her whole face flushed crimson. She stammered, "You—you—how are you back so fast?"

"I only went to buy a drink. How long could that take?" James grinned, baring eight clean white teeth. "Why don't we switch to a different posture to talk?"

Only then did Meng Wan realize she was still holding that "bizarre" pose. She straightened up in a flash, awkward heat pouring off her whole body, her throat nearly dry enough to smoke. She snatched the Coke from James's hand and sucked at the straw, gulping down the cool soda in great loud swallows.

The liquid soothed her parched mouth, flowing down her throat and through her whole body, calming her considerably. Her face wasn't quite so scorching anymore.

"Thanks." She lifted the empty bottle. "Where'd you buy it? You can return the bottle and get the deposit back."

She really had been thirsty. James watched her, his eyes full of mirth. "Just around that corner back there."

James went to return the bottle, and Meng Wan stood behind him with her hands in her pockets, waiting.

Beep beep beep, beep beep beep.

Meng Wan jumped at the sound. For a second she couldn't place what it was, only that it was coming from somewhere on her person. She patted herself down frantically, and finally, in her trouser pocket, found the pager still beeping away.

The screen read, "Saw your message, please call back." Meng Wan stared at the string of all-too-familiar digits that followed, her brow furrowing ever deeper.

It was that shameless ex-husband of hers.

Only then did it come back to Meng Wan—around this time, she'd already begun seeing that man.

How unlucky. Flustered, she couldn't figure out how to silence the thing, so she simply popped off the back cover and pried out the battery. In an instant her ears were at peace.

Assuming she had something to attend to, James suggested, "There's a phone over here. You could call back."

Meng Wan tucked the pager away and shook her head. "It's not important. Let's go."

"Where to next?"

Meng Wan fingered the fifty yuan James had given her in her pocket. She wanted to head to the department store first and buy the Barbie doll—she was afraid that if she went too late, the little tragedy of her past life would replay itself. Just picturing Zhizhi's scrunched-up little face bawling its heart out made her ache.

She lifted her wrist and checked her watch. "The department store!"
