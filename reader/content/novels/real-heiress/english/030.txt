Chapter 30

Meng Wan came home to the Meng household in her new dress, braced to face an interrogation from Meng Qingyan and Cheng Xuehua.

She'd expected the house to be heavy with tension, the family ready to put her on trial. But to her surprise, the moment she walked in she heard Zhizhi and Meng Xingyang giggling together. Sun Jing and Cheng Xuehua were busy with dinner in the kitchen, while Meng Qingyan sat off to one side grading his students' homework.

The whole family was warm and at ease, with no sign whatsoever of any intent to judge her. Could it be that Cheng Xuehua hadn't told anyone about what happened?

"Auntie!" The moment Zhizhi saw Meng Wan come home, she ran over waving her arms and threw them around her. "Auntie, your dress is so pretty."

Meng Xingyang and Meng Qingyan both turned to look. Meng Xingyang teased, "Our Miss Meng's been out buying clothes again."

Meng Qingyan picked up the pen cap by his hand and flicked it across the room—it caught Meng Xingyang square in the forehead.

Meng Xingyang yelped in pain. "Ow!"

Meng Qingyan reproached him, stern-faced. "What's all this 'Miss Meng' business? That's a title for capitalists. Your father here is a proper, upstanding member of the proletariat!"

"I'm sorry, I'm sorry." Meng Xingyang rubbed his forehead, looking thoroughly chastened. "Can't a man even make a joke?"

Meng Qingyan said crossly, "You're a grown man—can't you weigh for yourself which jokes can be made and which can't? Is this how you joke around at your work unit too?"

Meng Xingyang muttered under his breath, "It's only at home, though."

"That's enough, Dad." Meng Wan scooped Zhizhi up into her arms. "My brother knows his limits—he won't go talking nonsense outside."

Meng Xingyang pursed his lips and nodded along with her. "Exactly."

Meng Wan picked up the pen cap, then sat down in the middle of the sofa with Zhizhi, blocking the line of sight between father and son. Her eyes drifted toward the kitchen. "When did Mom get back?"

Meng Xingyang raked his fingers through his hair a couple of times, irritated. "Didn't Mom go out with you? Did the two of you have a fight?"

"Ah!"

Another yell from Meng Xingyang—a chopstick smacked the back of his head with a crack and then dropped onto the sofa.

Cheng Xuehua was just coming out of the kitchen with bowls and chopsticks. She glared at Meng Xingyang and snapped, "Can't you ever wish us well for once? Always saying things that stir up trouble in this family."

"I—"

"Haha, Daddy got told off by Grandma." Zhizhi clapped her hands gleefully—that was for Daddy refusing to buy her candy earlier.

Sun Jing tipped her head and shot Zhizhi a look. Little miss, don't you go adding fuel to the fire.

Today Sun Jing hadn't taken the child back to her mother's place, because her mother-in-law was going out with her sister-in-law, so she'd stayed behind to cook for the whole household. She'd assumed they'd be out a good while—it was a matchmaking occasion, after all, the sort of thing that should take two or three hours at least.

But who'd have thought they'd barely finished lunch before Cheng Xuehua came home alone, not saying a word, heading straight into the kitchen to eat two steamed buns in silence before shutting herself in the bedroom—not emerging again until it was time to make dinner.

Sun Jing guessed the matchmaking must not have gone smoothly. But even if it hadn't, surely it wouldn't have ended before they'd so much as eaten. It must have been her sister-in-law stirring up some new trouble again. That sister-in-law of hers was never one to make life easy.

Meng Wan stole a slightly guilty glance at Cheng Xuehua. By the looks of it, she hadn't told anyone about the resignation. There was no hiding it any longer, though—Meng Wan resolved to make a formal announcement to the whole family at the dinner table later.

"Dinner's ready." Sun Jing's eyes traveled between Meng Wan and Cheng Xuehua. From the atmosphere alone she could tell something was wrong.

Meng Wan picked up Zhizhi and set her down beside Sun Jing, then took the seat below Cheng Xuehua's.

With something weighing on her mind, Meng Wan ate distractedly through the meal, picking at a few mouthfuls of rice before setting down her chopsticks.

Seeing everyone nearly finished, she cleared her throat and prepared to speak.

"Dad, Mom, Brother, Sister-in-law—and Zhizhi too."

The others set down their chopsticks and looked at her, baffled, waiting for what came next. Only Cheng Xuehua, as if she already knew what was coming, kept her head down, picking the bones out of the fish in her bowl.

Meng Wan glanced at the top of Cheng Xuehua's head and stammered out, "I... quit my job."

"You what?" Meng Qingyan looked at his daughter, stunned.

Meng Wan decided she might as well let the broken pot smash. She shot to her feet and announced loudly, "I said—I quit my job!"

Sun Jing turned and exchanged a silent glance with Meng Xingyang. The young couple wisely held their tongues; this was not something they could afford to wade into.

Seeing her mother-in-law show no reaction at all, Sun Jing instantly pieced it all together. Cheng Xuehua must have come home early precisely because of this, and she'd been keeping a stony face ever since.

"Your reason!" Meng Qingyan's face was dark, the fury rising hot in his chest. He'd had to swallow his pride and ask his student's father for that job—it had taken no small effort to arrange it. And here she was, quitting after less than half a year. Whose face did she think she was slapping?

"I want to make money!" Meng Wan gave her answer without the slightest concealment.

Meng Qingyan couldn't accept this reason. "Has this family ever let you go hungry or without clothes? We've never once asked where your wages went. Whatever you want, we do our utmost to give you. What more is there to be unhappy about?"

"But I can't depend on you for my whole life!" Meng Wan's eyes reddened, her lips trembling without pause.

She wasn't their biological daughter. She couldn't rely on them forever. Once they learned her true identity, they wouldn't indulge her the way they would a child of their own blood. Sooner or later she was bound to leave this family.

At those words, everyone fell silent. No one could lean on their parents a whole lifetime; Meng Qingyan knew that as well as anyone. But he still couldn't accept his daughter's reason—the job paid well enough to live on, so why quit without breathing a word?

"Then what do you plan to do from now on?"

Making money was hardly so easy. Did quitting on a hot-headed impulse mean the money would come rolling in the very next moment?

Meng Wan paused, then answered, "I've become a tour guide."

Meng Xingyang said in astonishment, "A tour guide?"

He could never have imagined Meng Wan going into the service industry for money. Their idle, pampered Miss Meng, waiting on other people? It was beyond fantastical. Was this still his sister Meng Wan?

Meng Qingyan looked rather calmer than Meng Xingyang. "Since when?"

"A month or so now," Meng Wan admitted. "I got my tour guide license while I was still working at the cultural center, and only then did I quit and apply for the guiding job. This month I've already led two groups and made over a thousand yuan."

At the mention of money, Sun Jing's eyes lit up. No wonder people said tour guides made good money. She slaved away teaching four or five classes a day, and a whole month came to less than eight hundred. Meng Wan had led two groups and pulled in over a thousand—if she led more, wouldn't she make tens of thousands in a year? Just thinking about it set her heart racing.

Only now did Cheng Xuehua lift her head, looking at her daughter's stubborn face. "I don't agree. Go and quit the tour guide job. You'll come work in logistics at our hospital."

"Mom!" Meng Wan stared at Cheng Xuehua in disbelief. The last person she'd expected to stand in her way was her. "Why?"

Cheng Xuehua slapped her chopsticks down on the table with a crack and said sharply, "Look at you these past weeks—out before dawn, back after dark, busy day and night, never home. Who's going to want to marry a puff of empty air?"

Earlier, the moment Song Qingyang's mother heard Meng Wan was a tour guide, she'd dragged Song Qingyang off without even finishing the meal, claiming she felt a little unwell. Cheng Xuehua didn't have to think twice to know it was Meng Wan's new job that had displeased her—the woman simply hadn't wanted to embarrass Cheng Xuehua, so she'd grabbed at any excuse to leave.

So that was the reason. Meng Wan shot back, "But this is my life. I wasn't born just to be married off to somebody. If a man can't accept my profession, then he doesn't deserve to be with me!"

"You want to do it, fine." Cheng Xuehua lifted her face toward Meng Wan and, in a flat voice, delivered her threat. "Then move out of this house."

"Mom!"

Meng Xingyang knew Cheng Xuehua was speaking in anger and couldn't help stepping in to mediate. It was only a job—surely it didn't warrant throwing her out of the house.

Sun Jing's heart clenched too. She'd never have imagined her mother-in-law, who had always doted on her sister-in-law, would lose her temper so badly this time. And that sister-in-law of hers had a stubborn streak—she might actually take it to heart.

Though Meng Qingyan was furious that his daughter had taken this whole string of actions without telling them in advance, he wasn't ready to drive her out of the house. He didn't much mind her being a tour guide; knowing his daughter as he did, he figured this was just another of her three-minute fevers. Once the novelty wore off, in a few days she'd be crying and wailing that guiding was too exhausting and begging to switch to something easier.

In that instant the scenes of her past life and this one overlapped, the words "move out of this house" echoing again and again in Meng Wan's ears.

All the memories she'd been running from came flooding into her heart at once, like a waterfall. In her last life, after her divorce, she'd had nowhere to go, and in the end had come back to the Meng household—only by then the real heiress had already come to claim her place, and Meng Wan had become an outsider in this family.

Watching them all finally reunited as a family, it was as though the years she and the Mengs had spent together had never existed at all. To let their biological daughter feel a love that was hers alone, they had begun, deliberately, to keep Meng Wan at arm's length.

One day she simply couldn't bear it any longer—watching the Meng family fuss over their real daughter right in front of her, all that tender warmth and doting attention. She'd smashed a vase in front of everyone and made a tremendous scene, and the outcome she'd earned for it was, once again, Cheng Xuehua's "move out of this house."

Meng Wan knew that what she'd done had been anything but level-headed. But by then she'd been past caring about reason. For thirty years she'd called them Dad and Mom and Brother, and the instant they learned she wasn't their flesh and blood, within the span of a few short days their attitude toward her had spun a full one hundred and eighty degrees, their eyes filling with cold distance whenever they looked at her.

It was as if someone who'd spent her whole life in the tropics had suddenly been flung to the Arctic—the gulf in temperature between the two was something only she could truly feel.

Meng Wan didn't understand what she'd done wrong. Wasn't she a victim of this whole affair as much as anyone? She consoled herself: she'd usurped someone else's thirty years of a rich, happy life, so she'd just have to spend the rest of her own atoning for it.

Later, unable to endure the crushing gap, she'd picked fights with the real heiress more than once, openly and in secret. When the Meng family found out, they'd dressed her down to her face, and the look in their eyes had been full of disappointment.

To this day Meng Wan still remembered that expression—it filled her with boundless guilt and boundless resentment all at once. She knew then that she had lost them forever.

The revelation of her origins, the wreck of her marriage, the abandonment by her own family—one thing after another had ground away at Meng Wan, sinking her into those torments until she could no longer climb out, until in the end she'd wasted away of depression.

This time, she would no longer let anyone treat her so carelessly. She, too, was her own treasure. Even if no one else cherished her, she would cradle herself in the palm of her own hand and enshrine herself like an idol in a temple.

"Then I'll move out of this house today."
