Chapter 69

Meng Wan followed the police comrade to the station, where the officer skillfully and efficiently questioned the whole story out of them, start to finish.

According to the two men's confessions, they weren't brothers at all but fellow villagers and workmates. The short man was called Qiangzi, and the one who'd played dead on the floor was Lin Dashu. They'd come into the city from the countryside to find work, doing hard manual labor on a construction site—hauling bricks and carting sand. When the job ended, however, they never received their wages; the labor contractor had withheld half their final pay. They'd gone back to demand it several times to no avail, but they didn't want to go home completely empty-handed, so they'd hit on this scheme that was no scheme at all: swindling money by extortion.

Seeing that Meng Wan's place did good business and was run by a woman, they figured they'd shake her down with a "tainted food" story, take the money, and buy train tickets home that very moment—which was why they'd spent every last cent on them on that one meal.

Hearing what they'd been through, Meng Wan felt a stir of sympathy. But no matter how hard life got, a person had to keep certain lines—swindling people was always wrong.

Since no losses had been incurred, and given how willingly they'd admitted their fault, Meng Wan agreed to the settlement the officer proposed and signed a letter of forgiveness.

The officer gave them a stern verbal warning, telling them to collect their wages and get home quick, and the two nodded again and again in agreement.

But everyone knew those wages would be hard to wring out. The two men didn't even have the price of a train ticket home on them now.

The officer dug into his own pocket and gave them a hundred yuan, telling them to get a meal and buy their tickets home first—hanging around here unable to collect their money was no solution. The two took the money and thanked the officer over and over, bowing without pause, and the moment they'd finished bowing to him they turned to Meng Wan with a mournful string of apologies, all but dropping to their knees before her.

"All right, all right, off you go. Don't do anything foolish again." The officer spoke with a stern face but sympathetic words—a classic case of a cold face hiding a warm heart.

Meng Wan followed the two to the station gate and called out to them. "Where are you two planning to go next?"

The two stopped and exchanged glances. Lin Dashu—the one who'd played dead, the dried toothpaste foam still hanging at the corner of his mouth like a check mark across his right cheek—said somewhat dimly, "We'll head back to the site, see if the contractor's around. If not, we'll camp outside his house. If we can't get the money, we'll lie down in his doorway and not budge."

"Not budge!" Qiangzi chimed in.

That was no solution either. If, in a moment of recklessness, they ended up in a fight, hands didn't always know their own strength—and then it wouldn't be a matter of getting a few words of correction from the police. Everyone was just trying to make a living; it wasn't easy for any of them.

"How about this." After turning it over a few times, Meng Wan made a proposal. "Come work as waiters at my restaurant. I'll pay you wages, room and board included. You can leave whenever you finally collect your pay—or just leave whenever you like."

In any case, Sun Jing had mentioned a few days back that the restaurant was short on waitstaff, and Meng Wan had been on the lookout these last few days. Looking at the two of them, strong and sturdy, waiting tables shouldn't be a problem.

For a moment the two couldn't believe their ears. They'd only just thrown a huge scene in this woman's restaurant, and she'd turned right around and extended a helping hand—they really weren't fit to be called human!

"Miss, are you serious?" Lin Dashu's eyes betrayed some disbelief. "We almost swindled you just now. You're not angry with us?"

"Angry—of course I'm angry." Meng Wan made no effort to hide her feelings. "But being angry is one thing. Look at the reckless state the two of you are in—how could I rest easy letting you go? If you got into a fight and something happened, how would you ever get home? What would become of your families?"

Sometimes the choice between good and bad turned on a single thought. If, at that fork in the road, someone could reach out and pull a person back, the outcome might be entirely different. Meng Wan only wanted to give them a hand where she could—and the restaurant happened to be short a few waiters anyway, so call it doing a good turn that cost her nothing.

Hearing Meng Wan's words, Lin Dashu was struck speechless for a moment. He blinked rapidly, forcing the tears welling in his eyes back down, and his voice choked. "Thank you. You're a good person."

There were still good people in this city after all—not everyone was like that black-hearted contractor Huang Datou.

"One thing at a time, though." Meng Wan shifted her gaze, turning her head toward Qiangzi. "This grudge I absolutely have to settle today!"

And with that, Meng Wan delivered a sharp knuckle-rap to Qiangzi's forehead.

Qiangzi clapped a hand to his forehead and yelped. Seeing Meng Wan still watching for an opening to strike again, he took fright and bolted.

"Don't run!" Meng Wan tore after him for dear life. "You dare swindle me? I think you've got a death wish!"

The emotion Lin Dashu had just been working up was cut clean off by this "manhunt" of a scene. Inwardly he sighed with relief—thank goodness he'd been assigned to play the man on the floor and hadn't gone head-to-head with Meng Wan, or he'd have been the one taking the beating.

Poor Qiangzi—short-legged to begin with—was caught within a few strides.

James's hotel completed construction smoothly, and on the auspicious day the master had calculated, its grand opening arrived.

The hotel was named Yuyin Lodge—"the lodge of seclusion amid the bustle"—the meaning being a quiet refuge within the clamor, in the hope that every guest who came would pass a stretch of peaceful, leisurely time.

As the head of Youth Travel, and as James's girlfriend, Meng Wan attended the ribbon-cutting. And it wasn't only her agency in attendance; James had invited the heads of nearly every travel agency in the city to appear—Bai Yuexi, Qian Zhenyu, Qin Lan, all the people Meng Wan knew.

The night before the opening, James had taken Meng Wan on an early tour of the hotel. In her past life this site had been bought by Xu Tian, who had dredged up some designer who'd studied abroad somewhere, and the hotel that came out of it was neither Eastern nor Western—utterly without character.

James, by contrast, had used a thoroughly Chinese design throughout, reflecting the deep historical and cultural heritage of Yuyang as an ancient capital of more than a dozen dynasties.

The moment you stepped into the lobby, there was a Night Revels painting on the wall, and around the pillar at the center of the lobby coiled a dragon motif. The lobby was set with a large folding screen that displayed the elegance and refinement of Yuyang's history through lacquer painting and wood carving.

The guest rooms had the grace of a lady's chamber. Rather than the heavy red-rosewood tones of traditional Chinese style, they used refined hues—bean-paste green, lotus-root lilac, sky-clearing blue—paired with rattan weave and cotton-linen materials.

James had reserved a top-floor suite for Meng Wan, a room that was hers alone. It had a floor-to-ceiling window that looked out over the whole of Yuyang's outskirts—a winding river and a vast stretch of lush green woodland, the view wide open.

Kevin and James's other relatives had arrived in Yuyang once again a few days earlier and checked straight into James's hotel as its very first guests. That night Meng Wan stayed comfortably in the hotel as its second batch of guests, lying on that quiet, elegantly understated wooden bed and sinking into a deep sleep.

Meng Wan had, in a sense, witnessed the growth of this hotel—from the first negotiations to the finished building—and inside it there was now a room that belonged to her alone. The feeling was beyond words.

Thinking about it now, the fate that bound her and James really was extraordinary: one had brought her into the trade, the other had helped him secure the land. Neither could do without the other.

Per the contract he'd signed, James had hired local young people to work at the hotel, and before the official opening he'd brought in a professional hotel manager to train them. On opening day, every one of those handsome lads and pretty girls was the picture of energy—standing tall and smiling, dressed in new-style uniforms, beaming at everyone, their warm, fiery service putting every other hotel in Yuyang to shame.

In all fairness, when prices were roughly the same, of course tourists would choose higher-quality service—who'd want to pay good money to suffer?

The agency people knew about Meng Wan and James's relationship, but they hadn't realized James was the one running this hotel. They'd hoped to get closer to him and perhaps work together, but the best water doesn't flow to other people's fields—naturally his guests would go to his own girlfriend first. Even so, the connection to James had to be maintained. Anyone with eyes could see this hotel's business was sure to do well, and on top of that, James was a foreigner who surely had no shortage of foreign contacts. Meng Wan couldn't possibly receive all of them, so some would inevitably be sent to the other agencies.

Now that the domestic peak season had passed, every agency was straining to compete for the limited pool of tourists. Fair competition was fine; what everyone feared most was someone wrecking the rules of the market for their own private gain.

Luo Zhigang and Jiang Ke had complained to Meng Wan more than once that, lately, several of the larger agencies were waging price wars, leaving their agency with hardly any business—every time they landed a group, someone snatched it away with a lower price.

Luo Zhigang had gone forward to argue it out with them a few times, but the moment the tourists heard the lower price they insisted on going with the others, and he couldn't very well drag them back by force. He could only watch helplessly as the tourists were stolen away.

So Meng Wan wanted to seize this opportunity to have a word with Bai Yuexi, the man at the top, and hear what he had to say.

After the ribbon-cutting, everyone was touring the hotel.

"Manager Bai, I'd like a few words with you." Meng Wan threaded her way through the crowd to his side.

At her words, Bai Yuexi cast about for a moment, spotted an unoccupied table and chairs in a corner of the lobby suitable for talking, lifted his chin toward it, and said, "Let's talk over there."

The two sat down, and Meng Wan didn't beat around the bush in the slightest, going straight to the point. "Manager Bai, you're aware of the situation in Yuyang lately, aren't you—guides slashing prices and poaching customers all over the place?"

Bai Yuexi's pupils widened slightly. He leaned back in his seat and said, unhurried, "Is that so? There's such a thing going on?"

From his reaction Meng Wan understood at once: he knew. And since he wasn't saying so outright, there was no need for her to expose it. "Of course there is. It's the agency of Manager Qian Zhenyu—their guides keep saying they'll grab all the customers even if it means not turning a profit. Isn't that wrecking the market? This kind of slay-a-thousand-enemies-at-a-cost-of-eight-hundred-of-your-own behavior—you really ought to do something about it."

Bai Yuexi wore the air of a man to whom none of this concerned. "Manager Meng, you've come to the wrong person," he said casually. "I'm not a government official. How could I control how other people run their business?"
