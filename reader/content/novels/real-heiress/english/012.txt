Chapter 12

The moment Meng Wan's words rang out, every gaze inside and outside the courtyard fixed on her, and people murmured to one another, wondering who this unfamiliar girl was.

Meng Wan looked over at Lin Dong. His knuckles were still oozing blood, and watching the crimson trickle down the wall, she cursed him inwardly for the fool he was.

Lin Dong stood frozen, not moving a muscle, utterly at a loss over what was happening before him.

Lin Xi's aunt, Lin Qiaoyun, was the first to recover. She tipped her head back and looked Meng Wan up and down. "Who are you? What's any of this got to do with you?"

Meng Wan regarded the middle-aged woman—of medium height and slightly plump, with a round face topped by a head of frizzy curls that made her face look all the broader.

"You must be Lin Dong's aunt. I'm a friend of his."

Half believing her, Lin Qiaoyun glanced at her nephew, and seeing he didn't object, figured they must indeed be acquainted. "Even if you are Xiaodong's friend, his family's business is none of yours."

Some friend—barging into the house to make trouble for her.

Meng Wan smiled sweetly. "Isn't helping each other out exactly what friends are for?"

Lin Xinqiang sneered scornfully. "You? What could you possibly help with?"

The Lin family's troubles weren't only about money—there was his sister-in-law's eye disease too. Could she help with that? And even if she helped once, was she going to help for a lifetime? Who would keep pouring money into a bottomless pit?

Meng Wan's voice was steady and firm. "I can pay for Lin Xi's schooling."

Lin Xi's tears stopped. She gazed at the girl before her, scarcely older than herself, hardly able to believe her own ears. This person she'd never met before said she'd pay for her education?

Unreal as it was, a faint flicker of hope rose in her all the same.

Lin Qiaoyun gave a contemptuous snort. "Big talk for a little girl. Even if you cover the tuition, can you cure my sister-in-law's illness?"

This was the fatal blow aimed straight at Lin Xi's defenses. Lin Qiaoyun knew full well that Lin Xi was a deeply filial child—the easiest weakness to press.

Lin Xinqiang had nearly been thrown off by Meng Wan, but hearing his sister's words he recovered his confidence. Little Li's parents were both doctors at First Hospital. Even if this girl had money, could it compare to a doctor's connections?

A cocky little smirk tugged at Meng Wan's lips. Hands clasped behind her back, she circled once around Lin Qiaoyun. "Sorry to say, I may not have much going for me myself, but I do have an excellent mother."

Lin Qiaoyun rolled her eyes at Meng Wan in disgust and stepped back a pace. "Don't tell me your mother's a doctor too?"

"Congratulations—you guessed it." Meng Wan announced loudly, "My mother is the deputy director of First Hospital."

Before anyone could take this in, the young man surnamed Li who'd come to meet Lin Xi started pointing at Meng Wan and going "Mmh, mmh."

Meng Wan turned to look at him. He seemed faintly familiar, and she searched her memory for that face. Halfway through, listening to his "mmh, mmh," she slowly realized—he couldn't speak!

It came back to her now: Director Bai of the anesthesiology department had a son who was mute. She'd seen him at the hospital back in high school.

No wonder. That explained everything.

Lin Xinqiang knit his brows and soothed the suddenly agitated Li Feng. "Little Li, don't get worked up—write down what you want to say."

Li Feng took out the notebook and pen he carried, scribbled out a line, and held it up for them to read.

Meng Wan glanced over. It said: I know her. She's the daughter of the deputy director at my mom's hospital.

Lin Qiaoyun and Lin Xinqiang, who hadn't believed it before, read this and deflated on the spot. Success had been within their grasp—and now, halfway through, along came this spoiler.

"There—now you can set your minds at ease, can't you?" Meng Wan glanced again at the dumbstruck Lin Dong and Lin Xi. "I'll pay Lin Xi's tuition, I'll find someone to treat Lin Dong's mother, and as for the money you're owed, I trust Lin Dong can pay you back soon enough too."

Only then did Lin Dong come back to himself. He fished out of his pocket the fare James had settled with him the day before—one thousand and fifty yuan in all—split it into two, and pressed the halves into his aunt's and uncle's hands.

"The rest I'll have for you next month."

"No need to wait until next month." Meng Wan put her own money into Lin Dong's hand. "Count out whatever you owe them from this."

Lin Dong stared blankly at the stack of crisp bills in his hand. This too was the fare James had paid her—how could he have the face to use her money?

Seeing him hesitate, Meng Wan urged him in a low voice, "This money's a loan, not a gift. Better to owe me than to owe them, isn't it?"

Lin Dong lowered his head, and his trembling hands quietly began to move, counting out the five hundred still owed. "Paid in full. We're square from now on. Auntie, Uncle, don't come around here again—this household doesn't welcome you."

Lin Qiaoyun clutched the money tight, her eyes bulging as if they'd pop from their sockets, and she railed at him furiously. "You ungrateful little brat! If your uncle and I hadn't lent you the money back then, could your mother have afforded treatment at all? We set your sister up with a match for your own good, and now you team up with an outsider to throw it in our faces—you've well and truly lost your senses!"

Lin Dong could endure no more. His face turned fierce as he shouted back, "Can you swear you and Uncle wouldn't have pocketed a cut if it had gone through? Can you swear it before my father's spirit in heaven?"

Lin Qiaoyun was choked into silence, grinding her teeth in anger. "So what if we took a little something? Matchmakers don't work for free either—and the bulk of it would still have ended up in your hands."

Seeing his sister losing ground, Lin Xinqiang jumped in at once. "A young man as well-set-up as Little Li, willing to take Lin Xi—you should be burning incense in thanks. With your family's circumstances, who else would even look at her?"

Lin Dong let out a cold scoff. "If he's such a good catch, why not introduce him to your own Xiaoxiao?"

Xiaoxiao was Lin Xinqiang's younger daughter.

Lin Xinqiang was struck dumb. Their Xiaoxiao was going to sit the college entrance exam—she'd have her pick of fine men down the road.

"You just wait and regret it!"

With that, Lin Xinqiang grabbed Li Feng and made to leave.

Li Feng looked back at Lin Xi, reluctant, going "mmh" and refusing to budge, while Lin Xi turned her face away, unwilling to look at him.

"Stop staring!" Lin Xinqiang snapped, exasperated. "She doesn't want you."

And with that he hauled the man bodily out.

Before she left, Lin Qiaoyun shot Meng Wan one more vicious glare. If this girl hadn't barged in and stuck her oar in, the whole thing would have been settled today. Thinking of the five hundred yuan that had just flown out of her grasp, she ached with such regret she could have slapped her.

The neighbors in the courtyard had all been holding their breath for Lin Xi. She was the brightest, most obedient and dutiful child in the whole compound; for her to drop out and marry just like that would have been a real shame. The Lins were all good people—but all of them ill-fated.

Seeing everyone disperse, Meng Wan stepped forward of her own accord to greet Hao Xiangping and Lin Xi. "Hello, Auntie. My name's Meng Wan, I'm a friend of Lin Dong's. He was supposed to bring me here today to eat the scallion pancakes you make, but we ran into this nasty business instead. I can't stand to see a girl bullied, so I went and stuck my nose into something that wasn't mine to meddle in. I've caused you trouble—please bear with me."

The white-haired woman leaned her head forward, straining in vain to make out who was speaking. Groping toward the sound, she patted the air in front of Meng Wan, trying to catch hold of her hand.

Seeing this, Meng Wan immediately held her hand out and clasped the woman's in return. "Auntie, I'm right here."

The woman said nothing, only gripped Meng Wan's hands tight and began to choke up—a small, stifled sob at first that swelled into a heartrending wail.

The whole courtyard fell utterly silent, nothing but the ceaseless echo of her weeping.

The sound wrung Meng Wan's heart with bitter sorrow, and the unmistakable roughness and cracks of the woman's hands made her ache for her all the more.

She was the Lin family's younger daughter-in-law, yet she looked more than ten years older than Lin Xinqiang and Lin Qiaoyun, her withered face etched all over with the indelible marks the years had left.

Drawn by her mother's weeping, Lin Xi too began to cry softly, grievance and heartache tangling together as they tore at her wavering heart.

Hao Xiangping had married Lin Dong's father, Lin Xinhai, at twenty—something of a pioneer of free-choice love in that era.

They were both workers at the meat-packing plant; Hao Xiangping ladled out meals in the canteen. Once, a burly man insisted she'd deliberately shorted him a piece of meat and started cursing her out in front of the whole queue behind him, his words foul and ugly. Lin Xinhai, standing in line, couldn't bear to see a grown man bully a young woman like that, and he stepped straight up to take her side; only when the man saw she had someone defending her did he gradually back down.

After that, Hao Xiangping never switched her serving window again, waiting every day for Lin Xinhai to come for his meal. Anyone who paid attention would have noticed that Lin Xinhai's lunch box was always the fullest of all.

Lin Xinhai was no fool either. To repay her, he'd quietly slip Hao Xiangping a sweet, an orange, a few peanuts whenever she ladled out his food—any good thing he could lay hands on, he'd pass straight to her.

One thing led to another, and the two grew close. Lin Xinhai took to waiting for Hao Xiangping after work every day so they could walk home together; even with a bicycle he wouldn't ride it, just so he could spend a little longer at her side.

Before long the two reached the point of talking marriage, and like everyone else they married, had children, and settled into ordinary life, watching their two children grow up bit by bit and their little household grow, bit by bit, more comfortable.

Just as the days were taking on real promise, Lin Xinhai was diagnosed with late-stage lung cancer, and not long after, he passed away, leaving Hao Xiangping a widow alone with her children, the three of them with only each other to depend on.

From then on, the whole burden of supporting the family fell on Hao Xiangping, a young woman not yet thirty. She worked by day, and at night, when she got home, she pasted together paper boxes by candlelight. The long hours of straining her eyes in the dark, night after night, were how her eye trouble took root.

The family scraped by like this until Lin Dong turned eighteen and, through connections, got into the army, while Lin Xi tested into high school without trouble—and only then did life at home ease a little.

But the family had barely had a few settled days when Hao Xiangping's eyes started acting up again. At first she only felt a dryness; gradually her vision blurred, until she could make out little more than the rough shapes of people.

Lin Xi was still young then and couldn't make such a decision herself, so she wired a telegram to Lin Dong at his unit. Learning of his mother's condition, Lin Dong immediately filed for early discharge.

He'd only been in the army two years; leaving early to switch to civilian work meant he'd never land a decent job, so on a friend's recommendation he started driving a cab—just barely scraping together enough to get by.
