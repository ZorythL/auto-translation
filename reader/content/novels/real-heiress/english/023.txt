Chapter 23

The day she saw the tour group off was a brilliantly clear one, the sky a sheet of deep blue, the breeze warm and gentle—Meng Wan's favorite kind of spring wind.

The spring wind wasn't biting and cutting like the winter wind, nor stifling and airless like the summer wind, nor lonely and aloof like the autumn wind. It carried a sense of vigor, of teeming life. The spring wind had a scent all its own—a scent of jubilation, of high spirits, of wanting to give oneself over to the whole world.

On this lovely spring day, Meng Wan had completed the first group tour of her life—and, most importantly of all, earned her first bonus and share of the profits.

Meng Wan stacked all the receipts she'd collected in order by date and handed them over to Accountant Bi, along with the leftover money, returned in full.

Accountant Bi made his entries, reconciled the books, and handed her allowance over to her.

A full five hundred yuan. For Yuyang, where the average wage ran four or five hundred, this counted as a tidy sum.

If only she could lead big groups all the time, Meng Wan thought, indulging in a daydream. After finishing a group, the other guides all took a few days off, but Meng Wan was unwilling to rest. Take a few days off and five hundred yuan would vanish in a flash—that was harder to bear than taking her life.

Leaving the agency, she'd originally meant to try her luck at the train station, to see if she might pick up a few scattered tourists. Walk-in clients were easier to handle than group tours—it would practically count as resting.

Mid-thought, she saw Chen Hui coming toward her with Xiaohai in tow.

Meng Wan grew wary in spite of herself. What were they here for? Surely not to lodge a complaint against her with the agency?

Chen Hui drew Xiaohai close to Meng Wan, her face stacked with ingratiating smiles. Meng Wan let down her guard a little—they didn't look like they'd come to make trouble. She said nothing and waited for the other woman to speak.

"Guide Meng." Chen Hui greeted her with a smile and yanked Xiaohai up in front of her. "I've brought Xiaohai to thank you. If not for you the other day, Xiaohai would have brought real disaster down on himself."

So she'd come to express her gratitude—she had a conscience after all.

Meng Wan waved a modest hand. "I didn't do much, just my job."

Chen Hui wouldn't accept that. "How can you say you didn't do much? You pulled Xiaohai back from the very edge of crime—you gave him a second life. It wouldn't be too much to call you his savior."

Meng Wan smiled awkwardly. Surely it didn't rise to the level of being his savior.

Xiaohai couldn't stand to listen to his aunt fawn over Meng Wan like this either; he thought Chen Hui was overdoing it. "Auntie, just say it straight. Nobody wants to hear all this flowery talk."

Chen Hui shot Xiaohai a look and scolded him in a low voice. "What do you know? This is how people get on in the world. Whoever heard of someone asking a favor the moment they open their mouth?"

So there was a favor to ask after all. Meng Wan relaxed and waited for the rest.

Chen Hui looked at Meng Wan with a beaming smile, the words turning over three times on her tongue before they came out. "Guide Meng, I've come today because I'd like to place Xiaohai in your hands."

Wrack her brain as she might, Meng Wan would never have guessed this was the favor. She was floored. "In my hands?"

"Here's the thing." Chen Hui explained. "The hotel found out about Xiaohai's situation, so he lost his cleaning job too. Now he's an unemployed drifter again, and every day he goes out looking for work he comes back with nothing but rejections. I see it with my own eyes, and it aches in my heart. As his aunt, I can't bear to watch a boy who's mended his ways waste away like this, day after day. I've no real skills of my own, so all I can do is bring him out to beg for help."

Meng Wan didn't follow. "What is there that I could do for him?"

She was just a junior guide herself; the agency wasn't even hers, and she only earned a hard-won wage. What help could she possibly offer?

"I've been at this hotel over a decade—since it was still a guesthouse. In all these years I've seen no end of guides bringing groups through. Of all of them, you, Guide Meng, are the most earnest and responsible I've ever met, the one with principles of her own."

Meng Wan grumbled inwardly. She means I'm coldhearted.

"So..." Reaching the crux of it, Chen Hui paused. "...so I'd like Xiaohai to learn the guiding trade from you."

"Learn from me?" Meng Wan was aghast. "I haven't even had my guide's license a month, and you want him to learn from me?"

Meeting Meng Wan's astonished gaze, Xiaohai dodged it and ducked his head.

Chen Hui pressed on with her persuasion. "How long doesn't matter. What matters is that you've got the ability, Guide Meng. I may be no good at much else, but I'm a sharp judge of people—otherwise I'd never feel safe handing Xiaohai over to you."

Meng Wan couldn't decide on the spot, but inwardly she leaned toward refusing. She was attached to the agency now, running her own profit and loss—where would she find the energy to train a beginner? Besides, she had no money to pay him a wage.

"We don't want any pay." Chen Hui read her thoughts as precisely as a worm in her belly.

"But..."

She didn't think she had any skills worth passing on to Xiaohai. If he ended up learning nothing, wouldn't it all be thankless effort?

Seeing Meng Wan still wavering, Chen Hui simply played her trump card. "Yesterday the hotel got a phone call. It was from a friend of mine who's gone south to make his fortune—he wants me to recommend him a guide."

No way.

"And I recommended you."

That settled it—there was no refusing a favor once you'd taken one. This perk Meng Wan couldn't turn down.

Seeing Meng Wan's expression soften, Chen Hui knew she was tempted, and struck while the iron was hot. "This friend of mine knows all sorts of small bosses down south. This time he wants to bring them up to see his hometown. These bosses know plenty of wealthy people. If this trip goes well, they're bound to recommend you to others—and then you'll never want for clients. I've worked at the hotel so many years and gotten to know folks from all over. If anyone else asks me to recommend a guide, I won't even have to think—I'll give them your name straight off. And you won't have to split the commission with anyone; every cent goes right into your own pocket."

The temptation this held for Meng Wan was simply enormous. Take that group of twenty just now: because the clients came through the agency, she'd worked herself half to death and still got only half the allowance. Truly, working for someone else was a dead end—which only firmed up her resolve to become her own boss.

If a job like this were credited entirely to her, then after deducting the necessary tour expenses she'd clear at least two thousand yuan. Mind you, Meng Qingyan, a university professor, earned only a thousand a month.

And this group was all small bosses—they'd surely spend freely. After leading a group like that, she couldn't even imagine how fat her purse might get.

After a bout of inner struggle—though really one side held an overwhelming advantage—Meng Wan decided to grit her teeth and take it on. It was only mentoring an apprentice, after all. She might not have eaten pork, but she'd seen plenty of pigs run; even if she didn't teach him well, she couldn't teach him too badly. Anything that worked to her own advantage, she'd do; whether the result satisfied anyone else was their concern.

"All right. I agree to take Xiaohai on."

The moment Meng Wan got the words out, Chen Hui's face split into a grin too wide to close, and she came forward to shake hands.

"But—" Meng Wan made a hold-on gesture. "Let me be plain up front. I'm very busy myself; I don't have the time to deliberately teach him anything. Following me around, all he can do is watch and learn on his own. How much or how little he picks up comes down to his own aptitude. If his aptitude's not enough and he ends up learning nothing, you can't come back later and pin the blame on me."

"Of course, of course." Chen Hui answered, all smiles.

"And another thing—one year only. If he applies himself, a year is plenty. If he can't learn it, then he's just not cut out for this, and there'll still be time to go find other work."

"A year's enough, more than enough."

"And finally." Meng Wan fixed Xiaohai with a grave stare. "I hope what happened last time will never happen again. If I find out you've committed that kind of mistake again, I'll personally hand you over to someone who can rein you in."

Xiaohai hung his head and said, "Understood."

A weight off her mind, Chen Hui was overjoyed, beaming from ear to ear. She left Xiaohai with Meng Wan on the spot, and pressed into her hand a slip of paper bearing the train schedule and head count for the new tour group.

She urged Meng Wan to make a trip to the hotel that afternoon and book the rooms for the guests under her own name—that way the hotel would remember the goodwill, and steer any future groups her way as well.

Meng Wan had been managed and maneuvered into place, every benefit landing in her lap. Happiness was coming a touch too thick and fast; it left her a little giddy.

Fully at ease now, Chen Hui began to pour out her heart. "Then I'll thank Guide Meng on Xiaohai's behalf. I'm leaving him to you. If he won't listen, beat him or scold him as you see fit. His parents passed away early, leaving this one child with no one to care for him. As his aunt, there's been plenty I haven't done well—I didn't raise him right. Thank goodness there's still a chance to make amends. This is all I can do; the road ahead is his own to walk."

With that, Chen Hui patted Xiaohai on the back and the two of them bowed to Meng Wan together.

Having seen Chen Hui off, Meng Wan cast a faintly distasteful glance at Xiaohai and took him to the department store.

In the service trade, you had to keep up a good appearance before clients at all times. Your clothes needn't be lavish, but you had to manage basic cleanliness, neatness, and decency.

As for the outfit Xiaohai had on—toss him into a heap of beggars and you couldn't tell him apart. With the weather warming over the past couple of days, when Meng Wan got close to him she could even catch a faint whiff of sweat.

For some reason, she suddenly thought of James—a man who paid such careful attention to dress and bearing. Every time she got close to him she'd catch a faint scent of citrus, and it would lift her mood along with it.

Among all the men she'd ever met, James was, you could say, a rare specimen. Most men could barely manage clean and tidy, let alone grooming—as if grooming were something only women did. To her mind, it was men who were in greater need of it.

Afraid Meng Wan was spending too much, Xiaohai was deeply unwilling to change out of his shabby, sour-smelling getup. Meng Wan didn't waste words on him—she gave his backside a couple of kicks. "You do as I say, or you can go home right now."

Xiaohai didn't dare defy his master's orders, so he gave in obediently and let Meng Wan dress him as she pleased.

She picked out two spring outfits and two summer outfits for him, all on the casual side, then took him to get a haircut and to the bathhouse for a good scrub. When he came back out in his new clothes, he was practically a different person—his face even looked a few shades fairer. Xiaohai hadn't been unhandsome to begin with, and now he looked all the more presentable.

This was the kind of man who could stand at her side. Meng Wan surveyed her handiwork with satisfaction.

The two of them had a hearty meal, and then Meng Wan took him along to the hotel.

Neither of them had a car; they got around entirely on foot and by bicycle, and ordinarily she did all the pedaling herself. Now that she had a strapping young fellow like Xiaohai, she could save herself a good deal of effort. Sure enough, young men had strength to spare—even carrying a passenger on the back, he rode faster than Meng Wan did alone.

She consoled herself: at least she'd found the first merit in this apprentice of hers.

Once the bike was parked, Meng Wan adjusted her bag and made to go in. But ahead of her Xiaohai's face had turned heavy, and his steps faltered, dragging, refusing to move forward.

"What are you scared of? You're my apprentice now." Meng Wan, who looked out fiercely for her own, struck a posture of backing him up. "Straighten that back and walk in there with your head held high. Don't embarrass me."

Word of Xiaohai's past had already spread through the hotel; everyone knew. For a young man of twenty, face was like the foundation of a house—it held up the whole structure. Now that the foundation had collapsed, no house could stand on it.

Meng Wan's words lent Xiaohai a bit of courage, and he obediently squared his shoulders. His body stiffened up, but inside he was still soft and short on nerve.

On duty that day was a different manager, a man named Qin Weimin, with whom Meng Wan had dealt a few times. He greeted her first with a smiling face, but the moment Xiaohai shuffled in behind her, he put on an abrupt change of face. "What are you doing here again?"
