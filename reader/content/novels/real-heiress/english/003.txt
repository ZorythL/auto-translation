Chapter 3

Meng Wan saw Xu Qiuyun down to the foot of the building and watched her go, until that slender figure disappeared around the corner. A great weight in her chest eased, one stone of it slowly settling. The success of this first step gave her tremendous confidence, and only now did returning to 1995 feel real.

"Auntie!"

The soft, sweet voice of a little girl danced into Meng Wan's ears, pulling her drifting thoughts back.

It was her niece Zhizhi's voice.

Meng Wan's heart leapt with joy. She spun around at once to see Zhizhi trotting toward her, beaming, swaying from side to side, her two little hands waving in the air like crab claws.

"Slow down, don't fall."

Meng Wan took a few steps forward and crouched in front of Zhizhi, gathering her into her arms. She kissed each of her two soft, fair little cheeks, and after kissing her, studied the still-tiny Zhizhi closely. Who could have guessed that this little thing would, a dozen years on, grow into a one-meter-seventy young woman—a striking beauty the moment she stood anywhere?

Meng Wan gently brushed the tip of Zhizhi's nose with her forefinger, her voice softening without her meaning it to. "Long time no see, my little Zhizhi."

A Zhizhi this small—it really had been many years since she'd seen her.

Zhizhi blinked her big, round eyes up at Meng Wan, thinking that she seemed to have just seen her auntie yesterday morning. But a child wouldn't trouble herself over such details. She immediately seized on the thing she cared about most. "Auntie, did you get the Barbie doll you promised to buy me?"

Zhizhi adored Meng Wan, because every time Meng Wan bought something new and exciting, she would share it with her little niece. Sometimes, while Meng Wan was out, Zhizhi would secretly put on her clothes and shoes—one little foot only filling the front half of a leather shoe—and go clacking around the floor on tiptoe, jamming her toes against the points. The sight of it was hilarious.

Meng Wan knitted her brows. There did seem to have been such a thing. Around this time, Barbie dolls had just entered the country's market. She'd heard the local department store had brought in a shipment from the capital to test the waters and see whether there was any future in it.

The moment she'd learned this, she'd been the first to tell Zhizhi, and had even promised to make her the first child in Yuyang to own a Barbie doll.

In her past life, after the huge fight with her family, she'd left home and forgotten all about it. By the time she came back, that doll was sold out. When Zhizhi found out, she'd cried for ages—still blowing snot bubbles from all the weeping even as she slept.

This time she would not let Zhizhi be heartbroken. You couldn't break your word to a child.

Sun Jing had been standing off to the side the whole time. Seeing her sister-in-law's troubled expression, she assumed Meng Wan was reluctant—after all, she knew that doll-thing cost a small fortune, and even she couldn't bring herself to buy it for her child. So she immediately stepped in to set the girl straight. "Zhizhi, you can't always be asking your auntie for things. Earning money isn't easy for her either."

What she'd actually meant to say was that what Meng Wan earned wasn't even enough for herself, but the words turned into "earning money isn't easy" before they left her lips—she didn't dare offend this sister-in-law of hers, after all.

"It's all right, sister-in-law." Meng Wan scooped Zhizhi up and stood. "When I say I'll buy something, I always do. We grown-ups mustn't deceive children. It's only that..."

She did want to buy it, but where would she get the money? In her past life, when her salary ran out, she'd simply held out her hand to Cheng Xuehua for pocket money. But after the scene she'd just made, she had no face left to ask the family for money—and she mustn't ask again from now on, either.

Meng Wan smiled awkwardly and went on, "It's only that Auntie's a bit short on cash right now. Give it a few days, and the moment Auntie's pay comes in, she'll go and bring the doll home for you straightaway. All right?"

Zhizhi didn't understand what "short on cash" meant, but from her auntie's drift, she gathered Meng Wan had no money. Yet Mom said Grandma loved Auntie best, and even now that Auntie was working, Grandma still gave her a pocket-money allowance every month. Mom also said Auntie was the first person she'd ever met who needed an allowance even while holding a job.

So Zhizhi blurted out, "Auntie, if you don't have money you can ask Grandma. Mom says Grandma is Auntie's big wallet."

At this Sun Jing's face changed. Her eyes darting away, she took Zhizhi from Meng Wan's arms, and to cover her embarrassment she scolded, "What nonsense are you talking, child? When did I ever say that about your auntie?"

Though Sun Jing harbored some dissatisfaction toward this sister-in-law, she had never once let it show. After all, in this household she was the outsider, while Meng Wan had parents and a brother to shield her—and Meng Wan really was good to Zhizhi. Sun Jing just grumbled now and then under her breath, nothing more.

Zhizhi's little face was a picture of grievance. Mom had said it—she really had. Yesterday they'd stayed at Grandma's place, her mother's mother's, and that was exactly how Mom had put it to her.

Talking about people behind their backs was no good habit, true, but what she'd said was the plain truth. The old Meng Wan would surely have bristled at once. Now, though, she could smooth it over for Sun Jing with a smile. "Don't worry, sister-in-law. I know those weren't your words. It's surely someone who can't stand me, wagging their tongue right in front of you so Zhizhi would overhear. Pay it no mind."

Sun Jing had been worried Meng Wan would be angry, and never expected her to go out of her way to make excuses and smooth things over for her. It left her rather taken aback.

Seeing the mood had cooled, Meng Wan said cheerfully, "Let's go up, sister-in-law. We don't want our little Zhizhi catching cold."

It was late March, and winter's chill still lingered at its tail. Meng Wan had come down without a coat; she clenched her hands and sniffled.

*

The moment the two women stepped through the door, they found Cheng Xuehua already bustling about making lunch. She was in good spirits today and planned to roll out dough and make dumplings, so the whole family could have a happy meal together.

And just as the Mengs were preparing lunch in a mood as festive as New Year's, a knock came at the door.

The visitor was Meng Qingyan's student, Song Qingyang.

In her past life, Meng Wan had left home after the big family fight, so she'd missed Song Qingyang's visit this time. Their first meeting had instead come when her ex-husband wanted a plot of land and had Meng Wan pull strings through Song Qingyang to get it.

Having dealt with him, Meng Wan summed Song Qingyang up in a few words: deeply calculating—not someone a simple-minded creature like her could ever manage.

So afterward she stopped meddling in any business that involved him. The two of them did meet once more, at Meng Qingyan's retirement ceremony, and after that she heard he'd been arrested for taking bribes and sent to prison.

Of that charge, Meng Wan felt a vague pang. By rights, given his background and his talents, he should have seen through such worldly trappings. But then, she'd never stood in that position herself, and didn't know how great a temptation he'd faced. Were she the one in his place, she couldn't be sure she'd have resisted either.

Meng Qingyan was very fond of this student. From the moment Song Qingyang came in, he pulled him onto the sofa to chat away at length, and even called Meng Wan out from the kitchen to keep their guest company.

"This is my younger daughter, Meng Wan." He introduced them to each other. "And this is one of your dad's finest students, Song Qingyang."

Song Qingyang rose from Meng Qingyan's side, bent slightly from the waist, and extended his hand to Meng Wan with a gentlemanly air, introducing himself. "Hello, Miss Meng. My name is Song Qingyang. I'm Professor Meng's student."

Meng Wan rolled down her pushed-up sleeves to cover her fair forearms, wiped a couple of times at the leftover water on her hands from washing vegetables, and—worried her hands were too cold—offered only the front halves of her fingers for a light touch. She gave a slight nod. "Hello, Section Chief Song. Since it's our first meeting, just call me Meng Wan."

She studied him. He really was the sort of junior the elders adored—gentle, mannerly, not a drop spilled in anything he said or did.

Song Qingyang drew his hand back and, without showing a thing, rubbed at the faint chill she'd left on the pads of his fingers. Hearing Meng Wan address him by his official rank, he asked, puzzled, "Miss Meng... knows of me?"

A chill ran down Meng Wan's spine. Oh no—she'd let it slip out of habit. She smiled awkwardly, glanced at Meng Qingyan beside her, and covered for herself. "My dad's mentioned you at home. He said you're the youngest section chief at the land administration bureau, and his proudest student."

Meng Qingyan thought back along with his daughter's words. He didn't seem to recall ever having said that—but afraid it was just old age and a failing memory, he didn't contradict her.

Hearing Meng Wan heap such praise on him, the corners of Song Qingyang's mouth lifted of their own accord. "Actually, this isn't quite the first time I've met Miss Meng."

"Oh?" Meng Wan sat down accordingly and racked her brain over their past encounters, but couldn't recall a single other point of contact between them.

Meng Qingyan was equally baffled, and looked over at Song Qingyang.

Seeing father and daughter turn the same puzzled gazes on him, Song Qingyang gave a light laugh. "On my teacher's desk, there's a family photo."

Meng Wan and Meng Qingyan understood all at once. Meng Wan joked, "And? Am I not very photogenic? Everyone says I look better in person."

Song Qingyang's expression caught for a moment. He'd assumed she'd be just as her name suggested—graceful, demure—and hadn't expected such a lively, irrepressible temperament. He quickly picked up the thread. "You were already beautiful in the photo. In person, it's true, you look even better."

In the photo Meng Wan was serene, standing there with a smile, carrying the air of a well-bred young lady. Song Qingyang had naturally assumed a girl raised in a scholarly household would be just so—and hadn't expected that in person, the moment she opened her mouth, she'd reveal such an unfettered, mischievous nature. It was a sharp contrast to what he'd imagined.

Afraid his daughter would make a bad impression, Meng Qingyan hastened to explain. "Qingyang, don't mind her. This daughter of mine just has this casual way about her, speaks without thinking. She was only teasing you, really."

Then, feigning anger, he scolded Meng Wan. "And you—what girl is so thick-skinned as to come right out and praise her own looks?"

Meng Wan leaned back against the sofa and shrugged, muttering under her breath, "Can't even tell the truth now."

Seeing this, Song Qingyang spoke up for her at once. "It really is the truth. And the truth should be told as it is."

Just then Cheng Xuehua came out with freshly brewed tea. Pouring for the guest, she defended her daughter. "The child's not wrong. If you won't praise her, you can't even let her praise herself?"

"Madam." Song Qingyang stood to greet her.

Cheng Xuehua gestured for him to sit and set the teapot down with its spout turned outward. "Don't stand on ceremony, Xiao Song. Have some tea."

"Thank you, madam."

Unable to out-argue Cheng Xuehua, Meng Qingyan offered a feeble defense. "I praise her plenty too."

Seeing that, for all their bickering, this family of three was so warm and harmonious, Song Qingyang was moved despite himself. Meng Wan must have grown up surrounded by boundless love to develop such a rare and easy temperament.

Cheng Xuehua sat down on Song Qingyang's other side and, looking at the tea and box of pastries on the table, said, "You should've just come empty-handed. Why bring all these things?"

Only then did Song Qingyang remember his purpose, and he explained, "I had business out this way, and I remembered Teacher Meng saying he lived here, so I thought I'd pay a visit. It's my first time calling on my mentor, so I brought a few simple things. I hope you and Madam won't think them too shabby."

Seeing how well the young man spoke, Cheng Xuehua's brow smoothed. "How could we? We're delighted you came. Stay and eat with us in a bit."

"Yes, next time just come as you are. Your teacher and I don't stand on these empty formalities," Meng Qingyan chimed in. The more he looked at this student, the more he liked him. If only his own daughter had half this much grace, he'd count himself blessed.

Meng Wan said nothing, her eyes falling on the few boxes of gifts on the table—Jixing-brand tea and Ganso pastries. That he called these "a few simple things" stirred some unpleasant associations in her. Could it be that, even now, he'd already...

She didn't dare follow the thought any deeper, and clamped down on it at once, telling herself over and over that it was just one upscale gift—the man was simply being generous, that was all.

Watching the Meng parents fuss so warmly over him, Meng Wan thought of how, in her past life, when Meng Qingyan learned that Song Qingyang had taken bribes, he'd been so distraught he couldn't eat for days, and had even sent Meng Xingyang to find out whether there'd been some injustice in it. He'd refused, with all his heart, to believe that his upright, kindhearted student could ever have the words "corrupt official" attached to his name. Cheng Xuehua, too, had lamented endlessly—such a fine young man, gone so badly astray.

Lost in these memories, Meng Wan lifted her eyes a fraction, meaning to sneak a glance at Song Qingyang—only to find him looking straight at her. Meeting his clear, gentle gaze head-on, her heart gave a jolt, as if she'd been caught red-handed badmouthing someone behind their back. Guiltily, she turned her face away at once.

As luck would have it, Meng Xingyang poked his head out of the kitchen just then and called loudly, "The dumplings are done! We can eat!"

Meng Wan shot to her feet as though making her escape. "I'll go set the table."

Watching her flee in such disarray, Song Qingyang's mouth curved up faintly.

Cheng Xuehua, who had been observing the whole time from the side, took in every bit of the "exchanged glances" between the two, and a perfectly reasonable idea began slowly to rise in her heart.
