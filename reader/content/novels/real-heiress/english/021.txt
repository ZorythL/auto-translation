Chapter 21

Meng Wan's eyes flew open, her blood seeming to freeze on the spot as every nerve snapped taut. She'd known it couldn't all go this smoothly!

The one who'd lost the earring was none other than her old acquaintance, Auntie Bai. Whenever Meng Wan had seen her before, she'd always tucked the hair on both sides behind her ears, deliberately—if casually—showing off that pair of solid gold earrings.

Just yesterday Meng Wan had happened to overhear a few of the other aunties quietly grumbling that, ever since Auntie Bai's daughter had bought her those gold earrings, she'd never once let the hair at her temples down. Every time they passed her, she'd find some excuse to pretend-casually sweep her hair back—as if anyone didn't know she was just flaunting her earrings.

Auntie Bai kept her hair in a fluffy, ear-length perm; if she didn't sweep it up, it covered her ears completely, hiding them from view. This morning, perhaps because she still wasn't fully awake, she hadn't gotten around to sweeping it up. It was only when she went to tidy herself on the coach that she discovered just one earring still dangling, lonely—the other had long since gone who knew where.

Meng Wan went up first to calm her down, urging her to think carefully about whether she might have left it in her hotel room. Auntie Bai said she treasured these earrings; she took them off to sleep and put them back on in the morning. So when she'd left the room, she'd had both ears done up.

That meant it had fallen off somewhere along the way. Meng Wan had Yang Zhiming take everyone to breakfast first, even handing him some money, while she herself retraced their route to look for the earring; they'd meet up later at the first scenic spot.

Auntie Bai made a fuss about going with her, but Meng Wan wouldn't agree. The woman was in such an unsteady state right now that she'd not only be hunting for the earring but also coddling her—an even bigger waste of time.

Meng Wan didn't dare say so, of course. She only said she knew the area well, so the search would go quickly. Auntie Bai's husband was more blunt still—he said outright that her coming along would only make a mess of things, and told Meng Wan to be careful, that it was no great matter if she couldn't find it; the group's schedule came first, and their private affair mustn't hold up everyone else.

Meng Wan had the driver stop and got out partway, then flagged down a taxi along the route and rode back to the hotel.

Seeing her turn up again, Chen Hui assumed she'd left something behind and came over considerately to ask, "What's wrong? Did you forget something?"

Meng Wan scanned the floor all around, her brows knit tight. She didn't dare make a fuss yet, and only said she'd lost her guide's license and had come back to look for it.

Chen Hui happened to be free and helped Meng Wan search. They combed every corner for a good while without a trace of it. By rights, a guide's license was a fairly large thing—if it had dropped on the floor, they'd have spotted it long ago. After searching a while with nothing to show, Chen Hui went back to the counter to write up the day's log, her eyes still flicking now and then toward Meng Wan.

Meng Wan didn't let a single corner go unchecked—under the sofa, beneath the flowerpots, the stairwell, the corridor, she turned it all over, and came up with nothing. Despair filled her chest. Since Auntie Bai had said both earrings were on when she left the room, the earring could only have fallen somewhere between the hotel and the coach.

She'd searched so carefully and still hadn't found it—which left only one possibility: someone had already picked the earring up.

Meng Wan was so frantic she could have cried. This was her very first time leading a group. If Auntie Bai's earring couldn't be found, the woman would surely lose all heart to continue—she might even kick up a huge scene—and the others in the group would be swept up in her mood. In the end, the only one left in a tight spot would be her, the guide.

"Sister Hui." A voice suddenly rose behind her. "Xiaohai's gone missing."

Chen Hui set down her pen. "How could he be gone? The two of us came in together this morning!"

The woman explained, "Right after that tour group left, the two of us mopped the lobby floor together, and then in the time it took me to go rinse out my mop, the boy vanished."

It clicked for Meng Wan at once—Xiaohai must be that strapping young man from the morning.

A bad premonition struck her brain. She sharply linked the two incidents together, and the seed of suspicion took instant root and sprouted. Even if it was only suspicion, Meng Wan was past caring about caution. Almost frantic, she demanded loudly, "Where does Xiaohai live?"

By now Chen Hui too had begun to vaguely guess what was going on. What Meng Wan had lost was surely not just a guide's license, and there was definitely something fishy about Xiaohai's disappearance. As much as she hated to suspect her own nephew, at a moment like this, as the hotel's manager, she had a duty to get to the truth.

She said calmly, "I'll take you."

With that, she led Meng Wan outside and flagged down a car. On the way, Chen Hui learned from Meng Wan that what had been lost wasn't a license but an earring, and her heart began to clench, praying inwardly that it hadn't been her nephew who picked it up.

Because Meng Wan had reacted so quickly, when the two of them arrived Xiaohai was still at home packing his luggage. At the sight of them, he hastened his movements in a panic.

Seeing his reaction, Chen Hui had a fair idea of what was going on. "What are you packing your bags for?"

Xiaohai's hands trembled as he yanked the zipper shut and tucked the cloth bag behind him, putting on a show of composure. "A friend asked me to go out and have some fun for a few days."

"Where?" Chen Hui blocked his way and kept pressing.

Xiaohai blinked rapidly, avoiding her scrutinizing gaze, and answered haltingly, "Just over to Linchuan City next door, for a few days."

Chen Hui took a step forward and tried to snatch the bag from his hands. Xiaohai wouldn't let go, and the two of them began to grapple over it.

"Let go." Chen Hui barked. "Let me check it!"

"There's nothing in here!" Xiaohai went on protesting.

"If there's nothing in there, why are you so panicked?" Chen Hui kept pulling hard. "If you don't let me check this bag today, don't even think about leaving here."

Slowly, Xiaohai released his grip and watched as Chen Hui began rifling through the bag he'd only just packed.

Chen Hui turned the bag inside out—not so much as a glint of metal in sight.

"Auntie, what exactly are you looking for?" Xiaohai let out a quiet breath of relief and put everything back the way it had been.

Chen Hui was breathing hard. She exchanged a glance with Meng Wan, as if seeking her opinion.

Meng Wan looked Xiaohai over and said coolly, "On his person."

After all, no one who'd stolen something would put it in the most obvious place.

Receiving the cue, Chen Hui turned to Xiaohai again. "Let me search you too."

Xiaohai resisted fiercely. "Auntie, what on earth are you two looking for? I really didn't take anything—you have to believe me."

"Auntie wants to believe you too." Chen Hui wore the look of someone bitterly disappointed in a loved one. "But right now you're the prime suspect, and besides..."

Xiaohai's eyes went red around the rims. He glared and shouted at Chen Hui, "Aren't you about to say—besides, I've done time, so the moment anything underhanded happens, it's all on me?"

Chen Hui wiped at her tears, guilt-stricken. "Auntie didn't mean it like that."

"That's exactly what you meant." Xiaohai flung the bag to the floor in a fury, raised both arms, and shouted, "Search, then. Search wherever you want."

Wiping her tears, Chen Hui felt over the places on his body where something might be hidden. Of course she hoped it wasn't Xiaohai—but she had to do the whole thing in front of Meng Wan, to put her doubts to rest, so that no one else would suspect Xiaohai either.

Chen Hui patted down nearly every inch of him and found nothing except some loose change in his pocket. Only then did she exhale in relief—thank goodness it wasn't Xiaohai.

She turned her head and shook it at Meng Wan, weighed off her chest.

From the way they addressed each other, Meng Wan gathered they were aunt and nephew, and she didn't quite trust Chen Hui. "May I search him myself?"

Xiaohai gave a derisive snort and spread his arms wide, openly, as if to say she could do with him as she pleased.

Meng Wan steered clear of the sensitive spots and searched him front and back. There really was nothing. Though Xiaohai remained the prime suspect, she had no choice but to swallow her reluctance and apologize to him as well. "I'm sorry. I misjudged you."

Xiaohai pressed his lips tightly together and said nothing, picking the bag up off the floor. "Can I go now?"

Meng Wan stepped aside and made a gesture inviting him on his way.

All that fuss and nothing to show for it—it left Meng Wan deeply troubled. Regret laced with unwillingness kept swelling inside her like an inflating ball, threatening to burst her chest open.

How could it not be him? Working away on the job, and then suddenly he drops everything, goes home to pack up, and heads off to "have some fun"? Did that make sense? And at the exact moment the earring went missing, no less—it didn't add up at all.

But to catch a thief, you need the stolen goods. No matter how suspicious she was, without a shred of evidence she couldn't do a thing to him.

Watching Xiaohai's retreating back, the more Meng Wan looked, the more wrong it felt. She must have overlooked something. But what?

The next second, Meng Wan caught a clue, and called out loudly to stop him.

"Stop right there!"

He didn't so much as turn his head at the shout—he just broke into a run, bolting outward.

His flight kindled Meng Wan's fighting spirit. She swung her shoulder bag around to the small of her back and shot off like a gust of wind, sprinting at a hundred-meter-dash pace. She would catch this kid today if it cost her her very life.

When you're doing the right thing, even Heaven lends a hand.

Meng Wan had just raced out of the alley—Xiaohai's figure was about to vanish around the corner of the street—when several uniformed police officers came walking straight toward her. Meng Wan halted and shouted, "Officers, stop him!"

The two officers reacted with lightning speed, each seizing an arm and pinning Xiaohai facedown to the ground. Xiaohai yelped and howled in pain.

A crackdown on hooligan crime was underway just then. At the sight of such a scene, the officers immediately assumed Xiaohai had done something unsavory to Meng Wan.

Seeing him subdued, Meng Wan walked up in small steps, gasping for breath, one hand braced against her waist.

"Thank you, officers." Meng Wan panted in shallow breaths.

One officer with a stern face asked directly, "What did he do to you?"

Meng Wan glanced at Xiaohai kneeling on the ground. Her mouth opened, then closed again. If she told the truth, this fellow would surely be hauled off and locked up today. Xiaohai looked to be only in his early twenties, and from what she'd just heard he'd already been to prison; if this blew up, he might land right back inside. Meng Wan didn't want to give up on the young man that easily.

"Officers, it's a misunderstanding. He's my cousin, Chen Xiaohai. I found him a job, but he won't take it—he's set on running off to the next city over to work, so I chased after him."

"Cousin?" Hearing it was a family matter, the officer slowly loosened his grip on Xiaohai. For thoroughness's sake, he double-checked with Xiaohai, "Is this your cousin?"

Xiaohai shook out both arms with a pained look on his face. Meeting Meng Wan's warning eyes, he stammered out a grudging "Mm."

"Xiaohai."

Just then Chen Hui came chasing out too.

Afraid she'd let something slip, Meng Wan got in first. "This is my mom—his aunt."

The sight of two tall, broad officers standing at the corner stunned Chen Hui; she assumed Meng Wan had already reported Xiaohai to the police. She was about to open her mouth to explain for him when she saw Meng Wan turn and shoot her a look.

"Mom, didn't I tell you to wait at home? Your legs aren't good—if you fell again, that'd be real trouble."

Chen Hui quickly parsed the message buried in Meng Wan's words. It seemed this was a coincidence, and Meng Wan had no intention of turning Xiaohai over to the police. Having pieced it together, she played along. "I was afraid you couldn't stop him on your own, so I came out to help."

It did look like a family matter, so the officers paid it no more mind, only telling them to go home and talk it out properly and not to disturb the public order out on the street.

Meng Wan bowed and scraped the officers off, and the next second the face she turned on Xiaohai had gone cold as ice.
