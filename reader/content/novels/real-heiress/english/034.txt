Chapter 34

Early the next morning, Lin Dong drove Meng Wan and James to the train station, and the two of them set off together on the road to find her family.

In her past life, after learning the truth of her origins, she had refused to face it. Her birth mother, Qin Yi'e, had come to her door several times, and each time she'd driven her away. Only in the final stretch of her life had she returned to her mother's side.

Looking back on it now, Meng Wan saw how heartless she'd truly been. Even if Qin Yi'e had done wrong, her intentions had been for Meng Wan's sake—and in the end she'd left the woman to bury her own child, white-haired sending off the young. She must have regretted it too, back then.

The wrongs and rights of the past, all the tangled accounts—Meng Wan no longer wanted to dwell on them. Better than imagining how things might have turned out had something never happened was to face the problem head-on and solve it.

This time she'd come not only to stop Zhou Nian from marrying, but to bring her back to keep studying, and to take Qin Yi'e to the hospital for a checkup. In her past life, the thing that had tipped off the real heiress to the truth was Qin Yi'e falling gravely ill and needing a blood transfusion—only their blood types hadn't matched.

This time she hoped to head it off early. If there was an illness, catch it early.

Dongping Village was a remote hamlet in neighboring Dongshan Province, the village where Cheng Xuehua and Meng Qingyan had lived during their years of rural resettlement. Meng Wan herself had spent a few years there too. Her only memory of the place was of poverty and desolation.

She and James had to take the train first to Dongshan's provincial capital, then a long-distance bus to the county town. From the county town to the village, they'd have to figure their own way.

Because the train ride took a full day, Hao Xiangping had packed Meng Wan a whole bag of food.

There weren't many trains running in those days, so no matter the hour, there were always crowds.

Meng Wan had asked Lin Dong to buy two hard-seat tickets. She'd figured she and Lin Dong would just rough it through together, but now that Lin Dong had been swapped for James, she felt a little guilty—worried that sitting for so long might leave him uncomfortable.

Three people shared each row of seats; the two of them had the two by the window.

"Why don't I take the middle seat instead?" Meng Wan glanced at the burly young fellow to James's left, one of whose arms was already pressed up against James's left arm—the two of them practically inseparable.

James gave her a faint smile. Looking at her slightly puffy eyelids, he said, "I'm fine. Close your eyes and rest a while. I'm right here beside you."

She really hadn't slept well the night before. The thought of setting out today had pulled the taut string in her mind tighter and tighter, her heart thudding with the unknown of what lay ahead.

Seeing he showed no sign of discomfort, Meng Wan stopped fussing. She leaned into the gap between the window and the seatback, found a comfortable position, and got ready to doze off.

Afraid she'd be uncomfortable leaning like that, James took one of his own cotton shirts from his bag, folded it into a block, gently eased her head over, and tucked it underneath. "That should be more comfortable."

Meng Wan curled up in the corner, blinking at this man who was right within arm's reach, and couldn't help recalling the words she'd blurted out in a rush of heat that day—"Let's be together."

She felt her face go a little hot. The moment her eyes met James's, she guiltily squeezed them shut, her heart hammering away.

The aftermath of that day came back to her unbidden.

When she'd finished saying those words, James had clearly frozen. After a full minute of silence, he'd finally spoken: "Next week is my birthday."

Meng Wan had let out an "Ah?", not grasping the connection between the two.

"I'll give you my answer then."

So particular about it—he even had to pick a day. Meng Wan had no idea whether his answer would be yes or no. In any case, she'd started regretting the words as soon as they were out.

Would James think her a girl with no sense of restraint?

But this was the first time in her life she'd ever confessed to anyone. Before, it had always been others chasing after her.

As long as they hadn't officially made things official, the two of them couldn't take a step closer, so now whenever Meng Wan saw him, a shy sort of awkwardness would come over her. Perhaps this was what they called the in-between phase.

Breathing in the familiar scent, Meng Wan kept up a calm exterior while her mind ran wild with fantasies of their future together, forgetting for a moment that she still had a big matter to handle.

The train was very noisy—chatter, snoring, the calls of hawkers, one rising as another fell.

Across from Meng Wan sat a mother traveling with two daughters, one older and one younger. The mother looked a bit younger than Cheng Xuehua, with a narrow forehead and a sharp chin, very lean of build, her skin somewhat tanned.

From the moment they'd boarded, the eyes of the three of them had kept flicking over James's face—wanting to look yet not daring to stare too openly.

The younger daughter, unable to keep her thoughts to herself, tugged at her mother and whispered in her ear, "Mama, how come this uncle looks different from us?"

She'd meant it quietly, but with such a short distance between them, James caught every word. He deliberately lowered his book so she could get a clearer look.

This mother had never seen a foreigner either, but she'd eaten a few more years' worth of salt than her children. She explained to the girl, "The uncle is a foreigner. Foreigners look like this."

"Do foreigners speak Mandarin too?" the older daughter asked beside her, curious.

Her mother let out an "Uh—" and couldn't come up with an answer. Foreigners probably didn't speak Mandarin, yet this foreigner in front of them spoke it so fluently.

James smiled, set the book down on his lap, and said, "Foreigners have their own languages too—they speak the language of their own country. But I'm a little different."

"Why are you different?" the little girl asked, blinking her big round eyes in curiosity.

James was utterly charmed by the look of her. With a smile playing on his lips, he said softly, "Because I'm mixed, so I can speak the languages of two countries."

"Mixed?" Hearing the new word, the little girl gazed at James, puzzled.

"I know this one." Her older sister raised her hand to speak. "It means his papa and mama come from two different countries, and a child born to people like that is called mixed."

The teacher had explained it in class.

James gave the older sister a thumbs-up. "Exactly right. My mama, like your mama, is from Country Z, and my papa is from Country M, so I can speak both Chinese and English."

"Wow!" The older sister's eyes lit up. "I've only ever heard my teacher speak English, and now there's actually a foreigner who can speak English sitting right in front of me."

A handsome foreigner, too—though, being a little shy, she didn't say the second half out loud.

She pulled a clearly well-used notebook from her satchel and flipped to a page filled with English.

She looked up at James eagerly. "I copied this sentence out of a book. Could you read it for me?"

Intrigued, James took the notebook, read the sentence through carefully, and recited it in a reading cadence: "I cannot fix on the hour, or the spot, or the look, or the words, which laid the foundation. It is too long ago. I was in the middle before I knew that I had begun."

"This is from Pride and Prejudice, isn't it?"

"You've read it?" the sister asked, astonished.

"Years ago now." It must have been back in his university days—the book had been very popular then.

The sister stared at James with an excited, longing look. "Could you translate what this sentence means for me?"

She could render it word for word, but she knew its meaning was surely not so simple.

James gazed at the passage for a moment, two languages switching back and forth in his mind as he weighed how to capture the English in Chinese so its full sense came through.

After a long pause, he spoke slowly: "I can't say at what hour, in what place, by what look or what words you first won me over and made me begin to love you. It was a long time ago. By the time I realized I had fallen in love with you, I was already well along the way."

The sister asked, puzzled, "What does 'already well along the way' mean?"

What did love have to do with a road?

James let out a soft laugh and explained for this endlessly curious little girl: "What this sentence means is that by the time the hero realized he liked the heroine, the liking had actually begun long before—he'd already fallen for her before he ever noticed."

The sister glanced at Meng Wan, dozing with her eyes shut against James's shirt, and lowered her voice. "Then, big brother, is it like that for you and your sister too?"

The question stumped James completely. He turned to look at Meng Wan, her breathing soft and even, and figured she must be asleep.

He gave a quiet, self-deprecating laugh. "When I read this book, I thought that if I ever liked someone, I'd surely realize it the very first moment."

The sister quickly finished his thought for him: "But now you're one of the people in the book too, aren't you?"

Was love really so wondrous? She so wanted to try it for herself.

His eyes brimming with a smile, James nodded at her, then closed the notebook and handed it back.

The sister's mother, listening to her go on about love with a strange man, couldn't help shooting her a glare. "All day long, what kind of books are you even reading? I never see you this interested in your studies."

The sister put the notebook away and made a face at her mother.

In a corner no one noticed, Meng Wan's eyes rolled once beneath her closed lids.

------

By the time Meng Wan woke, her stomach was growling with hunger. Bleary-eyed, she draped James's shirt over her lap and took out the scallion pancakes Hao Xiangping had packed, meaning to share them with him. "Do you have any allergies?"

The last time, his not eating the pancake must have been over this very concern.

"Peanuts, tree nuts."

"Then there's no problem." Meng Wan had even brought him a pair of chopsticks. "This is just an ordinary flatbread with scallions—it won't cause any trouble."

The last time Lin Dong had shared, James had had this worry and so hadn't eaten with them. The truth was, he'd wanted to try one.

Meng Wan handed him the lunch box and chopsticks and ate from the lid herself. "How is it? I tucked them at the very bottom of the bag, covered up—they're still a little warm."

"Delicious." James chewed in small bites. "No wonder you like them so much."

Meng Wan gave him a radiant smile, her big eyes narrowing to slits.

As the two of them ate, Meng Wan noticed the little girl across from them staring with naked longing, swallowing hard.

"Want some?"

The younger sister clutched her own stomach and nodded eagerly, like a chicken pecking at grain.

Seeing this, her mother hurriedly pulled a steamed bun from her bag and said reproachfully, "It's not like I didn't pack you anything. Why are you staring at someone else's food like a little beggar?"

Meng Wan thought the mother a bit harsh, and worried the little sister would be hurt, she spoke up at once on her behalf: "Big sister, don't scold her—it's my fault for these pancakes smelling so strong they coaxed out her little appetite. By way of apology, let me give her one."

Seeing how gracious Meng Wan was, and glancing again at her daughter's aggrieved little face, the woman sighed. "Then thank you, young lady."

In truth she knew it was no great matter. It was only that she'd just been through the experience of being looked down on by her own family, and at the moment she was a touch over-sensitive.

Hearing her mother agree, the little sister's face instantly lit up with delight. Meng Wan still had two pancakes left in the box, so she simply gave one each to the older and younger girls. The older sister didn't take hers right away but looked to her mother for permission, accepting it only after her mother gave a nod.

Watching the two of them eat with such relish, Meng Wan happily put the lunch box back in the bag.

The woman looked at the handsome young man and lovely young woman across from her, and warmth rose in her heart. They were strangers she'd only just met, yet they were a thousand times kinder than her own family.

She struck up some idle conversation with the two of them. "Where are you two getting off?"

"Qiyang City."

Qiyang was the capital of Dongshan Province.

"What a coincidence." The woman brightened. "So are we."

"Really?" Meng Wan went on, "We're heading to Linchuan County, which is under Qiyang, and then from Linchuan to Dongping Village."

"My husband is from Dongping Village!" The woman's eyes went wide in disbelief.

Meng Wan turned and exchanged a smile with James. Neither had expected such a coincidence—they hadn't even arrived, and already they'd met a local.

"You don't sound like you're from Dongping, by your accent. Do you have relatives there?"

Her husband had never mentioned anyone in the village who knew a foreigner.

Meng Wan gave an awkward, hard-to-explain smile. "Yes—I'm going back this time to reunite with family."

Afraid she'd start dwelling on something painful again, James reached over and patted the back of her hand.

Meng Wan gave him a smile to show she was all right.

The woman, now thoroughly interested, pressed on: "Who are you going to see? Maybe I even know them."

Though her family had since moved to the county town, she'd spent a good many years in Dongping Village from the time she'd first been sent down, and she knew most of the people there by now.

"Qin Yi'e." Meng Wan spoke the somewhat unfamiliar name in a level voice. It seemed to be the first time she'd ever said it aloud to anyone.

"Aiya!" The woman shed her composed manner and cried out, "So we're family!"
