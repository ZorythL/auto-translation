Chapter 46

Out of the travel agency, Meng Wan hurried off without pause to Yuyang University, where Meng Qingyan worked.

If there was one thing Meng Qingyan regretted, it was that neither of his two children had attended the school where he taught—Meng Wan hadn't even gotten into university at all.

Before she'd started high school, Meng Wan often went along to the school with Meng Qingyan and did her homework in his office. Later, once she was in high school, people were always asking her which university she wanted to attend, what major she wanted to study—as if she had only to want it and it would happen.

In their eyes, a teacher's child was bound to be brilliant, and getting into university was naturally a sure thing. But weren't they teachers themselves? Didn't they understand the simple fact that intelligence isn't hereditary?

After that, Meng Wan rarely went to Meng Qingyan's school anymore—not that she found it embarrassing for herself, but because she was afraid it would cost Meng Qingyan face.

When she reached his office, he had just gotten back from teaching a class, with several students still clustered around him asking questions.

Meng Wan leaned quietly against the doorframe, waiting for them to finish.

She waited a long while. The bell for the next class had already rung before the students gradually dispersed, and only then did Meng Qingyan catch sight of his daughter.

Surprised, he came forward to meet her. "What's brought you here today, of all days?"

"Dad, there's something I'd like to ask for your help with." As she spoke, Meng Wan pulled out his chair for him to sit.

The office held four desks in all, and the other teachers happened to be out at the moment—just as well, since Meng Wan had been worried it'd be awkward to speak with others around.

Meng Qingyan had talked himself hoarse. He unscrewed his glass and took a sip of tea before asking slowly, "Go on. You wouldn't come to the temple of the Three Treasures unless you wanted something."

Meng Wan dragged over a chair and sat down. First she gave Meng Qingyan a cheerful report on her work over the past while. When he learned that his daughter was now a leader at the travel agency, he couldn't help being thoroughly astonished.

When he'd first heard she'd become a tour guide, he had privately concluded she'd never stick with it—just a three-minute enthusiasm. Yet here she was, already a leader, and making a real go of it too. It quite changed his view of her. It seemed he ought to do some self-reflection: he shouldn't have judged people through the lens of preconception, all the more so when the person in question was his own daughter. If others didn't believe in her, so be it—but as her family, he ought by rights to support her.

At that thought, the gaze Meng Qingyan turned on his daughter brimmed with warmth. "Whatever your dad can do to help, just say it."

Anything that could help advance their daughter's career, the whole family would throw their full weight behind.

Meng Wan laid out her request in full, and Meng Qingyan took her straight to the logistics office.

He'd assumed it would be some big affair—a matter of a few desks and chairs? Even if the logistics office couldn't spare them, he'd simply buy them out of his own pocket.

The head of the logistics office was a middle-aged man who looked about the same age as Meng Qingyan; Meng Qingyan called him Director Xie.

Director Xie said it just so happened they'd recently swapped out a batch of office desks and chairs, and he'd been meaning to find a time to haul them to the secondhand market and sell them.

Meng Wan followed him to the warehouse. The replaced desks and chairs were far cleaner and sturdier than she'd imagined—they couldn't have been used long.

"Uncle Xie, the school doesn't want furniture this nice anymore?" Meng Wan gave a desk a shake to test how solid it was.

"A while back, one of our school's alumni came to visit—he's in business over in Hong Kong now and has made quite a fortune. He came back specially to pay his respects to the teachers who'd taught him, and donated a batch of brand-new desks and chairs to the school. So these got swapped out."

So that was it—that was why Director Xie had been planning to cart them off to the secondhand market. The desks and chairs still looked seventy or eighty percent new, and to leave them piling up in the warehouse, or just throw them out, would be a real waste.

"Uncle Xie, could I buy a few of these to take back?"

If she waited for him to sell them at the secondhand market, a middleman would take his cut. Buying straight from the source would save her a good deal of money.

Director Xie answered readily, "Of course. If you've got somewhere to put them, you can cart the whole lot away for all I care."

It'd spare him having to find someone to move them.

"Then... how should we figure the price?"

"Easy." Director Xie waved a hand grandly. "Ten yuan a desk, five yuan a chair. Take your pick of whichever ones catch your eye."

These were idle goods piling up in the warehouse anyway. He'd have sold them for a bit more at the secondhand market, but Meng Wan counted as one of their own—he wouldn't have minded giving them to her for free. The small charge was only token, just so she wouldn't feel beholden.

Meng Wan glanced happily at Meng Qingyan, thanked Director Xie with feeling, and then set about picking through them one by one.

She'd wanted to call Lin Dong right away to haul the lot back, but it was already past noon, and Meng Qingyan insisted on first taking her to the school cafeteria for a meal. For once he urged her to put her health first—it was a good thing to be passionate about her work, but the body was the capital of the revolution.

There'd been a time when he couldn't wish enough work-passion onto his daughter; now here he was advising her to mind her health instead. It seemed people really did need to do what they loved. Meng Qingyan chalked his daughter's transformation up to "passion."

In his view, only loving something could make a person carry it on with such tireless drive.

After the meal, Meng Wan asked Meng Qingyan for one more favor.

The signboard of their "Youth Travel Agency" had long since faded yellow in the sun. She wanted Meng Qingyan to write out an inscription for her, which she'd make into a plaque and hang up.

Meng Qingyan was well versed in such things—which typeface stood out most boldly, what kind of wood best withstood wind and rain—he knew it all inside out. He told her to set her mind at ease; he'd handle this matter from start to finish.

That afternoon Meng Wan called on Lin Dong to help her find a truck and haul all the desks, chairs, and benches to the agency.

Lin Dong brought a friend of his along to help, and when they'd finished, Meng Wan slipped each of them twenty yuan—she could hardly let them make the trip for nothing.

Old Bi was the only one at the travel agency. He'd been run off his feet all morning and had only just snatched a moment to go home for a bite to eat; he'd barely sat back down before Meng Wan came hauling in all these big pieces.

Accountant Bi hadn't known Meng Wan long. She was only a guide loosely affiliated with the agency. At the last meeting he hadn't even meant to notify her—it was only because Xiao Hai happened to be there, and he knew Xiao Hai was Meng Wan's apprentice, that he'd mentioned it in passing. When Xiao Hai said she'd gone away on a long trip, he hadn't been much disappointed, since he'd held out no real hope to begin with.

Who'd have guessed she'd show up not long after—and that was the moment everything began to turn.

Before, all the matters of the agency, big and small, had fallen to him. The guides went out every day and had it hard; the most he could do was keep the home front running smoothly for them. Bit by bit, he'd become the agency's de facto man in charge.

Reconciling receipts, chasing payments—however thorny these small tasks got, he could still handle them. But when it came to something as big as restructuring, he too was at his wits' end.

He'd been worried the agency would simply be bought up and turned into a billiard hall—and then this young woman he'd never paid much mind to had suddenly stepped forward.

From planning, to negotiating, to raising the money, signing the contract, assigning tasks, right down to these desks and chairs she'd just hauled back—every single thing was solid, real work. This child was truly dependable.

Looking at it this way, he could retire with an easy mind. With her here, the agency was sure to go from strength to strength.

Over there, Meng Wan had just sent Lin Dong off when another familiar figure appeared at the mouth of the alley.

"Xiao Hai?" Meng Wan smiled and waved. "Perfect timing—I'm short on hands for the work."

Xiao Hai came hurrying over, took one look at the desks and chairs piled outside the door, and rolled up his sleeves to pitch in.

Meng Wan quickly stopped him. "Don't rush to move them inside yet. Go ask Old Bi where there's paint for sale. Buy two buckets and paint the inside clean first, then we'll put these new desks in."

She handed Xiao Hai a hundred yuan and told him to buy one bucket of white paint and a smaller bucket of red. The red paint on the doorway had long since dulled to a faded whitish gray—at a glance it looked shabby and unremarkable.

After Xiao Hai left, Meng Wan started hauling things out with Accountant Bi.

Accountant Bi immediately swept every document touching the accounts into a drawer and locked it. Anything else could be lost, but the account books absolutely could not.

The two of them shifted things out little by little—first piling the junk from the back room out at the street corner, then carrying out the desks and chairs and lining them up in a row along the curb.

Their street had a residential block and a school, and the road was reasonably wide. Meng Wan kept everything heaped on their side of it as much as she could, so as not to block the main thoroughfare.

"Hey there, miss—got any scrap to sell?"

A middle-aged man pedaling a tricycle, dressed head to toe in dark blue coarse cloth, pulled the brake and stopped a little way from Meng Wan.

Meng Wan had just carried out the last desk. Panting, hands on her hips, she looked over at the heap of wastepaper at the corner. "How much per jin?"

"Four fen," the man answered.

"Sold." She turned to Old Bi. "Old Bi, you start tidying up inside. I'll go sell off the scrap."

With that she walked over to the corner. The man handed her a woven plastic sack. Afraid there might be something important mixed in, Meng Wan looked over every sheet, one by one, before stuffing it into the sack.

It took the better part of an hour, and she filled three sacks before it was all done.

A hundred and twenty jin in all, at four fen a jin—four yuan eighty in income.

By the time she got back to the agency, Xiao Hai and Old Bi had already draped newspapers over themselves and started painting the walls. Meng Wan followed their lead: she cut two holes in a sheet of newspaper and pulled it on, folded another into a hat and put it on her head, then took up the red paint and a brush and went out to paint the doorway.

The afternoon sun fell warm against her back, and the work didn't tire her at all.

The three of them, old and young, toiled away the whole afternoon, and at last got both rooms—lintel and all—painted clean and white.

Meng Wan threw open every window to let the smell air out quickly. Xiao Hai drew a bucket of water, rinsed out the mop, and wiped away the white paint spatters that had dripped onto the floor, along with the red drops Meng Wan had let fall on the steps.

Looking at the mop now blotched in every color, Meng Wan, a little disgusted, told Xiao Hai to just throw it out, and gave him another twenty to go buy a whole new set—mop, broom, dustpan, and bucket.

The walls weren't fully dry yet, so the new furniture couldn't be moved in today.

Old Bi went home and fetched a large sheet of plastic. He and Meng Wan covered all the desks and chairs with it, then used rope to tie it down around the legs.

"What do you plan to do with those old desks and chairs?" Old Bi asked.

"I've got no particular plan." Meng Wan glanced at them. "If you've got a use for them, they're yours."

"That'll do, then." Old Bi didn't stand on ceremony. "I'll knock them into shape back home and make a writing desk for my little grandson."

By the time Xiao Hai got back, things here were squared away. Meng Wan gave him two hundred yuan and told him to buy plenty of groceries to take home so Qin Yi'e could cook him something nice.

"I've told Liu Fei that from now on he'll be the one mentoring you. He's a careful, steady sort. Stick with him and learn properly—you're bound to pick up a great deal from him."

"Master." Xiao Hai clutched the money, gazing at her plaintively. "Are you giving me up?"

Meng Wan laughed in spite of her exasperation. "Do you think I've still got time to mentor you now? You study hard and learn to stand on your own two feet, quickly—don't go wasting the arrangements I've made for you."

Xiao Hai nodded earnestly. "Understood, Master."

Meng Wan then took out from her bag the work summary she'd written herself—it held the talking points for every scenic spot, the timing for each route, and a rundown of some of the mistakes she'd made.

"Take this and copy it out for yourself. Once you've got summaries of your own someday, you can add them in."

Xiao Hai hastily wiped his hands and took it, leafing through page by page.

Meng Wan asked, "Are they settling in all right at your place? They're not putting you out, are they?"

She'd been so busy these past few days she hadn't had a chance to go see them.

"No, not at all—we're living really happily together." Afraid Meng Wan might feel guilty, Xiao Hai hurried to explain. "Granny Qin cooks so well. Every day now when I get home there's a hot meal waiting. Ever since my mom passed, it's been ages since I lived like this."

It was all true. Ever since his parents died, his home had been little more than a place to sleep. Only now that they'd moved in did it feel lived-in, warm, and full of life.

Hearing this, Meng Wan let out a quiet breath of relief. "Then tell me if you all need anything. And tell them—I've been too busy these past few days to come see them. When I go to pick up the medical reports next week, I'll come by and collect them."

"Understood, Master."

The sun dipped to the west. Meng Wan checked the time—Lan Xiuli should be off work by now. She'd asked Meng Xingyang for the Lan family's address, and meant to go pay them a visit.
