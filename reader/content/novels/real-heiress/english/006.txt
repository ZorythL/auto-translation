Chapter 6

At the words "department store," James couldn't help frowning.

He'd been in Yuyang for three days now. At first he'd hired a guide too, but the man had been thoroughly unprofessional and devoid of ethics. From the hotel to the restaurants to the cars he was driven around in, the prices had been so steep James half thought he was back in New York.

On his first day in Yuyang, he'd been taken to a jade shop and pressured to buy something. Luckily James had come prepared and produced the name of an embassy official he'd looked up beforehand, claiming the man was an acquaintance—only then did he manage to bluff his way out.

That very night he'd packed his bags and left that overpriced, mediocre hotel for the one he was staying at now.

So for the next two days he'd toured on his own with a map. That way he wouldn't be swindled, but his efficiency was abysmal—which was how the daytime encounter with Meng Wan had come about in the first place.

Having just heard Meng Wan's commentary, he'd thought highly of her expertise and assumed she'd be a different sort of guide. But it turned out she was no better than the rest, birds of a feather.

Still, curiosity got the better of him. He wanted to tag along with Meng Wan and see for himself—see how she'd talk him into buying something, or strong-arm him into it.

Seeing him hesitate, Meng Wan assumed he was worried about wasting time, and hurried to explain. "Don't worry, it'll be quick. It won't hold up your dinner."

James said nothing, only made an "after you" gesture for her to lead the way.

*

The Yuyang of 1995 had only one traditional state-run department store. Like the city itself, the store stood at a crossroads between old and new—on one side the traditional enamel mugs and loose-weighed biscuits, on the other gleaming Toshiba color televisions and lavish gold jewelry.

From the daily necessities of food, clothing, shelter, and transport to the fashionable goods of a more materially aspiring life, the store had nearly everything, which was why on weekends the crowds were beyond counting. Fortunately Meng Wan had come in the afternoon, so it was relatively less packed than the morning.

She asked James whether he'd like to come in with her or wait outside. With a touch of puzzlement, James said he'd go in with her—how was he supposed to get talked into buying anything if he waited outside?

Meng Wan urged James to stay close. The place was no small thing, and once you got separated it took a good while to find each other again.

Threading once more through these familiar surroundings gave Meng Wan a grounding sense of reality—she really had returned to 1995. If she remembered right, in just over a year this department store would be bought out by foreign investors and turned into a large shopping mall, and after that, her youthful memories tied to this store came to an end.

As she walked deeper inside, Meng Wan scanned around for the toy counter, while keeping half an eye on James beside her.

There he was, hands clasped behind his back, surveying everything thoughtfully like a leader inspecting a worksite, every so often muttering a few lines of English she couldn't understand.

James paid no attention at all to Meng Wan's gaze. From the moment he stepped inside, he'd been observing the foot traffic in the department store. By now, malls and supermarkets of all kinds had become commonplace in his country; with its lower population density, you'd only see a mall this lively on a major holiday. Yet here the customer flow held at this dense a level even on an ordinary weekend afternoon. This was a tremendous business opportunity.

From his cursory observation, the store's range of goods wasn't exactly complete, and the styles weren't all the latest—plenty of the clothing was already last year's fashion.

"Found it!"

Meng Wan's cry shattered James's train of thought. Following the direction of her finger, he saw a counter laden with toys, ringed by children of all heights, every one of them flushed with excitement, chattering and haggling with their parents—all for the sake of owning some pricey new toy.

James looked at Meng Wan, baffled. "You brought me to the department store to make me buy a toy?"

"I'm the one buying the toy." Meng Wan didn't know why he'd ask that, and gave him a brief account of the Zhizhi situation.

Understanding dawned on James, and a flush of guilt rose in his chest. He'd judged a gentleman's heart with a small-minded measure.

Meng Wan had no inkling of all this inner churning. Eyeing all those children, she felt a keen sense of crisis and wanted nothing more than to claim the doll at once.

She strode forward in a single bound, gently nudged aside two little girls draped over the counter gazing longingly at the doll, pointed at the Barbie in the glass case, and announced loudly, "Hello! I'll take this doll!"

The counter clerk was a young woman who had no idea how many people had asked her the price of this doll over the course of the morning. Every time she said it, the parents would wear a "you might as well be robbing me" expression and forcibly drag their children away. Those two little girls at the case had been pressed there drooling all morning, refusing to leave no matter what.

She was already losing patience. She shot Meng Wan a glance and said coolly, "Seventy a set. If you're buying, pay up quick; if you're not, don't crowd around here."

In this era, the notion that the customer was king hadn't yet caught on. It was still a seller's market, so the service attitude of clerks in state-run stores was basically "take it or leave it," "buy it or don't."

As someone who'd already enjoyed the privilege of quality service, Meng Wan bristled at these words. She was the consumer—the esteemed consumer!

But angry as she was, all she could do now was swallow her broken teeth along with the blood. Gritting her back molars and feeling the fifty yuan in her pocket, she genuinely couldn't afford it.

Yet if she turned and walked off now, wouldn't that just prove she'd been scared off by the price too? The two little girls beside her were grinning up at her, blinking their big eyes, their expressions all but saying, "She can't afford it either."

Just as Meng Wan was stuck astride the tiger with no graceful way down, a lifeline suddenly came from behind her. "We're together. I'll take this doll."

With that, a crisp, new hundred-yuan note was laid on the counter.

Meng Wan turned to look at James in disbelief. Had she just run into the god of wealth?

The corners of the clerk's mouth, taut a moment ago, ticked up imperceptibly the instant she saw James's face, her manner no longer so brusque as it had been with Meng Wan. She took the money, checked it for authenticity, then rummaged in the drawer for a good while and picked out three fairly new ten-yuan notes to give James as change.

"Here's your change. Mind you keep it safe."

Then, under the gaze of the watching crowd, she wrapped that glittering Barbie doll in a pink box and presented it to James with both hands.

James didn't take it. Instead he said politely, "This is a gift from me to this young lady."

Meng Wan blanked for a few seconds before realizing the "young lady" he meant was her. Looking at the doll the clerk held out, under the envious eyes of all the children around her, she accepted this expensive gift—cradling it across her chest like a newborn baby, one hand supporting the bottom and one the head.

Just before leaving, Meng Wan even gave the two little girls beside her a smug wink. Sorry—this doll was hers now!

James couldn't help laughing. He felt Meng Wan must possess some kind of magic; a single simple gesture could strike right at his funny bone—like a moment ago, stretching against the tree, and like now.

*

To express her thanks to James, Meng Wan decided to use the fifty yuan in her pocket to treat him to a meal.

The meal couldn't be too shabby, but it couldn't be too extravagant either—after all, she didn't have much money. In the end she settled on Yuxiang Pavilion.

As the story went, back in the Chongli reign there lived a chef of consummate skill, whose dishes the people of Yuyang praised again and again as marvels.

Word of this reached the palace and came to the emperor's ears, and he too wished to taste this chef's cooking. So he ordered the chef brought into the palace, where the man prepared a fresh, novel table of dishes for the imperial nobles and won the court's acclaim. For this, the emperor bestowed upon his establishment the name "Yuxiang Pavilion."

From then on, Yuxiang Pavilion shot to fame, its custom never ceasing day in and day out, everyone flocking to it on its reputation, hoping to sample the dishes the emperor himself had eaten. Generation after generation of chefs came and went, but this famous name never once toppled.

In modern times, Yuyang weathered countless wars great and small, and Yuxiang Pavilion changed its storefront more times than anyone could count. Handed down to the present day, it now had a history of over three hundred years.

Meng Wan was a regular here. The moment a waiter saw her come in, he deftly moved to lead her to a private room. With the doll cradled in one arm, she hurriedly caught the waiter with the other hand and said in a low voice, "We won't go in today. My friend would like to sit outside and enjoy the view of Yuyang."

She was nearly out of money for the meal—where would she find the funds for a private room?

The waiter cocked his head to glance at the friend behind Meng Wan—a foreigner, no less. Since he wanted to take in the view, then a foreign friend certainly ought to be given the best seat, so he could properly behold their grand Yuyang.

The corner of the second floor by the window was quiet and also offered, through the glass, a sweeping panorama in the distance—unquestionably a prime spot for taking in the scenery.

Meng Wan couldn't help giving the waiter a thumbs-up.

The waiter shot her a look back. No trouble at all.

Smiling, Meng Wan set the doll down on the chair beside her, lining it up in the row with herself, and took the seat across from James.

"James, is there anything you can't eat?" Afraid he might not know what "can't eat" meant, Meng Wan explained further, "I mean, is there anything you don't eat? Scallions, cilantro, that sort of thing?"

James took off his overcoat, folded it in half, and hung it over the back of the chair across from the doll. "I don't eat organ meat."

As a boy he'd lived a stretch in Hong Kong with his mother, Ye Xinzhu. The granny next door, seeing he was a little Westerner, had wanted to make him something novel he'd never tasted, and—having heard somewhere that foreigners had never eaten organ meat—had stewed up a whole pot of pig heart, pig liver, and pig intestines for him.

James genuinely thought it was some fine new delicacy and tried several bites of each. The taste was neither good nor bad—nothing remarkable either way. Only after he'd finished did Ms. Ye tell him what he'd just eaten.

One can well imagine: as a fastidious little gentleman, James couldn't accept that he'd eaten a pig's waste-disposal system, and so he proceeded to retch and retch, nearly heaving up his own intestines. For three days afterward he couldn't keep anything down and could only manage thin rice porridge.

Meng Wan understood, and with practiced ease ordered three dishes and a soup from the waiter, plus a serving of cloud cake. The cloud cake here wasn't as good as Liufuzhai's, but it could rank a solid second.

She tallied up the prices in her head—around forty yuan or so. She could afford it.

Thinking back now, the old her had been so wasteful. The cost of this one meal was enough for an ordinary household's vegetable money for a week or two, and she used to eat out like this two or three times a month.

"Thank you so much for today." Meng Wan took the white teapot the waiter brought over and poured James a cup of tea. "This is our most famous restaurant in town. I've brought lots of friends here, and they all say it's delicious."

James looked around. From the decor to the service, it really was the best of all the restaurants he'd visited these past few days. From where he sat, looking straight out, across the way stood a tall tower—the very Qingyun Tower where they'd met that morning.

Just as he'd thought: he really did need a local to show him around to seize the essence of the place precisely—it saved time and enriched the experience besides. And this Miss Meng before him truly was a capable guide; it was only that her financial situation seemed a bit tight. When the chance arose, he might steer some business her way.

"Are you free tomorrow, Miss Meng?" James lifted his teacup and sniffed it near his nose. It was green tea—the kind he liked.
