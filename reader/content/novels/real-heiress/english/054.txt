Chapter 54

The newspaper that Meng Wan's travel agency had landed in was called the Yuyang Radio and TV Gazette, the highest-circulation paper in Yuyang at the time, already expanded to a sixteen-page, four-column format.

In this era, newspapers were a vital channel through which people gathered all kinds of information. They carried nearly everything—from social news and current affairs to film, entertainment, and lifestyle services. Published just once a week, their column space was precious indeed, and nearly half the residents of Yuyang subscribed to this paper.

Meng Wan's brow creased lightly; the hand folding the map went still, and her heart gave a thud. From the alarmed look on Xu Qiuyun's face, this was almost certainly nothing good. But their travel agency was such an obscure little outfit—what could possibly have happened to land them in the newspaper?

Meng Wan held out her hand, her face taut. "Let me see."

Taking the newspaper and following the point of Xu Qiuyun's finger, Meng Wan spotted her travel agency's name in an article in the lower-left "Society Wide-Angle" section.

The headline read: "Tourists Visit Yuyang—Don't Buy Anything and You Won't Be Allowed to Leave?"

The article described exactly what had happened the day Luo Zhigang clashed with the tourists at the jade shop. Most of it was objective fact, but the single most important point—the "won't be allowed to leave unless you buy something" in the headline—was pure fabrication.

A nameless fury rose in Meng Wan's chest. Wasn't this just making things up to grab eyeballs?

She quickly pinned down the reporter who'd written the piece and the outlet behind it: the Yuyang Press, reporter Xue Guangming.

Guangming—"bright and upright"—my foot. "Dark and shady" was more like it!

In an instant Meng Wan threw the restaurant matter to the back of her mind. Snatching up the newspaper, she rushed out the door. She was going to go have a word with this Mr. Bright-and-Upright—she wanted to see just how someone who made up stories and stopped at nothing to grab attention had ever become a reporter in the first place!

Afraid Meng Wan might lose her temper and come to blows with the man, Xu Qiuyun hurried out the door after her.

Old Bi watched the two girls go storming off and could only pace in circles where he stood, fretting helplessly.

How had they ended up in the newspaper again, just like that? What rotten luck!

Meng Wan pedaled furiously the whole way, working the anger out through her body, so that by the time she arrived most of the rage in her heart had burned off.

She recited to herself silently: she wasn't going there to pick a fight, she was going there to solve a problem.

It was Meng Wan's first time at the Yuyang Press. People bustled in and out. She stopped a passing young woman carrying a stack of documents and asked, "Excuse me, is reporter Xue Guangming with your press?"

The young woman looked Meng Wan up and down, then replied flatly, "Reporter Xue is out on assignment—he's not at the press right now. What do you need him for?"

Meng Wan's eyes rolled as she came up with a reason guaranteed to get her in to see Xue Guangming. "I read the article reporter Xue published in the paper, and I came to report a case of a guide forcing tourists to spend money."

The young woman knew the article Meng Wan meant—it was the first piece Xue had published since joining the press. If he learned his article had had such a fine effect, encouraging the public to come forward with reports, he'd surely be delighted.

She thought it over and decided to take this visitor inside. "Come with me, then. Reporter Xue went out first thing this morning—he should be back soon."

The young woman led Meng Wan and Xu Qiuyun to a long bench in the corridor and told them to sit and wait a while.

Before long, the young woman returned with the man.

"These are the two who said they read your report and want to make a report to you."

Meng Wan tilted her head up and looked Xue Guangming over from head to toe. He was young, wore glasses, kept his hair in a short crew cut, and had a camera slung across his chest—the standard look of a literary youth.

"So you're Xue Guangming?" Meng Wan's tone was none too friendly.

Xue Guangming had been delighted to hear someone had come to make a report because of his article. But Meng Wan's tone didn't sound like a person seeking help—more like someone come looking for trouble.

"You've come to make a report to me?"

Meng Wan stood up and introduced herself politely. "I'm the manager of the Youth Travel Agency from your article. My name is Meng Wan."

In an instant Xue Guangming understood the reason behind Meng Wan's attitude, and a flicker of distaste rose in his eyes. "What, I expose the unscrupulous thing your agency did, and you come looking to settle the score because you can't take it?"

What a shameless man!

Meng Wan held her ground, neither cowed nor overbearing. "Our Youth Travel Agency conducts itself honestly and aboveboard. I don't know what 'unscrupulous thing' reporter Xue is referring to."

"Honest and aboveboard?" Xue Guangming said with contempt. "You force tourists to spend money and beat them up—is that what you call honest and aboveboard?"

Meng Wan suppressed the urge to punch him and clenched her fists to calm herself down. "Reporter Xue, what evidence do you have for saying so? On what grounds do you publish an article like this in the newspaper? This is slander against our travel agency. If it harms our business, I can take you to court."

Hit with Meng Wan's string of questions, Xue Guangming couldn't help but feel a bellyful of anger. "Every word of my article was written on the basis of solid, genuine evidence. I never publish an article without evidence to support it—that's my professional bottom line!"

"Fine, then tell me what this evidence is!" Meng Wan was fired up now too, her voice climbing higher and higher.

Afraid she might get physical, Xu Qiuyun tugged at her sleeve from behind, reminding her to stay calm.

"That day I was right there at the scene. I witnessed the whole thing happen with my own eyes. Afterward I interviewed one of the tourists, and he stated plainly that he'd been dragged off and forced to spend money!"

This assignment had been the very first task the editor-in-chief had handed Xue Guangming after he joined the press—on the problems plaguing the development of Yuyang's tourism. The angle Xue had chosen was the phenomenon of crooked guides forcing tourists to spend. So lately he'd been staking out the city's jade shops and silk shops one by one. By chance, the day he came to this particular jade shop, he'd run straight into the whole episode with Luo Zhigang and the tourists. He'd followed them all the way back to their hotel and, after everyone had left, interviewed the central figure of the incident—the man in glasses. The article he'd written was based entirely on what he'd seen and heard and on the facts as recounted by the party involved.

Hearing how categorically he stated his case, Meng Wan couldn't help sinking into thought. If what he said was true, then either Luo Zhigang had lied to her—or there was another possibility: the tourist had given false testimony.

She leaned toward the latter, because Luo Zhigang had no reason to lie to her.

Seeing Meng Wan fall silent, Xue Guangming assumed she'd conceded. "Well? Nothing left to say, is there? You didn't think we reporters were the same as you guides—doing whatever it takes to make money."

Meng Wan lifted her chin slightly, her gaze locked on his face, and challenged him. "How can you be sure the testimony the tourist gave you was all true? What if he held a grudge against our travel agency and fed you false testimony?"

"Impossible!" Xue Guangming flatly denied it. "That tourist wrote me a statement attesting that everything he'd said was true. He even signed his name at the end and pressed his fingerprint to it."

"May I see this statement?"

Xue Guangming hesitated for a few seconds, but to prove himself right, he produced the statement for Meng Wan to see.

He pulled a folded sheet of paper from his briefcase, spread it open, and thrust it in front of Meng Wan's face. "Here, take a good look—this is the one."

Meng Wan's gaze went straight to the signature at the very bottom. Three characters were written there: "Hong Sanbao," with a red fingerprint pressed over the name.

She rapidly ran through the roster of that batch of tourists in her mind. After the incident, when she'd organized the paperwork, she'd looked over that list, and she was quite certain there was no one surnamed Hong in the group. This "Hong Sanbao" was a fake name.

"Reporter Xue, did you verify this Hong Sanbao's identity documents? Did his ID also say 'Hong Sanbao'?"

Xue Guangming's heart gave a lurch. At the time he'd been so intent on getting the evidence quickly that he'd completely forgotten to verify it—or rather, he'd taken it for granted that the man was telling the truth, so there'd been no need to check his identity.

Although a thread of doubt had already crept into his mind, he stubbornly refused to back down on the surface. "Of course I verified it. That's the professional competence of us reporters."

"Then let me ask you this, reporter Xue—if you reporters publish a piece of false news, what would your professional competence have you do about it?"

"Print an apology and publicly set the record straight!"

"And how do we account for the damage this article has done to our travel agency's reputation?"

From Xue Guangming's words, all Meng Wan sensed was sheer arrogance—as if his publicly apologizing were something she ought to be deeply grateful for. But apologizing when you've done wrong is the most basic of decencies.

For a moment Xue Guangming was lost for words. Her travel agency had clearly done something wrong, yet with her relentless, aggressive air it was somehow as if he were the villain. Sure enough, criminals always proclaim their own innocence.

"Would resigning be enough to make it up to you?"

He firmly believed he'd done nothing wrong. That Hong Sanbao had spoken with such heartfelt sincerity at the time—he hadn't seemed like a liar.

"Good!" Meng Wan held her head high, stubborn as ever. "Then do you dare come with me to our travel agency right now? The identity records you don't have, I do. Let's go and see exactly who this Hong Sanbao really is!"

Tourists in a group booked through the agency all had to submit their identity information in a standardized way and sign a registration form that recorded the personal details they'd filled in themselves.

If the name "Hong Sanbao" was fabricated, then the truthfulness of the information provided under that false name was in doubt. And if so, Xue Guangming's report lost any standing it had to be published at all.

"Fine, you're on—let's go!"

------

After Meng Wan showed Xue Guangming the tourist roster and records, his first reaction was that she'd faked them all. In the end, at his insistence, Meng Wan placed a long-distance call to one of the tourists who'd left a landline number.

The person on the other end personally confirmed that there was no one named "Hong Sanbao" in the group, and that the guide, Luo Zhigang, had not forced them to spend money at all—he'd merely taken them to the jade shop, without ever using a single word of threat to make them buy anything.

One phone call wasn't enough. Suspecting Meng Wan might have coached the witness, Xue Guangming proceeded to make several more calls in a row, and every answer he got was the same.

At last Xue Guangming's hopes died. The very first report he'd taken such pride in had turned out to be a slander smearing other people. The principle he'd learned in school—that a news reporter must take objective fact as his foundation—had become a sharp blade turned back upon himself. He had failed his alma mater, failed the people at the center of his story, failed the very profession of journalism.

"I'll print my letter of apology in the next edition of the paper, to clear your travel agency's name." His face utterly crestfallen, he paused. "As for resigning—I'll have to go back to the press first and explain things to the editor-in-chief. Once I've handed over the work on my plate, I'll resign of my own accord."

"Good. I look forward to your apology!"

After Xue Guangming had gone, Xu Qiuyun, who'd been watching the whole battle from the sidelines, finally voiced the question on her mind. "Do you really want this reporter Xue to resign?"

Reporter Xue had indeed done wrong, but he'd been wholly devoted to his work, hoping to make Yuyang's tourism better through his reporting. His intentions were good—he'd simply lacked experience, and a good heart had brought about a bad outcome.

Meng Wan crossed her arms, her eyes fixed on the direction Xue Guangming had gone, and said in a flat tone, "Don't worry. He won't resign. I only wanted to take him down a peg, to make him understand that letting first impressions rule you is a reporter's gravest sin."

Nowadays, the process of training someone from school all the way into a fine reporter was a long one, so reporters were a scarce commodity. The press wasn't about to let him go over a single mistake.

Besides, Meng Wan hadn't the time just now to worry over whether Xue Guangming resigned. She was busy fretting over the negative fallout this article would bring her travel agency. After all, people were always more willing to believe the worst of someone, and as for a correction, everyone would have their own take on it—some might even decide Meng Wan had bribed the reporter and the press into writing that apology piece.

It really was a case of a rumor taking one mouth to start and a thousand running feet to dispel.

"Hello, I'm looking for Meng Wan."

A voice drifted in from outside the door, and Meng Wan heard someone calling her name.

Xu Qiuyun recognized him and brought him to Meng Wan's office.

Meng Wan looked up. A familiar face came into view, and she said, mildly surprised, "Song Qingyang?"
