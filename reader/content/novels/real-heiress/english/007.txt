Chapter 7

Tomorrow was Monday, and Meng Wan still had to go to work at the cultural center, but if James needed her, she could always ask for time off.

"Are you planning to keep sightseeing tomorrow?"

"Not just tomorrow. I'll be here the whole coming week, so I'd like to find a guide who knows the local area and understands its history and culture to spend the week with me."

James savored the lingering sweetness on his palate. It was very fragrant; he liked this tea.

A whole week. Meng Wan rapidly drew up her plans, already slipping into the role of guide and starting to map out an itinerary for him.

Seeing that she hadn't made a sound, James assumed it was a question of money, so he immediately quoted his price. The previous guide had charged fifty yuan a day, which was already a mid-to-high rate, and he was willing to offer Meng Wan the very top rate.

"How about a hundred yuan a day?"

The moment money came up, Meng Wan snapped to attention, her eyes lighting up as she looked at James.

James pressed his lips into a faint smile. Today he was witnessing a real-life case of eyes lighting up at the sight of money.

"Deal!" Afraid she might change her mind, Meng Wan agreed at once.

"Food's coming up!"

The waiter's footsteps thumping up the stairs cut off their conversation.

"Pork aspic with garlic, braised Yellow River carp, tofu with glass noodles, and pork-rib soup with pickled cabbage. That's everything—please enjoy."

Meng Wan arranged the dishes neatly and looked at James expectantly. "Go on, try it."

James picked up his chopsticks and tasted each dish in turn, from left to right. He liked them all, but his favorite was the garlic pork aspic—an ordinary aspic lifted by vinegar and minced garlic into something sour, spicy, and appetizing, wonderfully refreshing.

It was the best meal he'd had in days.

Meng Wan felt as though she'd sat an exam, and seeing James eat with such relish, she figured the results were satisfactory.

The leisurely dinner came to an end, and as the sky darkened, James suggested they call it a day after this meal. Meng Wan apologized for taking up some of his afternoon—otherwise she could have shown him the wild grottoes.

James, for his part, was easygoing about it. He had several more days; there was no rush.

Meng Wan promised that the moment she got home tonight, she'd draw up a detailed travel plan for him, guaranteeing he'd eat well and have a good time for the rest of the week.

James looked forward to whatever new surprises Meng Wan might bring him.

When the bill came, James again insisted on paying for the meal. He said a gentleman would never let his female companion pay—it simply wasn't polite—and he added that he'd thoroughly enjoyed the dinner and thanked Meng Wan for bringing him here.

In the past, when Meng Wan brought other friends here to eat, they thanked her too, but James's thanks weren't like theirs. Every word he said was so sincere that it couldn't simply be brushed aside.

Since the Yuxiang Restaurant wasn't far from the Meng home, Meng Wan said she could walk back on her own, but James insisted on seeing her home himself. In his view, a girl walking alone at night was a dangerous thing.

Seeing how firm he was, Meng Wan let him have his way. She'd walked this road countless times, but with a kind, handsome man insisting on seeing her home, she had no reason to refuse.

Along the way, Meng Wan asked which hotel James was staying at, the two of them agreed on a time to meet tomorrow, and she told him about the history of Yuyang.

Talking as they walked, they soon reached the foot of the Meng family's building. Meng Wan, still reluctant for it to end, thanked James—not just for seeing her home, but for dinner, and for the doll. In this half a day, she'd come away richer than she could have hoped.

It was nearly eight o'clock, and the moon had drifted up into the sky. Meng Wan didn't go inside. Holding the doll, she stood beside the tree and quietly watched James's retreating figure until the man in black melted completely into the endless night.

For some reason, Meng Wan had a powerful premonition: this would be the beginning of a new life for her, a new road being paved just for her.

*

Brimming with happiness, Meng Wan carried the doll home, ready to give little Zhizhi a surprise.

In this era there weren't many forms of entertainment, and one of the family's few shared pastimes was watching television.

When Meng Wan got back, the TV was showing Empress Wu Zetian, with Grandma Liu Xiaoqing slaying all comers on screen.

Cheng Xuehua had turned to check the time on the clock, thinking that Meng Wan had been out so long without coming back and worrying that something had happened to her. Just then she caught her daughter tiptoeing in through the door, holding a pink box. She was about to ask why she was back so late when Meng Wan immediately made a shushing gesture and pointed at the doll in her arms.

Cheng Xuehua understood her meaning at once. Pretending nothing was amiss, she turned away and shot a sidelong glance at Zhizhi, who was watching the TV intently.

Sun Jing, just then, happened to be carrying a basin of freshly washed laundry out to hang up, and she saw her young sister-in-law creep up behind Zhizhi and slowly lower the box holding the doll from above the child's head down in front of her eyes.

Zhizhi, who had been absorbed in watching a man and a woman talking on the TV, felt a sudden "whoosh," and as if by magic, the gift she had been longing for day and night appeared right before her.

"A Barbie doll!"

Zhizhi cried out in her soft, sweet little voice, full of surprise and excitement. She'd been sitting cross-legged on the sofa the way Meng Wan had taught her, but now she shot straight to her feet, her two short little arms hugging the doll box tight, her face so happy it practically dripped honey.

It really was better to be a child—happiness could be that simple.

Seeing their granddaughter so delighted, Cheng Xuehua and Meng Qingyan exchanged a glance. This was the joy of family, nothing more.

The smile in Meng Wan's eyes deepened. She sat down next to Zhizhi and fished for praise. "Well? Didn't Auntie keep her word? I didn't lie to you, did I?"

Zhizhi's big eyes sparkled, and the corners of her mouth nearly flew up to the sky. She lavished on Meng Wan every flattering word she'd ever learned: "Auntie, you're the best auntie in the whole world. I love you so much, I love you the most. When I grow up I'll definitely take good care of you. I'll buy you lots and lots of new clothes and take you out for lots and lots of yummy food."

Smiling, Meng Wan stroked Zhizhi's smooth little cheek, thinking, Which man did she learn to make such big empty promises from?

"Auntie doesn't need you to take care of her. Auntie just hopes you grow up healthy and happy."

Sun Jing, hanging up the laundry, listened to the goings-on over there, and her eyes couldn't help welling up, her heart filling with regret and guilt.

The day before, she'd taken Zhizhi back to her own mother's home to stay the night, and in the course of chatting, Meng Wan had come up—which was how Zhizhi had come out with those things during the day.

Her own family was very ordinary. She had a younger brother and a younger sister, and the whole family had tightened their belts to put her through university. As a student her monthly living allowance had been less than a hundred yuan, and it wasn't until she started working that she owned her first set of new clothes bought from a department store.

So ever since she'd married into the Meng family and watched Meng Wan effortlessly live the life she herself had dreamed of, it had been hard for her to accept. Meng Wan's attitude toward money battered, again and again, against everything Sun Jing had been raised to believe.

A whole salary spent on clothes; when the money ran out, she just asked her parents for more; every month she went to the Yuxiang Restaurant to feast several times; song-and-dance halls, the disco square—she went wherever and whenever she pleased.

In her heart of hearts, Sun Jing thought her sister-in-law was thoroughly spoiled and immature. More than once she'd wanted to teach her a lesson—but her own father- and mother-in-law said nothing, so what standing did she have to step forward and find fault?

She didn't like this sister-in-law of hers; her heart was full of resentment toward her. Yet the laughable thing was that Meng Wan had never done anything to wrong her. On the contrary, she loved Zhizhi dearly and would give Sun Jing the clothes she no longer wore and the skincare products others had gifted her.

The truth was, Sun Jing had always known: she disliked Meng Wan not because Meng Wan threw money around, but because of her own jealousy. If she could choose, she too would have liked to live as Meng Wan once. It was this warped jealousy that had bred her loathing—and the person she loathed had done nothing to hurt anyone. Meng Wan had simply lived life her own way, and for that alone she'd drawn down such great resentment.

While Sun Jing was quietly reflecting, Meng Wan was explaining to her parents why she'd come home late.

She couldn't yet tell them about meeting James. The cultural center job had been hard to secure, and she'd only just started; if she told them now that she wanted to take time off to guide someone around for money, Meng Qingyan would certainly never agree. So Meng Wan planned to keep the family in the dark for the time being.

So she gave the excuse she'd prepared in advance: a new batch of books had come in at the cultural center and needed to be registered, and once she'd finished she'd gone to buy Zhizhi a doll and had dinner with a colleague that evening.

Watching his daughter so happy, Meng Xingyang thanked Meng Wan on her behalf. "That doll wasn't cheap, was it? You barely have enough to spend on yourself—stop buying her things on a whim from now on."

Hearing Meng Xingyang's brand of "thanks," Meng Wan curled her lip. "I'm happy to. What business is it of yours if I spoil my niece?"

"Auntie's the best. Daddy's mean."

With one hand clutching the doll, Zhizhi flung herself into Meng Wan's arms.

Meng Xingyang gave a helpless laugh. "Fine, you and your auntie are family, and I'm the outsider."

Hearing this teasing remark, Meng Wan's eyes dimmed a little. In this household, from beginning to end, she was the only true outsider.

*

Once everyone had gone off to their rooms to rest, Meng Wan phoned a colleague and asked her to put in for leave on her behalf—gastroenteritis was the excuse, and she'd need at least a week off.

Her colleague even kindly offered a few words of comfort. Afraid she'd give herself away if she said too much, Meng Wan mumbled something vague and hung up.

With all that settled, Meng Wan got down to burning the midnight oil. The last time she'd worked this hard was cramming the night before the college entrance exam.

Meng Wan's room wasn't large, but she'd made it very cozy. A single bed, three and a half feet wide, was spread with the pink floral sheets she loved. Beside the head of the bed stood a desk—an old office desk discarded by Meng Qingyan's school—facing two windows, on the sill of which sat a pot of crab-apple blossom that Cheng Xuehua had transplanted from the hospital. The wardrobe stood by the door; hers was the largest in the family, yet even so she felt it wasn't enough and was forever clamoring to buy a smaller one to put at the foot of the bed.

Meng Wan first took a long-unused fountain pen from the pen holder and filled its belly with ink, then pulled a new diary out of the drawer. She remembered that there'd been a craze for keeping diaries for a while, and full of enthusiasm she'd bought several, ready to record her days—but she'd written for three days and then couldn't be bothered, and the notebooks had sat gathering dust in the drawer ever since.

She'd also asked Meng Xingyang for two new maps of Yuyang and dug out a Gazetteer of Yuyang City she'd brought home from the cultural center. You didn't notice it until you looked closely, and once she looked closely it gave her a fright.

Yuyang, it turned out, had more than thirty scenic spots, large and small, open to visitors. Spread over seven days, that meant five or six spots a day. Being a guide really did take some brainpower—right now she had to work out how to spend the least money, walk the least distance, and still see as many spots as possible.

Meng Wan decided to divide things by area, working from far to near. Some places she'd been to and knew roughly the route and layout of; others had only opened in recent years, weren't very famous, and she hadn't visited yet. Fortunately there weren't many of those—she'd been to most of the spots on the map, which was why she'd dared to take James's job in the first place.

On the map she circled together the spots in the same area, then arranged them in her notebook in order from far to near, estimating the distances and, based on the size of each site, allotting time for the visit. She also marked any good restaurants nearby as best she could, to avoid wasting time hunting for somewhere to eat on the day.

By the time she'd written it all out, she'd filled exactly seven pages, one for each of the seven days. She glanced at the time—already two in the morning. Satisfied, Meng Wan set down her pen and looked up at the moon outside the window. There was a strange thrill in writing furiously like this in the dead of night.

Her work wasn't finished, though. The site commentaries were what mattered most—the very reason James had chosen her. Meng Wan opened the Gazetteer of Yuyang City and went over everything they'd visit on the first day. Luckily she'd read this book several times; times had changed, but because the subject genuinely interested her, picking it back up came easily.

Slowly her eyelids grew heavier and heavier, and as she read on, she slumped over the desk and drifted off to sleep.
