Chapter 37

To keep Meng Wan's true identity under wraps for the time being, Qin Yi'e invented a cover story for her: she was the granddaughter of a maternal cousin of Qin Yi'e's mother, come back this time to honor her grandmother's dying wish—to set eyes once more on the old homeland.

This branch was the only relative in the family who had married into a big city. They had long since fallen out of contact over the years, and no one had imagined the connection would ever come in handy like this.

Qin Yizhong did remember that his mother had a female cousin, though she had married and left Dongping Village back when he was small. He never would have guessed that her descendants were now living so well.

Zhou Nian stood off to one side, silent, secretly sizing Meng Wan up. A moment ago her little aunt had clearly said she knew Zhou Nian was about to be married—but how did she know that?

And ever since Qin Yi'e had come back from talking with this woman, something about her had been off; her eyes were red, as though she'd been crying. Could a distant relative she hadn't seen in all these years really stir such deep feeling?

The two questions circled overhead, leaving Zhou Nian doubtful about who Meng Wan really was.

Zhou Nian had an elder brother and an elder sister above her. The brother was called Zhou Zheng, the sister Zhou Fang; both had married and started their own families and no longer lived with Qin Yi'e's household.

Learning that Meng Wan really was a relative of the family, Ding Chunling hurried to play the warm hostess. "Don't just stand there—come in, come in and sit."

A country household had no sofa. Ding Chunling dragged over two wooden stools, their grain blackened with age, and set them out for the guests.

Meng Wan sat down, her gaze roaming over the room, which could only be described as run-down. Against the far wall stood a tall cabinet, with a low chest fitted up beside it; on top sat a clock whose pendulum swung back and forth. The wooden furniture had long been smoke-darkened. In the very center of the room stood a table and a few stools—and that was all. Bare, with nothing else.

Qin Yi'e's mind had long since been knocked loose by the shock, leaving her in no state to play the gracious host. Seeing that her sister-in-law made no move, Ding Chunling stepped in to fill the role herself, pouring tea and water for Meng Wan and James.

If they'd come to reconnect with the family, it meant these relatives still mattered to them. As for herself—she was stuck in this little backwater for the rest of her life. But her two children had to get out, and this Meng Wan was clearly a worldly type from the big city. If she ever proved useful down the road, it could only help to stay on her good side.

Smiling, she settled in close to Meng Wan and said warmly, "The moment I laid eyes on you back on that train, I felt such a closeness to you. Who'd have thought we'd actually turn out to be family? It's almost too perfect to be chance."

Meng Wan only smiled, cradling her cup and keeping her head lowered, saying nothing.

Qin Yizhong leaned against the door frame and couldn't hold back his curiosity. "So you're from Yuyang too?"

Ding Chunling's home village was there. She had gotten together with him during the years she'd been sent down to the countryside, and afterward had never managed to go back.

"Yes. I grew up in Yuyang."

"And your parents—what do they do?" he pressed on.

If you traced it out by that logic, her parents would count as his cousins too.

Meng Wan let her eyes flick over the head-bowed Qin Yi'e, then over Zhou Nian. "My father is a university professor. My mother is a doctor."

Well now. That was something.

Qin Yizhong had taken Meng Wan for an ordinary city person, the same as his wife's family—who, though they lived in town, were six people crammed into two little flat-roofed rooms, an ordinary worker's household. His eyes lit up in an instant. Her family had to be loaded, then. That made sense—you only had to look at how the girl was dressed; there was clearly no shortage of money at home.

To think his own family—farmers who had tilled the soil for generations—had relatives this high up, real intellectuals.

"So are you in school, or...?"

"Right now I work as a tour guide."

Qin Yizhong scratched the back of his head, puzzled. "What's a tour guide do, exactly?"

Shanshan jumped in to answer. "I know! A tour guide is like a teacher who explains things to people who go traveling."

Money was a wonderful thing, Qin Yizhong thought with a sigh. He'd only had enough to eat for a few years, and already folks like her were off traveling. The world out there really was vast.

Meng Wan turned to Zhou Nian, who hadn't made a sound the whole time, and asked gently, "Are you still in school?"

She already knew the answer; she only wanted an opening to get the conversation going.

Zhou Nian clasped her hands behind her back, fidgeting. At the question her eyes dimmed, and she gave a small shake of her head.

"Do you still want to study?"

Zhou Nian didn't understand why she was being asked this. She turned to glance at her head-bowed mother, then said, a little stiffly, "I'm already twenty-two. In a few more days I'm getting married."

What she meant was: she was a woman on the verge of marriage—at her age, how could she go back to school? People would laugh.

Meng Wan drew her own conclusion. "So you do want to."

She followed it up at once. "This Sun Jianxing—do you like him?"

A question that blunt startled Zhou Nian a little. She bit her lip lightly and answered in a voice no bigger than a mosquito's hum, "I don't know."

No one had ever asked her that before. She and Sun Jianxing had met only twice before the match was settled. All she knew was that the two of them were about to marry and make a home; as for the liking or not-liking that Meng Wan spoke of, she didn't understand it. At any rate, she didn't dislike him.

"So you don't like him." Meng Wan had a rough read on it now. If Zhou Nian really did like this Sun Jianxing, things would have been genuinely difficult.

Just as everyone sat listening, bewildered, to this string of questions of Meng Wan's that seemed to come out of nowhere, someone else arrived in the courtyard.

It was a young woman with a single thick braid and high, prominent cheekbones, shrewdness written all over her at a glance. Beside her followed an old woman with a stooped, bowed back.

Ding Chunling murmured in surprise, "Isn't that Sun Jianxing's mother and his sister-in-law? Why have they come together?"

There was an elder brother in Sun Jianxing's family too, except the eldest wasn't his mother's natural son. His mother was the wife his father had taken after the brother's birth mother died—a replacement bride.

So the two Sun brothers weren't on especially good terms, though it never came to drawn daggers either; things between them were simply flat. Children bound through the father's line, after all, were never as close as those bound through the mother's.

But Sun Jianxing's sister-in-law, Zhao Xiuqin, was a formidable piece of work, and on top of that Sun Jianxing's mother had a soft temperament—so these days the affairs of the Sun household were basically run by the sister-in-law.

Meng Wan looked toward the courtyard, the two black pupils rolling in her eyes like beads on an abacus. The turning point, she thought, might just have arrived.

Qin Yi'e stole a glance at Meng Wan, then went out to the courtyard to receive the visitors.

The moment Zhao Xiuqin came through the gate, she saw Meng Wan and James seated dead center. She clearly hadn't expected two such out-of-place figures to surface at the Zhou house, and for a moment her gaze raked over the pair, until she forgot what she'd even come to do.

Ding Chunling planted herself in front of Meng Wan and James with a disdainful air and asked, frowning, "What instructions has the eldest sister-in-law come to deliver?"

Zhao Xiuqin rolled her eyes at Ding Chunling and, not about to tire herself out, marched straight over and sat down on the stool Ding Chunling had just been using.

"Can't I come have a look at my future sister-in-law?"

As she said it, she shot a glance at Zhou Nian in the inner doorway.

Her tone was anything but kind. This was no friendly visit—she'd plainly come to pick a fight.

Qin Yi'e helped the Sun mother into a seat and asked, her face cold, "Whatever you've got to say, just say it."

She knew Zhao Xiuqin's character all too well. From the engagement talks to now, the woman hadn't shown her face once; if she'd come picking faults today, it was surely because something had displeased her.

"All right, then I'll come right out with it." Zhao Xiuqin seized the opening. "That bride-price money—half of it has to come back."

"What did you say?" Ding Chunling thought she'd misheard. It was the first time she'd ever seen anyone ask for a bride-price back.

Not feeling in the least that her demand was unreasonable, Zhao Xiuqin gave her head a haughty toss and repeated it loudly. "I said—the bride-price money—give me back half of it!"

Even the usually composed Qin Yizhong couldn't sit still for that. He raised his voice. "Since ancient times, a bride-price given out has never been one to be taken back. And besides, that bride-price is for my sister and my niece—why on earth should it go back to you?"

Even if the marriage fell through, it would be returned to the Sun family.

"Whether it makes sense or not is none of your concern." Zhao Xiuqin shot him a look. "I've got my own reasons."

Before anyone else could speak, Meng Wan cut in sharply, "And what reasons would those be?"

Meng Wan's tone, though level, pressed down on Zhao Xiuqin with real weight. She didn't carry herself with the same swagger she'd used on the Qin couple; instead she shot back sullenly, "What business is it of yours? Who even are you?"

"I'm a relative of this family. Their affairs are my affairs." Meng Wan put on another smile. "Eldest sister-in-law, whatever grievances you've got, lay them all out for the rest of us to hear. Money matters are easy to settle. If you've got right on your side, there's no harm in giving the whole sum back to you."

Give it all back and there'd be no wedding to hold, Meng Wan muttered inwardly.

"Really?" At the word "money" Zhao Xiuqin's eyes lit up.

Ding Chunling and Qin Yizhong both turned to look at Meng Wan, wanting to ask what scheme she was running. But Qin Yi'e hadn't said a word, so they didn't feel they could speak up either.

"Then I'll say it." Zhao Xiuqin pushed up her sleeves and began, unhurried. "Sun Jianguo and I married for love. When I married into the Sun family, the household was dirt poor. I didn't ask for a single cent of bride-price back then—I just had my heart set on him as a person. I figured, as long as he was good to me, we could get through it no matter how hard life was."

She let out a long sigh. "Things have eased up a bit these last couple of years. The only thing left undone in the family was the little brother-in-law's marriage. We've all worn ourselves out worrying over it. When the little brother managed to land Zhou Nian for a bride, I was happy for him too—but..."

Everything up front was courtesy. What came after was the crux.

The crowd listened in silence.

Zhao Xiuqin's tone shifted, turning sharp. "But why is it that when I got married, the family couldn't bear to part with a single cent of bride-price, yet the moment it's the little brother's turn, they can suddenly cough up this much?"

Yes, she had said she wouldn't take any. But not taking it didn't mean none was owed her. Hand over a bride-price like this for Zhou Nian, and wasn't it announcing to everyone that she, Zhao Xiuqin, was the cheap goods, lesser than Zhou Nian the second daughter-in-law? With what face would she go on living in the Sun household after that?

The Sun mother explained anxiously, "Half of that is my own dowry money. It isn't all the Sun family's funds."

"Your dowry?" That set Zhao Xiuqin off. "Day in and day out you swear you treat both sons the same—so why didn't you bring out your dowry when Jianguo got married? Now it's your own son's turn, and out comes the money, and the bracelet gets handed over too?"

With that she grabbed the wrist of the Zhou Nian standing nearby, and a clear, translucent jade bracelet swayed and swung on the slender wrist.

The Sun mother, stung into anger, started bickering for her son's sake. "Yes, I said those things—but Jianxing is flesh and blood of my own body. Of course I'm going to think of him more. You're a mother yourself now—surely you understand what I'm saying? And aside from the bride-price and the jewelry, I've never treated Jianguo unfairly. Good clothes, good food—whatever could be split evenly, I split; what couldn't, I always let him have first. As a stepmother, my conscience is clear!"

"A clear conscience, what a fine thing." Zhao Xiuqin came right back, relentless. "Those piddling little favors you put on for show, and you talk them up as if they were some great kindness. Add the whole lot together and it still wouldn't outweigh this one jade bracelet."

As she spoke she clamped down harder on Zhou Nian's wrist. Yanked into pain, Zhou Nian let out a soft hiss and tried several times to pull her hand free, but it was no use.

Meng Wan, her eyes fixed on the wrist, stood up and walked over, prying Zhao Xiuqin's hand off by force. "Talk if you want to talk. Keep your hands to yourself."

Zhao Xiuqin gave an angry snort and turned her face away.

"Everyone, calm down." Meng Wan took on the role of peacemaker. "Here's what we'll do. Since you can't reach an agreement on the bride-price, today we'll return all of it. Once you've talked it out among yourselves, there's no harm in revisiting the marriage then."

By that time she'd have whisked Zhou Nian far away. Sun Jianxing could go marry whoever he pleased—the one thing that couldn't happen was Zhou Nian marrying into this.

"That won't do!" The Sun mother shot to her feet in a panic, words tumbling out. "The wedding date is set, the relatives have all been told—how can you just call it off like that!"

Meng Wan knew she'd never agree, so she went to work on Zhao Xiuqin instead. "Is that what you think too?"

For a moment Zhao Xiuqin was at a loss. All she'd meant to do was make a scene, throw her weight around, let the Zhou family see just how formidable the eldest Sun daughter-in-law was, so they wouldn't dare slight her over bride-price money in the future. She never imagined they'd actually be willing to give the money back.

This touched on her brother-in-law's marriage. If she wrecked his match, she'd have no way to answer to her father-in-law.

Seeing her waver, Meng Wan threw another log on the fire at once. "You're the eldest daughter-in-law of the Sun family. By rights half the bride-price should be yours—and if you claim this sum now, it goes straight into your own pocket. You won't have to hand it to your parents."

It was a powerful temptation. In this era a bride-price all went to the bride's parents; how much, if any, trickled down to the daughter depended entirely on their conscience.

Zhao Xiuqin's eyes rolled as she rapidly worked the abacus in her head. Even if she turned the money down today, no one would think the better of her for it—better, then, to harden her heart and claim it back. Let people think what they would; money in her own hand was what mattered most.

At that thought, Zhao Xiuqin made the second bold decision of her life. "I want the money!"
