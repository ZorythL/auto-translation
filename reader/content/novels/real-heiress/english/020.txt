Chapter 20

At the hotel, Meng Wan checked the guests against the rooming list, registered each of them at the front desk one by one, and handed out the room cards. She reminded everyone to keep their luggage close and guard their belongings carefully, never to let valuables out of their sight if they could help it. Then she asked them all to gather in the lobby in half an hour—sweetening the request with the promise of good food.

Yang Zhiming came back down quickly, saying he wanted to go over the schedule with her. Meng Wan took out her itinerary notebook to show him; it covered nearly every place he'd asked for, and she'd added a few more sights of her own that captured the essence of Yuyang.

Her itinerary was meticulous. Taking into account that the elders' stamina wouldn't keep up, she hadn't packed it too tightly, leaving ample time for rest in between. During those breaks she would tell them about Yuyang's history, so they could better appreciate how each of these sights had come to be.

Yang Zhiming was very pleased with the itinerary. Whatever Meng Wan's ability turned out to be, the sheer thoroughness of this plan revealed how seriously she took the work—and sometimes attitude mattered more than ability.

"So what's Guide Meng planning to feed us?" Half his burden lifted, Yang Zhiming finally relaxed and fell into easy conversation with her.

Meng Wan put the notebook away, keeping up the suspense. "You'll find out soon enough."

As expected, for the group's first meal on Yuyang soil, Meng Wan played it safe and chose Yuxiang Restaurant. It catered to every taste and could hardly go wrong.

She'd already reserved two large round tables there the day before, each seating ten; with Meng Wan and Yang Zhiming squeezing in, there was room enough. The restaurant had a set menu for tourists—ten dishes in all, signifying "perfection on every count"—encompassing every one of Yuxiang Restaurant's signature specialties.

The server led them up to the second floor. Meng Wan instinctively glanced toward the window side—empty. She didn't quite know what she'd been expecting.

Once she had everyone settled, Meng Wan deliberately seated the two rivals, Auntie Bai and Auntie Luo, at different tables, to keep their paths from crossing as much as possible.

The dishes came out fast. As they arrived, Meng Wan introduced each one—its origins, how it was made. All those times she'd eaten here hadn't gone to waste; at last they were proving useful.

The old folks listened to Meng Wan's commentary, tasting each dish as it was served and discussing it afterward, full of praise.

Once all the dishes were out, everyone tucked in with lively cheer. Meng Wan found an opening and squeezed in, and just as she was about to throw caution to the wind and dig in, she looked up and caught sight of a familiar figure coming up the stairs.

It was James, accompanied by a man in a sharp suit carrying a briefcase—evidently here to discuss business.

In an instant Meng Wan lost her appetite. She was still hesitating over whether to go say hello when James's eyes met hers full-on.

"Meng Wan?" James called her name in delight. They hadn't seen each other in a long while, not since they'd parted at the hotel last time.

Meng Wan set down her chopsticks and gave a small, dazed smile. "What a coincidence, James."

The old folks keenly sensed the undercurrent stirring, and their eyes all turned toward the stairway. Auntie Bai's eyes lit up at the sight of the handsome young man, and she said gossipily, "Guide Meng, who's this? Come, introduce him to us."

This Auntie Bai—one moment she'd been questioning Meng Wan's abilities, and the next, the sight of a handsome young man had her wearing an entirely different face.

Meng Wan was still hesitating over how to begin when James walked up with a smile and long strides. "Hello, uncles and aunties. I'm a friend of Meng Wan's. My name is Chen Jinghua—you can also call me James."

"Guide Meng even has a foreign friend." One of the uncles marveled. "And this foreign friend speaks pretty good Mandarin too."

Hearing how the uncle addressed Meng Wan, James gathered she must have properly become a guide.

"I'd say I was the first tourist Meng Wan ever guided. We've been friends ever since." James curved his lips and looked at Meng Wan. "She's the best guide I've ever met, and I'm sure that, with her help, all of you will come away with a beautiful memory of Yuyang, just as I have."

Meng Wan gazed at James, stunned. Was he—speaking up for her?

"Just what I said." Auntie Luo seized the moment to strike. "Some people have nothing better to do than fret over what's none of their business—jealous that Guide Meng here is young."

Auntie Bai flared up at the words and slapped her chopsticks down, demanding loudly, "Who are you talking about?"

Yang Zhiming had been on edge these past days; the instant the two of them opened their mouths, he'd leap in to break it up. "Harmony brings prosperity, harmony brings prosperity. Out here we represent the spirit of the capital's people—we mustn't bring shame on the capital."

These words worked wonders on the older generation, especially cadres like these, who had a fierce sense of collective honor. The two of them turned their faces away and ignored each other.

Seeing the situation steady, Meng Wan drew James aside to catch up.

"How's your hotel coming along?"

James had been wanting to share with her for ages. "I've already lined up the best architect and construction team. The build is expected to take half a year."

He glanced over his shoulder. "This is the top graduate I brought in from Singapore—a Nanyang Technological University alumnus. He's in charge of the hotel's architecture."

Meng Wan followed his gaze to the man behind him and nodded with a smile, greeting the architect.

"So you're a full-fledged guide now?" James asked curiously.

Meng Wan said nothing, just pulled her guide's license out of her bag and pointed proudly at her name. "Meng Wan."

James recognized her name, and the corners of his mouth lifted, caught up in her delight. When they'd parted, he'd had a feeling she would end up on this path. He'd sensed the love for it in her commentary—some people were simply born for this kind of work.

James was generous with his praise. "You're going to make an outstanding guide."

It was the first compliment Meng Wan had received since becoming a guide, and she thought James entirely correct.

She accepted it without a shred of modesty. "That's exactly what I think too."

Forthright, candid, self-assured—that was the Meng Wan James saw.

*

After lunch, Meng Wan led the group to nearby Langya Pavilion—the very place where she'd given James his first tour.

The site wasn't large, in truth, nor especially famous, but anyone fond of calligraphy and old texts was sure to come. These retired cadres were all well-read, with ink in their bellies, so they would likely take to it.

Meng Wan bought the tickets and led everyone in. Worried that those at the back couldn't hear her, she had to raise her voice; when it came time for the actual commentary, she'd gather everyone in close to spare her throat.

Just as she'd expected, the old folks watched and listened more attentively than she could have hoped, some even carrying notebooks and pens, taking notes as they listened. Their earnestness moved her.

She hadn't noticed it when guiding James—the two of them had moved through it briskly and efficiently. But leading a large group now was far slower than she'd anticipated. The elders were getting on in years and couldn't hold their water; over a single afternoon they had to visit the restroom several times.

There were also those who tired and needed to rest, those who wanted hot water, those who wanted to wander off on their own. Over that one afternoon Meng Wan pulled out every trick she had to bring them all back to the hotel safe and sound.

Meng Wan counted heads, ran through the faces one more time, and finally reminded them, "Thank you all for your hard work today. Get some rest tonight. Tomorrow's destination is a bit far, so we need to gather in the lobby at seven sharp. I hope everyone can be on time."

Yang Zhiming was the last to go. Before heading up, he confirmed the next day's itinerary with her one final time. He was very satisfied with how she'd performed that day—she'd done well at both managing the group and giving commentary. The next time his unit had an outing like this, he'd recommend Meng Wan as their guide. That was as much as to say he approved of her.

Meng Wan kept the smile pinned to her face as she saw Yang Zhiming off. Only when she watched his back disappear did she finally let it drop, hunching over, drawing her neck in, and exhaling a long, deep breath. This was, without exception, the most exhausting day of her entire life.

The manager at the hotel's front desk was a woman in her early forties with a very kindly face. Seeing Meng Wan looking ready to fall apart, she poured her a cup of hot water.

"Have some hot water, rest a bit before you go." The manager set the cup in front of Meng Wan.

Meng Wan raised her eyes and offered a warm smile. "Thank you, Sister."

The two of them sat down on the sofa, and Meng Wan cradled the cup, recovering her strength.

"How did a young thing like you end up a guide? It's grueling work." Every day she saw plenty of guides at the hotel, and almost all of them were men. They'd be waiting in the lobby first thing in the morning and would be the last to leave at night after bringing their groups back—nearly every one of them came back hoarse.

Meng Wan gave a weak, wry smile. "I'm starting to see that. But it's too late to regret it now."

A flicker of tenderness showed in the manager's eyes as she offered comfort. "Still, your line of work pays well. At least the exhaustion isn't for nothing."

Meng Wan chatted with the manager a while longer and learned that her name was Chen Hui and that she'd been at this hotel for over a decade. It had once been a guesthouse; later, mismanaged, it had been bought out and turned into a hotel. She knew Bi Guisheng from the agency too, and often steered guests their way.

Meng Wan quietly filed the name away. She'd have to keep on good terms with her—who knew, the woman might send some business her way down the line.

Bright and early the next morning, Meng Wan appeared in the lobby right on schedule. There was still half an hour until seven, and she ate a steamed bun she'd brought from home.

Starting their shift alongside her were the cleaning staff, already at work on the morning tidy-up. There was nothing remarkable about that—and yet Meng Wan couldn't help glancing over a few extra times.

Most of those doing the cleaning were middle-aged aunties, with a few middle-aged uncles among them, but a strapping young man was something Meng Wan had never seen before.

He was quite sturdily built, and worked briskly enough, though his expression was glum—probably, like her, not yet fully awake.

The old folks, by contrast, slept little, and every one of them flooded into the lobby a few minutes before seven, sparing Meng Wan a good deal of worry.

In good spirits, Meng Wan offered her morning greetings, counted heads, reminded everyone to bring hot water and their personal belongings, and led them onto the coach.

It was springtime, and around seven the sun was slowly climbing into view. The early-morning air carried a hint of fragrance.

The coach was very quiet at that hour, everyone still surfacing from sleep.

Meng Wan had rolled her window halfway down, letting the morning breeze blow over her as she closed her eyes to rest, her mind still running through what to order for everyone once they reached the breakfast stall.

She was only halfway through the thought when someone in the back rows cried out, "Ah! My gold earring is gone!"
