Chapter 63

The moment the words left Meng Wan's mouth, everyone fell silent for a good while.

Sun Jing asked hesitantly, "You want me to manage your restaurant? But I've never been a boss before. I'm afraid I won't do it well."

"I'd never been a boss either, and look—aren't I running things pretty respectably now?" Meng Wan said, bolstering her confidence. "Sister-in-law, you've been married into our family all these years, keeping this whole household running in perfect order. That's a real skill. And you're educated on top of it—you won't do badly at anything. If you don't know how at first, you can learn. Even the Great Leader said we should learn by doing."

Sun Jing took a bit of heart from Meng Wan's words, then asked about the thing that concerned her most. "If I go work at the restaurant, my salary..."

"Don't you worry about that." Meng Wan laid out the wage she'd long since worked out. "A restaurant manager, going by the market rate, should earn around seven or eight hundred a month. I'll pay you a thousand, and if there are plenty of customers and business is good, I'll add a commission on top every month."

"Really?" Sun Jing shot to her feet, thrilled. "I could really earn that much?"

"Of course." Meng Wan gave her assurance. "My goal is to build this restaurant into the biggest tourist-reception restaurant in all of Yuyang. By then your salary will only keep going up—though your workload will keep growing too. But by that point Zhizhi will be a bit older, and you'll be able to put more of your energy into work."

Sun Jing was already lost in Meng Wan's vision, imagining herself running a restaurant.

Meng Xingyang, however, looked unconvinced. "How big is your restaurant right now?"

"Two floors, upper and lower. Seating ten tables at once is no problem."

"And what's the daily turnover at the moment?"

"I just filed the application for the business license. It'll be at least another month before it can open."

Seeing how she'd been talking with such conviction, Meng Xingyang had assumed her restaurant was already turning a profit—and it turned out it hadn't even opened yet.

"After all that talk, it isn't even open. How can you guarantee it's bound to make money? What if the restaurant goes bust not long after opening—and your sister-in-law's already quit her school job? You want her to live on the northwest wind?"

At this, Sun Jing too was pulled out of her sweet daydream by Meng Xingyang's words. "He's right, Xiao Wan. I really can't be left without a job."

But Meng Wan had confidence in her restaurant. "First of all, the cook I've hired has skills everyone unanimously vouches for—I'm certain the vast majority of people will love what he makes. Second, we're not short on customers: the first stop for every tour group our agency's guides bring in will be to eat at our restaurant. On top of that, we'll draw in some of Yuyang's locals to come and try it too. Word of mouth will spread, and our business is bound to keep growing."

She paused for a few seconds, her gaze settling on Sun Jing. "Of course, all of this is what I'm projecting based on the resources we currently have. What I'm trying to say is this: no decision a person makes can ever be a hundred-percent sure thing. Sister-in-law, what you'd have to do is own the consequences of your choice. If you don't even have that much capacity to bear the risk, then I wouldn't advise you to come work at the restaurant—because this job is bound to be more complicated and harder than being a teacher."

Sun Jing lowered her eyes and thought it over, then said solemnly, "Xiao Wan, give your sister-in-law a few days. I'll give what you've said careful thought."

"No problem. You should think it through—after all, this concerns the whole direction of your future."

Meng Xingyang shot Sun Jing a reluctant glance, sighing and stamping his foot, then turned and went back to his room.

——

Qin Yizhong sent Meng Wan a telegram before setting out, bringing Ding Chunling and their two children. As soon as Meng Wan got word that they were coming, she went straight to find them a place to stay. As it happened, Old Bai had said the workshop didn't have enough room—they needed to hire more people—so Meng Wan rented the vacant courtyard next door to Old Bai's house, using half of it as living quarters and half as a workshop.

Meng Wan intended to put Ding Chunling in charge of selling souvenirs, so having her move in directly also made it convenient for her to pick up stock.

After she'd brought everyone over, Meng Wan told Ding Chunling her plan, which thrilled her to no end. Patting her chest, she promised she'd absolutely carry out the task Meng Wan had given her.

Some time back, a letter from home had said that both her eldest and second brothers had been laid off, leaving the family suddenly without its main source of income. They were anxiously job-hunting at the moment. Laid-off workers in their situation were no rarity—just among the Ding relatives there were a dozen or more.

Now that Ding Chunling had a say in things and could find work for her own relatives, her standing in the family was sure to shoot straight up. From now on, who that saw her wouldn't treat her with respect? The last time she'd left her parents' home she hadn't been in the best of moods, precisely because her two sisters-in-law had given her the cold shoulder. This time, she was determined to win her face back at home.

With the help of Meng Qingyan, Meng Xingyang, and Cheng Xuehua, Meng Wan's agency had secured a steady source of inspection-tour clients, almost all of them from the various government units of Yuyang City, taking advantage of the summer break for exchange and study.

Xiao Hai had now basically completed his training, and Meng Wan had him start leading groups on his own.

The number of tourist groups was also slowly increasing. Jiang Ke had a good foundation; after leading just one group alongside Luo Zhigang, he'd more or less memorized the agency's procedures and commentary, and could now hold his own as well.

As for Lan Xiuli and Yu Fei, their preparations were nearly complete, but they still lacked real combat experience. As it happened, James's family was coming to Yuyang to tour that week, so Meng Wan decided to have the three of them lead this group together. They called it a tour group, but it was really just receiving family—no formal procedures involved.

To ramp up the agency's publicity and draw in more tourists, Meng Wan personally put together a travel brochure, printed three hundred copies, and had Old Bi and Xu Qiuyun hand them out outside the train station.

The night before receiving the foreign visitors, Meng Wan gathered everyone for a meeting. Once she got busy, she'd have no time to look after the others, so she wanted to set everyone's mind at ease beforehand and let them step out boldly and do their work.

"The guide handbooks I handed out earlier—has everyone finished reading them?"

Meng Wan had written into them all the commentary, points to note, and route plans she'd accumulated since becoming a guide herself, doing everything she could to ensure tourists could fulfill their expectations for the trip within their limited time.

She'd designed weekend tour routes for the visitors, including: short weekend getaways, aimed at the Yuyang-area crowd; in-depth culture-themed tours, aimed at high-end clients and out-of-town independent travelers; long-distance specialty sightseeing tours, suited to small boutique groups with guaranteed margins; and finally senior tours and student tours, two groups that each needed specially tailored itineraries.

She'd also specially prepared sun hats, red wrist cords, and towels for the tourists as welcome gifts. She figured she'd done everything she could possibly think of.

"We've finished," everyone answered in unison.

Jiang Ke laid on the flattery with a grin: "Manager Meng, this handbook of yours is in a class entirely of its own, truly one of a kind. With it, it'd be hard for our agency's business not to do well."

"All right then—you won't be taking a single day off these two months. The agency's business is riding on you." Meng Wan tossed off a reply to Jiang Ke, then asked, "Does anyone else have questions?"

Jiang Ke muttered sheepishly under his breath, "I wasn't planning to take any time off anyway."

Everyone had already agreed: over these two months of summer, they'd work without a single break unless something came up, aiming to double the agency's earnings—and double their own take-home commissions—laying a foundation of good reputation for the future. With money as the lure, everyone was charged up like they'd had a shot of adrenaline; no amount of hard work scared them.

Liu Fei raised his hand. "Manager Meng, I'm worried that handing out brochures with all this fanfare means our tour routes will get copied by the other agencies."

He'd read the brochure Meng Wan made—it really was very appealing. But promoting it on such a wide scale, the other agencies would learn it sooner or later, and then their agency's edge would be gone.

Luo Zhigang nodded in agreement. "Just the other day I noticed some guides had already borrowed our commentary. This brochure will probably be plagiarized before long too."

"No need to worry." Meng Wan reassured them. "That's just how business goes—whoever has a new idea gets copied right away. But by the time they've finished copying it all—designing, laying it out, printing, distributing—it'll take at least a month. By then we'll already have drawn in a batch of clients with our creativity, built a steady channel through word of mouth, and we'll roll out all sorts of discount promotions to attract new customers, keeping them forever chasing along behind us."

She'd thought all of this through long ago. Being copied was only a matter of time. As long as their service was attentive, they held on to their old customers, and kept drawing in new ones, then they had nothing to fear from the copycats.

Meng Wan rallied everyone: "So the most important thing right now is to fire that first shot. Everyone has to throw all their passion and energy into it and seize this summer with everything we've got!"

"Seize it with everything we've got!" the crowd echoed in a loud chorus.

——

Early the next morning, Meng Wan, together with Lan Xiuli and Yu Fei, arrived at the hotel where James used to stay. His relatives had checked into this hotel the day before, and by today their jet lag should have settled.

Lan Xiuli had worked for a good few years, so she stayed fairly steady when things came up. Yu Fei, on the other hand, was a green young thing facing her very first job, nervous out of her wits—her little face was pale, her hands twisting together, not saying a word the whole way.

Meng Wan gently soothed her nerves. "It's fine. When the time comes, just play it by ear—translate what I say, get a feel for the flow, and keep a smile on your face at all times. If there's anything you don't understand, come ask me. They won't understand you anyway, so whatever you want to ask, just ask it straight out."

Yu Fei nodded blankly. "Thank you, Manager. I'll keep your words in mind, and I promise not to cause you any trouble."

"Even if you cause trouble, it doesn't matter—it's perfectly normal for new people to make mistakes at work." Meng Wan turned her head. "Don't believe me? Ask your Sister Xiuli."

Lan Xiuli joined the comforting brigade too. "Back when I first started working in the archives, I put a personnel file for someone with the same name and surname in the wrong cabinet, and the whole unit spent three days searching for it together."

"Really?"

"Of course." Lan Xiuli laughed at the memory of her younger self. "Back then I didn't understand anything, and no one taught me—I had to feel my way through it all on my own. Who knows how many mistakes I made. So now I've got a thick skin, and a little slip-up doesn't faze me anymore."

"So just relax—you've got us." Meng Wan patted Yu Fei's hand.

With these reassurances from the two senior hands, Yu Fei finally settled down.

Meng Wan had always believed that the older generation had a responsibility to guide the younger one. So when it came to young people, the older generation should do everything they could to support them and give them chances to climb higher—because this world would always, in the end, belong to the young.
