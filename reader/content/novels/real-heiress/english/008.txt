Chapter 8

Early the next morning, Meng Wan very reluctantly slapped off the alarm clock. Her arm had gone numb from pillowing her head on it, and bleary-eyed, she rubbed it for several minutes before the feeling came back.

Today Meng Wan's outfit was all about comfort and practicality. She dug out a pink suit, and since she'd be walking for long stretches, she changed into a pair of sneakers. Pairing a suit with sneakers would be common enough in later decades, but right now it was rather avant-garde—in the eyes of the fashionable, she was disrespecting both the suit and the sneakers.

Not long ago she'd watched a film from Hong Kong in which the heroine wore her hair in lazy, shoulder-length waves. Meng Wan had loved the look so much that she'd gone specially to the best salon in the city and had her hair permed into something similar.

She washed her hair and set it with gel, then added a red headband and a pair of red coral earrings—last year's birthday present from Meng Xingyang.

Meng Wan had fair, clear skin, so she didn't usually bother with powder; a swipe of lipstick and she was ready to go.

By the time she'd finished, the meeting time she'd agreed on with James was drawing near. In the living room, Sun Jing had bought breakfast bright and early—today's pairing was youtiao with spicy peppery soup.

Everyone was leisurely eating breakfast at the table. Meng Wan found an empty spot, sat right down, and started wolfing her food.

Cheng Xuehua, who had already finished and was heading off to the hospital, told her daughter to eat more slowly on her way out. Meng Qingyan had no class that morning, so he had plenty of time and sipped his peppery soup unhurriedly, now and then glancing in puzzlement at the harried Meng Wan.

Meng Wan usually bolted her breakfast like this, so that was nothing new. What was new was that today, before work, she'd spruced herself up from head to toe. Apart from her very first day on the job, she'd never shown such energy—after that, she'd always gotten up at the last possible minute.

Seeing her young sister-in-law come out, Sun Jing turned and fetched from the kitchen a plate of cloud-slice cake she'd warmed for her, set it down in front of her, and explained a little shyly, "These came fresh out of the steamer this morning. I was afraid they'd gone cold, so I warmed them up again for you."

Meng Wan looked at the neatly arranged cake on the plate, then up at Sun Jing, pleasantly surprised. "I get an extra treat too?"

Sun Jing sat down next to Zhizhi, doing her best to seem casual. "A peddler happened to come by selling them. I remembered you like them, so I bought some."

In fact she'd gotten up early specially to buy them fresh out of the steamer at the Liufu Confectionery, but she didn't want Meng Wan to feel she owed her any great favor, so she'd made up the little lie.

Meng Wan had loved Liufu's cloud-slice cake since she was small; that sweet, mellow taste that was never cloying was utterly familiar to her. The moment the cake melted in her mouth, it was unmistakably Liufu—how could any peddler match Liufu's standard?

Meng Wan was rather taken aback that her sister-in-law would go to such trouble for her. Liufu's morning cloud-slice cake was hugely sought after; you almost always had to queue a good while for it. It seemed yesterday's doll had done the trick—she'd planted a willow without meaning to, and now it had grown into shade. All in all, a good thing.

Since Sun Jing didn't want her to feel indebted, Meng Wan didn't expose her. "Thanks, sister-in-law. You're really good to me."

With that, she dropped two slices into Zhizhi's bowl. Zhizhi had been dying to dig in for ages, but her mother had told her that Auntie had only just bought her a doll yesterday, so the first bite of cake had to go to Auntie first. Zhizhi had had no choice but to press down the greedy little worm in her belly and wait at the table all this time for Meng Wan to come out.

Listening to Meng Wan's thanks, Sun Jing couldn't help a faint blush rising to her cheeks. If anyone should be doing the thanking, it was her. Meng Wan had bought such an expensive toy without a second thought—real, heartfelt generosity toward Zhizhi—and here Sun Jing had been stirring up talk about her at home. It really wasn't right.

"Brother." Eating happily, Meng Wan turned to Meng Xingyang and said through a mouthful, "From now on I'm going to ride my bike to work, so you should go back to riding your own."

"Why?" The moment Meng Xingyang heard this, he set down the bowl he was holding and looked up at Meng Wan. "Don't you take the bus to work?"

Before Meng Wan started the job, Meng Qingyan had bought her a brand-new Phoenix bicycle as a reward for landing her first job, but Meng Wan found riding too tiring—she sweated in summer and hated getting sweat on her clothes—so she took the bus to work every day.

A new bike couldn't just sit idle, of course, so Meng Xingyang had asked Meng Wan if he could ride it. His own bike's brakes had gone wonky, which suited him perfectly. Now that he'd gotten used to the new one, being asked to go back from luxury to thrift naturally made him reluctant in the extreme.

"I've realized my fitness is terrible and I need to exercise. Cycling's a great way to do it. Now I finally understand the trouble Dad went to in buying me that bike." Meng Wan didn't forget to flatter Meng Qingyan a little.

Hearing this, Meng Qingyan straightened his back ever so slightly, a small flush of pleasure rising in his heart. True, his only thought in buying the bike had been to give his daughter a gift, but her interpretation wasn't wrong either—of course cycling was good exercise.

Keeping a straight face, he spoke up on his daughter's behalf. "It was bought for your sister in the first place. You only get to ride it on her account. If you want a new bike, buy one with your own salary."

Meng Xingyang's mouth puckered. He tugged hard at a youtiao and stuffed it into his mouth, muttering under his breath, "Playing favorites."

Just the night before, he'd wiped the bike down inside and out, thinking that with good care it would last him a long time—only to find he'd been making a wedding gown for someone else.

Pretending he hadn't heard, Meng Xingyang went on instructing his sister, "Ride carefully. When you meet people, let them go first. Whatever you do, don't crash into anyone trying to go fast—if you get hurt, your mother will blame me again."

"Got it, Dad." Meng Wan finished the last slice of cake, wiped her mouth, and bade the family a warm, energetic farewell. "Bye, everyone—I'm off to work!"

Like a put-upon little wife, Meng Xingyang pursed his lips and muttered sarcastically under his breath, "It's just going to work. All this fanfare—anyone who didn't know better would think she was off to the battlefield."

Seeing his daughter so full of vigor, Meng Qingyan was overjoyed. This was more like it. Young people should go about their work and their lives full of energy every day.

Sun Jing gazed in the direction Meng Wan had gone, lost in thought. She couldn't shake the feeling that her sister-in-law was somehow different.

*

The Yuhu Hotel where James was staying was in the city center—the most upscale hotel in Yuyang. In her past life, Meng Wan's ex-husband had once tried to buy it, but for lack of funds he'd been beaten to it by a mysterious figure. After the hotel was acquired, renovated, and reopened, Meng Wan had gone—behind her ex-husband's back—to experience it for herself, and from the service to the surroundings, everything was the finest she'd ever known.

At the time she'd even admired this mysterious figure's ability, transforming so large a hotel so dramatically in a mere six months—a genuine miracle. Only later, the mysterious figure had died unexpectedly, and the hotel really had been picked up cheaply by her ex-husband. She'd marveled then at her ex's good luck.

Meng Wan parked her bike outside and headed straight for the lobby. James was already dressed and seated on a lobby sofa; when Meng Wan came in he was reading a newspaper.

He was dressed very casually today—a gray turtleneck sweater, blue jeans, and a pair of sneakers.

"So Mr. James not only speaks Mandarin, he can read Chinese characters too." Meng Wan slipped off her shoulder bag and sat on the sofa across from him.

Hearing her, James closed the newspaper and lowered his crossed leg. "Half and half. I've been learning to write Chinese characters lately."

"That's impressive—Chinese characters are very hard to write." Meng Wan praised him mindlessly. As a child, learning to write had cost her no end of effort; writing the character xi in Wang Xizhi's name had made her question her whole existence. During a dictation she'd gotten that character wrong and been made to copy it a hundred times, and by the end she no longer even recognized it.

James folded the newspaper neatly and put it back on the rack beside him, nodding in full agreement. "It really is hard to write, but it's also very interesting."

Not knowing how to respond, Meng Wan could only borrow the words Cheng Xuehua used to comfort her. "It's all right, take it slowly. As long as you stick with it, one day you'll write it well."

The conversation had reached the polite-pleasantries stage, which was the cue to change the subject. James was first to raise the question he cared about most. "What's our plan for today?"

Meng Wan immediately pulled the notebook from her bag and, heart fluttering, turned to the first page and handed it over with both hands, saying expectantly, "This is the plan I made. Please take a look, Mr. James."

James took the notebook and began studying it in earnest. Though she had no idea how much of it he could read, Meng Wan couldn't help growing nervous—like a criminal awaiting judgment, her heart bobbing up and down as she waited for the verdict on her sentence. Seeing James take so long to say anything, she'd already rehearsed it in her head: was it bad, very bad, or terrible?

"Wow, wonderful!"

Meng Wan didn't quite catch the word, but reading his expression she asked, half-believing, "Mr. James is satisfied?"

James closed the diary and praised her without stint. "Miss Meng, this plan of yours covers every place I wanted to see—it even includes some places whose names I'd never heard of. To put together such a detailed arrangement in so little time, you must have gone to a great deal of trouble. I like it very much."

Meng Wan felt a weight lift. As long as he was satisfied, all her hard work the night before hadn't been for nothing.

"Then let's set off."

Before they left, James took half the guide fee from his wallet and paid Meng Wan as a deposit; the other half he'd pay at the end of the trip.

Seeing that Meng Wan had come by bike, James had the doorman wheel the bicycle to the hotel parking lot, and he gave the man a tip. Delighted to receive it, the attendant handed Meng Wan a token, beaming, and wheeled the bike away.

Watching him, Meng Wan thought of herself. When she'd taken James's money just now, had she smiled this openly too?

Drawing her gaze back, Meng Wan was about to head to the intersection to flag a taxi when a yellow boxy cab pulled up smoothly right outside the hotel's main doors. A tall driver with a buzz cut hopped out and briskly hauled two waist-high suitcases from the trunk for his passengers.

With one right there, she was spared the time of having to hail one.

Before Meng Wan could even open her mouth, the driver glanced their way and strode over to them, stopping in front of the two of them and asking a touch bashfully, "Excuse me, do you two need a taxi?"

The driver had big eyes and a pair of thick brows that gave him a slightly fierce look. The way he walked, with that air about him, you'd think he was coming to pick a fight with Meng Wan—but the moment he smiled, it was guileless. To Meng Wan he seemed both fierce and honest, and rather endearing at that.

"Will you go to the Tomb of the True and False Princesses?" Meng Wan asked.

The place she named was some distance from the hotel, almost out by the suburbs. Apart from tourists there were hardly any people there, and on top of that it was a Monday, so visitors would be few. The driver would very likely face an empty run back, which burned a fair amount of gas.

James thought of this too, and in the moment the driver hesitated, he proposed directly, "How about I hire your car for the whole day? You just need to show us around all day and bring us back this evening."

This was exactly what Meng Wan had in mind—that way she wouldn't have to worry about not finding a cab to get home in the evening, and it would save the time in between as well.

The driver had never had anyone hire his car by the day before, and he didn't know how to charge for it. He wanted to take the job, but he was afraid that if he named too high a figure they wouldn't agree, and too low wouldn't be worth his while. He was still doing the sums in his head when Meng Wan, seeing through him, said frankly, "If you don't mind my asking—how much do you usually make in a day?"

The driver held nothing back. "On a busy day I can make one or two hundred; on a slow day, forty or fifty."

Meng Wan rapidly ran the numbers in her head. They were going to six spots, which meant at least five legs of driving; with the round trips and meals, it should add up to about thirty-four kilometers—and since the car was hired, the driver had to stay with them the whole way.

She quickly arrived at a rough figure, one that wouldn't make her employer pay over the odds yet wouldn't shortchange the driver either. But since it was James footing the bill, she still had to check with him first.

Meng Wan shot James a questioning look. James understood her meaning at once and gave her a confirming nod.

She was a local; she naturally knew the going rate better, and he trusted that Meng Wan wouldn't quote him a crooked price the way the previous dishonest guide had.

With her employer's backing, Meng Wan spoke without reservation. "How about a hundred and fifty yuan for the day? I've thrown in an extra thirty as a bit of hardship pay."

The price Meng Wan offered was indeed generous. Unable to work it out himself, the driver gladly agreed. This way he wouldn't have to drive around drumming up fares—he just had to stick with them—and he'd earn a little extra for his trouble.

"I don't even know your name yet." Meng Wan smiled, making conversation. After all, they'd be together all day; she could hardly just call him "driver." And if today went well, they might keep working together over the next few days.

A little shyly, the driver answered, "Lin Dong. Everyone at home calls me Xiaodong."

Meng Wan joked, "Don't tell me you've got siblings called Lin Xi, Lin Nan, and Lin Bei too?"

"How did you know?" Lin Dong said in astonishment. "I really do have a younger sister named Lin Xi—she's in high school now."

James, too, looked astonished and gave Meng Wan a thumbs-up. He didn't say a word, but Meng Wan could swear she heard his expression saying, "Wow, wonderful!"

These two men had her grinning from ear to ear. It wasn't exactly hard to guess.

She didn't forget to introduce herself. "I'm Meng Wan, and this is our foreign friend James—you can also call him by his Chinese name, Chen Jinghua. I'm his guide, in charge of his itinerary in Yuyang."

Lin Dong greeted them artlessly. "Miss Meng, Mr. Chen."

He couldn't make sense of the foreign name, let alone say it; it felt embarrassing to even try.

The moment Meng Wan got in the car, she caught a faint scent of soap. The interior was very clean, the windows polished till they gleamed—clearly the owner cherished his car.

This Lin Dong really was a study in contrasts. He looked so tough, yet he turned out to be an honest, meticulous man. You truly couldn't judge a person by appearances.
