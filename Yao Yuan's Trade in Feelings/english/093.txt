Chapter 93 — The Mirror Pact's Backlash Comes True: Shuai Hongqiang's Daughter Is Hurt in an Accident

Christmas sunlight poured through the dining room's floor-to-ceiling windows, cutting bright patches across the solid wood table. Shuai Hongqiang was bent over, peeling shrimp for Shuai Wenyao, while the boy chattered away about wanting to visit the newly opened science park that afternoon. Lin Wan ladled out a bowl of soup and handed it over, emerald scallions floating on the surface, steam curling up.

That was when the phone rang.

His ex-wife, Qiu Ying. After all these years divorced, they barely spoke beyond what their child required. The instant his fingertip slid across the answer key, something in Shuai Hongqiang's chest gave an inexplicable lurch.

"Hello?" His voice sounded normal.

Qiu Ying's voice came through, stripped of its usual crisp efficiency, carrying a tremor and panic she was fighting hard to hold down. "Hongqiang... something's happened to Wenrui."

The chopsticks in Shuai Hongqiang's hand clattered onto the porcelain plate with a sharp clink. Both Lin Wan and Wenyao looked up at him.

"Don't panic, take it slow. What happened?" He heard his own voice come out steady enough, but a layer of clammy cold sweat instantly seeped from the palm gripping the phone.

"She went skiing with classmates... she fell coming down an intermediate slope. Her left leg—her left tibia and fibula are fractured, and she has a mild concussion. She's at the Provincial People's Hospital, just out of surgery..." Qiu Ying's account came in broken fragments, threaded with suppressed sobs. "The doctor says the surgery went well, but afterward... it's hard to say how the recovery will go. It depends on her..."

Skiing. Fracture. Surgery.

Each word dropped like a cold stone into Shuai Hongqiang's abruptly frozen mind. But the first thing that surged up in him was not heart-wrenching pain over his daughter's injury, nor a father's instinctive urgency—it was a chill, bone-deep terror that shot up from his spine.

Like a bolt of lightning splitting open the chaos in his head, it lit up the words he had never dared to dwell on, the ones that had hung over him day and night: the Mirror Pact's backlash, cause and effect coming due.

Old Mr. Wang Baoguo's low voice seemed to echo in his ears again: "...The force of cause and effect is mysterious beyond words. The backlash need not fall on you yourself—it may also strike your closest blood kin..."

So that was it. It had come. Retribution isn't absent, it just bides its time. The smooth fortune he had stolen, the luck he had overdrawn—someone would have to pay the interest in the end. He just never imagined the interest would be charged to his daughter.

Wenrui had only just started her first year of university. Her life had barely begun to unfold. She loved to dance so much; as a little girl she'd clung to him begging to learn ballet, and he'd refused on the grounds that it would "interfere with her studies." If her leg... Shuai Hongqiang couldn't let himself think further, his stomach churning with nausea.

"Daddy?" Shuai Wenyao tugged at his sleeve, his small face full of worry. "Is Jiejie sick?"

The child's tender voice pulled Shuai Hongqiang briefly back from the ice pit. Only then did he notice that Lin Wan had risen at some point and was standing beside him, her hand resting lightly on his tensed shoulder, her gaze calm and questioning.

"...I understand." Shuai Hongqiang said into the phone, his voice dry in a way that didn't sound like his own. "Orthopedics at the Provincial People's Hospital, right? I'll come right over."

He hung up. The dining room fell silent. Shuai Wenyao looked at his father, then at his mother, not daring to make any more noise.

"Wenrui fell skiing. Broke her leg. She's in the hospital." Shuai Hongqiang told Lin Wan plainly. He tried to force a smile to reassure his son, only to find the corners of his mouth too heavy to lift. The terror and the guilt pressed down on him like two mountains. In all these years, what had he ever given Wenrui beyond the generous living allowance deposited into her account on schedule and the occasional perfunctory holiday greeting? He didn't even know what major she'd actually enrolled in, which celebrity she was crazy about lately, or whether she had a boyfriend. He had provided her abundant material comfort while being absent for her entire upbringing. And now this "retribution" had landed with precision on his daughter—which cut him deeper than if it had fallen directly on himself, and frightened him all the more at the cold, exact cruelty of that invisible cause and effect.

"Don't panic." Lin Wan's voice was steady and gentle. Her hand pressed his shoulder with a little more force. "Eat first. When we're done, I'll drive you to the hospital. Wenyao, eat quickly—when you finish, Mommy will take you and Daddy to see Jiejie."

Her composure was like a piece of driftwood, letting Shuai Hongqiang—almost drowning in guilt and fear—barely grab hold of some sense of reality. He nodded, picked up his chopsticks again, but everything tasted like wax. The rest of that lunch ended quickly, in a stifled silence.

On the way to the hospital, Shuai Hongqiang kept staring at the streetscape rushing past the window. Christmas decorations were everywhere, red and green intertwined, brimming with cheap cheer that only made his heart feel colder and grayer. Lin Wan drove with focus, glancing at him now and then in the rearview mirror, saying little. Shuai Wenyao seemed to sense the heavy mood too, sitting obediently in his car seat, clutching the plush teddy bear he'd brought for his sister.

The orthopedics ward corridor at the Provincial People's Hospital was filled with the crisp, distinctive smell of disinfectant. Shuai Hongqiang's steps felt unsteady. At the ward door he actually had to pause, draw a deep breath, before he could push it open and go in.

In the single room, Qiu Ying stood by the window speaking quietly on the phone, her back looking somewhat thin and frail. Hearing the door, she turned. After several years apart, she was still well kept, only the fine lines at the corners of her eyes had deepened, and right now her brow was full of exhaustion and worry. Seeing Shuai Hongqiang, she hung up and walked over quickly.

"Hongqiang." She called out, her gaze passing over Lin Wan and Wenyao behind him, pausing slightly, then nodding politely to Lin Wan. "Lin Wan came too."

"Sister Qiu." Lin Wan stepped forward, her voice gentle, setting a fruit basket on the bedside cabinet. "How is Wenrui? What exactly did the doctor say?" Her form of address was pitched just right—neither too distant nor too close.

Just then, a man rose from a chair toward the back of the room, behind Qiu Ying. He was about fifty, wore glasses, with a refined, scholarly air. He was Qiu Ying's current husband, surnamed Chen, a university professor. Shuai Hongqiang had only glimpsed him from a distance once, years ago, when dropping off Wenrui.

"Professor Chen." Shuai Hongqiang nodded to him and greeted him first. His tone was as calm and courteous as he could manage, betraying little emotion.

"Hongqiang's here." Professor Chen nodded back at once, his face carrying the composure of an intellectual along with a measure of the appropriate gravity such an occasion called for. He stepped half a pace forward but did not extend his hand, expressing only in words a familiar, shared-concern stance: "Long trip. Wenrui just fell asleep. The doctor came to check on her and said the surgery went very well, told us not to worry too much."

Their exchange was brief and restrained, like two friends drawn into contact only by a matter of mutual concern, swiftly trading the most essential information and then having nothing more to say. There was a faint subtlety in the air, the kind grown-ups silently understand. No hostility, no comparison—just a clear, tacit acknowledgment of their respective positions and relationships. Having said his piece, Professor Chen naturally stepped back half a pace, leaving more room for Qiu Ying and Shuai Hongqiang, and turned his gaze toward Lin Wan, who was quietly arranging the fruit basket, nodding to her with the same courtesy.

This process of "greeting each other like friends" was quick and bland, yet like an extremely fine needle, it drew across Shuai Hongqiang's already tangled state of mind another mark—nearly invisible, but real—reminding him that in his daughter's life he had long since retreated to a peripheral position, one maintained by the identities of "ex-husband" and "father," while the daily companionship and the vigil at this very moment were another man's duty and right. This realization, mingled with the towering terror and guilt in his heart—the suspicion that he himself had brought down this disaster—left a taste beyond words.

"The surgery's done. They put in a plate. The anesthesia hasn't fully worn off, so she's sleeping." Qiu Ying rubbed her brow and told Shuai Hongqiang the details of what had happened. "They said she rolled down the slope, twisted her left leg, and couldn't move it on the spot. The classmate she went with was terrified, called an ambulance and brought her straight here. Thank goodness her head and spine weren't hurt—it's just this leg..." Looking at her daughter's pale face on the hospital bed, her voice choked again.

"Who's the attending physician? Is there a post-op rehab plan yet? Did they say whether it'll affect her in the future..." Shuai Hongqiang threw out a string of questions, each one hiding a fear he didn't dare voice—would it affect her walking down the line? Could she still run and jump? Could she still... dance?

"The doctor will come around for rounds in a bit. Ask him the details." Qiu Ying said, her gaze turning again to Lin Wan, forcing something that barely passed for a smile. "Thank you both for making the trip. Wenyao's gotten so big." She crouched down, wanting to pat Wenyao's head, but the child instinctively leaned toward Lin Wan's leg. Qiu Ying's hand paused slightly in midair, then withdrew naturally, her smile fading a little.

"It's the least we could do." Lin Wan gently nudged Wenyao forward. "Wenyao, say hello to Auntie Qiu."

"Hello, Auntie Qiu." Shuai Wenyao said in a small voice, his big eyes darting toward the bed the whole time.

There was a subtle barrier in the air—not openly confrontational, yet undeniably present. Two women, who at different stages of life had formed the most intimate bond with the same man, now stood together in one hospital room. No hostility, even a courteous concern, but the invisible line between "ex-wife" and "current wife" was as clear as the curtain dividing the room into sections. The past marriage hung like a faint shadow across the simple pleasantries of the present. Lin Wan was considerate but would not overstep; Qiu Ying was courteous, yet couldn't quite hide a trace of complicated feeling—after all, the woman before her was occupying the place she had once held, keeping her ex-husband company.

"I'll go see Wenrui." Lin Wan broke the subtle stillness at just the right moment and walked to the bedside.

Shuai Wenrui was still in a deep sleep, no color in her young face, her lashes casting a small shadow beneath her eyelids. Her left leg was in a heavy cast, suspended by a brace—a startling sight. Lin Wan looked at her quietly for a moment, then reached out and ever so lightly tucked in the corner of the blanket. That was all she could do. As a stepmother, her contact with this stepdaughter was genuinely scant; their few meetings had mostly been polite holiday meals together. Too much concern now would only seem false. She straightened up and said softly to Shuai Hongqiang and Qiu Ying: "You two talk. I'll take Wenyao out for a bit—I'm afraid it's stifling for him in here."

Qiu Ying nodded. "Thank you."

Lin Wan led Wenyao out—the boy looking back every few steps—and gently closed the door behind them.

With the door shut, the air in the ward seemed to loosen a little, only to fill again with another kind of heavy worry. Shuai Hongqiang walked to the bed and gazed at his daughter. When was the last time he had looked at her this closely? It seemed to have been her birthday last year. He'd booked a restaurant; the gift was the newest phone, picked out by Lin Wan. She'd said thank you then, her smile sweet and well-mannered, but with no real delight in her eyes. Somewhere along the way, all that was left between them was this fixed routine—flowing with money, yet drained of intimacy.

Now, watching his daughter lie there so fragile on the hospital bed, the guilt mixed with fear in Shuai Hongqiang's heart surged forward like a tide, nearly drowning him. Was it him? Was it truly because of that moment of greed, that pact he'd made with that uncanny mirror, the fortune he'd stolen that was never his to take—was that why the retribution was now falling on his daughter? If Wenrui's leg really did carry some lasting damage, casting a shadow over her whole life... Shuai Hongqiang squeezed his eyes shut, not daring to think further. That self-loathing and panic left him feeling more powerless than any business disaster ever had.

"The doctor said she's young, with strong recovery, and as long as she does her rehab properly there won't be any major problems." Qiu Ying's voice came from beside him—whether to comfort him or herself, who could say.

Shuai Hongqiang opened his eyes, pulled his wallet from his pocket, drew out a card, and pressed it into Qiu Ying's hand. "Use the best medicine, hire the best rehab therapist. Don't worry about money. The PIN is Wenrui's birthday."

Qiu Ying looked at the card in her hand, silent for a few seconds, then took it without refusing. "Mm."

Just then, Shuai Wenrui on the bed let out a faint moan. Her eyelids fluttered and slowly opened. Her gaze first focused blankly on the ceiling, then turned slowly toward the bedside.

"Wenrui?" Shuai Hongqiang immediately bent over her, his voice careful in a way even he didn't notice. "You're awake? How do you feel? Does your leg hurt?"

Shuai Wenrui's eyes lingered on his face for a moment, as if only now fully waking, and she called softly, "Dad." Then she looked toward Qiu Ying and Professor Chen. "Mom. Uncle Chen." Her voice was hoarse and weak.

"You're awake, that's good, that's good." Qiu Ying hurried over and pressed the call button. "Is anywhere especially uncomfortable? Do you feel nauseous, like you might throw up?"

A nurse came in quickly, checked all the readings, and said her condition was stable. As the anesthesia wore off, the pain grew clearer, and Shuai Wenrui's brow knit slightly—but she was very quiet, not crying out.

The ward door was nudged quietly open a crack, and a small head peeked in. It was Shuai Wenyao. He was hugging that plush bear tightly. Seeing that his sister was awake, his eyes lit up, and only after a nod from Lin Wan did he tiptoe in and sidle up to the bed.

"Jiejie," he said in a tiny voice, full of a child's worry, "does your leg hurt?"

Seeing her little brother, a faint, genuine smile crossed Shuai Wenrui's face. "A little," she said honestly.

Shuai Wenyao immediately held out the bear in his arms. "Here, you hug my teddy. If you hug it, it won't hurt. Last time I fell down, Mommy had me hug the teddy."

The childish words eased the heavy atmosphere in the ward a little. Shuai Wenrui didn't take the bear; she only reached out and gently stroked her brother's hair. "Thank you, Wenyao. Jiejie's a grown-up now, I'm not afraid of pain. You keep the teddy yourself."

"Oh." Shuai Wenyao looked a little disappointed, but soon tilted his face up again. "Then Jiejie, get better quick. When you're better, Daddy can take us to the amusement park together. You said next time we'd ride the big roller coaster."

The child's offhand words pricked at Shuai Hongqiang's heart like a fine needle. He had promised "next time" so many times, only to break his word, mostly because he was "busy." Watching the simple exchange between brother and sister—Wenyao's complete closeness and reliance on his sister, Wenrui's natural tenderness toward her brother—he suddenly realized with clarity that in the days of his absence, his daughter had quietly grown into a gentle adult, one who knew how to take care of others and bear pain. And he—beyond providing the ever-growing number on a bank card, what else had he given her?

"All right. When Jiejie's better, we'll definitely go." Shuai Hongqiang heard himself promise, his voice slightly hoarse.

He stayed a while longer, carefully asking the nurse and the attending physician who came in afterward a whole string of questions, confirming the treatment and rehab plan over and over. Finally, he smoothed back the loose strands of hair on his daughter's forehead, the gesture a little clumsy.

"Wenrui, Dad has some things to handle at the company, so I'll head back first. Listen carefully to your mom and the doctors, cooperate with the treatment, don't think about anything—Dad's got it all handled." He paused. The words "It's Dad who let you down" rolled and rolled on his tongue, but in the end they were choked back by the enormous fear and shame, and never made it out. They turned instead into something heavier: "Dad will come see you again tomorrow."

"Mm, Dad, go do what you need to. I'm fine." Shuai Wenrui nodded, ever the sensible one.

Stepping out of the ward, closing the door, shutting that thick disinfectant smell and his daughter's pale face behind him, Shuai Hongqiang leaned against the cold wall and only then let the shoulders he'd been holding up collapse just a sliver. The light from the corridor's overhead lamps was a glaring white, yet his vision kept blacking out in waves, his ears ringing—filled with the words "cause-and-effect backlash" and "closest blood kin" screaming wildly.

Lin Wan waited quietly off to the side, holding the hand of an already yawning Wenyao. She didn't rush him, didn't press him with questions—only, as he straightened up again, placed Wenyao's small hand into his.

"Let's go home," she said, her voice low but carrying a steady strength that could pierce through frozen ice.

The soft, warm touch of the child's hand reached his palm. He lowered his head, meeting his son's innocent, clear eyes, and his other hand was gently taken by Lin Wan. Her hand wasn't cold and fragile like his daughter's was right now—it was warm and strong. Shuai Hongqiang's thoughts, muddled and stinging, gnawed at by fear, were tugged by these two patches of real warmth, dragged bit by bit back to the cold, concrete reality.

The family of three—their figures stretched long under the lamps of the empty hospital corridor—walked silently toward the elevator. On Christmas night the hospital was still ablaze with light, faint voices drifting through. Shuai Hongqiang walked in the middle, one hand leading the son bound to him by the blood of his present family, the other gripped firmly by the wife tied to him by present law and life, who at this moment was his only support. And behind that door, the daughter who might be suffering an undeserved disaster because of his moment of greed and its backlash was enduring her pain, watched over by another family—one he felt grateful to, and yet one that left him with nowhere to hide his shame.

All of it formed a picture full of fated irony and silent torment, nailing him firmly to the dock of cause and effect's judgment. The future stretched ahead like a dark, endless tunnel, the ninth time-travel hanging at its far end like a faint glimmer—he couldn't tell whether it meant release or final judgment—while the warmth pressing into his palm from those beside him, the warmth of ordinary human life, was the only fragile, real piece of driftwood he could grasp as he struggled, suffocating, in the dark. He gripped that hand tightly, like a dying man clutching his last hope of survival.
