The Temple of the Spirit in Marriage
"But here's the thing—" Just as the scrolling comments were buzzing back and forth, Lin Wan's tone shifted abruptly. She leaned slightly forward, those clear eyes seeming to pierce through the screen and look straight into each viewer's heart. "Have we ever stopped and asked ourselves one question: when I push so hard to 'get stronger,' when I work so painfully to maintain some kind of balance—who exactly am I trying to prove it to? Do I truly, deep down, want to be someone who's forever tense, forever locked in a contest, forever in 'combat' mode? Or is it… simply fear? The fear that if I let go, I'll slip in the relationship, lose this so-called 'balance of power,' lose my sense of security?"
【The comments were struck silent for a moment by the question, then a flood of raw confusion poured in:
[…I've never once thought about this.]
[I guess… it's to prove something to my husband and his family?]
[Afraid—of course I'm afraid. Afraid of being looked down on, afraid of being abandoned.]
[But if I'm not strong, doesn't that just make me weak? Doesn't it mean anyone can push me around?]
"Let me use myself as an example again." Lin Wan eased back into her chair, her smile carrying a candid, self-searching honesty. "Old friends who know me well know that Brother Qiang's business is doing pretty well. If I wanted to, I could of course grind harder, go full wolf-pack mode, chase more money, fight for the most central resources, work to become the kind of poised, halo-crowned 'power woman' who moves with ease through the arena of fame and money and all its tangled politics. Objectively speaking, I have the potential and the ability—I'd do a decent job of it, at the very least."
"But the real Lin Wan, deep in her heart, doesn't enjoy and doesn't long to live forever in that state of high-tension 'combat.' I feel that if a life were entirely filled with 'competing,' 'proving,' and 'maneuvering,' it would be utterly draining—and it would make me miss too many of the soft, lovely experiences in life, the ones you can only feel when you quiet down." Her pace slowed, and she spoke with a clear understanding of her own needs. "So you'll notice: whenever Brother Qiang isn't home, what do I spend most of my time on in this livestream? Sharing some fun but useless book I read recently, whether the gardenia on my balcony has bloomed again, a little noodle shop I found tucked away in an alley whose owner has quite a story, or some new thought I've had about a historical detail or a philosophical idea… I'm 'playing.' I'm feeling life itself. And when he comes home and needs me, in my social role as his 'partner,' to take part in certain matters or handle certain occasions together, I can switch channels in an instant—bring out the professionalism, the social energy, and the sharp mind it calls for, to coordinate and to handle things. In plain terms, that's—'earning money for him to see,' or rather, fighting for the shared 'interest entity' that is us."
【The mood in the comments lightened, with more laughter and shared recognition:
[Hahaha, too real! When my husband's out I just lie flat and binge dramas; the second he's back I sit bolt upright reading professional books.]
["Earning money for him to see"—that phrase is both bittersweet and hilarious.]
[So Wanwan, this is a kind of… strategic "playing the pig to eat the tiger"?]
[Isn't this just "acting"? Doesn't switching like that every day get exhausting?]
"This isn't 'acting.'" Lin Wan shook her head, her gaze clear and certain. "It's actively choosing your own state of being and the rhythm of how you allocate your energy. As for 'strength' and 'weakness' in marriage—if our eyes are fixed solely on comparing financial figures, ranking social titles, or even who does more housework, all those quantifiable metrics, then the whole measuring system is too one-dimensional, too flat, and it's especially easy to make a person anxious and lost. Of course we should grow, should possess the ability to demonstrate our social value and to stand on our own—that's the pair of wings we have as independent individuals. But wings are for flying, for taking in the scenery of a wider world, for experiencing a richer, more varied life. They're not just for flapping endlessly in front of your partner, merely to prove that my bird's wings are stiffer than yours, that I flap with more force."
She paused, letting the metaphor sink in, then went on in a steadier voice. "And maybe we don't have to be so fixated on becoming the kind of woman who 'never spends a single cent of a man's money,' who splits every bill down to the second decimal place, drawing a sharp line through everything. In a healthy intimate relationship, measured, mutually willing giving and receiving—including the natural flow of money—is itself an expression of emotional connection and deepening trust. The key is this: you spend his money because you need it or want it, and he happens to have extra and is glad to give—your relationship has simply grown that harmonious. Not because you 'have nothing,' so you 'must hold out your hand and ask.' The former is sharing, nourishment; the latter is begging, depletion. Different mindsets, and the whole flavor and quality of the relationship changes."
【The comments were digesting this finer distinction:
[Sharing versus begging… worlds apart. Mindset decides everything.]
[I used to insist on splitting everything; it felt so distant, and eventually he found it pointless too.]
[Financial independence is the bottom line and the backbone, but it doesn't mean severing every flow of money between two people. That's not a married couple—that's roommates.]
[The point is "natural flow" and "mutual willingness." Anything forced or calculating is meaningless.]
"So then, when we try to lift our gaze, for a moment, off these external, quantifiable, easily-compared metrics of 'strength' and 'weakness,'" Lin Wan's voice dropped lower and softer, yet grew more penetrating, as she posed the night's central question, "what is the truest, most core 'strength' in a marriage—the one that actually determines the lasting quality of the bond, that determines how both people genuinely feel within it?"
She asked and answered herself, her gaze clear as starlight. "I believe it's spiritual stability—the capacity to carry yourself and to nourish. I call it the invisible 'temple of the spirit' inside a person."
"Try to imagine," her voice was like a gentle night breeze, guiding her listeners into a state of inner reflection, "maybe I don't earn the most in the household, maybe I do the housework only so-so, sometimes lazy and unwilling to move. But from the bottom of my heart I like myself this way, I accept this state, and I don't feel anxious or attack myself for it. When I'm not flush with money, I can still feel a real, delicate joy because of a beautiful sunset outside the window today, a cool breeze passing through the leaves, or a cluster of flowers in full bloom I happen to meet by the roadside. I can keep up some habit that keeps me healthy—exercise, an early bedtime—because I cherish my body, the 'vessel' of this temple. I can easily find small things that make me happy and absorbed: reading a few pages, watching a good film, or just having one quality, unhurried chat with a close friend. My emotions aren't easily swayed by the outside world or other people's judgments; my sense of worth isn't built on my partner's feedback and attitude."
Her gaze, under the camera, became utterly firm and bright—a strength sought from within and that cannot be taken away. "A me like this is stable inside, self-sufficient, with an endless wellspring of vitality and joy. So then, in this marriage, my partner—no matter how successful he is out there, no matter how much wealth and social standing he holds—if his inner world is adrift, panicked, hollow, unable to find self-recognition or inner peace, then without me he might be like a 'lost, wandering soul' sitting amid splendor with nowhere for his heart to rest. He'd have so much, yet nowhere to settle his soul. But because I exist, because of this 'field' we've built together—full of stable emotional energy, of deep mutual understanding and acceptance—no matter what storms, pressures, and challenges he meets out there, the moment he returns to this space he finds a deep peace and a sense of being mended, the feeling that he's truly 'come home.' And this 'home' isn't only the house as physical space; it's a harbor where the heart can fully relax and be nourished."
"That," Lin Wan concluded, her tone solemn and calm, "is what I understand the 'temple of the spirit' to be. It isn't swaggering dominance, it isn't a show of control—it's a quiet, unshakable inner strength and order. You don't need to be baring your teeth and brandishing sharp words every moment to prove how impressive or how right you are. Your very existence—your stable, joyful, vital, self-governed state—is the most precious contribution and the deepest nourishment to this family system. Once you've built your inner temple well, made it solid, bright, and warm, you'll naturally draw to you people on the same frequency who need that kind of energy, and you'll be able to give the people you love a real, effective shelter from the storm—the storms of the spirit."
【The comments were deeply moved by this, surging with strong emotional resonance and reflection:
[Crying… I feel like I'm exactly that "lost, wandering soul" inside. On the surface I can do anything; inside I'm hollow.]
["Temple of the spirit"—what a phrase. I'm carving it into my mind as a goal for my life's practice.]
[So true strength is an inner calm, is having the ability to make yourself happy. What Wanwan said tonight is worth a million.]
[Please, Wanwan—how exactly do I start building my own "temple of the spirit"? I have no idea where to begin.]
"How to start building it?" Lin Wan saw that last comment and smiled gently, the smile full of encouragement and conviction. "It really is a long process that takes ongoing patience and self-awareness—there are no shortcuts. But we can begin with very small, concrete things. For example: every day, even just fifteen minutes, given entirely to yourself—handling nothing, doing nothing for any practical gain, only one small thing that is purely for your own happiness and relaxation. Practice noticing your emotions; when one arrives, don't rush to judge it good or bad, don't immediately act on it—just observe and accept its presence gently, like a bystander. Actively develop one or two hobbies or activities that absorb you so fully you forget the passing of time. Treat your true inner feelings and needs with sincerity—don't brush them off, don't run from them, practice being tender with yourself. Slowly, you'll grow more and more familiar with yourself, understand yourself, accept yourself, and then truly come to like and trust yourself. Your 'temple of the spirit' will, in this process, be built up brick by brick, growing steadily firmer."
"When your temple is built solid enough and bright enough, you'll be amazed to find that the petty grievances in marriage that once gnawed at you, the contests of strength and weakness that filled you with fear and unease, the anxieties born of comparison—they all quietly fade, and matter less and less. Because you no longer need to desperately extract attention, approval, and security from the other person to fill the hollow inside you. You are already complete in yourself. And so you can give love and care more freely, more abundantly, and you can receive the other's love and devotion more openly, more easily. Your relationship will gradually move from a tense 'survival cooperation' full of power-jockeying toward something deeper—a seeing of each other's souls, a leaning on each other, a shared dance of life."
The background music in the livestream had shifted, without anyone noticing, to a more ethereal, soothing piano piece, the notes flowing like water through the quiet summer night.
Lin Wan looked into the camera, her gaze tender as water yet holding the clear strength of someone who had passed through the fog. "So, dear friends—while we work to hone our social survival skills and raise our external value, please, truly, don't forget to spend more thought and time turning back to water the garden inside us, to build that 'temple of the spirit' that belongs to you and you alone, that can stand serene no matter how the storms rage outside. That is the most precious, most unassailable estate of your whole life—and the most solid, deepest home and far shore of your existence."
"All right, let's end tonight here. May we all, amid the noise of reality, walk step by step toward inner steadiness, becoming people who own their own temple and are unshaken by the storms. You're welcome to leave a comment with your own views and confusions about marriage—I'll see you to discuss them in the next stream. Good night."
The lights slowly dimmed, and the livestream ended. But the concept of the 'temple of the spirit'—together with Lin Wan's words tonight, a fusion of the keenest clear-eyed insight into reality and a profound inner pursuit—was like a seed brimming with life, planted deep, along with the stillness of the summer night, into the heart of every listener. In many days and nights to come, this seed might quietly sprout and grow, guiding them through the tangled maze of marriage and life to find their own quiet and powerful strength.
The livestream screen went fully dark, and the last bit of light from the running equipment went out too. The room held only the hazy glow seeping in from the distant city lights beyond the window, sketching the furniture into quiet outlines.
Lin Wan was still sitting in the chair, holding her pose toward the camera, only her spine relaxing almost imperceptibly—a hidden trace of exhaustion slipping quietly from the straight line of her shoulders. Two solid hours of high-intensity output, demanding both emotional empathy and rational self-disclosure, had left her temples aching faintly. She raised a hand and rubbed them lightly with her fingertips.
Almost the instant she lowered her hand, the study's half-open door was pushed silently inward. A tall figure came in carrying the cool air from outside, his footsteps light, clearly slowed on purpose. He didn't turn on the main light; by that faint glow alone he walked straight to her side and set a cup of honey water, at just the right temperature, gently on the table before her. The base of the cup met the wooden surface with the lightest "tap."
"Master tactician," the man's voice rose in the dim quiet, slightly hoarse after work, carrying a faint, private note of teasing. "How about a report on tonight's groundbreaking ceremony for the 'temple of the spirit'?"
It was Shuai Hongqiang. He'd just gotten back from a social engagement, still carrying the warmth of the summer night breeze and the very faint smell of smoke and drink, but his eyes were clear—he obviously hadn't had much.
Lin Wan didn't turn around right away; the corner of her mouth curved up first. She reached out and grasped the cup of warm water, the warmth spreading from her fingertips all the way to her heart, driving off some of the drained feeling that followed a stream. Only then did she turn her face and tip her head up to look at him. In the dim light his profile looked softer than usual, but the steady, faintly imposing aura ground out by years in the business arena was still there.
"A sweeping victory," she said, taking a sip to wet her slightly dry throat, her voice returning to its usual clarity, carrying a sly, childlike smugness, as if waiting to be praised. "The enemy forces—oh, no, our dear viewers—have been completely won over by the 'temple' theory my side constructed, hearts yearning toward it. The comments are full of longing, everyone declaring they're going home to start hauling bricks and building their own. Emotional value output held steady. Strategic objective: accomplished in full."
Shuai Hongqiang looked down at her; even in the dark he could clearly feel the lively, faintly show-off light in her eyes. He gave an almost inaudible low laugh, the sound trembling faintly in his chest. He passed no judgment on her "battle report," only reached out, his warm, dry palm with its thin calluses landing naturally on her cropped, neat hair, ruffling it with a touch neither light nor heavy. The gesture shed the habitual distance and need for control of the business world, leaving only a familial, indulgent intimacy.
"One crooked theory after another." His tone was flat, impossible to tell as praise or criticism, but the hand ruffling her hair didn't stop. "'Temple'… aren't you afraid that one day the wind will pick up and blow the roof clean off this little shrine of yours?"
"My temple's foundations run deep," Lin Wan said, narrowing her eyes slightly like a cat being stroked the right way, her tone certain. "No wind or rain can touch it. You, though—great benefactor that you are—might want to consider finding a steady dwelling for your own soul too. So you don't keep wandering around out there as a… mm, 'lost, wandering soul'?" She cleverly turned his own metaphor from the stream back on him, her eyes teasing.
The motion of Shuai Hongqiang's hand in her hair paused, and then he crooked a finger and flicked her forehead, neither lightly nor hard. "No respect for your elders." He withdrew his hand, his tone still even, but deep in his eyes a very faint, complicated, hard-to-read emotion flickered past, too fast to catch. "My dwelling—isn't it right here?" He glanced around this room that doubled as study and livestream space, steeped in her personal presence, his words pointed.
Lin Wan dodged away with a laugh and didn't pursue the topic. She set down the cup and pushed off the table to stand. She'd sat too long, and her legs had gone a little numb; she hissed softly and swayed.
Shuai Hongqiang's hand shot out almost at the same moment, steadying her arm firmly. His palm was large, the force judged just right—offering support without making her feel pinned. "If you're tired, go rest." His voice dropped a few notes, the teasing gone entirely, replaced by an unquestionable practicality. "Aren't you meeting that new business contact, President Wang, tomorrow? Another hard fight. Get enough rest, so you'll have the capital to keep 'conquering' your viewers—and… your 'benefactors.'"
He bit down a little harder on the word "benefactor," carrying a teasing weight only the two of them understood, mixed with some deeper meaning.
Steadying herself against his support, Lin Wan tipped her head up and winked at him. "Got it, Boss Shuai. You'd best turn in early too, sir—tomorrow I'll still be counting on you, this grand 'Buddha,' to sit in command and hold down the room for me." Her tone was light, but the meaning underneath was clear to them both. Tomorrow's meeting concerned a new product-pushing brand she had her eye on, and she needed his network and his judgment to help.
"Mm." Shuai Hongqiang let go, answering faintly, and watched her gather the scattered prompt cards and script pages on the desk. He didn't leave—just stepped back half a pace, leaned against the edge of the desk, and waited quietly. His tall figure in the dim light was like a silent, dependable mountain.
Lin Wan quickly tidied everything and shut down the computer. After one last check, she picked up her phone and the cup and turned around. Shuai Hongqiang straightened up too, falling naturally into step half a pace behind her as they headed toward the bedroom together.
"Oh, right," at the bedroom door Lin Wan suddenly remembered something and looked back at him. "Did you eat tonight? There's soup keeping warm in the kitchen."
The night closed over the quiet house once more. In the study, the Paulownia tree and the dried eucalyptus leaves were nothing but blurred outlines in the dark, as if they too had sunk into sleep. And tonight's deep dissections of "interest-binding" and the "temple of the spirit," the crossing of wits, and the complex feelings and unspoken understanding flowing beneath the surface—all of it, with the stream's end, quietly settled, becoming another layer of the solid, unspoken backdrop that existed between this husband and wife. When tomorrow's sun rose, life and the game would both go on.
