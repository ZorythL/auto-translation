Zhao Yiming's Love White Paper
At ten to six on Saturday evening, Yao Yuan's car rolled slowly into the private lot of "Yunting."
It was a members-only club tucked away in an old garden on the west side of the city — no sign out front, reservations only. On this late-spring evening, the sky hung in that not-quite-dark in-between, the air carrying the faint mingled scent of new leaves and tuberose, the wind still sharp with the last bite of the cold season.
Tonight she had chosen a lilac silk-crepe gown that, in the dim golden glow, took on a hazy, refined grayed-violet, as if she had gathered the lingering trace of the last sunset onto herself. The dress was cut close to the body, the V-neck open just right, tracing the elegant line of her collarbone and throat. Over it she wore a knee-length cream belted trench, its lines crisp, lending her a certain dashing edge. The trench wasn't fully buttoned, left casually open to reveal the outline of the lilac dress beneath, setting off her slender figure. At her ears, a pair of violet jadeite teardrop earrings swayed softly. The purple was richer than the dress's, crystal-clear, the light turning in them with a restrained, sumptuous sheen. With her smallest movements they cast a faint violet shadow against the side of her neck, forming an exquisite gradation with the soft lilac of her whole ensemble.
Her makeup was vivid, her brows sharply defined, her lips painted a deep retro true-red that struck a dazzling contrast against all that lilac, lifting her color just right — and masking some of the fatigue these past days of time-travel and the Mirror Pact had left on her.
At her throat, the "heart-guarding" old jade hung on a fine platinum chain, worn against the skin just below the collarbone. The luminous white jade looked all the more warm and pure between the lilac silk and the cream trench, and the few wisps of pale purple mist-grain naturally suffused through it answered, miraculously, the violets of her clothes in a quiet echo.
The night wind passed, stirring her loosely waved hair and the hem of the trench. She drew the front of the coat a little tighter, and her fingers brushed by chance against the old jade at her chest. A gentle warmth kept flowing up from beneath her fingertips, like a silent barrier, letting her hold on to a strange inner composure even after the successive jumps through time and the shock of the Mirror Pact's truth. That reassuring warmth seemed to draw a quiet layer between her and both the lingering spring chill and the tangle of thoughts crowding her mind.
The Bamboo Rhyme room lay at the garden's deepest point. She followed the attendant along a winding bamboo path; the evening wind whispered through the grove, and the lanterns under the eaves washed the scene in a hushed, warm gold.
Zhao Yiming was already there.
He sat at the tea table by the window, brewing tea. Hearing her footsteps, he raised his head. Tonight he wore a pale blue Oxford-cloth shirt, sleeves rolled casually to the elbow, no tie — a touch more at ease than how he looked in the lab. Yet the steadiness and certainty of a man steering a top-tier tech company had not dimmed in the slightest.
"Boss Yao, right on time." He came toward her, took the thin trench from her hands quite naturally, and passed it to the attendant beside him. His gaze lingered on her for a moment, then politely moved away as he pulled out her chair. There was clear appreciation in that single glance, perfectly pure, not the kind that puts one off.
"When Boss Zhao invites me, I wouldn't dare be late." Yao Yuan smiled and took her seat. The room was classic "Yunting" — a long solid-wood table, white walls, a whole wall of floor-to-ceiling glass overlooking a carefully tended dry-landscape garden, a paper lantern hung beneath the eaves glowing warm against the thickening night. Besides the tea set, the table held a deep-gray leather portfolio, not thick, but of fine quality.
"Try this — Mengding Ganlu, brought by a friend this year. The fragrance is a little mellower than Anji white tea. Good for the evening." Zhao Yiming sat down across from her, lifted the white porcelain pot, and poured for her. The motion was smooth and natural, his wrist steady, the stream of tea drawing a clean, shallow arc into the cup.
Yao Yuan raised it, breathed in lightly, then took a small sip. A clear, elegant fragrance bloomed on the tip of her tongue, settling the mind. "Good tea."
"The tea master here is excellent, but I wanted to do it myself." Zhao Yiming took a sip too, set down his cup, his eyes falling on the gray portfolio, then lifting to her, open and frank. "So — shall we get to the point?"
"Let's." Yao Yuan set down her cup as well, sat up a little straighter, the social softness fading from her face, replaced by the caution and focus that belonged to "Boss Yao."
Zhao Yiming picked up the portfolio but did not open it at once. He looked at her, and in his eyes was the earnestness and candor of a scientist facing an important experiment.
"In Phuket, I said I'd prepare a white paper on a 'system interaction protocol.'" He began, his voice level. "After I got back, I spent some time organizing my thoughts. This isn't merely a business plan for deep cooperation merging 'Pretty Big One's' core emotional data with the 'Genesis Engine' technology — though that part is its base framework."
He paused, as if weighing his words. "It's more like a systematic projection and tabletop simulation of every interaction mode, potential risk, expected return, and… sustainable path of joint development that I can foresee arising between our two 'systems.'"
Yao Yuan's fingertips traced lightly along the warm wall of her teacup. She said nothing, only signaled with her eyes for him to go on.
At last Zhao Yiming opened the portfolio. Inside was a neatly bound printed document, the cover the very image he had sent her earlier — "Preliminary Risk Assessment and Benefit Analysis White Paper on a 'System Interaction Protocol' (Draft)." Below the title was a smaller line: Proposed by: Zhao Yiming; Potential collaborating system: Yao Yuan.
"I'm used to thinking through problems this way, especially the important ones." Zhao Yiming turned the document toward her, but did not push it across, as if he wanted to walk her through it verbally first. "Breaking a complex system apart piece by piece — especially one involving people — to see its threads, its key points, its hidden risks. It helps me see the essence of things."
"Part One is an analysis of each system's current state and core assets." He turned to the first page, where a concise list ran down the sheet. "'The Yao Yuan System': core assets include — a mature database of emotional decision-making cases and a uniquely original analytical logic, seasoned commercial operating ability and resource-integration capacity, a deep personal network in Jinshi and certain specialized fields, and the public recognition and trust-momentum of the 'Pretty Big One' brand. Potential risk modules include: a business model heavily dependent on the personal IP, challenges to the compliance and long-term scalability of the data sources, and the possibility that the core algorithm could be imitated or surpassed."
He spoke in pure business terms — cool, objective. Yao Yuan gave a slight nod, no objection. It was all fact.
"The Zhao Yiming System: core assets are the 'Genesis Engine's' underlying architecture and computing power, a top-tier R&D team in brain science and affective computing, an ultra-large-scale dedicated data center already in operation, and my credit endorsement in academic and frontier-tech investment circles. The risks: long R&D cycles, an extremely fast burn rate, uncertainty in landing market applications, and a continual dependence on — and competition for — top talent."
"Part Two is a feasibility analysis of the interaction interfaces and the protocol." Zhao Yiming turned to the next page, where a simple system-connection diagram was drawn. "Our current cooperation rests on a primary, data-interface-level protocol — you license the emotional data, I provide algorithm-model optimization, the goal being to build that 'emotional super-advisor' AI you envision. But after Phuket and the exchanges since, I believe there's room to establish a deeper, more multidimensional interaction protocol."
Yao Yuan nodded. This was precisely her most central demand — an AI advisor that could deeply understand, analyze, and offer concrete strategy, not a high-end chat companion.
He raised his head, his gaze meeting hers, no ambiguity in it, only the pure clarity of discovering a new path and a restrained excitement. "For instance, a deep binding of capital and strategy. Western World Technology is already a commercially mature tech company; our smart chips and solutions have landed with leading clients across multiple industries. Cooperation with 'Pretty Big One' isn't just opening a new, hugely promising application scenario for us — affective computing — it's an important window into understanding 'people,' the most complex terminal of all. We could explore more flexible equity exchanges, set up a joint venture focused on the emotional-AI track, even co-plan the future product forms and business ecology of that track. That would bring long-term value and strategic initiative far beyond any technology-licensing fee." His tone stayed level, but the words revealed a vision that treated 'Pretty Big One' as a major strategic partner.
Something stirred in Yao Yuan. Zhao Yiming's Western World had long since stopped being a startup that needed round after round of funding; in proposing a deep binding, what he valued was the unique worth of 'Pretty Big One' and the space it held for the imagination. That recognition carried real weight.
"The closed-loop flywheel of R&D and data." He went on, his tone touched with a researcher's excitement. "Your livestream room is a 'rich vein' continuously producing high-quality, high-concentration emotional-interaction data. If we could build a safe, compliant, efficient mechanism for data and research collaboration — letting this real-time, living data, once anonymized, feed back into the training and iteration of the 'emotional super-advisor' model more directly and quickly — then we'd have a closed loop of 'real scenario — data feedback — model optimization — better service.' Which means your 'Pretty Big One' would have an AI core that grows with its users, evolves in real time, and gets smarter the more it's used, building a barrier of time and data that's almost impossible to imitate."
This was exactly what Yao Yuan had dreamed of — an AI brain that kept learning and evolving, not an algorithm frozen at some version.
"Mutual empowerment of insight and influence." Zhao Yiming looked at her, his eyes earnest. "You are a top expert in understanding the emotional dilemmas and growth needs of modern high-net-worth women; your insight and content influence are scarce resources. I work at the frontier of technology, especially brain science and artificial intelligence. Our deep cooperation could let us speak together, set standards, even shape the direction of the field, in this emerging space of 'making emotion scientific' and 'making growth measurable.' That would not only cement 'Pretty Big One's' authority in the industry, it would also add cases of genuine human warmth and real significance to Western World's work on 'AI for Good' and 'responsible AI.' It's a shared appreciation of both brand and influence."
He used no obscure metaphors; every point came through clear and penetrating, striking straight at the core demands and future bottlenecks of 'Pretty Big One's' development, and offering deeply attractive solutions and points of shared interest.
Yao Yuan listened quietly, ripples stirring within her. Zhao Yiming's thinking was clear, sharp, aimed straight at the heart of the matter. He saw not only the possibility of cooperation but the path to a fusion and upgrade. The first half of this "white paper" was already a highly attractive, sincere proposal for strategic cooperation.
"So then, Part Three," Yao Yuan said, her red lips parting slightly. "The risks and challenges. The deeper we go, the tighter we bind, the bigger the storms will be."
"Yes." Zhao Yiming's expression turned grave as he flipped to the latter part of the document. "Deep interaction necessarily comes with risk. I've laid out the few most important ones."
"One: technical-ethics and data-security risk. The boundaries of emotional AI matter enormously. Users' absolute trust is your bedrock, and a bottom line we must guard together. We need to build the industry's strictest data-security and privacy-protection system, and reach a high consensus on the AI's ethical principles and values alignment — even set up an independent ethics-review mechanism. On this point, there's no room for compromise."
"Two: commercial-integration and interest-coordination risk. The brand-association effects deep cooperation may bring, the definition of property rights over core data and knowledge, the disputes that could arise in dividing interests, and… the shock to each system should the cooperation fail. This part needs extremely rigorous legal documents and exit mechanisms — safeguarded by the most rigorous contracts and the most frequent, candid communication."
He paused, his tone softening, warmer but still clear.
"Three: system-compatibility risk — the risk of two people running themselves in and building trust." His gaze toward Yao Yuan was frank and direct. "This is the most central risk, and the least controllable. Two highly complex, highly independent systems, each with its own powerful self-driving logic, will inevitably generate friction, energy loss, even momentary 'crashes' in deep interaction. No deep cooperation can avoid it. We're both people with strong convictions, our own logic of action, our own rhythm. Our priorities, our risk tolerance, even our ways of communicating may differ. If these differences aren't handled well, they'll burn up precious cooperative energy and turn what should be a promising thing into a hard slog. So in my vision, beyond the black-and-white agreement, an efficient, candid, mutually tunable mechanism of communication and trust between us may matter just as much as the business terms themselves."
He spoke plainly. Yao Yuan thought of her own experiences with partners and past collaborators, and knew well how true his words were. The heart-guarding old jade lay warm against her skin, letting her keep a strange calm even as she weighed these very real challenges.
"And finally, the 'far shore' we might reach if we can cross all this." Zhao Yiming turned to the last few pages — no complex charts here, only headline text. "The benefit analysis. The most direct is enormous commercial success and an unshakable position in the industry. Deeper than that, I believe this kind of high-quality, deep collaboration lets each of us break through some of our existing limits, reaching heights of insight and networks of resources we could never touch going it alone. You'd gain a constantly evolving, truly intelligent strategic-grade weapon, completely solving the over-dependence on the personal IP, upgrading 'Pretty Big One' from an excellent brand into a platform with a powerful technical core and ecosystem. And I — and Western World — through this most complex 'human nature' application scenario, could temper our technology, expand the boundary of our understanding of 'intelligence,' and leave behind genuinely influential work at the intersection of technology and humanity."
Yao Yuan lowered her lashes, watching the tea leaves rise and sink in her cup.
He closed the portfolio and gently pushed it once more toward Yao Yuan.
"All my thinking is in here. You can take it back and weigh it carefully against 'Pretty Big One's' actual situation." He leaned back in his chair, showing for the first time tonight a flicker of something almost like "uncertainty," though it was quickly covered by his habitual calm, giving way to a faint but genuine smile. "This is only a projection based on the present and on future possibilities. In actual interaction, the variables are endless. But I think, with the abilities and resources we both have, it's worth a try."
The room fell quiet, only the faint sound of wind outside and the rustle of bamboo leaves brushing together.
Yao Yuan did not reach for the document at once. She raised her head and looked at Zhao Yiming. The lamplight cast soft shadows on his face; he looked earnest to the point of reverence, like a scientist submitting his life's work to a review board.
"Very Zhao Yiming." She suddenly let out a soft laugh, and there was a touch of appreciative feeling in it. "You saw not just where 'Pretty Big One' stands now, but the bottleneck it may hit in the future — and the key to breaking through it. Especially the parts on the data closed-loop flywheel and on cognitive empowerment — those are exactly what I've been thinking about lately. Including… human relationships."
Zhao Yiming did not deny it, saying frankly, "It's the only thing I'm good at, and the way I consider most responsible. Define clearly, assess fully, then choose. It's more likely to reach a sustainable state of coordination than vague feelings and impulsive decisions."
"A sustainable state of coordination…" Yao Yuan repeated the phrase, her fingertips unconsciously brushing the heart-guarding old jade against her skin. The gentle warmth made her think of Old Chen's warning, of the Mirror Pact's eerie, un-modelable bond, of the fragments of fate that kept dragging her uncontrollably back into the "past."
The man before her was trying to use the most rational framework to define and plan a relationship full of uncertainty. While she had only just struggled free of the most irrational, fate-bound entanglement, still carrying against her chest a piece of old jade meant to "settle the soul."
The contrast was absurd, and yet, strangely… it made her feel grounded.
At least here, everything could be analyzed, discussed, defined. Even the risks, if there were any, were risks written down on paper.
She paused, then spoke slowly, meeting his waiting gaze. "Your 'white paper' draft has a clear framework, rigorous logic, and well-considered risks. As a proposal for commercial and strategic cooperation, it already holds high value for discussion. But, Zhao Yiming — in your blueprint, when our cooperation deepens to a certain point, say when we diverge on strategic priorities, or differ in philosophy on handling some thorny problem — what do we rely on to reach consensus? Do we look at equity ratios, at short-term data, or do we return to the shared 'North Star' goal we both agreed on when we first decided to join hands? And if even our understanding of that 'North Star' drifts as we grow, how do we recalibrate? This conflict-resolution mechanism — does your agreement have a preset plan for it?"
The question pointed sharply at the most central decision-making mechanism and values-alignment problem in deep cooperation — the most essential question of power and coordination in any relationship.
Zhao Yiming had clearly thought it through. He leaned forward slightly, hands clasped on the table, the posture of someone in earnest discussion.
"An excellent question, and the one I care about most." His pace was steady, his reasoning clear. "In my understanding, deep cooperation — especially the kind we're discussing, involving strategy and core resources — shouldn't rest on simple equity votes or one party's say-so. It should be built on co-governance."
"First, right at the start, we must clearly define our shared 'North Star' — not just commercial success, but what kind of product we want to create, what kind of users we want to serve, what ethical bottom line we hold. Write it down as the highest principle."
"Second, establish a regular, high-quality mechanism for strategic alignment. Not reporting — real brainstorming and synced understanding. Making sure we always stay on the same frequency about the big direction."
"Then, on specific decisions, we can design a tiered decision mechanism. Pure commercial and technical questions can go by professional division of labor and pre-agreed authority; anything involving strategic direction, core values, or major risk must reach deep consensus between both core decision-makers. And if we truly can't agree…" He looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes clear and firm. "The preset 'safety valve' is to pause, step back, or bring in a third-party advisor we both trust for perspective — but never to force something through at the cost of the 'North Star' goal or of damaging each other's trust."
"As for whether the 'North Star' itself needs recalibrating," he continued, "of course it does. Times change, we grow. But the process of recalibration must be one both sides take part in, debate fully, and finally, willingly, agree to. That in itself is the deepest test and tempering of the cooperative relationship."
He looked at her, his eyes clear and firm, and finished: "The foundation of all this requires highly efficient, candid communication and deep mutual trust. That can't be written into a contract, yet it's the soil in which every contract either thrives or fails. I think we at least both have the will to cultivate that soil."
Yao Yuan held his gaze. His answer was rational, thorough, and full of respect for a partner's independence. He hadn't dodged the difficulties; he'd tried to build mechanisms to solve them. That attitude was more reassuring than any flowery promise.
"I need time to assess this draft in detail with my core team." Yao Yuan said at last, her tone professionally cautious, yet the shadow in her eyes — from fatigue and the matter of the Mirror Pact — seemed lit a little by the other's clear, firm thinking. "But there's no doubt: the directions for deepening you've pointed to, especially building the data closed-loop and jointly defining industry standards, align closely with 'Pretty Big One's' strategic vision for the next stage. I'm personally very interested."
A relaxed smile spread across Zhao Yiming's face, a pleasure that came from the heart. "Wonderful. The value of this draft is exactly that it can open a conversation like this. Take your time assessing it. Any thoughts at all, and we can talk again whenever you like."
