Chapter 7 — Traveling Back to Tell My 22-Year-Old Self to Break Up

It wasn't until she was sitting in the taxi, had given the address of that little nest, and had shut the door, sealing out the whole world, that she let her ramrod-straight spine slowly collapse. She didn't cry. She only felt tired — a cold exhaustion seeping out from the marrow of her bones.
She went back to that little apartment, once full of sweetness, now offering nothing but suffocation. She closed the door and bolted it. With her back against the cold panel, she slid slowly down to sit on the floor. The room still carried the smell of the aftershave he'd used that morning; the shirt he'd changed out of last night lay draped over the sofa; the snacks they'd bought together at the supermarket the week before, half-eaten, still sat on the coffee table.
Silence — boundless silence — surged in like a tide and drowned her.
Then the tears came, without any warning, pouring out. Not soft sobbing, but the broken, hoarse weeping that bursts from deep in the throat after being held back to the breaking point. She clamped a hand hard over her mouth, but the tears ran wild, blurring her vision in an instant, dripping onto the cold floor and spreading into dark stains.
She cried until her whole body shook, until she couldn't stop. Reason screamed in her head: What good is crying? Does it solve anything? Will it change his mind? Beyond showing your weakness and helplessness, what use is it? But emotion was a flood that had breached the dam, sweeping away every levee reason had built. All that past sweetness, those warm little moments, those late-night embraces, those careful, hopeful glimpses of a future together — all of it turned into sharp shards, churning madly inside her heart. She'd thought she was a clear-eyed player, counting her chips, when in truth, at some moment she couldn't even pinpoint, she had already wagered her whole heart. That heart was now the biggest joke of all, and the deepest pain.
She didn't know how long she cried. The tears seemed to run dry, leaving only a physical hiccuping and a dull, throbbing ache at her temples. Slowly the crying stopped. In the dim daylight she looked at the puddle of tears on the floor. Her gaze, blank with pain, gradually gathered a cold light again.
It was over. It had to be over.
But — how to end it?
Tear off the mask, make a scene like a woman cast aside? No. That would be ugly, and it would change nothing; it would only make him and his family despise her more, and make her even more pitiful.
Pretend not to know, keep up this hollow relationship, and wait for him to pass sentence? No. Her pride wouldn't allow it.
That left only one path: to leave with dignity. But before she left, she needed compensation. Not for the money itself, but for the two years of youth she had given, the sincerity she had given, and the dignity that had been so cheaply trampled. A "settlement" large enough to let her leave the capital and start over — or a job opportunity somewhere far from here, where she could find her footing.
Jiang Hai surely felt some guilt toward her, and some residue of feeling. But that guilt and that feeling had to yield before his career. You only had to look at how his career had stalled the moment he defied his family's authority, and how, for the sake of that career, he had chosen to compromise and go meet Miss Zhou, to understand. Men, where their own interests are concerned, are always more practical than women.
Use that guilt to win herself the greatest advantage, and then turn and leave, cleanly. No clinging, no lingering — close this chapter for good.
That was what she, Yao Yuan, should do.
The only question was how to broach it, so as to get what she wanted. Negotiate calmly, or play the victim and plead through tears? How much to ask for, so that he would feel it "worth it" to pay and put the matter to rest, yet not so much that it provoked a backlash from him and his family?
She needed to think it through. Carefully, coldly, think it through.
It was deep into the night by the time Jiang Hai pushed the door open.
Only a single dim floor lamp was on in the room, its light barely tracing the curled-up figure on the sofa. Yao Yuan hugged her knees, her face buried in the crook of her arm, her long hair fallen loose and disheveled, hiding most of her face. She was still wearing the outfit she'd gone out in that afternoon, only badly creased now, as if she had held this pose for a very long time — long enough to almost melt into the gloomy play of light and shadow.
At the sound of the door, her shoulders trembled almost imperceptibly, but she didn't lift her head.
The air was thick with a silence close to solid, and with… a very faint, lingering trace of the salt of dried tears. Jiang Hai's steps halted in the entryway, his fingers tightening reflexively around the cold metal door handle. Everything familiar in the living room — the beige sofa cover she had chosen with such care, the spider plant on the windowsill that she always forgot to water yet that stubbornly lived on, the photo-booth strip on the TV cabinet from their trip to the amusement park last year — all of it now seemed coated in frosted glass, gone blurry and far away. His throat tightened. The serene, almost empty face she'd worn at the mall that afternoon overlapped with the figure now huddled in the shadows, so fragile it might shatter at a touch, and it struck him with a dull, heavy blow.
He changed his shoes, his movements soft, as if afraid of disturbing something. He walked to the sofa and stopped at a distance — not too close, not too far. The lamplight threw a long, silent shadow at his feet.
"Yuanyuan," he said. His voice was badly hoarse, as if he hadn't spoken in a long time, or as if something were lodged in his throat.
The figure on the sofa didn't move.
He stepped closer, crouched down, brought his eyes level with hers. From this angle he could see the sliver of profile that showed past the crook of her arm. Her skin was bloodless and pale, her eyelids red and swollen, her thick lashes wet and clumped together, still trembling ever so faintly. She hadn't cried out loud, hadn't even hiccuped, but that silent posture of utter, abandoned fragility hit harder than any wailing. This was not a performance — Jiang Hai knew it. Yao Yuan might scheme, might weigh her options, might put on all kinds of masks, but this kind of instinctive pain, completely drained of strength, could not be faked.
His heart felt as if an invisible hand had wrung it hard, and a sour ache spread instantly to every limb. The panic, the embarrassment, even the flicker of irritation at being questioned that he'd felt the moment he was caught that afternoon — all of it had long since vanished, leaving only an overwhelming guilt and… a deep helplessness. He reached out, wanting to touch her shoulder, but his fingertips froze in midair just before they met the thin fabric.
"This afternoon…" He struggled to assemble the words, each one like grit scraping his throat. "That was Uncle Zhou's daughter. She just got back from England, and the family insisted I keep her company — have a meal, walk around a bit. I… I didn't tell you because I was afraid you'd read too much into it." The explanation was pale and powerless; even he couldn't convince himself.
At last Yao Yuan responded. Very, very slowly, she lifted her head. Tear tracks crisscrossed her face, her eyes were red and swollen, but her gaze was blank, a calm that bordered on lifeless. She looked at him with those eyes as if looking at a stranger, or as if looking through him at some empty point beyond.
"Afraid I'd read too much into it?" she said. Her voice was hoarse and broken from long silence and from crying, yet strangely flat, with little emotional rise or fall — she simply repeated those words evenly, as if savoring some utterly absurd joke. Then she tugged ever so faintly at the corner of her mouth, a curve uglier than crying. "Jiang Hai, I look… pretty stupid, don't I?"
"No! Yuanyuan, I…" Jiang Hai tried desperately to defend himself, tried to grab her hand, but she dodged it lightly and yet with absolute firmness. The motion was small, but it fell between them like a cold chasm, suddenly and starkly.
"Don't bother," Yao Yuan cut him off, burying her face back in the crook of her arm, leaving only the crown of her dark hair showing. Her voice came out muffled, carrying a hollowness exhausted to its limit. "I'm tired, Jiang Hai. Really tired."
That night, for the first time, they lay in the same bed, but back to back, with a gap between them wide enough to fit another person. The silence was heavy as something solid, pressing on the chest, making it hard to breathe. Jiang Hai could hear the small, suppressed tremor of her breathing, could feel her body held rigid, perfectly still. He knew she wasn't asleep — maybe crying, maybe just lying with her eyes open, staring into the thick darkness. He wanted to turn and hold her, to melt that cold barrier with his body heat, to say something more, even the most useless apology. But his body felt nailed in place. The shadow of his family, his parents' icy words, the Zhou daughter's tasteful, sure-of-herself smile, and Yao Yuan's hollow gaze that afternoon… countless images wove and tangled together, binding him so he couldn't move. In the end, he too just lay with his eyes open, staring at the blurred outline of the ceiling, until the sky outside the window slowly turned a cold pale blue.
The days that followed became a silent, drawn-out torture.
Yao Yuan no longer brought up that day, no longer pressed him, no longer even showed any clear emotion. She went to rehearsal with her dance troupe as usual, came home, cooked, tidied the apartment. On the surface, life seemed to return to some kind of "normal" track. But everything was different. She spoke very little now, her gaze often vacant, able to stare quietly at some spot for a long time. She no longer smiled at him, no longer clung to him to share the funny things from the troupe, no longer left that warm lamp on to wait up when he came home late. She still prepared his meals, but no longer troubled herself over the flavors he liked — just simple food, enough to keep a body alive. At night she always washed up early, lay down with her back to him, her breathing shallow as if already asleep. But Jiang Hai knew she wasn't.
A refined, icy politeness hung between them. Like two people forced to live together — the most familiar of strangers.
Jiang Hai tried to make amends. He turned down unnecessary social engagements and came home on time whenever he could. He bought expensive skincare, bought the dress she'd once glanced at twice but couldn't bring herself to buy, booked restaurants that were hard to get into. She accepted everything, only saying a flat "thank you." The dress went into the back of the closet with its tag still on; the restaurant reservations were always canceled in the end because of "an extra rehearsal called at the troupe" or "not feeling well." He fumbled for things to talk about, mentioning matters at work, mentioning a recent movie. She only answered with "mm" and "oh," her gaze drifting, plainly somewhere else.
This silent, all-encompassing distance suffocated Jiang Hai more than any quarrel. A quarrel at least vented emotion, carried a desire to communicate. But the Yao Yuan of now was like a plant withering fast, drawing in all its leaves, sealing herself inside a glass dome he could not reach. Her pain was turned inward, yet it was everywhere, filling every corner of the place they had once called "home," silently condemning him.
He watched her cheeks grow gaunt so quickly, watched the dark circles under her eyes deepen, watched the profound exhaustion and sorrow in her profile when she sometimes stared blankly out the window. The guilt and helplessness inside him grew by the day, nearly drowning him. He knew she was in pain, and he was the one who had caused that pain yet could offer no cure.
Finally, after a dinner that was just as maddeningly silent, Yao Yuan put down her chopsticks, her bowl of rice barely touched. She raised her eyes and looked at him, her gaze calm and unrippled, yet it made Jiang Hai's heart clench hard.
"Jiang Hai," she said softly, every word clear, "there's no point in us going on like this."
The hand holding his chopsticks jerked. His throat went dry. "Yuanyuan…"
"I'm not making a scene with you." Yao Yuan cut him off, even smiling faintly — a smile thin as a sliver of early-winter ice, breaking at a touch. "I just think we both know some things can't go back to how they were. And I'm tired. I can't keep performing."
She used the word "performing." Jiang Hai's heart felt as if a needle had pricked it.
"I'm sorry…" Of a thousand things he might say, what finally rose to his lips were those three pale, powerless words.
Yao Yuan shook her head, her gaze sweeping across this little apartment full of memories. For an instant her eyes turned dazed, then settled back into that heart-stopping calm. "Sorry doesn't solve anything. Jiang Hai, we're not children anymore. Reality is right here in front of us." She paused, as if gathering her courage, then looked straight into his eyes, with a resolve like burning one's boats. "I started dating you at twenty-two. Half a year in, you asked me to move in together, and we agreed it was on the premise of marriage. And now my best years have all gone into this. I'm about to turn twenty-five. Four of the girlfriends my age are already married, and you still can't decide your own marriage. I never wanted anything from your family, but now…" She drew a breath. Her voice stayed steady, but carried a barely perceptible tremble. "I want some account settled. Not with you — with myself, for these years."
There it was. Jiang Hai closed his eyes. He'd known this moment would come. Guilt, pain, the residue of love, and that family yoke he couldn't break free of all collided fiercely inside his chest. He couldn't give her marriage, couldn't give her the future she wanted, couldn't even give her a clear promise. All he seemed able to give was something… practical, to patch over the debt he'd created, to ease his own guilt a little, and perhaps — humbly — in the hope that it might make her road ahead a little less hard, might let her be… a little better off.
He opened his eyes, the whites laced with red, his voice dry. "I understand. I'm the one who wronged you." He paused for a long time, long enough that the air seemed to congeal, before forcing out a string of numbers. "Is this amount enough? I know it can't make up for anything, but at least… it can give you some breathing room for a while, let you live well."
He named a fairly considerable sum. For him at the time, it wasn't easy, but he could manage it. It was more than just money — it was like an ugly, one-sided compensation agreement, putting a clear price tag on the feelings they had once shared.
Yao Yuan looked at him quietly, no expression on her face. No anticipated anger, no humiliation, no joy. Just that — looking at this man she had once loved with all her heart, with whom she had planned a future, now pricing their relationship in the most practical of terms. A sharp pain stabbed where her heart was, but it was quickly covered over by a colder numbness. So be it, she thought. This way was fine. Bare and naked was always better than dragging on through hypocrisy and entanglement.
She didn't answer at once, only lowered her eyes, her long lashes casting faint shadows on her pale face. After a long while, she said, barely audible: "All right."
No haggling, no crying or accusations — just a brief "all right." That was even harder for Jiang Hai to bear than a violent reaction. He would rather she curse him, hit him, throw the money in his face. But she simply accepted it, with the resigned calm of someone accepting an established fact.
The money soon arrived in Yao Yuan's account. The figure sat cold in the text notification. She didn't touch it, as if guarding some pitiful boundary. The days went on in that suffocating silence and politeness, only now that enormous sum standing between them was an invisible yet heavy wall, pushing them further apart. They still lived under one roof, yet were colder than flatmates. The occasional physical contact — unavoidable, like fingertips brushing when handing something over — sprang apart as if from an electric shock, leaving an awkward blank.
Both of them grew haggard at a visible pace. Jiang Hai smoked harder and harder, his eyes often bloodshot. His work seemed to hit even greater resistance, and he came home wrapped in a low pressure so thick it wouldn't disperse. Yao Yuan grew even more silent. Sometimes, cooking in the kitchen, she would suddenly stop, staring blankly into space until whatever was in the pot burned and the smell of scorching drifted up.
It was a slow, mutual torment, a wearing-away. Each bore the consequences of their choice, and the pain of losing the other. Only, in Yao Yuan's pain there was tempered a clear-eyed hatred and a resolute self-preservation; while in Jiang Hai's pain there twined more helplessness, more guilt, and a surrender to his family's fate.
Another month passed in the long ordeal, and the weather had turned from hot summer toward cool autumn.
One weekend, early in the morning, the sky was overcast, low leaden clouds pressing down. Yao Yuan got up very early and, rarely, put on light makeup to cover the worst of her haggard color. She changed into a crisp shirt and trousers and walked up to Jiang Hai, who was sitting at the dining table staring blankly at his coffee.
"Jiang Hai," she called him, her voice clear and calm, as if rehearsed countless times. "Let's break up."
The fingers holding his coffee cup tightened suddenly, knuckles going white. He raised his head and looked at her. She stood at the window, backlit, her features a little blurred, but her spine straight. He opened his mouth to say something, only to find every word pale and powerless. Beg her to stay? With what would he hold her? Promises? He couldn't afford them. Delay? That would only prolong the pain.
In the end he heard his own dry voice come out, carrying a resigned exhaustion: "…All right."
No question, no struggle — just an equally brief "all right." It set the final seal on this slow death that had dragged on for nearly months.
Yao Yuan nodded, as if she'd long foreseen this answer. She turned, took a folded document from her bag, set it gently on the table, and pushed it toward him.
"This is my job-transfer application," she said, looking at him, her gaze clear to the bottom, holding no resentment and no longing — only a calm desolation. "I want to transfer out of the capital, go back to my hometown, leave here. I'll need you… or your family's connections, to put in a word."
At last, in the most dignified and most thorough way, she demanded the final "compensation" for this love she had calculated over, sunk into, and finally lost so badly: a chance to leave behind every sorrowful piece of the past and start completely over. Not a plea — a cold transaction, trading the last scrap of feeling and guilt left between them for a clean exit.
Jiang Hai looked at that thin application, then at Yao Yuan's resolute, calm face. He knew that this time, she really was leaving. Leaving this apartment, leaving his life, leaving this city full of memory and pain. And he didn't even have the right to ask her to stay.
He reached out and took the application. The paper was light, but in his hand it weighed more than a thousand pounds.
"…All right." He said the word a third time, his voice low and hoarse, as if he'd spent every ounce of his strength. "I'll handle it."
Yao Yuan took one last look around this small "home," her gaze resting briefly on each familiar object, then turned without hesitation and walked toward the door. She didn't look back, and she didn't say goodbye.
She opened the door, and the gloomy wind outside rushed in.
She straightened her spine, walked out, and gently pulled the door shut behind her.
A soft "click."
Shutting two people, and that old time tangled with sweetness, scheming, sinking, and betrayal, forever behind her.
Over the next two months, time flowed with an almost eerie efficiency — fast enough to make her dizzy, yet slow enough to grind certain scars into rough calluses.
Yao Yuan never went back to that apartment. She moved into the temporary dormitory the troupe provided, a narrow but tidy single room with nothing in it but a bed, a desk, and a crude wardrobe. Most of the things that belonged to the "past" she gave away if she could donate them, threw out if she couldn't, keeping only a few necessary clothes and the smallest handful of personal items, packed into one not-very-large suitcase. That case stood in the corner like a silent footnote, ready to set off at any moment.
She and Jiang Hai never met again. All further communication was handled through cold, efficient texts. The money in her account she didn't spend a cent of — a sleeping stone of shame, settled at the bottom of the bank account. The transfer seemed to move along on Jiang Hai's end with unusual smoothness — or perhaps some force, eager to smooth over the "trouble," was quietly pushing it through. She only had to fill out the forms as required, submit her materials, and wait.
In the waiting days she went to the troupe as usual. Rehearsing, performing, smiling and chatting with colleagues, joining group activities. She acted perfectly normal, even more "part of the group" than before. Only she no longer danced lead, stepping back of her own accord to an inconspicuous spot in the rear; and during breaks she often sat alone in a corner, watching the young figures spinning and leaping in the mirror, her gaze vast and empty, as if through them she were watching herself in another time and place, equally full of longing, equally sure she had grasped her destiny. The troupe was not without its whispers — about her sudden quietness, about how her "distinguished" boyfriend seemed to have stopped showing up. But Yao Yuan wrapped herself in a flawless, polite, distant calm that sealed off every probing gaze. She was like a plant abruptly transplanted to barren ground, drawing in all its showy branches and leaves, maintaining only the most basic signs of life.
The notice came on an overcast afternoon. The text alert sounded, from an unfamiliar internal-system number. In terse official wording it informed her that her transfer application had been approved: she was to report to the Jinshi Provincial Department of Culture and Tourism, posted as a choreographer and grassroots arts instructor, with the term to be announced separately. No congratulations, no welcome — just a cool personnel order.
She stared at the phone screen for a long time, until the black characters blurred and bled together before her eyes. There was none of the relief she'd imagined, nor any deeper pain. The place where her heart was felt hollow, only a faint, numb chill spreading along her limbs. At last, it was settled. This way out, bought with nearly three years of youth, a heartbreak, and a cold sum of "compensation," now lay clearly before her.
She didn't tell Jiang Hai. He seemed to learn of it through other channels anyway. The day after she got the notice, that long-silent number sent a text — just four characters and a single mark:
"Safe travels."
Yao Yuan didn't reply. She deleted the message, and the number along with it, wiping it from her contacts completely. Like brushing away the last speck of dust on a table.
The day she left the capital, the year's first scattered snowflakes drifted down, melting into a cold, wet sleet before they reached the ground, dampening the shoulders of passersby and the surface of suitcases. No send-off, no farewells. The troupe leaders said a few perfunctory words of encouragement, some colleagues showed a flicker of regret, but most were busy with their own schedules. Pulling her not-very-large suitcase, a simple backpack on her shoulders, Yao Yuan stood at the troupe's entrance and took one last look back at the gray building from which she had once thought she would leap into a brilliant future. A snowflake landed on her bare neck, ice-cold.
She turned, ducked into a waiting taxi, and headed straight for the train station. She hadn't chosen the faster option of flying, as if she needed a slow, tangible journey over land to confirm the widening distance, to leave behind, bit by bit, that vast city that had carried too much dream and too much pain.
The train rumbled out of the platform, the city's high-rise skyline gradually receding under the leaden sky, shrinking, finally vanishing at the horizon. The scenery beyond the window shifted from the bustle of city streets to the bare winter fields, the gray hazy villages, the bleak woods dusted with thin snow. Yao Yuan sat by the window, her face pressed to the cold glass, her eyes unfocused on the monotonous landscape rushing past outside. The noisy chatter in the car, a child crying, the rattling wheels of the snack cart… all of it seemed to be behind a thick pane of glass, distant and unreal.
She remembered many years ago, also winter, when she had carried a big suitcase, her acceptance letter and a leaping heart tucked close, traveling from her third-tier hometown to the capital, that political center where dragons crouch and tigers hide. Back then the world beyond the window had been brand new, glittering, full of endless possibility. And now, the same journey, run in reverse, carried away only a suitcase emptied of its contents and a weary, patched-up shell of a body.
In the pure white void, Yao Yuan and the young Jiang Hai on the balcony looked at each other in silence across that invisible yet unmistakable boundary.
Time seemed stretched and frozen by this eerie scene. Yao Yuan could make out the fine down on his lashes lit by the sun, could see his Adam's apple roll faintly, could even see a faint, not-quite-washed-out stain at the collar of his white T-shirt. After she asked, "What year is it this year," her heart hung suspended, waiting for the foregone answer, and waiting too for… some verification or shock she herself couldn't quite name.
And yet the young Jiang Hai's expression did not change at all after she spoke. The sharp scrutiny didn't disappear, but it mixed with a thicker confusion, even… a barely perceptible doubt about his own senses. His gaze didn't land precisely on her eyes, but drifted slightly off, as if staring at the blank white emptiness behind her, or, through her, at some point he couldn't bring into focus.
He… couldn't hear her? No — perhaps it wasn't only that he couldn't hear. Yao Yuan raised her voice again, even taking a step forward, her hand almost touching the edge of the scene. "Jiang Hai! Look at me! Can you hear me? I'm right here!"
This time Jiang Hai seemed to sense something, tilting his head slightly. Not the sound — more like a faint change in the air current, or simply instinct.
He frowned, lifted his arm to wipe the sweat from his forehead, and turned, as if to examine every inch of the balcony — his gaze sweeping over the rusted railing, the thriving green plants, the cluttered corner, then settling back on the "empty spot" where Yao Yuan stood, where the scene met the pure white. In his eyes, the clear confusion was replaced by an almost instinctive wariness, as if he had sensed some discordant "presence" he couldn't capture with his senses.
"Strange…" he muttered to himself, shaking his head, then turned back, twisted on the faucet, and went on focusing on the work in his hands, water rushing loudly.
Yao Yuan stood frozen, cold all over. She understood now. In this time and place, in this "mirror" world, to Jiang Hai she did not exist — or was at least a "ghost" he could not perceive. She could see and hear everything about him, while he had no awareness of her at all. An immense sense of absurdity and an even deeper loneliness drowned her. She was like a prisoner forced to watch footage of bygone days — able to see, but unable to touch, unable to change, unable even to be seen.
Just as Yao Yuan felt a cold helplessness at this utter "being ignored" and "untouchability," the glass door connecting the balcony to the living room slid open with a sharp rasp.
A wave of heat, wrapped in the breath of youth, rushed at her.
"Jiang Hai, Jiang Hai! Quick, quick, quick! Catch it! It's melting!"
A figure came barreling onto the balcony, bringing a roomful of liveliness and commotion. It was the twenty-two-year-old Yao Yuan. Her hair was in a high ponytail, and from running and the heat a few loose strands clung wetly to her smooth forehead and slender neck. She wore a goose-yellow sleeveless dress, the hem just reaching her knees, baring straight, fair calves, simple white sandals on her feet. Her face flushed with healthy color from running and excitement, her eyes bright, she held up two cream popsicles dripping fast with sugary water, almost too slippery to hold.
"Slow down, you scatterbrain." Jiang Hai's words were scolding, but his tone was full of laughter and indulgence. He quickly set down what was in his hands, reached out to take the two "imperiled" popsicles, and handed one back to her. "Here, yours. Why'd you buy these? Half of it's melted on the way home — don't we have some in the fridge?"
"Couldn't wait till we got home! And these are the best!" The young Yao Yuan eagerly licked the cream about to drip off, narrowing her eyes in satisfaction, like a cat that had stolen a treat. She sidled up to Jiang Hai and, naturally, rested her free hand on his sweat-damp arm, not minding the stickiness at all, craning to look into the sink. "What're you washing there?"
"A circuit board." Jiang Hai turned his head to look at her, the sun scattering fine light at the smiling corners of his eyes. Easily he lifted a hand and, with the clean back of it, wiped a bit of nearly melted cream off the tip of her nose. "Look at you, sweating buckets, like you've been fished out of the water. Go inside and sit under the fan."
"Don't want to, there's a breeze on the balcony." Yao Yuan refused to budge, taking another bite of the popsicle off Jiang Hai's hand, cheeks puffed full, mumbling indistinctly, "So hurry up and wash. There's the singing contest on 'Tonight's Great Voice' tonight — you're watching it with me!"
"Fine, I'll watch it with you." Jiang Hai agreed readily, his gaze falling on her lips, gone glossy from the popsicle, his eyes deepening.
Yao Yuan seemed to notice, her face going a little redder, but boldly she met his gaze, licked her lips, and smiled like a sly little fox. The air was thick with the sweet, cloying scent of the popsicles, the dry heat of the summer afternoon, and the sweet, sticky pull between young lovers that needed no words.
Standing in the pure white void, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan watched this scene in silence. That girl in the goose-yellow dress, vivid as a summer sunflower — every expression, every small gesture, radiated unreserved love and joy. This direct, blazing sweetness, without a trace of shadow, was like a blunt instrument warmed by the sun striking her chest, bringing a dull ache and a powerful, time-displaced dizziness. This was the summer she was twenty-two, the most passionate and purest period of her love with Jiang Hai, not yet eroded by the frost and storms of reality — when her heart and eyes were full of nothing but the other person and a future within reach, when her biggest worry was probably what gift to choose for the coming Qixi.
She watched her young self rise on her toes and quickly kiss Jiang Hai on the cheek, then run off giggling, the goose-yellow hem skimming the old floor like a butterfly. Jiang Hai touched the spot where he'd been kissed, shaking his head with a helpless laugh, his gaze following that figure the whole way until she vanished inside. Only then did he draw his eyes back and pick up the circuit board again, the smile lingering long at the corners of his mouth, even his washing motions turned lighter.
That evening they squeezed onto the small sofa, excitedly watching 'The Voice of China,' kissing at the dull parts, embracing, planning how they would decorate the bigger house they'd have someday, bickering and making up over what to name their child…
All of this, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan remembered. More clearly, even, than what she now saw with her own eyes — because it was a faded memory she had turned over and over with years and pain. And now this memory replayed before her, so bare, so vivid, while she was nothing but a ghost shut out from it. She watched her young self, and in her eyes were complicated tremors, a cold scrutiny, and a distant nostalgia she herself was unwilling to admit. That naive girl, who thought she had grasped the whole world, whose eyes and heart held only love and the future — how strange she was, and how… pitiable.
The night grew late. Jiang Hai washed up in the bathroom, water running loudly. The young Yao Yuan finished her shower first and, in a cotton nightdress printed with cartoon characters, her wet long hair wrapped in a towel, hummed a song and sat down at the dressing table. That oval dressing mirror set into the cheap particle-board desk reflected, under the energy-saving lamp, her cheeks flushed pink from the steam and her clear, bright eyes. Her skin was smooth and firm, untouched by the marks of time, the corners of her mouth turned up naturally, holding a baseless expectation for tomorrow. She picked up a bottle of some then-popular Korean-brand aloe gel in flashy packaging, about to apply it.
The mirror's surface suddenly rippled, ever so faintly, like water touched by a breeze.
The young Yao Yuan's hand stopped. She leaned closer, puzzled. The image of herself in the mirror began to blur and warp, as if a film of bathroom steam had settled over it. But the steam didn't disperse; instead, within it, another face slowly took shape — a face more mature, more refined, yet also colder and lonelier, its gaze so complicated it made her heart lurch in an instant. That face had outlines startlingly like her own, but the skin was the carefully cared-for fairness of someone who tended it, the brows trimmed clean and crisp, the lips a vivid red, the eyes deep as a cold pool with no visible bottom, settled with things she couldn't begin to understand — complicated pain, a cold clarity, and a trace of… almost pitying scrutiny? She wore what looked like a very fine silk nightgown.
The young Yao Yuan gasped in fright and jerked back, her spine slamming into the chair back with a dull thud. Her heart hammered wildly in her chest, and the jar of aloe gel in her hand clattered onto the desk. "Who's there?!" Her voice was tight, with an obvious tremor. Instinctively she glanced back at the closed bathroom door, behind which came the sound of Jiang Hai washing up, then whipped back around to stare hard at the mirror.
The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror looked at her young self's terrified eyes, a cold calm in her heart. She knew that perhaps only through this mirror, in this eerie space, could a conversation across time become possible. Slowly, she opened her mouth to the frightened girl in the mirror. Her voice didn't travel through the air, but sounded directly and clearly in the depths of the young Yao Yuan's mind, carrying the particular, unquestionable penetration of someone who has been through the world, each word like a bead of ice falling on a jade plate:
"Don't be afraid."
"Look at me."
"Listen to me."
"Leave Jiang Hai."
"What?!" The young Yao Yuan's pupils shrank suddenly, shock overtaking her initial fear in an instant, replaced by a flame of anger at being violated by this absurd prophecy. "What nonsense are you talking? Who are you? What right do you have to tell me to leave Jiang Hai? We're together and we're fine, and then we'll definitely get marr—!" Her hand instinctively clenched the soft fabric of her nightdress, fingertips going white with the force.
"There's no future in it." The Yao Yuan in the mirror cut her off, her tone flat and absolute, without the slightest room for turning back, as if stating a theorem already proven countless times. "Between the two of you, there is no future. His family will not accept you, and in the end he will compromise. You'll be hurt, you'll be in great pain, you'll lose everything you now hold dear, including your confidence in yourself and your hopes. In the end, you'll only walk away in disgrace with a pitiful bit of compensation, and it'll take you years just to barely piece your broken self back together. The arcs you two trace are destined from the start to cross, and then to part. Family, class, the realities you'll have to face in the future… the sweetness now is only a summer cloudburst — it looks gorgeous and freeing, but in the blink of an eye it leaves nothing but damp and stifling heat. Keep walking this road and you'll be soaked through, you'll catch cold, you'll need a long time to dry yourself out — you might even be left with a chronic ailment."
"No! You're lying!" The young Yao Yuan shook all over with rage, tears rushing uncontrollably to her eyes — not from fear, but from extreme anger and a refusal at being dragged forcibly into a nightmare. "Jiang Hai loves me, we're going to get married! He promised me! What on earth are you? Why have you come to tell me all this? A hacker? Or am I hallucinating from too much stress lately? Are you the future…?" She seemed to catch onto something, yet didn't dare follow the thought, for it was too terrifying. She even reached up and pinched her own arm hard. A clear pain came through, but the image in the mirror did not vanish.
In the eyes of the Yao Yuan in the mirror there seemed to flicker a very faint, almost pitying glimmer, but it was gone so fast it might have been an illusion, immediately covered by a deeper, ice-locked calm — half pity, half mockery. "Who am I — I'm you, thirty-six years old, inexplicably seeing myself from fourteen years ago. I think it's an illusion too. But what I'm telling you matters. You have to think it over seriously, treat it seriously. This is destined to happen. The sweetness you see now is all a soap bubble — one poke and it's gone. While you haven't sunk in deeper, while you can still pull yourself out relatively whole, leave him. Go pursue your career, your dancing, your self. Don't stake your future on a man who will betray you in the end — or rather, who isn't his own master. I'm the far shore you're sailing toward. While there's still time, before the boat has left the harbor too far, you can still turn around. Rely on yourself, and swim out to wider waters. Don't drop your anchor at a dock that will never be yours."
"I don't believe you!" The young Yao Yuan's tears spilled over, but she stubbornly wiped them away and glared at the "self" in the mirror. "Jiang Hai isn't that kind of person! We'll prove it to everyone! You… if you're really the future me, then why are you even here? Why are you so… so cold? What on earth did you go through?"
"What I went through is what you're about to go through." The voice of the Yao Yuan in the mirror grew colder still, as if to freeze every possible ripple of feeling. "Pain, betrayal, self-doubt, a long repair. Believe me, it's not worth it. What you now feel you can't bear to give up will look, to the future you, like just one… expensive mistake. Leaving him now, so that when you look back you can still remember it as a romantic, sweet love — that is the greatest mercy you can show yourself."
"No! I won't listen!" The young Yao Yuan suddenly thrust out her hand, wanting to knock the mirror over, but her fingers only passed through the cold glass and met the solid wall behind it. The image in the mirror swayed, but did not vanish.
"You need to calm down. What I'm telling you is the optimal choice for your life right now — the shortcut that skips the detours and goes straight there. Don't forget why you got together with Jiang Hai in the first place. Rather than relying on someone else to vault you across class lines, make better use of every useful resource in your own hands and turn yourself into someone more excellent, more formidable. Stand on your own, and let the higher class invite you up."
"Of course, you can also choose not to listen. As you can see, the thirty-six-year-old me is doing fine too — only my body and heart have been through breaking, and that feeling of patching yourself back together with everything you have is not pleasant. I trust you don't want that kind of suffering. We're the same person, with the same cool reason. Leaving him is cutting your losses, saving yourself. I trust you'll come around."
With that, the faint light on the mirror's surface receded like an ebbing tide. The Yao Yuan in the mirror gave her young self one last deep look, and that face belonging to the future Yao Yuan quickly blurred and faded…
The pure white space began to ripple faintly, the scene before her like a poorly received television picture, starting to flicker and pull out bars of static. The image of that warm, softly lit little apartment was growing unstable, as if it might dissolve at any moment.
Yao Yuan knew this eerie "mirror" journey was perhaps about to end. But the seed of doubt and unease planted tonight in her young self's heart — would it break the soil at some future moment, speeding up or changing certain trajectories?
Or perhaps some roads are meant to be walked once, oneself, battered and bloodied, before one learns to turn back. Some pains are meant to be tasted firsthand before they can be carved into the bone.
She didn't know.
Before the scene vanished completely, she took one last look at that small home that had held her earliest love and pain. Then she closed her eyes.
The endless, all-devouring white settled over her once more.
