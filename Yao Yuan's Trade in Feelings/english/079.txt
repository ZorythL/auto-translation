Feelings are the projection of human desire, the exchange of value needs, the contest for position and power.

It was August, the season of golden osmanthus. The summer heat hadn't fully let go, yet the air already carried the faint first traces of early autumn—dry, clear, the scent of grass and leaves.

Jiang Tiantian's team worked fast. From the moment they'd settled the idea at their last get-together, the two teams had hammered out every detail in just half a month. The interview was set for three in the afternoon on August 16th, at Jiang Tiantian's personal podcast studio in the Cultural and Creative Industry Park in Jinshi. It was sooner than Yao Yuan had expected—proof of just how much Jiang Tiantian cared about this.

At two thirty in the afternoon, Yao Yuan arrived right on time.

The studio was tucked away on the top floor of a grey-brick building, an old factory that had been converted. The elevator doors opened onto a heavy soundproof door and a clean metal sign. An assistant was already waiting and showed her in. The space was more open than she'd imagined, with a striking high ceiling. The original factory's steel framework had been kept, but everything was immaculate. Broad cement-grey walls were paired with dark hardwood floors and bookshelves, and beyond the huge floor-to-ceiling windows lay the park's lush trees and, in the distance, the city's skyline. The air held a faint smell of coffee and old book paper, mixed with that cool, particular scent of metal and leather that comes with professional audio gear. There were no dazzling livestream lights here, no eternal view of the Yellow River outside the window—only a settled, quiet stillness that invited deep thought.

The makeup artist was waiting in a temporary station. Today Yao Yuan's look leaned more toward "intellectual" and "texture" than her livestream style. Her base was sheer, letting the natural glow of her skin show through; her eye makeup was just a soft wash of earth tones, emphasizing each separate lash and the clarity of her gaze; for her lips she'd chosen a matte rosy-bean pink, gentle but with strength to it. Her hair was simply parted to one side, long, the ends slightly curled, falling loose and full over her shoulders. For clothing she'd chosen an off-white three-piece suit, cut to fit her perfectly, with a matching silk camisole underneath—professional and crisp, yet still with a feminine grace. Her nails had been changed to a clean, sheer, healthy color, with a single tiny pearl set on the little finger. At her ears was a small pair of diamond studs; on her wrist were the Patek Philippe Nautilus and the red cord of her "heart-guarding" old jade—the former announcing her standing and taste, the latter offering silent comfort and steadiness. From head to toe, there was nothing aggressively showy—only a beauty and confidence that had settled and matured.

She quickly read through the final, confirmed interview outline. Jiang Tiantian's questions were indeed sharp and probing, never skimming the surface, going straight to the core. Yao Yuan closed her eyes to rest for a moment, ordering in her mind the threads of thought she'd worked through countless times.

At two fifty-five, she followed the assistant into the actual recording booth.

The booth felt more private and more professional than the outer room. The walls were covered with special sound-absorbing material, and the lighting was a soft, warm yellow, creating an atmosphere made for intimate conversation. In the center stood two dark-brown high-backed leather chairs, with a small round table between them holding two glasses of water and Jiang Tiantian's signature black-framed glasses. Expensive professional microphones, a mixing console, monitor headphones—everything was there, silently declaring how seriously this was being taken.

Jiang Tiantian was already there. Today she wore an outfit with a strong personal style, a real "intellectual" look. A pale-apricot linen-blend shirt of excellent quality, the fabric crisp but with a natural crinkled texture, the sleeves casually rolled up to the elbows to reveal clean-lined forearms. The shirt's hem was loosely tucked into a pair of high-waisted dark-blue straight-leg jeans, the waistline sharp. On her feet was a pristine pair of white canvas sneakers. She wore no jewelry—only a black leather-strap watch with a clean dial on her wrist, and the thin black-framed glasses she'd taken off and set on the little round table. At twenty-nine, bare-faced, her skin a healthy wheat tone, her short hair cut in distinct layers with the ends carrying the irregular edge of a wolf cut, she radiated a relaxed, sharp, vital, bookish air. Seeing Yao Yuan come in, she stood, held out her hand, her smile sincere and infectious. "Teacher Yao, welcome. Did you have a smooth trip?"

"Very smooth. Your place puts a person at ease, Teacher Jiang." Yao Yuan shook her hand, her grip neither too firm nor too soft, her smile gracious. She noticed that Jiang Tiantian's hand was dry and strong, her gaze clear and focused.

"Shall we get started, then?" Jiang Tiantian gestured for her to sit and took the chair opposite, adjusting the angle of her microphone. "Don't be nervous. Just think of it as two friends brewing a pot of tea and chatting. The outline's only a reference."

Yao Yuan nodded and put on the monitor headphones. The world went instantly, utterly quiet—she could hear only her own steady breathing and heartbeat, and the faint, faint electrical hum coming through the headphones. That amplified, focused sense of hearing made it easier to concentrate.

Jiang Tiantian made a gesture toward the director outside the control room, then turned to Yao Yuan, her eyes looking deep in the warm yellow light.

"Hello to all our listeners, and welcome to Conversations with Tiantian. I'm Jiang Tiantian." Her opening voice was calm and magnetic, coming through the headphones with a force that went straight to the heart, forming a strange contrast with her somewhat androgynous appearance. "Sitting across from me today is a very special woman. She has many labels—top-tier livestream host in the emotions space, leader of women's growth, successful serial entrepreneur. But to me, she's more like a clear-eyed 'observer of the human world' and 'decoder of its rules.' Her name is Yao Yuan. Yao Yuan, welcome."

"Hello, Tiantian, and hello to all our listeners. I'm Yao Yuan. Thank you for the invitation, Tiantian, and thank you for those kind words—I don't deserve them." Yao Yuan's voice came through the top-end equipment clear and steady, carrying that convincing quality she had on livestream, but with a slightly slower pace that made her seem even more composed.

After the pleasantries, Jiang Tiantian didn't spend long on setup. She went straight to the point, her questions like a scalpel—precise and cool.

"Yao Yuan, today's conversation may touch on some fairly fundamental, even rather pointed, topics. First: I've watched your livestreams and your content for a long time, and you seem to have a unique ability—you can dissect, with extraordinary calm, the complex and subtle emotional dilemmas modern people face, especially today's women, and then tie them to the deeper logic of human gamesmanship, even to the traffic rules of commercial society, forming a self-contained 'methodology.' Where does this perspective, this ability, come from? Is it simply your nature, or was it shaped by certain particular experiences later in life?"

It was a big question, and a deep one. Yao Yuan didn't answer right away. She leaned back slightly, her fingertips unconsciously brushing the red cord and old jade at her wrist, feeling its warm, gentle heat. The warm yellow light cast faint shadows beneath her long lashes.

"Teacher Jiang, that question goes to the root of things." She began slowly, her voice especially clear in the quiet booth. "If we're talking about nature, then maybe I was indeed more sensitive than some people, earlier on, to 'relationships' and 'rules.' But what truly shaped this perspective, I believe, was experience—particularly the experiences that weren't so lovely, the ones full of failure and even injury."

She paused, her gaze growing distant, as if it crossed time and space to look back along the road she'd traveled.

"I've been through love that was based on pure youthful stirrings, yet came to a dismal end because of notions of social rank; I've been through the pain of being scrutinized and weighed in a relationship, and finally cast aside for not being 'valuable' enough; and I've been through those cold moments in more complicated relationships, where I was treated as a resource or a chip to be traded. If those experiences had stayed nothing but a victim's anger or self-pity, they'd have had no meaning beyond wearing me down. But I chose another road—to review, to dissect, to learn."

"I took every heartbreak, every disappointment, every time I was outmaneuvered, and treated it as an extremely precious sample of human nature, a case study in gamesmanship. I stopped asking the emotional questions—'Why doesn't he love me?' 'Why did he treat me this way?'—and started asking instead: 'In this relationship, what were each side's core demands?' 'How did the power flow?' 'How were the chips cashed in?' 'Which rule—stated or hidden—ultimately decided the outcome?'"

Her pace was steady, carrying an almost anatomical calm.

"Once I began looking back with this perspective, a lot of the fog cleared. Feelings are no longer just romance and moonlight—behind them lie the projection of human desire, the exchange of value needs, the contest for position and power. And traffic-based commerce is nothing more than that same human gamesmanship, restaged on a more public, more data-driven stage. The underlying logic is the same. My 'methodology,' if I have to put a name to it, is the 'cognitive map' I've drawn after performing 'clinical dissection' on countless stories—my own and others'—mapping out the laws by which human nature, feelings, and interests interact and convert into one another."

Jiang Tiantian listened with deep focus. When Yao Yuan finished, she didn't jump in at once, letting the echo of that thought hang in the air for a few seconds. Then she nodded and pressed on.

"That's a wonderful idea, this 'cognitive map.' So, based on that map, how do you view this era—the collective mood women so widely feel, where the choices seem greater but the dilemmas are in fact more complex? The call-in cases on your livestreams seem to concentrate exactly this kind of anxiety: disappointment in love, doubt about marriage, a wavering sense of self-worth, a helplessness about balancing money and feelings…"

Yao Yuan let out a soft breath. This question was just as vast.

"The women of this era really are caught in an unprecedented, tension-filled crack." Her voice was calm, carrying deep empathy and clear-eyed insight. "On one hand, we're encouraged to be independent, strong, to make money, to have careers—these are the wings and armor that social progress has given us. On the other hand, the implicit conditioning of thousands of years—that a woman 'should be gentle, considerate, devoted to home, sacrificing for love'—along with the marriage pressure from family and society, hasn't truly left the stage. It's become a heavy background noise. These two forces constantly tear at the same person."

"And so we see this: many women throw themselves into their careers, while inside they may hide a secret unease—'If I'm this strong, am I still lovable?' They give in their relationships, while staying always on guard, calculating that they 'mustn't lose out, mustn't be taken advantage of.' They long for a pure emotional connection, yet have no choice but to soberly assess the other person's 'conditions' and 'sincerity.' This tug-of-war leaves so many relationships utterly exhausting, full of testing, defense, and calculation. This isn't women's problem—it's the inevitable spiritual growing pain of an era in transition, where old scripts and new ones collide as they change places."

Jiang Tiantian leaned forward slightly, elbows on her knees, her posture relaxed but her eyes intent. "So, where do you think the true clear-eyed growth of women's power really lies? Is it in throwing out any expectation of feeling altogether and becoming a pure 'calculator of interests'? That absolute rationality you sometimes show on your livestreams?"

"No—absolutely not." Yao Yuan answered fast, and with conviction, a sharp light flashing in her eyes. "Total rational calculation is just another kind of alienation. It turns you into a purely economic, rational creature, an object, stripped of the warmth and connection of being human. That doesn't bring real happiness or real strength. The clarity I'm talking about is precisely about breaking free of that either/or, binary opposition."

Her tone grew heavier, every word clear and strong.

"Clear-eyed growth means first acknowledging and accepting the complexity and many sides of human nature and feeling. Is there calculation in love? Yes. Is there no binding of interests in marriage? Impossible. But turn it around—is there really not a shred of true heart in calculation? Can deep dependence and feeling never grow out of a union of interests? Equally possible. True clarity is being able to see and understand all of these layers at once, without being hijacked by any single narrative."

"It means you can believe in the beauty of love while knowing how to protect your own property; you can fight for your career with everything you have while not being ashamed to admit you long for intimacy; you can shrewdly calculate the pros and cons of a partnership while never losing the ability to treat people with sincerity. Clarity is the ability to move freely through multiple realities and make the choices that best serve your overall happiness—not just your economic interests. It lets you see through the rules of the game while never fully becoming a cold pawn within it. You can join in the gamesmanship when you must, and enjoy what's pure when it's safe to."
