Zhao Yiming's Fastball

The fourth day was their day out at sea.

A double-deck yacht carried the team toward Emperor Island. The water shifted from emerald green to a deep, fathomless blue, like a spilled paint palette. The staff changed into swimsuits, snapping photos and horsing around on deck, young laughter spilling across the water.

Yao Yuan wore a long white linen dress and a wide-brimmed straw hat, leaning against the railing on the upper deck. The sea breeze brushed her cheek, carrying a salty, briny scent of freedom.

"Not going for a swim?" Zhao Yiming had appeared at her side without her noticing, holding out a glass of iced lemonade.

Today he wore a plain white T-shirt and beach shorts, sunglasses perched on his nose. He looked less like a stern academic and more like a man on vacation, relaxed and easy.

"I burn easily." Yao Yuan took the drink and smiled. "Besides, someone has to keep an eye on these kids so they don't get too wild."

"You always put yourself in the role of caretaker." Zhao Yiming leaned against the railing and turned his head to look at her. "Doesn't it wear you out?"

It was a blunt question. Yao Yuan was caught off guard for a moment, then shook her head. "I'm used to it."

"Habits can be changed." Zhao Yiming took off his sunglasses, his gaze serious. "Like a neural network. Give it enough stimulus and feedback, and it'll rewire its own connection paths."

The yacht dropped anchor near a coral reef. The staff jumped into the water to snorkel, schools of bright fish darting through the clear sea.

Zhou Yifan swam over and clung to the side of the boat. "Sis, the coral down here is gorgeous. You're not coming in to see it?"

"You all enjoy yourselves. I'm fine right here."

"I'll keep you company." Zhao Yiming picked up the thread naturally, then said to Zhou Yifan, "Go on. Be careful."

Zhou Yifan glanced between the two of them, flashed a knowing little smile, and turned to swim off.

The deck fell quiet, leaving only the soft slap of waves against the hull.

"Your brother is impressive," Zhao Yiming said. "Sharp mind, a real gift for research. Our lab needs young people exactly like him."

"He's been that way since he was little—once he decides something matters, he burrows right into it." There was an older sister's pride in her tone. "Sometimes I think he's perfectly suited to be a scientist—pure, focused."

"And you?" Zhao Yiming asked. "What do you think you are?"

The question made Yao Yuan fall silent for a moment.

"A businesswoman? An entrepreneur? Or…" She paused. "A walking contradiction—someone who tries to deconstruct emotion with reason, then feeds that reason back with emotion?"

"That's good self-awareness," Zhao Yiming said with a nod. "But don't you think that very act of deconstruction is itself a kind of defense mechanism?"

Yao Yuan turned to look at him.

"Analyzing everything down to the bone, calculating it all out, putting a price tag on it." Zhao Yiming's voice was soft, yet it dropped like a pebble into the lake of her heart. "That way you can't get hurt, can't lose control, can't… be disappointed."

The sea breeze turned suddenly cool.

Yao Yuan tightened her grip on the glass, beads of condensation sliding down between her fingers.

"Mr. Zhao," she said, her voice level, "we're just business partners. We don't really know each other outside of work—"

Zhao Yiming corrected her. "But some people's presence isn't measured by how well you know them."

He paused, then went on. "I supervised a lot of doctoral students at MIT, and I've met countless brilliant people. But you're one of the rare few who make me feel—I can't crack the algorithm running inside this person's head, but I very much want to try to understand it."

It was a candid thing to say, almost academically romantic.

In the distance came a cheer from the staff—someone had reeled in a big fish.

Yao Yuan watched the bustle and said quietly, "It took me a long time to build the order I have in my life now. Work, family, relationships… everything runs on schedule, controllable and predictable."

"And then?" Zhao Yiming asked. "Are you content?"

Content?

Yao Yuan thought of the nights she was still revising proposals at three in the morning, of the nights she carried the company alone through a funding winter, of the social functions she had to face with a smile when all she wanted was to turn and walk away.

She thought of the white hairs multiplying in her mother's hair, of her brother's eyes gone red from late nights in the lab, of her empty apartment where only the chime of the smart-home system answered her.

"A person shouldn't be too greedy," she said at last.

"Greed is the engine of human evolution," Zhao Yiming countered. "If our ancestors hadn't been greedy, they'd never have walked out of Africa. If scientists weren't greedy, they'd never explore the universe. If you weren't greedy, you'd never have grown your company to the scale it is today."

He turned to face her directly. "Yao Yuan, you let your team be greedy—you say you'll take them to Japan, to Europe. You let your family be greedy—you bring them here to Phuket to experience a life they've never had. So what about you? Do you let yourself be greedy?"

A seagull skimmed across the sky, letting out a bright, clear cry.

Yao Yuan didn't answer.

Because she didn't know the answer.

On the way back at dusk, the yacht headed for the dock in the setting sun. The sky was dyed gold and crimson, the clouds like burning silk.

The staff had worn themselves out and lay sprawled asleep across the deck. Zhou Lufei and Zhang Fengxia sat on the lower level, heads resting against each other, eyes closed in rest. Zhou Yifan was still discussing something with a few young researchers, sketching experimental steps in the air with his hands.

Yao Yuan stood alone at the bow, watching the sun sink inch by inch into the horizon.

"The sunset is beautiful."

Zhao Yiming's voice came from behind her. He handed her a thin blanket. "The wind's picking up."

Yao Yuan took it and wrapped it around her shoulders. The blanket still held the warmth of the sun.

"Thank you."

"You're welcome." Zhao Yiming stood beside her, also gazing out at the sea. "You know, in neuroscience, our perception of 'beauty' is really the end result of processing a series of neural signals. The visual cortex analyzes shape and color, the limbic system generates an emotional response, the prefrontal lobe assigns meaning… But no matter how you deconstruct it, when you actually stand before a scene like this, all the theory yields to the most primal sense of awe."

He tilted his head to look at her. "Just like—no matter how I analyze your behavioral patterns, when you stand here, gilded gold by the setting sun, the only thing I feel is: this moment is worth remembering."

It was too direct, almost nothing a thirty-nine-year-old scientist would say out loud.

Yao Yuan turned to look at him. The last light of the setting sun danced in his eyes—those eyes that always coolly analyzed the world were now brimming with undisguised admiration.

"Zhao Yiming," she said, calling him by his full name for the first time, "have you ever been in love?"

The question came out of nowhere. Zhao Yiming blinked, then laughed. "I have. At MIT, with a German girl. Three years. Later she went back to Munich to take over her family's business, and I came home to start my company, so we split."

"Any regrets?"

"At the time, yes. Now I think it was inevitable." He spoke frankly. "She wanted a stable, predictable life. I wanted uncertain exploration, full of challenges. Our underlying needs didn't match—like two incompatible operating systems. Force them to run together and they just crash."

"So what do you want now?" Yao Yuan asked.

Zhao Yiming was silent for a few seconds.

"Someone who can explore the unknown with me," he said. "Someone who isn't afraid of chaos—who can even create order out of chaos. Someone smart enough to understand my work, and emotional enough to make me forget the algorithms."

He turned toward her, the rippling light reflected in the eyes behind his lenses. There was no glib flirtation in them, only the focus and fervor of a scientist who has found the key variable. "I think I've found her."

The sea breeze suddenly gusted strong, sending Yao Yuan's long hair whipping wildly, the hem of her dress snapping in the wind. She raised a hand to hold down her straw hat—and that slightly hurried gesture, set against the grand backdrop of sunset, sea, and wind, revealed something vivid and real, something that belonged to being human.

"I'm thirty-six," her voice carried through the wind, as calm as if she were stating the parameters of a test sample. "Never married, but I've been through plenty. I've started a business, carried heavy burdens, been betrayed, and schemed against people to protect myself. My life is a complex system I've spent more than a decade writing and patching over and over—a stable version, but also… relatively closed off."

"Thirty-six is wonderful," Zhao Yiming answered at once, his tone a fact-based affirmation. "It means you're mature enough to know what you want and what you don't. Your system has been through a long enough 'stress test'—the core architecture is stable, the risk resistance is strong. Never having married is wonderful too—it means you've avoided having a certain set of default settings overwrite you, and you've kept your operating system independent and adaptable. Having had relationships is wonderful—it means you've been through the breaking and rebuilding of intimacy, so you know better how to cherish it. Having started a business is wonderful—it means you have the courage to begin from zero and the ability to bear the consequences."

He stepped forward, closing the distance between them until they could feel each other's breath.

"The betrayal, the scheming, the self-protection, the entrepreneurship you mentioned… all of those experiences are the most precious data. They're the truest part of your system logs—a record of your decision paths, your bug fixes, and your performance evolution under extreme conditions. It's precisely these 'non-standard' experiences—not the smooth, idealized data—that make you, as a system, one of a kind. And they make it possible for the 'human-emotion-decision' model I study to come close to the real thing."

"As for the operating logic," he smiled, "rewriting logic is my specialty. And the best systems always leave an interface open for upgrades."

The yacht's docking horn sounded, startling the seabirds off the pier into flight.

The staff began gathering their things, getting ready to disembark. Zhou Yifan waved at them from the upper deck. "Sis, Mr. Zhao, time to get off!"

Yao Yuan looked at Zhao Yiming for a long, long moment.

Then she smiled—a smile that held release, resignation, and a certain decision.

"Zhao Yiming," she said, "you're a very dangerous man."

"Why?"

"Because you know too well how to persuade people." Yao Yuan turned and walked toward the gangway. "And what I hate most is being persuaded."

Zhao Yiming followed behind her, amusement in his voice. "Then let me put it another way—I'm not persuading you. I'm inviting you. Inviting you to take part in an experiment, on the question: when two highly ordered systems meet, what chaos and what new life will they produce?"

Yao Yuan didn't look back, but her voice drifted off into the sea breeze.

"An experiment needs a design proposal, ethics review, and risk assessment."

"I'll submit them," Zhao Yiming said. "With the most rigorous scientific attitude."

Dinner was a beach barbecue. The staff cooked it themselves, the charcoal fire reddening one young face after another.

Yao Yuan sat in a beach chair a little apart, watching the lively scene. Her phone screen was lit—Yu Hao's fourth message: "Yuanyuan, can we talk?"

She still didn't reply.

Zhou Yifan came over carrying a plate of grilled shrimp and sat down beside her. "Sis, you and Mr. Zhao…"

"We're talking about the partnership." Yao Yuan took the shrimp and smoothly changed the subject. "You and he seem to get along well?"

"Yeah!" Zhou Yifan's eyes lit up. "He promised to take me on a tour of their lab once we're back in Jinshi. Sis, do you know what their brain-computer interface can already do? The new flexible electrodes they've developed barely trigger any immune response after implantation, and the signal stability is three times better than current technology! If this matures, Parkinson's, epilepsy, conditions like that…"

He talked on and on, pure passion on his face.

Yao Yuan listened quietly, and suddenly remembered how, many years ago, when she'd just started her own business, she too had talked about business models and market prospects with her eyes shining just like that.

That state of burning for something you love—she hadn't felt it in a very long time.

Over these years she'd become more and more like a precise machine: input a problem, output a solution, endlessly optimizing the algorithm—and forgetting that what had driven all of it in the first place was something more primal, more alive.

"Yifan," she cut her brother off, "do you love doing research? Truly love it?"

Zhou Yifan was startled, then nodded earnestly. "I do. It's hard, I fail all the time, but every time I make even a little progress, that feeling… it's like finally finding the handle of a door in the dark."

"Then keep at it." Yao Yuan patted his shoulder. "Your big sister's behind you."

"And you?" Zhou Yifan asked. "Sis, do you love what you do now?"

Love it?

Yao Yuan thought of the flashbulbs the day the company broke a hundred million in revenue, of the thrill when her livestream following crossed a million, of the grateful eyes of the staff whose fates she had changed.

She also thought of the countless sleepless nights, of the hollow politeness of dinner-table functions, of the compromises and calculations she'd had no choice but to make.

"Sometimes I love it. Sometimes…" She paused. "It's just responsibility."

Zhou Yifan looked at her and said suddenly, "Sis, you're too tired."

The words made her nose sting.

She turned her face away and pretended the smoke had gotten to her. "What are you talking about. Go tend to your shrimp."

Zhou Yifan didn't move. Instead he said softly, "Mom told me in secret yesterday that you haven't been sleeping well lately—you keep waking up in the middle of the night."

Yao Yuan was silent.

"Sis, there's no end to making money." Zhou Yifan mimicked the tone she'd used to win over Zhou Lufei. "You should… experience life a little too."

Experience life.

What a luxurious phrase.

Yao Yuan thought of Zhao Yiming's words: "You let everyone else be greedy—so what about you?"

Maybe it was time.

The last day of the trip was free time.

Yao Yuan went alone to a temple near the hotel. The ancient pagoda glowed gold in the sunlight, the air thick with the mingled scent of incense and frangipani.

She knelt before the Buddha, but didn't know what to pray for.

Pray for her career? The company was already on a steady track.

Pray for health? Her family was all safe and well.

Pray for love and marriage? She no longer believed in any so-called match made in heaven.

In the end, she simply knelt there quietly, listening to the crisp ring of the wind chime as the breeze passed through the eaves.

"Does something trouble you, benefactor?"

An aged voice spoke. Yao Yuan looked up and saw an old monk standing nearby, his face kind.

She rose and pressed her palms together. "Master."

"Confusion is like clouds—they come, they go." The old monk smiled. "There's no need to cling to driving them away. Just watch them drift past."

"And if the clouds block the road?" Yao Yuan asked.

"Then wait," the old monk said. "Wait for the wind, wait for the clouds to scatter. Or—take a different road."

He handed her a string of sandalwood prayer beads. "There's a knot of worry between your brows, benefactor—you think too much. In all things of this world, seven parts effort, three parts letting fate take its course. Cling too tightly to control, and you'll only be trapped by it."

Yao Yuan took the beads, the scent of sandalwood seeping into her.

"Thank you, Master."

When she walked out of the temple, the sunlight was just right. She raised her hand and looked at the beads on her wrist, and suddenly she smiled.

Wait for the wind, wait for the clouds to scatter.

Or—take a different road.

On the flight home, Yao Yuan leaned against the window, watching the clouds spread out beneath her feet.

Zhou Lufei and Zhang Fengxia were already asleep, holding hands. Zhou Yifan was reading a paper with his headphones on. Most of the staff were catching up on sleep, and the cabin was quiet.

Zhao Yiming's WeChat had come in just before takeoff: "Once we're back in Jinshi, may I buy you a coffee? About that experiment design proposal."

She replied: "Sure."

A single simple word—yet it was like pushing open a door.

The plane passed through a cloud layer and hit a jolt of turbulence. Yao Yuan gripped the armrest and suddenly remembered how nervous she'd been the first time she ever flew, many years ago.

Back then she'd clutched her mother's hand tight, and her mother had said, "Don't be afraid. We just have to fly across it."

Yes. We just have to fly across it.

Clear skies or thunderstorms ahead—you only find out once you've flown across.

She closed her eyes and felt the slight weightlessness as the plane climbed.

This Spring Festival, the sun was bright and the sea breeze was warm.

And some new possibilities were quietly taking root, thirty thousand feet in the air.
