Chapter 86 — The Mirror-Master's Descendant and the Way to Undo the Mirror Pact

Another month slipped by in a blur of work. Outside the window, the ginkgo leaves turned from brilliant gold to a withered yellow, until one morning a cold wind swept them off the branches and scattered them in a mottled carpet across the ground. The launch of the "Pretty Big One" Emotional AI was like dropping a smart little stone into a calm lake; the ripples spread far beyond anyone's expectations. The strength of user feedback, the paid-conversion rate, the word of mouth spreading on its own across social media — all of it pointed to one clear fact. This was not just a clean technical rollout. It was a beautiful piece of commercial monetization, the kind that comes when insight into human nature locks precisely into market demand. Yao Yuan's team was sunk deep in a high-intensity, high-reward mix of exhilaration and exhaustion. And on the back of the "Pretty Big One" success, Zhao Yiming's Western World was now going all-in on a fully general AI, no longer confined to the vertical emotional lane.
Just as the business was sailing on a full wind, the leads on the Mirror Pact finally produced something real.
Shuai Hongqiang called in every gray-market connection he had built up over the years, and Old Chen reactivated long-dormant old ties. After much back-and-forth and many relayed introductions, they finally pinned down the mirror-master's descendant — an old man named Wang Baoguo — and got hold of his contact details. The conversation went unexpectedly smoothly. The man, well past seventy, spoke in a calm, gentle voice on the phone. He asked almost nothing and simply agreed to Old Chen's invitation to "meet and talk." The place was set: the Guangu Hall.
On the day of the meeting, the season still lingered in late autumn, winter not yet arrived, yet a sky full of fine snow came early. Jinshi saw its first frost-snow of the year, settling over the autumn traces that had not finished falling.
The snow was light — dry, fine grains driven by the north wind, hissing as they struck the windowpanes. The sky was a heavy lead gray, but with the snow-light reflecting up, the room felt clearer and quieter than usual. Inside the Guangu Hall it was warm, the scent of tea curling through the air. On the curio shelves the collected pieces glowed faintly under the lamps, like countless silent eyes watching the strange chain of cause and effect about to be unveiled here.
Yao Yuan arrived early. She wore a smoke-gray cashmere turtleneck and black trousers, her long hair loose, her makeup light — her whole bearing settled into the kind of comfort and calm that made for a long sitting and talking. Only the faint blue shadows under her eyes, dark from constant worry and the intermittent time-travel, betrayed how unquiet she was inside. Shuai Hongqiang came in almost on her heels. He was bundled in a heavy black overcoat, unmelted snow grains still clinging to his shoulders. He shrugged off the coat at the door, a crisp shirt underneath, but there was a tension in his expression he couldn't hide. When he looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze flickered with something complicated. He murmured a greeting, then dropped his eyes and sat down.
Old Chen still had that air of an immortal sage. He sat in the host's seat, unhurriedly scalding and rinsing the tea set. The air filled with the rich, mellow aroma of aged pu'er, that medicinal note unique to it.
About a quarter of an hour later, faint footsteps sounded outside the hall. An assistant led an old man in.
He was perhaps a little past seventy, lean of build, dressed in a half-worn but stiffly starched dark-blue Mao suit, a light-gray sweater beneath it, collar and cuffs immaculate. He wore an old-fashioned pair of black-framed glasses. The eyes behind the lenses were small but bright and clear, carrying the gentle, careful gaze of a man who had spent his life among old papers and documents. His graying hair was combed neatly, his face thin and refined. This was Wang Baoguo. He looked more like a retired scholar from some local-gazetteer office — cultured, quiet, composed.
"Master Wang, you've braved the snow to come — thank you. Please, sit." Old Chen rose to greet him, his manner courteous.
"You're too kind, Old Chen. You called, and of course I came." Wang Baoguo spoke slowly, his voice not loud, with the crisp enunciation of an old-school intellectual. He gave Old Chen a slight bow, then nodded in acknowledgment to Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang. His eyes rested on Yao Yuan's face a moment longer, with a professional kind of caution, as if examining an artifact. Then he took the guest's seat, posture upright, hands resting naturally on his knees.
After a round of tea, Old Chen steered the conversation naturally toward the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror, and the many phenomena surrounding Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang that defied all ordinary explanation — how Yao Yuan would travel back, beyond her control, to some pivotal node in her past fate, and how Shuai Hongqiang, because of her travels, received the "good fortune" that shifted along with them. Old Chen told it as objectively as he could, stating only the facts, adding no embellishment.
Wang Baoguo listened in silence, his fingers tapping lightly and unconsciously on his knee, as if rapidly searching and cross-referencing information in his mind. It was only when Old Chen mentioned the seven involuntary "spirit-wandering" states — when Yao Yuan seemed to return to some point in the past — that his brows drew together slightly, a flash of understanding and gravity crossing his eyes.
"The mirror — did you bring it?" Wang Baoguo set down his teacup and asked, his voice still even.
Yao Yuan took the bronze mirror from the bag at her side, carefully wrapped in a thick, soft cloth. She unwrapped it and set it gently on the tea table. The archaic Kui-dragon cloud pattern gave off a dull glow under the lamplight, the dragon's eyes like two bottomless black holes.
From a plain canvas bag he carried, Wang Baoguo took out a pair of white cotton gloves and put them on, then produced a small magnifying glass. He did not reach for the mirror at once. Instead he bent forward, keeping a little distance, and studied the patterns on its back through the glass, inch by inch — especially that ring of faint, half-hidden cloud pattern and a few barely perceptible nodes. His movements were professional and absorbed, his very breathing softened, as though he were appraising a crucial excavated relic.
Time ticked by, second by second. The only sounds left in the hall were the hiss of snow grains against the window and the faint murmur of water on the verge of boiling. Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang both held their breath in concentration. Old Chen stroked his beard, his gaze deep.
At last Wang Baoguo straightened slowly, took off the magnifying glass, and carefully peeled away the gloves. He looked at Old Chen, then at Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang, his gaze finally settling back on the bronze mirror, and let out a long, barely audible sigh.
"Yes — this should be it." There was a settled calm in Wang Baoguo's voice, and a deep wistfulness. "My forebears dealt with bronze, stone, and old artifacts for generations, and they passed down certain records about particular objects, things not spoken of to outsiders. This mirror, by the scattered notes my ancestors left, is no ordinary old mirror. Its prototype may trace back to the Tang dynasty, the Tianbao years, to a 'Time-Tracing Mirror' cast by a Daoist adept named Lingxuzi."
"Lingxuzi?" A sharp light flashed in Old Chen's eyes.
"Yes. My ancestors' notes record it in detail. This man was deeply versed in yin-yang and the star signs. By some chance encounter he obtained a thread of the 'Breath of the First Opening of Chaos,' and then, with otherworldly mysterious bronze, he poured his whole heart into casting three mirrors, each hiding its own mechanism." Wang Baoguo spoke slowly, his tone more like an academic report than a tale of the uncanny. "This mirror reflects not form, but… time, and fortune."
"Reflects time? Reflects fortune?" Shuai Hongqiang's throat went dry as he repeated the words.
"Just so." Wang Baoguo pointed to the pattern-nodes on the mirror's back that he had examined so carefully. "Look — this outer cloud pattern is in fact an extremely intricate, ancient 'element-gathering' formation, meant to lock in that one special thread of 'qi' within the mirror. And these few key nodes — the way the patterns run secretly aligns with the constellations, with two complementary ancient talismanic-array systems embedded inside. One is called the 'Lead of Time-Tracing'; the other, the 'Pivot of Fortune-Shifting.'"
He paused, as if weighing a more accessible explanation. "By the notes, this 'Lead of Time-Tracing' is triggered when it meets a person of a certain fate-mark, at a moment of intense emotional upheaval. It draws on that special thread of 'qi' in the mirror and pulls that person's 'spirit-thought' — briefly — back to some pivotal node in their past life. It might be just before a major decision, or at the moment a regrettable thing happens. The time is never long — perhaps a few hours, perhaps a day or two. The returning 'spirit-thought' knows what came after, like an observer from the future, and can warn and guide the past self."
Yao Yuan's heart sank hard. This matched her experience exactly.
"Then… can the past self change fate?" Shuai Hongqiang asked urgently, his voice trembling.
"Hard to say." Wang Baoguo shook his head gently, his gaze behind the lenses rational and careful. "The notes record it: the 'spirit-thought's' guidance is like words spoken in a dream. Whether the past self grasps it, whether it heeds it, and how the track of fate actually shifts afterward… the variables are enormous. Perhaps a few small bumps can be avoided. But the great current of destiny — the brief influence of a consciousness returning — is, I'm afraid, limited. However…"
His tone took a turn, growing more grave. "The crux is this: once the 'Lead of Time-Tracing' is activated, the 'Pivot of Fortune-Shifting' is bound to trigger with it. Every attempt the mirror-holder makes to interfere with their own past fate — whether or not anything is truly changed in the end — that 'disturbance' itself is treated by the 'Pivot of Fortune-Shifting' as a refining and extraction of a fixed 'fortune.' This more concentrated 'fortune-energy,' born of the disturbance, is guided by the formation, fused and refined with that special thread of qi in the mirror, until at last…"
Wang Baoguo turned his gaze to Shuai Hongqiang, with the cool air of dissection. "And you, Mr. Shuai. You are the one who gave the mirror. You formed an invisible link with this special 'fortune-conversion' system in the mirror, which made you the receiving end of this 'fortune-shifting' chain. Each time the person of that certain fate-mark — that is, Ms. Yao — has her 'spirit-thought' drawn back by the 'Lead of Time-Tracing,' creating one 'disturbance' to her past fate, the 'fortune-energy,' refined and purified by the mirror's system, flows along that invisible link and turns toward you instead. Whatever the 'bearer' seeks, that is mostly where the 'fortune' lands. Seek wealth, you get wealth. Seek fame, you get fame."
Shuai Hongqiang's face paled slightly, his fingers unconsciously gripping his knees. Yao Yuan kept her face calm, only the hand resting on her thigh going faintly cold at the fingertips. Old Chen stroked his beard, his expression heavy. What Master Wang said matched the notes handed down through his own master's line.
Wang Baoguo's face was solemn as he went on slowly. "The notes carry a warning: 'Cross the mind too often, and you harm the root; fortune shifts without constancy, so bear its fruit with care.' What it means is this — for a person's primary consciousness to cross the barrier of time and space again and again is an enormous drain on the heart and the soul, like overdrawing one's own source. Do it too many times, and at the least you'll see your spirit wither and your memory grow disordered; at the worst… the consequences don't bear thinking about. And the one who borrows the fortune—" he looked at Shuai Hongqiang, his eyes like a mirror, "—seems to reap without sowing. But the fortune he receives comes from the disturbance and refining of another's fate. It was never stable, and it is tangled deep in cause and effect. What is gained must be repaid; the way of Heaven keeps its balance. What you gain extra in this one area, you may lose in another — in health, in offspring, in family ties, or in something else you treasure — meeting a corresponding loss or trial. This is the conservation of energy, and it is the cycle of cause and effect. When you first gave the mirror, your mind was set on 'wealth,' so the return came mostly as wealth-fortune. But that gift was, in the dark, already marked with a price."
Shuai Hongqiang jerked his head up and looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes flooding with red in an instant, a guilt beyond all measure churning within them. He wanted to say something. His lips moved, but no sound would come.
Yao Yuan avoided his gaze and looked only at Wang Baoguo. "Master Wang," her voice rose, unnaturally calm, though listen closely and you could catch a thread of tension, "now that the root cause is clear — is there a way to undo it?"
Wang Baoguo looked at her, a trace of approval crossing his eyes, as if commending her composure in this moment. He nodded. "There is. The family precept says: if some later generation should truly touch this mirror's mechanism by mistake and seek out our line, we must not shirk it. We must help them sort out the cause and effect, and find a way to dissolve the pact."
He pointed to the bronze mirror on the tea table. "We need one drop each of fingertip blood — from the mirror-master's heir, and from the two who forged this cause and effect — dripped together into the eyes of this Kui-dragon. Then we wrap the bronze mirror tightly, nine layers, in pure-yang red cloth soaked through with a specially prepared cinnabar. Then we choose a spot on the Yellow River where the current runs swift and the water's nature is most yin, most heavy, at a particular hour, and sink it to the riverbed — so that it never sees the light of day again. In this way, the Mirror Pact can be undone, the link severed."
A glimmer of hope lit in Yao Yuan's eyes.
"But," Wang Baoguo's tone turned, growing heavier, "timing is crucial. The rite can only succeed after the 'great fortune-bearer' has undergone her third 'travel,' or some multiple of three. The Daoists say: one gives rise to two, two to three, and three to the ten thousand things. 'Three' is the variable, the small completion of a cycle — and the only node at which the cycle can be cut and life begun anew. Miss a multiple of three, and you can only wait for the next multiple of three."
He looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze like a torch. "Miss Yao, you say you have already crossed seven times. Then you have already missed the 'three' and the 'six.' The next workable moment is after the ninth."
Nine. Yao Yuan's heart sank slowly, and yet, strangely, a fated sense of "so that's how it is" rose in its place. Two more times. That unknown, draining journey.
"And moreover," Wang Baoguo's voice dropped lower and graver, his eyes turning to the ashen-faced Shuai Hongqiang, "Mr. Shuai, you must understand. The fortune the Mirror Pact has 'granted' you is not an effect without cause. Heaven's way keeps its balance; yin and yang wax and wane. The more you have gained from this Mirror Pact, the greater the 'cause and effect' you may have to repay one day when it is undone — or if the pact lasts beyond a certain limit. As the saying goes: 'the gift of wealth must be drawn back out somewhere else in your life.' When you first gave the mirror, your mind held 'wealth,' so the return to you was a flourishing wealth-fortune. But Heaven will not let a man have everything perfect. What you gain extra in this, you may suffer in health, in offspring, in family love, or in some other thing you treasure — an unexpected loss or stripping away. This is the way of 'yin-yang in balance.' And it is the most venomous thing about the Mirror Pact — what it gives, sooner or later it will take back, with interest, in the way you would least want to lose it."
Shuai Hongqiang slumped down into his chair, none of the smug ease of a man whose career was sailing smooth. After a long while, he forced a hoarse sound from his throat. "I… I understand now… Thank you, Master Wang."
Yao Yuan looked at Wang Baoguo, her gaze firm again. "Master Wang — so you're saying I must go through two more travels, and after the ninth, the three of us must gather again, at the bank of the Yellow River, to perform the rite of undoing the pact. Is that right?"
"Yes." Wang Baoguo nodded. "I will bring the cinnabar red cloth that's needed when we next meet. The two of you must take care of yourselves as well. Especially you, Miss Yao — be sure to keep the 'heart-guarding' old jade close against your body at all times. It can protect your spirit and soul, and lessen the drain. Mr. Shuai… conduct yourself well. Before the pact is undone, act only so as to keep your conscience at ease, and build up as much merit and virtue as you can — it may slightly soften the backlash to come."
The hall fell into a long silence. Only the tea's fragrance, the sound of snow, and the heavy breathing of a few people.
The riddle was solved, yet the road ahead was still thick with thorns. But at least there was a direction now, a method, a clear and hopeful endpoint to look toward — after the ninth travel.
Yao Yuan let out a soft breath and carefully wrapped the bronze mirror — the source of so much trouble — back up in its soft cloth. The cold of it came through the fabric, but there was no great fear left in her heart now, only a calm acceptance of a fixed fate, and the planning that went with it.
Shuai Hongqiang sat there as if his soul had fled, as if all his strength had been drained away in an instant. He watched Yao Yuan's calm profile, and the vast guilt and the dread of the coming "backlash" wove themselves into an airtight net that bound him fast.
Old Chen rose and made a solemn cupped-hand salute to Wang Baoguo. "Master Wang, thank you for clearing our doubts, for all your trouble. A debt this great needs no words of thanks."
Wang Baoguo waved a hand and stood as well. His gaze swept once more over Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang, and he left them with these words: "Everything has its appointed time. Forcing it does no good; go with it, that is all. Take care, each of you. After the ninth — I'll see you at the Yellow River." With that, he gave Old Chen a slight nod, and just as he had come, he walked off with a steady stride into the fine snow beyond the door. His figure soon vanished at the far end of the lane.
The snow was still falling. Little by little it laid a thin white over the bluestone path, as if to bury every word and secret of this day.
Yao Yuan rose to take her leave as well. At the doorway she paused, and without turning back, her voice came clearly — meant for Shuai Hongqiang, and as if meant for herself too: "Two more times. Get yourselves ready."
Then she opened her umbrella and walked into the vast curtain of snow. Her figure drew farther and farther away, until at last it merged with the silver-white silence of heaven and earth.
Shuai Hongqiang still sat in the warm room, yet felt a cold that cut to the bone. He gazed toward the direction in which Yao Yuan had disappeared, then dropped his eyes to his own faintly trembling hands. These hands had once cradled that mirror and passed it to her — and had caught the "wealth-fortune" that came of it, stained with her blood and tears. Now, at last, the bill of fate lay clearly before him.
He slowly closed his eyes. The sound of snowflakes striking the window, in that moment, came to him like some distant, heavy countdown.
The ninth time. The Yellow River. Undoing the pact. The backlash.
All of it was heading toward a final verdict. And before then, whether it was Yao Yuan or him, Shuai Hongqiang, both of them had to keep walking the tightrope of their own fates, waiting for that destined moment — to break the shackles, or to bear the price.
