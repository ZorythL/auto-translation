The Seventh Time-Travel: Echoes of Destiny
As the car pulled into the underground garage, the last thread of sunset was drawing in along the horizon. The September evening breeze had shed the day's dry heat, carrying the faint chill of early autumn; it brushed the treetops, slipped through the gap in the window, and settled on Yao Yuan's face. She brought the car to a stop, cut the engine, and didn't get out right away. A full day of work, the lingering glow of a successful test, and a deeper feeling she couldn't entirely untangle even for herself all blended together. Out of habit she glanced at the rearview mirror, lifted a hand to smooth a few stray strands of hair, and calmly checked her makeup in the glass. Years of riding the highs and lows of her career, of being tossed about by the warmth and coldness of human dealings, had made composure a habit carved into her bones.
But in the very instant she was about to look away and push open the door, a familiar suffocating sensation suddenly wrapped around her whole body.
The dizziness of her soul being torn loose struck hard; a sharp pain in her skull spread in a heartbeat to every limb. Before she could react at all, her vision went black and she lost all sense of her body, of time, of space.
In the second before the darkness swallowed her consciousness, all that was left in Yao Yuan's heart was a numb understanding—here it was again.
This was the seventh time.
The seventh time she had been dragged into this boundless, pure-white void.
When her consciousness returned, everything around her was still that absolute emptiness: a pure-white space with no edges, no sound, no temperature. Yao Yuan slowly straightened up, long past the panic, the bewilderment, the cautious probing of the first few times. She knew this place too well. Six journeys through time had made this eerie space her most familiar "foreign land."
A cell that held her consciousness captive by force; an uncanny way-station linking the different "selves" across different times and spaces. Its very existence was a mockery of every physical law and logical framework she knew.
Yao Yuan mocked herself with absurd dark humor—she was like a patient forced to come back for regular check-ups, knowing the treatment was painful and the cause unknown, yet not even granted the right to refuse.
Before she could settle her mind, the flat white void ahead suddenly rippled, like still water disturbed by a fingertip, the ripples spreading slowly outward. The scene went from blurred to sharp in moments, building into a setting she knew all too well—
It was the living room of the Jinshi apartment where she lived now.
But the person in that living room was not the her of the present.
A younger Yao Yuan, in wrinkled loungewear, sat curled up with her knees drawn in on the wool rug. An empty wine bottle lay tipped over at her side, and between her fingers she held half a glass of leftover wine, the liquid swaying faintly, reflecting her dejected face and red-rimmed eyes. The whole person looked as if all her backbone and edge had been drained away—broken and helpless.
Thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan watched quietly, an unfamiliar ache welling up in her chest.
She knew she had many faces—radiant, stubborn, shrewd, exhausted, even heartbroken.
All her life she had been good at masking her emotions. When she was sad she never drank alone or sank into private gloom; she would call on friends, wrapping her fragility in lively dinners and noisy crowds. Even if the smile was fake and the merriment was an act, she would never let herself drown alone in grief. When she got drunk she would simply sleep it off, wake up, turn the page, and heal; failing that, she would arrange one gathering after another, embrace fresh faces and new things, and let her small sorrows fade slowly amid the bustle. She never let herself fall this low—this wretched, this defeated, with no one to save her.
But this look of being utterly crushed, as if even her soul had been wrung dry, was something rare in her own memory.
For her—always so proud and so determined to win—to lose her composure like this, she must have suffered a blow that shattered her from the inside.
The worried thought had barely formed when she saw her younger self stagger to her feet and walk on unsteady legs toward the bathroom.
Only then did the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan grow even more certain—this wasn't just her Jinshi apartment; even the decorating details, the placement of the furniture, were exactly the same. A chill crept quietly up her spine. The younger "me"... how could she have come back from Hu City? And moved into this apartment that was like a replica made by fate itself?
The Yao Yuan in the pure-white space found herself drawn along, "watching" toward the bathroom. The younger Yao Yuan turned on the faucet, splashed cold water on her face, then lifted her head and looked into the mirror. The mirror reflected a pale, puffy face with red-rimmed eyes, written all over with helplessness and confusion.
In the very instant those bloodshot eyes met the mirror, the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan felt a force push at her, and her image slowly, clearly, appeared in that vanity mirror belonging to the past.
Two Yao Yuans, separated by the cold surface of the glass, looked at each other once again.
"What happened? How did you let yourself get into such a state?"
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan spoke first, pressing down the alarm and the countless questions in her heart. Her voice had shed its old sharpness, leaving only the worry and calm that the years had settled into. She looked at herself in the mirror—eyes red, putting up a brave front—as if gazing back at an old dream she had lived through but was still powerless to rewrite.
The moment the younger Yao Yuan in the mirror saw the face of her future self, it was as if a dam had finally found its crack: the tears she had held back so long suddenly spilled over. She didn't wail like a little girl; she only bit down hard on her lower lip, her shoulders shaking violently, letting the tears come in a flood. After a long while she managed to steady her breathing, raised her wet, reddened eyes toward the "future" in the mirror, and said in a choked, hoarse voice:
"I... something's gone wrong between me and Yu Hao." She sniffled, trying to keep her words coherent. "Last time... after we met at the place where he proposed, I went to America with him as we'd planned, to visit his parents and family."
She paused, her voice full of bitterness. "Then... his father pulled some strings and looked carefully into my past. He could overlook the fact that I'd had one brief marriage, but... he found out that eight years ago, the massive miscarriage from that car accident damaged me at the root. The doctors said... it would be very hard for me to conceive naturally, and even the success rate of IVF would be extremely low."
Every single word was like a needle dipped in ice, pricking the heart of the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan. So it really was... still this. No matter what shape her earlier "changes" had twisted the road into, the great boulder of fate always seemed to roll back into the same valley—fertility, that measuring stick society, certain classes, and so many men treated as a woman's core value, had once again become the scale on which she was judged.
The younger Yao Yuan's tears fell harder, yet strangely they carried a kind of numb calm. "His father's stance was hard as iron; he flatly opposed us being together. He said the Yu family business needed an heir to carry on the bloodline, and they absolutely could not take a daughter-in-law who... couldn't bear children. Yu Hao... at first he tried to fight for us, but he couldn't hold out against the pressure from his family. In the end, he came to me and proposed a 'solution.'"
She tugged at the corner of her mouth—a self-mocking smile uglier than crying. "He said he was still willing to marry me, that he loved me and wanted to spend his life with me. But he needed an heir of 'his own.' So... he would find another woman, make it a pure money transaction, and have a child. The child would be brought back to us as soon as it was born, and we would raise it. He swore there would be absolutely no emotional entanglement with that woman—it would only be a commercial surrogacy."
She lifted her tear-filled eyes toward the future self in the mirror, her gaze full of the confusion and torment of someone driven to the edge of a cliff. "Tell me... what should I do? Accept this condition, marry him, and keep him... or...?"
"Enough."
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's voice cut her off—calm, even carrying a very faint, absurd trace of amusement. But there was no warmth in that amusement, only endless irony and ice. Here it came, again. In the present timeline, Yu Hao wanted her to bear a child but wouldn't give her marriage. In this past timeline she had "changed," Yu Hao gave her marriage (or at least a promise), but wanted her to raise a child he had with another woman.
A vast, suffocating sense of fate, like a thick black tide, drowned her.
No matter how she struggled, how she "corrected" things, how she tried to pry at the levers of fate, in the end she always seemed to be placed on a scale—"to be examined," "to be chosen," "to be appraised." And the weights on that scale always came back to that ancient system of judging women's worth—marriage, childbearing, the "completeness" of a family. Only the form of the humiliation had changed: from being reduced to a tool in "marriage refused, only a child wanted," to being made a puppet in "marriage granted, but raise another's child."
She had thought that her repeated journeys through time were fixing bugs, applying patches, helping her younger self dodge the risks. But now it looked more like an elaborate, malicious joke designed by fate. You avoided trap A and thought you'd stepped onto a path of flowers, only to find a more hidden cliff B at the path's end. What changed was only the form of the suffering, not its essence. Worse still—because of the hope that had risen from believing the "change" had succeeded, the fall hurt all the more when a more refined betrayal struck, all the more despairing.
"Stop crying." The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan spoke, her voice recovering its usual clarity and composure—the voice of the "Godmother of Feelings." But beneath that composure lay a weariness, like someone who had seen through the cycle of rebirth. "Tears are the most useless thing there is. Besides draining you, they move no one—least of all the people who are already measuring you by their interests and your family genes."
She looked at that face in the mirror, so like her own yet written all over with fragility, as if looking at the self of many years ago who had also once clung to luck, who had also tossed and turned over a relationship. They were essentially the same person; some truths needed no spelling out.
"You're the me of the past; I'm the you of the future. We both know in our hearts that you're just overwhelmed by emotion for the moment. But once you've vented, dry your tears." Her tone was decisive, carrying a hard core forged by weathering storms. "Go and settle accounts with Yu Hao—completely. I don't need to give you any specific steps; I know you won't really swallow your pride and submit. We've gritted our teeth and fought our way here, crawled and clawed, stood up covered in scars—not to be some man's backdrop, not to play along in some performance of being 'gracious and magnanimous,' and certainly not to become a high-end 'nanny' with a marriage certificate, an 'emotional stand-in for a surrogate mother.'"
She leaned slightly forward, her gaze blazing, as if to pierce through the mirror and pour her strength directly into the other's heart. "Listen—so what if you can't have children? If the only way this world has left to measure a woman's worth is her womb, then that's the narrowness and tragedy of the value system, not your flaw! You have financial means, social resources, a clear head. If you love children, there will be plenty of paths in the future. Adoption—giving a child with no blood tie your unreserved love and education—makes you just as great a mother. Child-free—enjoying a pure two-person world with a partner (if there's truly one worth it), pouring your energy into your career, your own growth, and a wider world—is just as fulfilling a life. And even if your thinking changes someday, medicine is advancing too; every possibility exists. The key is—the right to choose must be held in your own hands, built on your will and your happiness, not as a compromise traded for a ticket into marriage!"
The younger Yao Yuan listened, dazed, her tears stopping without her noticing. Her future self's words were like a heavy hammer, smashing the shell that had trapped her in the mire of emotion, exposing the proud core of a soul inside—wounded, humiliated, but never truly dead. She raised a hand and forcefully wiped the tear-tracks from her face, her eyes slowly focusing, recovering a trace of their old brightness and stubbornness.
"You're right." Her voice was still hoarse, but much steadier. "I... I'm just a little reluctant to let go. Yu Hao... apart from this one thing, we're really compatible in every other way—our thinking, our careers, even physically... And I'm already thirty-five." A bitter look crossed her face. "To go and get to know someone all over again, start from scratch, build trust, deal with all the scrutiny and calculation that another person's family might bring... I feel so tired, like I don't have the time or energy for it anymore. And more importantly—" her voice dropped, carrying a deeper wound, "—I can't stand that feeling of being appraised like a product, head to toe, inside and out, and then rejected and abandoned because some 'metric' didn't pass. What gets hurt isn't just my pride..."
"It's your sense of being worth something." The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan picked up her words, hitting the mark, her tone cold. "You start to wonder—besides childbearing, is the person I am simply not worth being loved, not worth being firmly chosen? Are all my efforts, achievements, the charm of my character, utterly worthless in the face of so-called 'carrying on the family line'?"
The younger Yao Yuan nodded hard, her eyes reddening again—but this time with the grievance and anger of finding someone who understood.
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan looked at her and suddenly, half-exasperated, spoke in the tone of a Godmother of Feelings who almost wished she could knock some sense into her: "Look at the state of you! So what if you're thirty-five? Your life isn't even half over! By now you're someone with a bit of success—a career, assets, brains, and a face even I can't help but praise! A three-legged toad may be hard to find, but two-legged men are everywhere—does it have to be Yu Hao and no one else? If you can't find a soulmate who's your equal in career, then find someone you genuinely like, who can look after you fully in daily life, who's emotionally steady and can nourish you—what's wrong with that? Broaden your horizons!"
She swept a disdainful glance over the dejected, lovelorn figure of her younger self in the mirror, her tone cutting: "In the state you're in now, never mind Yu Hao's father looking down on you—even I look down on you! We fought tooth and nail to get to today, and was it so we could pick one of two options a man hands us? It was so we'd have the backbone to tell every disrespectful, unjust term to get lost! It was so we could turn life's multiple-choice questions into essay questions that we set ourselves!"
The younger Yao Yuan flushed red under her words, her self-pity scattered to pieces, while that familiar, unyielding fire she knew so well slowly rose up from within. A little embarrassed, she lowered her head, then raised it again and asked quietly, "Then... what about you now? Are you okay?"
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan fell silent for a moment. Was she okay? There was the unresolved Mirror Pact hanging over her head, the old scar with Shuai Hongqiang that had just been laid bare, the success of her career and the greater responsibilities that came with it, and that brand-new relationship with Zhao Yiming—rational and tender threads woven together, still being felt out. Complicated, exhausting, but a future full of strength and possibility too.
"I'm with Zhao Yiming now." In the end she chose a relatively clear answer, her tone calm. "The 'Pretty Big One' Emotional AI we developed together—the model just passed its final test. It's about to officially launch."
The younger Yao Yuan visibly froze, an astonished look crossing her face; clearly this was completely beyond what she'd expected. "Zhao Yiming? Zhao Yiming of Western World? The one I... the one I've been investing in all along? She seemed to need time to digest it. "A year ago, his core data center had a major accident—the core servers crashed without warning, the whole data-center system collapsed across the board, every link lost connection in an instant. The project nearly fell apart for good, and my investment almost went up in smoke."
"At the time no one had any faith in him; the team's morale fell apart, and the whole industry was writing him off. But he really does have talent, nerve, and backbone—he clawed his way back from a dead end, rebuilt the entire system, and the new architecture was more precise, more solid, more refined than before."
Speaking of those past career upheavals, the thirty-five-year-old Yao Yuan's eyes lit up, her tone touched with anticipation: "I always believed in his ability, and I've always backed him with everything I have. Now we're joining forces to develop an all-purpose AI—not limited to emotional interaction; in the future it'll cover more scenarios, more functions. We're preparing to..."
Before her words had even landed, while the syllables still hung in the air, the light and shadow in the mirror suddenly trembled.
"Wait—" The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan only had time to send half a syllable through her mind.
The scene before her—the younger Yao Yuan's astonished face, the familiar surface of the apartment mirror, even those unfinished words—was instantly twisted, stretched, and then brutally dragged into a dark vortex by an irresistible force.
The mirror surface returned to calm, reflecting only the thirty-five-year-old Yao Yuan's face—tear-tracks still on it, but the light already gathering back within, marked with confusion and deep thought. She gazed at the spot where her future self had vanished, and for a long time did not look away.
...
Cold nothingness; a swift plunge downward.
And then, a heavy suffocation and return, as if struggling up to the surface from the deep sea.
Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open!
Her vision was blurred, her heart pounding wildly in her chest, her eardrums buzzing. She was still in the car, in the driver's seat. The seatbelt pressed against her chest, bringing a touch of real, physical weight. Outside the window, the ghostly white lights of the underground garage shone in, cold.
She was back. Once again.
This journey through time seemed shorter, but the mental shock it brought was more violent than any before. Not only because she'd seen another "self" caught in a similar predicament, but because of the deep powerlessness and absurdity of it—that no matter how she changed things, she seemed unable to escape some core script of fate.
Destiny.
This was a word she hadn't believed in before. She believed that things were made by human effort, that cognition changed choices, and choices changed fate. All her efforts, all her "clarity," were built on the belief that man could conquer heaven (or at least conquer himself).
But these seven journeys in a row, this one most of all, were like resounding slaps across the face of her belief.
The self in another timeline had changed her relationship with Jiang Hai and avoided being humiliated over class and family—yet her mother fell gravely ill, she still went back to Jinshi, and she gave up dance.
The self in another timeline had changed the ending with Shuai Hongqiang, had married him—yet lost the child, and the marriage still fell apart.
The self in another timeline she hadn't interfered with, and Zhuge Jinye—the same blazing fire, the same ashes.
The self in another timeline had changed the fate of being traded by Ding Shitong, had gained profit and the upper hand, and seemed to be walking a better road.
She had warned that self to be careful of anyone surnamed "Yu"—yet the self in another timeline still ran into a "better version" of Yu Hao, and thought she'd found happiness.
And then, just when she thought "that version" might really be happy, she traveled back—only to see that beneath the bubble of "happiness" hid another, more exquisite knife, this one in the name of "childbearing."
While the self in reality, with Yu Hao, was still stuck in the same dead knot of "child versus marriage."
It was as if an invisible pair of hands kept plucking at the strings of fate—no matter which discordant note you dodged, the next one waiting for you was always another stretch of noise, seemingly different but just as grating at its core.
Could it be that all the struggling, all the suffering, all the learning and growth, in the face of some higher-dimensional "setting" or "probability," were nothing but futile, a plot already written out in the draft? Was the clarity she thought she had, the gamesmanship, the calculation, also just the illusion of one actor in this grand drama—an actor a little too immersed in the role, believing she could rewrite the script?
The "heart-guarding" old jade at her chest gave off a warmth noticeably higher than usual, as if striving to soothe her violently fluctuating emotions and over-spent spirit. But in this moment, that warmth made her feel a flicker of irony. The old jade could protect her from being dragged into time-travel too often, could ease the aftereffects—but it couldn't tell her the truth of the journeys, couldn't break this seemingly preordained cycle.
She sat in the car for a long time, until her pounding heart gradually settled and the cold in her fingertips slowly warmed.
No.
A faint but unusually clear voice rose from the deepest, hardest place in her heart.
Even if fate truly has a script, even if the forms of suffering change endlessly, how "I" feel, how I define it, how I respond, how I rebuild from the ruins—that part, no script can take away.
After being rejected by Jiang Hai's family, she had learned to recognize reality's thresholds earlier, and turned her energy toward bettering herself.
After losing her child with Shuai Hongqiang, she had tasted the impermanence of life and the limits of bearing things alone, had gone far away, forced into a rebirth.
After burning out completely with Zhuge Jinye, she had engraved the most extreme emotional experience into herself, and seen clearly the gap between pure passion and the reality of life.
After breaking free of the trade with Ding Shitong, she had mastered a higher level of strategic thinking, turning the exploitation of human nature into a ladder for her own growth.
Faced with Yu Hao's cold-hearted proposal, she had cut off her illusions about "men" entirely, and firmed up the belief that only her own strength was the one way out.
And this time, seeing the "self" in another timeline facing a similar but more hidden exploitation, what she'd given was an unhesitating "settling of accounts" and a "reaffirmation of self-worth."
See—the forms of suffering kept changing, but what "Yao Yuan" as a person forged out of that suffering kept changing too. From wounded and naive, to learning to defend, to taking the offensive in the game, to creating value, to trying to use the lessons and tools she'd summed up to help more people avoid similar pain.
Wasn't this itself a form of resistance? A struggle, within a given framework, to expand the boundaries of free will as far as possible—even to try to change the rules of the game?
Destiny might exist, but it was not a straight slide going downward; it was a sea of surging waves and hidden reefs. Clarity was the seamanship of learning to read the currents, dodge the reefs, even use the wind and waves. You couldn't make the sea calm, but you could choose your heading, reinforce your ship, and through battle after battle with the storms, become a finer, tougher helmsman.
The "Pretty Big One" AI was the sturdier ship she was trying to build—one she could share with more people. It was charted from her countless wrecks on the reefs of this sea of human nature.
Thinking of the "Pretty Big One," of the test that had just succeeded, of that connection with Zhao Yiming where reason and exploration coexisted, of the wealth, career, influence, and right to choose she truly possessed right now... the cold and emptiness that the sense of destiny had brought were gradually dispelled by a more solid strength that belonged to the present.
The Mirror Pact was unresolved; the time-travel went on. But at least in this moment, she was not the girl of eight years ago who could only lie alone on the operating table, not the woman who had to swallow her pride for love. She was Yao Yuan, thirty-seven, with her own business empire, with unfinished career ambitions, with complicated but real emotional ties—and with countless riddles waiting to be solved and people waiting for her to light their way.
She pushed open the car door and stepped out. Her high heels struck the concrete floor, ringing out crisp and firm, carrying across the empty space of the underground garage.
On her way to the elevator, she looked up at the ghostly white lights overhead. Then she took out her phone and sent Zhao Yiming a message—nothing to do with romance, purely work:
[About the visual presentation of the Pretty Big One AI at the launch—I have a new idea. Would it be convenient to discuss it tomorrow morning?]
After sending it, she put her phone away, the corner of her mouth curving into a very faint arc, tired but all the more resilient for it.
Whatever the starry sky overhead, whatever the torrent underfoot, whether fate handed her flowers or thorns—see it clearly, and then, keep walking.
That was the one destiny that belonged to Yao Yuan, the only one—and the strongest.
