All Beings Suffer, All Beings Are Imprisoned
In the pure-white laboratory, time seemed to have frozen solid.
Yao Yuan stood rooted to the spot, every ounce of strength drained out of her by the truth packed into those few short sentences. It was not her body that had given out. It was the spiritual bedrock that had carried thirty-eight years of belief, collapsing all at once.
Everything from the sandbox world was still churning vividly through her mind — the bone-deep pain, the joys — and in the space of one weightless sentence it was all negated: you are clone 001, your life is observational data, your struggle was a performance watched from start to finish.
Yao Yuan slowly lifted her eyes.
There was no loss of control, no wild fury, none of the hysterical breakdown one might have expected.
Thirty-eight years of rising and falling, nine trips through time — they had long since tempered a cold, quiet clarity into her very bones. Right now those eyes held nothing but a barren calm, like a field swept by a blizzard, where not a single blade of grass remained.
She looked at the six faces before her, familiar and yet strange, her voice dry:
"So I'm an experiment you built."
"Then who are you?"
Zhao Yiming took half a step forward.
He was the only one in the sandbox world who had never caused her pain, and he was the fiancé who, just moments ago at the edge of the Yellow River, had held her tight, his voice full of panic and worry. But now, in a white lab coat, his features cold and distant, he stood before her like a stone carving without warmth, every trace of the old tenderness gone.
"We are clone laborers of the Ruins Domain."
His tone was flat, as if he were stating an objective fact that had nothing to do with him. Yet what he stated was a truth about the layers of reality that Yao Yuan had never once touched.
"The small sandbox world you just left sits at the third layer of reality — what we call the 'Sandbox Illusion.'"
"The six of us dwell in the second layer, the Ruins Domain."
"And the topmost first layer, which you have never seen, is the Source Domain — the home of the native natural-born humans."
Yao Yuan's pupils contracted slightly.
For the first time, a whole new picture of the world, sharply tiered, was laid bare before her. Not parallel timelines, not a multiverse, but… a cage of layers stacked one atop another.
Unhurried, Zhao Yiming kept tearing away every disguise, each word a scalpel that cut precisely into the flesh of this world:
"You struggled half your life inside the sandbox, thinking you'd reached the limit of human suffering. But you didn't know that the thing you possess is a luxury we will never have in our whole lives."
"You had a complete life — joy and anger, sorrow and joy, love and hate and obsession. You had room to make mistakes, the chance to start over, ties of flesh and blood, the freedom to choose whom to love and whom to hate."
"We, from the moment of our birth, had our fate nailed shut by our genes."
As his words landed, a faint shadow passed across the faces of the other five in unison. That desolation was no act. It was the weariness and suppression carved deep into their genes by years of shackles and the grinding of fate, the marks left by carrying a heavy load year after year.
Yao Yuan's heart sank suddenly. She realized in a flash that these six people before her — gentle or cutting, the ones who had arranged her entire life — were just as deeply mired in the swamp of fate.
"Our genes, all six of us, are exact copies of the gene sequences of six top-tier native humans in the Source Domain." The next to speak was Shuai Hongqiang, his tone wrapped in heavy fatigue. "Our one and only purpose in life is to live for them, to labor for them, to bear all the worldly suffering, pressure, wear, and loss in their place."
Yao Yuan's breath caught.
"The native humans of the Source Domain have the most lavish resources, the most secure lives, the highest level of access to the world."
"They loathe toil, loathe hardship, loathe being worn smooth by life, loathe every 'dirty, exhausting job' that has to be done with their own hands."
"So they used the world's foundational laws to legally cultivate us — six cloned stand-ins."
Shuai Hongqiang raised his eyes and looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze too complex to read — self-mockery, numbness, and a very faint, almost kindred pity:
"They enjoy, we labor. They rest easy, we carry the load. They ponder philosophy and art, we handle data and crises. We are their shadows, their consumables, the tools they'll never feel a pang of pity for — used up and thrown away."
"Our lifespans are artificially compressed to one-third of the originals'. Our genes are permanently locked by the 'labor contract.' We can never defect, never break the contract, never possess a true 'self.' Even our emotional responses and thought patterns are tuned to the most 'efficient and pragmatic' specifications, so we can shoulder the maximum possible burden for them."
Each word fell, cold and piercing to the bone.
Yao Yuan stared at them blankly, and the spark of rage in her heart — at being deceived, at being toyed with — strangely guttered out, most of it gone.
So… they were not creators holding the power of life and death either.
They were prisoners too.
Prisoners trapped in a larger cage — a cage with genes for locks and class for walls, far more unsolvable than the sandbox world.
"We refuse to accept it."
This time it was Jiang Hai who spoke. This man, forever gentle and refined in Yao Yuan's memory, now had a defiance welling in his eyes, the residue of countless years.
"But we can't rise up against the native humans of the Source Domain. The pressure of the hierarchy, the shackles of our genes, the world's foundational rules — layer upon layer locks off every way out." Jiang Hai's voice was very soft, yet heavy as a mountain. "Upward, there is only a sheer cliff with no path."
"So we could only search downward for a way to live."
Zhuge Jinye picked up the thread. This man who had once burned his life away on the stage, who had given her a pure and blazing love, still had that focused gaze — but its object had shifted from art to a far crueler reality:
"We spent countless Ruins-Domain years secretly hoarding scraps of technology discarded by the upper layers, piecing together the data garbage that leaked between realities, and in the blind spots of the Ruins Domain's surveillance, we built this underground laboratory."
"And then we made you — clone 001."
At last, the original purpose of the whole experiment was fully laid bare.
Yao Yuan held her breath, listening to the true meaning of her own birth.
"We made you not to spectate suffering, nor to verify a theory." Duan Yanqing began slowly. This man, who had once been the guide on her 'life's ladder,' now spoke with a deep weariness and obsession. "It was to save ourselves."
"We wanted to observe a complete sample of a 'natural-born human' life — one with no stand-in, no one to lean on, no one to do the work, where everything had to be done with one's own hands."
"We wanted to know — when an intelligent being is thrown into a survival game it is entirely responsible for, facing real pain, temptation, betrayal, loss, hardship, and love, what kind of life force would burst out of it? Does true growth, the true breaking of a deadlock, the true tearing-free, come from outside opportunity, or from inner awakening?"
"We wanted to find, in your one lifetime, a sliver of possibility — a faint glimmer that could crack the shackles of our genes, dissolve the contracts bound to our originals, and free us from our eternal fate as 'consumables.'"
Yao Yuan's throat tightened, as though something invisible had it by the neck.
So her suffering, her struggle, the rise and fall of most of her life — none of it had ever been idle mockery or a cruel game.
It was the single straw that six people in a desperate corner, with nowhere left to turn, had clutched with all their strength. It was the one source of light they had gazed toward in the dark.
"In the first stage of observation, we watched how you turned your life around by yourself in a sandbox with no outside help at all." Yu Hao pushed up his glasses, his rational, analytical tone hiding a tension hard to detect. "The data confirmed one of our basic conjectures: under absolutely rigid class rules, the effort, endurance, even the local comebacks of a single individual cannot shake the rule structure at the top. The power of the individual has its limits."
"But the greatest obsession of the world has never been 'not trying hard enough.'" Zhao Yiming took over, his eyes settling on Yao Yuan's face with the focus of a researcher studying crucial data. "The deepest delusion people hold is, 'If only I could start over.'"
"If I went back to the past, corrected that wrong choice, avoided the person destined to hurt me, seized the chance I missed… would my life be different?"
The gazes of the six now fell on Yao Yuan all at once.
Calm, scrutinizing, yet carrying a trace of… an almost pitying understanding.
"So we launched the second stage of the experiment." Zhao Yiming's voice echoed clearly through the empty laboratory. "We implanted in you a fake 'time-reversal' access. Using your genes, we cultivated nine secondary clones — numbered 001-1 to 001-9 — each an exact copy of one of the nine pivotal moments of your youth. At the underlying logic level we locked the final outcome of every core fate node, opening up only the room to adjust the process variables."
Yao Yuan's fingertips began to tremble, faintly, beyond her control.
Those nine returns, nine all-out attempts at persuasion, nine times watching her 'younger self' nod in agreement and then walk toward the same ending in despair… all the scenes, emotions, and confusions now strung themselves together in an instant, forming a complete and cruel closed loop of logic.
At last she understood, completely.
Why, even when she had clearly persuaded her past 'self,' even when she had clearly steered around the disaster right in front of her, she still couldn't escape a similar ending.
It was not that heaven was merciless.
It was not that fate was unbreakable.
It was not that she hadn't tried hard enough or wasn't clear-eyed enough.
It was that from the very start, all her 'do-overs,' all the 'salvation' into which she had poured her entire hope and feeling, had been a carefully designed controlled experiment. The variables were the process; the constant was the ending. The proposition she was meant to verify had been written long ago.
"We wanted to verify a second answer." Zhao Yiming looked at her, word by word, sealing the verdict on her thirty-eight years. "To verify whether a human being can break free of a predetermined track of fate by making up for regrets, correcting the past, and achieving reconciliation with the self."
The answer had long been obvious.
Nine returns, nine changes, nine attempts at salvation.
Still… no solution.
"Your thirty-eight years of life, your countless struggles, pains, hopes, and despairs, proved one thing for us beyond all doubt." Shuai Hongqiang's voice dropped low, carrying a profound weariness, even a trace of… disillusionment. "Regret is useless. Starting over is useless. The self-salvation of an individual has limited effect in the face of systemic class shackles."
"An individual's obsession, awakening, even local breakthroughs, can never break through the established rules of the upper reality and the pressure of its structure. True freedom does not come from correcting the past; it comes from a structural understanding of the present and a response to it. But understanding itself, with no power to back it, cannot break through the systemic shackles of class — it remains only the suffering of being clear-eyed."
The laboratory sank into a deep silence.
Only that pervasive high-frequency hum remained, telling of the cold, absolute laws by which this world ran.
Yao Yuan closed her eyes.
Countless emotions churned in her heart — fury, desolation, absurdity, release, a weariness that reached the marrow, and… a strange thread of understanding.
Did she hate them?
She did.
She hated that her whole life had been manipulated, hated that her deep feeling and pain had been harvested as data, hated that all her effort and growth had been only a performance under observation.
And yet she couldn't bring herself to hate them entirely.
Because these six people who had deceived her, designed her, observed her whole life, were themselves pitiful creatures crushed by fate, struggling inside an even larger cage.
The native humans of the topmost Source Domain hid in their absolutely safe nest of comfort, reaping what others sowed.
The clone laborers of the middle layer were trapped inside an eternal 'contract,' carrying their load forward with nowhere to turn.
And she, at the bottom, was trapped in the Sandbox Illusion, struggling in vain, thinking she was fighting fate when in truth she had never once touched the real rules.
One layer pressing on the next, one ring locked into another.
All beings suffer; all beings are imprisoned.
No one was a truly, purely 'evil' person. Everyone was only a fish trapped in a fish tank of a different size, struggling with all its might to swim upward, to breathe, to break free.
The only difference was that some fish had seen the boundary of the tank, while others thought what lay before them was the whole world; some fish were ramming the boundary, while others were preying on smaller fish.
A long while passed.
So long it felt as if she had lived another whole life.
Yao Yuan slowly opened her eyes. The barren ice and snow in their depths gradually melted — not into warmth, but congealing into something colder, harder, and clearer: a clarity that cut through all lies and disguises straight to the essence.
"So what now?"
She raised her eyes and swept her gaze calmly over the six, her voice betraying no emotion.
"Is the experiment over?"
The six were silent for a moment.
Zhao Yiming slowly nodded, the motion carrying the deep weariness of someone who has just completed a great task.
"Of all our experimental samples, you are the only one who has perfectly awakened, broken free of the sandbox logic by your own consciousness, and possesses a complete, independent personality with reflection on your own existence — a successful sample."
"From the standpoint of the experiment, there is no longer any value in keeping you in the Sandbox Illusion."
Yao Yuan let out a soft "Oh," the tail of it lifting, carrying an almost innocent puzzlement — yet it made all six of their hearts clench at once.
"So," she asked, her tone as flat as if she were asking about the weather, "what is my ending?"
The six exchanged a glance.
In that glance there was struggle, there was guilt, but in the end it was all covered over by a deeper despair and survival instinct that belonged to 'clone laborers.' It was Duan Yanqing — the man who had once taught her the rules of 'value exchange' — who spoke the coldest reality:
"The original contingency for ending the experiment was this — to extract all of your consciousness data, your cross-reality gene-expression data, and the complete model of your growth trajectory. That data will be used to attempt to reverse-engineer the 'labor contract' in our genes, to look for a possible loophole or an override."
He paused, his voice dropping lower:
"The data extraction process will completely break down the carrier of your consciousness. For you, it will be a painless, eternal… dissolution of consciousness."
Boom.
The last layer of the illusion of tenderness shattered completely.
Even if the original intent was self-salvation. Even if every one of them was a prisoner of fate. Even if they too were covered in wounds.
When the moment of choice came, when the interests of the lower-layer 'consumables' clashed with the even-lower-layer 'experiment,' they still chose to sacrifice her.
To trade her total annihilation for their slim, uncertain hope of release.
This was the law of survival inside the cage. There was no right or wrong, only position.
After hearing it all, Yao Yuan did not break down, curse, or beg as they had expected.
She only stood up straight, slowly and with perfect steadiness. Inside that thin, twenty-three-year-old frame there suddenly rose an unbending, razor-edged pride, forged through thirty-eight years of storms.
She had known the deepest love and betrayal, walked through the most hopeless swamps, endured countless nights of breaking herself apart and rebuilding. She could accept that she was an experiment, accept that her life was a false sandbox, accept that all her struggles, seen from a higher dimension, were only data.
But she would never accept this — born to be a tool, ending as a consumable, her life and death decided by others, her worth defined by others.
She lifted her eyes, her gaze clear and sharp, like a drawn blade slicing through the silent air.
"I don't agree."
Three short words, clear and calm, yet carrying a force that brooked no question, echoing through the empty silver laboratory.
All six knit their brows at once.
"You have no right to choose." Jiang Hai's voice turned cold, the tone of a 'manager.' "Your very existence is a violation. Your life and death have never been yours to decide."
Yao Yuan smiled faintly.
That smile was very light, without warmth, yet strangely dazzling — like a poisonous flower blooming from the rubble of a hopeless place.
"I'm a clone you created by illegal means."
"My life is a sandbox script you set."
"My pain and my awakening are the data you needed to observe."
"My value is the experimental result you defined."
She took a step forward. Though she stood below them, her gaze carried a piercing power that met them eye to eye — even looked down on them:
"But, from beginning to end — my will, my feelings, every genuine sorrow and joy of my thirty-eight years, every moment I gritted my teeth and stood back up, every choice to seize a glimmer of light in despair… those belong only to 'Yao Yuan.'"
"You were in a desperate corner, and you used me to find a way out. I don't blame you for that."
"But now you want to trade my total annihilation for your possible release."
Her voice turned cold all at once, each word like an ice bead striking a silver plate:
"Impossible."
In this moment.
The fish that had climbed up from the bottommost sandbox, through one bloody, battered collision after another, one stubborn unextinguished thought after another, one rebuilding of meaning after another in the void, until at last it smashed through the glass wall…
no longer settled for merely 'seeing a bigger fish tank.'
It had grown teeth.
It had grown the ambition to tear apart every visible net — and even to ram the boundary of the tank itself, the boundary that had seemed impossible to shake.
The six clone laborers looked at the suddenly transformed Yao Yuan, and for the first time their faces turned truly grave.
They realized in a flash that this 'perfect sample' they had cultivated with their own hands, observed so carefully day and night… really did seem to have leapt out of all their preset models.
She was no longer the variable being observed. She was the new variable about to reshape the whole board.
