Chapter 46 — Back From the Other Side, a Deal on the Table

Like being yanked up to the surface from deep water, a violent rush of weightlessness and vertigo seized her. The last words she'd left unfinished still seemed to echo in her ears, mixed with the low hum of an elevator in motion.
Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open.
Her vision was a blur at first, then sharpened fast. Smooth metal walls, and in the mirrored surface, her own thirty-six-year-old face, a little pale. She was lying on the floor of an elevator, and Yu Hao was crouched beside her, one arm out, as if about to gather her up.
She was back.
From that mirror conversation with her thirty-year-old self, her soul had settled back into place.
Her throat was dry and tight. She opened her mouth, her voice hoarse. "Yu Hao..."
His gaze fixed on her unusually pale face. There was still a scattered, unfocused quality in his eyes, the aftermath of some violent emotional blow. His brow furrowed slightly, and he looked down at her with a natural, genuine concern. "Yao Yuan? Are you all right? Why are you on the floor of the elevator?" As he spoke, his hand was already reaching out of habit, about to help her up.
In the instant before his fingertips touched her arm, Yao Yuan flinched back, barely perceptibly but with startling quickness.
She'd dodged him.
Yu Hao's hand froze in midair.
Yao Yuan lifted her eyes. In that split second, she had already forced down all the daze and tremor the time-travel had left behind, restoring herself to her usual clear-eyed calm. Only beneath that calm there now seemed to lie a sheet of unbreakable thin ice. The conversation in the mirror with the "past," the rehearsal of betrayal, those naked rules of the game — all of it had been filed away in her mind at high speed.
She looked at Yu Hao, this man she had chosen to partner with, the one she currently rated as a "reliable ally" and a "no-strings companion." The worry in his eyes was real. But having just been through a "high-intensity simulation" about the limits of human nature and the exchange of interests, any approach or contact she hadn't actively granted instantly tripped her sharply tuned defenses.
"I'm fine," she said, her voice still slightly slurred from the drinking. "I had a few drinks with Jin Lan and the others at the Gilded Palace tonight. It's cold outside, and the moment I stepped into the elevator it got stuffy. The alcohol hit me and I blacked out for a bit."
"Nobody saw you home?" Yu Hao's eyes stayed on her face. "It's too dangerous for you to be like this on your own. Don't forget what happened last time, passing out at the Crimson Night Palace. Drink less from now on."
Yao Yuan braced herself against the elevator wall and slowly got to her feet. The chill of the metal seeped through her clothes into her palm. Without quite meaning to, she sidestepped the fact that Gao Jieyuan had been the one to bring her home. "Weren't you in Hong Kong? Why are you suddenly back?"
The elevator gave a soft "ding" as it reached the floor. The doors slid apart, and the warm yellow light of the corridor poured in.
"First day of the new year," Yu Hao's voice came from behind her, a softness hidden beneath the calm that was easy to miss. "I suddenly missed you. I had dinner with my parents and then rebooked onto a night flight back."
They stepped out of the elevator one after the other. Yao Yuan's steps were still a little unsteady, yet without seeming to try, she kept a distance from him that was cooler than usual.
Yu Hao drew back the hand he'd meant to offer her, his gaze lingering on the side of her face. He'd caught that flinch, and the faint, almost imperceptible barrier in the air now. This wasn't the Yao Yuan he knew — even exhausted, she'd always carried a certain sharp sense of control.
"You're really okay?" He kept pace with her, watching as she unlocked the apartment door with her fingerprint.
"Mm." Her answer was clipped.
Inside, Yao Yuan shrugged off her coat, draped it over the back of a chair, and curled into the sofa. Yu Hao headed for the kitchen as a matter of course, poured a glass of warm water, and brought it over. "Drink some warm water, it'll help."
She tucked herself tighter into the sofa and took the glass. The warmth seeped through the wall of the cup into her palm, but she didn't drink, just held it.
Then Yu Hao sat down beside her. Not too close, but his presence was unmistakable. After a moment of silence, he reached out and gently slipped an arm around her shoulders.
"Yao Yuan." He said her name, his voice lower than usual. "This time in Hong Kong, watching the fireworks over Victoria Harbour, I suddenly missed you."
She didn't move, didn't push him away, just listened quietly.
"I know what we agreed on at the start — 'companions,' no feelings, no tying each other down." He paused, as if choosing his words. "But some things don't seem to stay under control."
Yao Yuan lowered her eyes, watching the faint ripples on the surface of the water in the cup.
"I've started to care," Yu Hao said, his voice steady but carrying a rare, clumsy honesty. "Like last time at the Crimson Night Palace, that ridiculous drinking contest with Gao Jieyuan. That wasn't just possessiveness, I think. But I'm not sure what it was either. We're both too rational. You take feelings apart for a living, I take numbers apart. The word 'love'... for people like us, it's a little foreign. A little extravagant, even."
The air went quiet. Only the faint trickle of the humidifier broke it.
It was a long while before Yao Yuan spoke, her voice tinged with weariness. "Yu Hao, I've been drinking tonight. My head's spinning and I'm tired. Now isn't the time to talk about this."
The tiredness was real. Not just the residue of the noisy club, but the drain of the time-travel, the shock of facing down her past self, the crushing weight of those memories of betrayal storming through her mind.
She gently freed herself from his arm and stood. "Tomorrow. We'll talk it through properly tomorrow."
Yu Hao looked at her, and in the end, he nodded. "Okay."
That night they washed up as usual and lay down in the same bed as usual, a polite distance between them. Yao Yuan lay on her side with her back to him, eyes open in the dark, and it was a long time before she fell asleep.
The next day was the second day of the new year, a day off.
Morning light filtered through the sheer curtains, spilling pale gold across the teak floor. The winter sun at half past ten had no warmth in it, but it was bright enough. Yao Yuan woke first and lay where she was in silence for a moment, letting her mind rise fully out of the deep water of sleep. Last night's conversation, the rare emotion Yu Hao had let show, and her own mood still not fully settled after coming back from the other side — in the morning light it all felt both vivid and somehow distant.
She got up and went into the bathroom. When the water started running, Yu Hao leaned back against the headboard, looking out at the pale blue winter sky, and let out a very soft, almost soundless sigh.
He knew that some things, once said out loud, could never go back to that "just right" distance they'd had before.
And today, in the end, they had to talk it through.
While Yu Hao made coffee in the kitchen, Yao Yuan came out in her loungewear, her hair still slightly damp. Their eyes met. The barrier was still there, but the ordinariness of the morning had worn it down a little.
"Order in?" Yu Hao asked, holding two cups of pour-over.
"Sure." Yao Yuan took one of them. The nutty aroma of Colombian beans spread through the air.
Breakfast came quickly: Cantonese dim sum, crystal-clear shrimp dumplings, soft and tender chicken feet. They sat at opposite ends of the table and ate in silence, like so many mornings they'd woken up together before. Only today, the silence held a heaviness, a waiting to be broken.
After they cleared away the takeout boxes, Yu Hao brewed a fresh pot of Yirgacheffe. Cups in hand, they walked into the study. It was the quietest room in the apartment — a whole wall of bookshelves, a large desk facing the city skyline beyond the window. Sunlight slanted in, picking out fine motes of dust in the air.
Yao Yuan settled into the single armchair, drew her legs up, and cradled the warm porcelain cup. Yu Hao leaned against the edge of the desk, not sitting. He knew that some things were easier to say standing up.
"What I said last night, I didn't finish." He spoke calmly, the tone of a man talking business. "This trip to Hong Kong, my parents sat me down for a real talk."
Yao Yuan lifted her eyes and waited for him to go on.
"I'm thirty." Yu Hao's gaze settled on something out the window. "The family business needs someone to take it over, and more than that, it needs a clear heir. They want me to settle down, as soon as possible."
He paused, then turned his eyes back to her. "I told them I'm seriously seeing someone."
The air went still for a beat. Yao Yuan's fingers ran lightly over the wall of the cup.
"But my father looked into you." There was a barely perceptible difficulty in Yu Hao's tone. "Your livestream clips, the public controversies, those 'Godmother of Feelings' labels... they can't accept it."
He took two steps forward and sat down in the armchair across from her, leaning in, elbows on his knees. It was a posture of candor.
"Yao Yuan, I admire you. I like being with you. These past six months or so have been the most..." he searched for the word, "the least lonely stretch of my life in years. But marriage won't work. Your public image is too high a risk for our family's listed company. If we got married, the day it went public, the share price would swing at least five percent. And that's before the ongoing fallout."
He said it plainly, the bluntness of a businessman weighing risk. Yao Yuan listened in silence, no flicker of expression on her face, only the frozen lake at the bottom of her eyes seeming to go a few degrees colder.
"So," Yu Hao drew a deep breath and laid out the core of his proposal, "we don't have to get married. But I need an heir. I want you to have a child for me."
The study went quiet enough to hear the trickle of the humidifier. Beyond the window the city ran on under the winter sun, remote and indistinct.
Yao Yuan set down her cup. The base met the glass tea table with a crisp little "tap." She leaned back into the sofa, hands folded in her lap. The posture was exactly how she looked dissecting a case in her livestream — detached, rational, ready to take something apart.
"Yu Hao," she said, her voice as level as if she were discussing someone else's affairs, "let's go through this point by point."
"One: the child's status. A child born out of wedlock — legally you'd have a duty to support it and it would have inheritance rights, but in practice you'd run into plenty of problems. Will your parents acknowledge this grandchild? Will the family trust list him as a beneficiary? And if you marry in the future and have a child within that marriage, how is this child's standing protected?"
"Two: my cost. Pregnancy, delivery, nursing — at least two years, during which my career takes a massive hit. Does the livestream stop or not? Do I run the company or not? Through the postpartum recovery period, my public image could shift from 'sharp-tongued godmother' to 'single mother.' Have you thought about the reputational risk in that?"
"Three: our relationship. Once there's a child, we can't possibly stay simple 'companions' anymore. Will you be involved in raising it? How often? Who does the child live with? And if we each find new relationships down the line, where does the child fit in?"
With every point she made, Yu Hao's expression grew a shade graver. She was too clear-eyed — clear-eyed enough that all the calculation he'd hidden under "feelings" had nowhere to hide.
"Four," Yao Yuan went on, her gaze sharp as a blade, "and this is the crux — what makes you think I'd be willing?"
The question landed with weight.
Yu Hao was silent for a long time. The sunlight moved across his face, from his jaw to his cheekbone.
"Because I thought," he said at last, his voice a little rough, "that you cared about me, at least a little. And I know you've always been searching for a kind of... security. A child gives you legal protection. I'd provide enough financial support to keep you and the child free of worry for life."
Yao Yuan gave a faint laugh. There was no warmth in it, only a weariness that saw straight through everything.
"Yu Hao, you just said we're both too rational." She spoke slowly. "Then let's be rational all the way to the end. The plan you've handed me is, at its core, trading my body and the risk to my career for a legitimate source of a family heir for you, while insulating yourselves from the share-price risk my public image might bring. And what you put in is just some 'sufficient financial support' — which, to you, is nothing more than a number."
She rose and walked to the window, her back to him. The outline of the city unfolded beyond the glass — cold, vast, full of rules.
"If I agreed," she said to her own reflection in the glass, "then one day in the future, when you need to marry for family interests — take a wife of matching background and a clean record — my child and I would become a 'legacy problem' for you to handle. By then, what would the 'caring' and 'liking' you talk about be worth?"
Yu Hao didn't answer. He couldn't.
"You said your father looked into me," Yao Yuan said, turning to lean against the window. "Then he must also know that at twenty-four I was rejected by my first love's family on the grounds of class, that at twenty-eight a wealthy boyfriend gave up on marriage because I was 'too hard to handle,' that at thirty I was used as a bargaining chip by the boss I trusted most. I know this script all too well — a woman, because she's 'not suitable enough,' only ever gets the discounted treatment, only ever gets slotted onto the unofficial track."
She walked back toward the sofa but didn't sit, just stood there looking down at him.
"Yu Hao, I spent more than ten years climbing out of those scripts. I built my own career, took hold of my own voice, made myself something other than the one who gets assessed, picked over, compromised on. And now you want me to go back? Back, for your so-called 'caring' and 'financial security'?"
Her voice was soft, but every word was like an icicle.
"I won't have the child," she said. "It's not spite. It's the optimal solution after I've calculated the risk-reward ratio. My womb, my time, my professional reputation — they're valued very high. The price you're offering isn't enough."
The study fell silent again. But this time, something in that silence had shattered for good.
Yu Hao slowly got to his feet. He looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes complicated — the awkwardness of being refused, the embarrassment of having his calculation seen through, and perhaps a thread of genuine loss he hadn't quite registered himself.
"I understand," he said, his voice back to its usual composure, though there were small cracks beneath it.
He walked to the door and stopped at the threshold, not turning around.
"Yao Yuan," he said, "you're the most clear-eyed, and the most... unapproachable person I've ever met."
The door opened, then closed.
Yao Yuan stood alone in the sunlight at the center of the study. She walked slowly back to the window and watched Yu Hao's figure appear on the street below, cross to the car parked at the curb, get in, and drive off.
Her face in the glass reflection was calm, unrippled. Only the hands clasped in front of her, knuckles slightly white from the pressure.
Her phone buzzed on the tea table. She went over to look — a message from her assistant: "Yuan-jie, the call-in applications for next week's livestream are blowing up. There's one really classic case — the woman got pregnant before marriage, and the man's family is proposing a prenup that cuts the five-million bride price down to one million..."
Yao Yuan finished reading and replied: "Send me the case file. Also, set up a meeting with Lawyer Deng for me — I want to consult on inheritance rights for children born out of wedlock."
Message sent, she put the phone down, picked up the cup of Yirgacheffe that had gone stone cold, and took a sip. Bitter, but clarifying.
Beyond the window the city ran on. And on this winter morning, certain possibilities had been closed off for good — while certain new calculations were only just beginning.
She knew that from today on, the "just right" balance between her and Yu Hao was broken. But that was fine. She was good at rebuilding order out of a wrecked board.
It was only in some very deep corner of her heart that there was a faint, almost imperceptible trace of melancholy. Not for Yu Hao, but for a kind of confirmation — that no matter how high she climbed, in certain people's eyes she was still something that could be assessed, traded away, filed under the "risk" column.
But soon that thread of melancholy was covered over by a harder resolve. She went to the desk, opened her laptop, and pulled up next quarter's business plan.
Feelings are a variable. Interests are a constant. And she chose to build her undefeatable ground in the world of constants.
The sunlight moved slowly across the desk, lighting up the dense financial models and growth curves on the screen. Those numbers were cold, certain, loyal — they never abandoned her on grounds of "class" or "image."
That was enough, she thought.
For her, at least, that was enough.

The car door shut, sealing out the winter chill along with the heavy glass door of Yao Yuan's apartment building. Yu Hao didn't start the car right away, just sat in the driver's seat, hands resting on the wheel, his gaze passing through the windshield to the endless stream of headlights on the overpass in the distance.
The low hum of the engine was especially clear in the enclosed space. He needed that sound, needed that bit of mechanical, controllable vibration to press down a rare, near-chaotic disorder inside him.
He'd been refused.
The realization sat before him, clear and cold. Not a refusal out of spite, not an emotional pushback — a Yao Yuan-style rejection: methodical, dissected, a rational "no" based on an assessment of risk and return. He could even picture the evaluation model spinning at high speed inside her head — variables, weights, probabilities, discount rates — the same kind of thing he used when making investment decisions himself.
That was exactly what left him with such a complicated sense of defeat. They spoke the same language, ran the same kind of math, and yet arrived at opposite conclusions.
Yu Hao undid a button at his collar, leaned back against the seat, and closed his eyes. The city's blurred clamor was filtered out beyond the car, but in his mind some long-ago scenes replayed with unusual clarity.
He wasn't a born playboy. Quite the opposite — from childhood he'd seemed out of step with the kids around him. While other boys were still shouting over game rankings and wins and losses on the court, the twelve-year-old Yu Hao could already sit reading the newspaper, frowning over the international news pages and the financial channel. He remembered the first time he "made money": in his first year of middle school, by analyzing a few unremarkable policy shifts and tender announcements for infrastructure projects, he'd talked his mother into stockpiling, cheap and early, a batch of saplings whose prices later shot up. The "pocket money" he made wasn't much, but the thrill of getting a return through an information gap and good prediction was branded into his early sense of the world.
Precocious. Everyone said so. He'd seen through the workings of the adult world too early — the rules about resources, exchange, leverage, and timing. It made it hard for him to take any real, deep interest in his peers. Their joys and sorrows, their loves and hatreds, often struck him as blunt, shallow, even... a bit of a waste of energy. What he needed was conversation that could keep up with the speed of his thought, resonance that could grasp his complex calculations, a kindred mind that could read the state of the board shoulder to shoulder with him.
Until he met Yao Yuan.
She'd appeared at exactly the right time — just when he'd grown thoroughly tired of all those beautifully made-up but empty-headed women in the world of glamour. Yao Yuan was different. She was beautiful, no question, an aggressive, commanding kind of beauty, but she never relied on beauty alone. The sharpness with which she discussed traffic algorithms, the precision with which she took apart business cases, even the candor with which she admitted she "loved money" and "admired strength" — all of it gave him a rare sense of a match on the level of the mind.
With her, body and mind were satisfied in sync. His body drawn to her, his mind lit up by her. He could go with her from Merkel's political legacy to TikTok's recommendation engine, from Schrödinger's cat to the next likely wave of consumer downgrading. All the things he could never say to anyone else — his hidden worries about the family business, his cold detachment toward the capital markets, the moments of emptiness about the meaning of life — in front of her all seemed to find a kind of unspoken understanding. She was like a mirror polished razor-sharp, reflecting him, and letting him see clearly many of his own blurry zones.
Proposing a child wasn't an impulse. It was the optimal solution after a full assessment.
Biologically, their gene combination had a high probability of producing superior offspring — intelligence, looks, resilience. Practically, he needed an heir to ease the family pressure, and she could provide that "vessel." Emotionally, his care and possessiveness toward her had gone beyond the bounds of "companion," and a child would bind that connection in the most unbreakable way. From a risk-control standpoint, not marrying would maximally isolate the family from the potential negative impact of her public image.
As for what Yao Yuan herself might need? He'd thought it through, he believed. She loved money — he'd give her ample financial security, ensure she and the child leapt up a class and lived in comfort for life. She valued her career — he could promise to put resources to work, helping her career rise even higher after she gave birth. She needed security — a legal agreement could protect her and the child's rights to the fullest. He'd even considered that if she minded the out-of-wedlock status, they might in the future arrange a surrogate, keeping the disruption to her career to a minimum.
This was a plan he'd run through over and over, one that seemed to him logically self-consistent, mutually beneficial, win-win. He'd assumed that someone as realistic and as good at calculating as Yao Yuan would see the value in it.
But she hadn't.
"Aren't the weights enough?"
He opened his eyes, looked at his own blurred reflection in the car window, and asked it without a sound.
Did he need to raise the bid? By how much? Equity? Real estate? Set up an independent trust fund just for her and the child, large enough to make her completely financially free? Or... promise some form of "going official" down the line?
But those last words of Yao Yuan's came down like ice water.
"My womb, my time, my professional reputation — they're valued very high. The price you're offering isn't enough."
What she refused, it seemed, wasn't merely a specific price. What she refused was being placed on this scale and weighed at all. What she refused was the underlying logic of his proposal, which looked fair but in fact reduced her to a "high-quality reproductive vessel" and a "risk-insulation vessel."
Yu Hao's fingertips tapped lightly on the steering wheel. It was a habit when his mind was racing.
He suddenly realized he might have made a critical error. He'd always taken Yao Yuan's "realism" and "calculation" to be purely functional, something a higher bid could satisfy. But he'd overlooked the fact that her realism was born precisely from being objectified, traded, assessed, and then discarded, time after time. The reason she'd become so good at calculating was exactly to defend against the fate of being calculated.
And what he'd just thrown at her was precisely another "trade contract" that put a clear price tag on her core value — fertility, time, reputation.
How could she possibly accept it?
She'd climbed so long, bled so much, just to struggle out of that "priced" quagmire and reach a place where she could define the rules herself, even set prices for others. How could he still expect her, for a seemingly generous offer, to willingly jump back into the very rules of the game she despised?
This wasn't a question of how heavy the weights were.
It was a fundamental conflict in the rules of the game.
Yu Hao felt a deep wave of exhaustion, and something deeper still — interest. Yao Yuan was tougher than he'd imagined, and more complex. She wasn't a variable in his algorithmic model whose output one input could predict; she was an outlier that kept iterating, that could even turn back and bite the algorithm itself.
He started the car and pulled slowly into the lane. The city's neon flowed past the window in blurred ribbons of light.
The awkwardness of being refused was still there, but another, stronger emotion was taking root — the thrill of meeting a worthy opponent, and the drive to rise to a challenge that meant recalibrating his whole model.
He needed to reassess Yao Yuan. Not as a "suitable reproductive partner" or a "quality gene provider," but as a complete person with an independent will and a complex core, a worthy adversary in the game.
Maybe what he wanted had never been just a child, or an easy, low-maintenance relationship.
What he wanted was the one who made him feel constantly challenged, who forced him to run his mind at full power just to barely keep up, who could even now and then overload his processing — Yao Yuan herself.
And to get her — or rather, to build some more solid bond with her in a way he could accept without betraying his own core interests — seemed to require a whole new, more complex algorithm.
In the unseen interior of the car, the corner of Yu Hao's mouth twitched ever so faintly.
It wasn't a smile. It was more like the look of a hunter who has spotted unexpected, but far more worthy prey — that instinctive expression, a mix of gravity and excitement.
The car merged into the night traffic. His mind was already racing, casting off the old plan, building a new model. More variables, more complex constraints, and the objective function would need to be redefined too.
This game was far from over.
It had only entered the next round.
