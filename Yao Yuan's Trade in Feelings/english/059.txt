Chapter 59 — From Flower to Bamboo: Becoming New in the Cold Water of Marriage
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640306.html

"And then, as it happens, there was a coincidence." Lin Wan's tone lifted a little, steering the topic toward herself. "Some time ago, an old friend I've known for years came to see me. We were drinking tea, talking about these eight years of my marriage. She looked at me and said, with sudden feeling, 'Wanwan, I think the reason you can see things so clearly now, the reason you live so wide awake, is that you've been standing on Qiang's shoulders. That's how you got to see a farther, truer world.'"
The scrolling comments went quiet for a moment, and then a few people sent symbols of agreement.
"At the time, I just smiled. I didn't argue. But I turned that sentence over in my mind for a long while afterward." Lin Wan's fingertips unconsciously brushed the smooth glass of her cup, her eyes drifting into memory. "And later I understood it — she was right, but not entirely right."
"For a lot of women, marriage really is like standing on a higher platform. The person who brings you up there might be your partner, or it might be some perspective the marriage itself grants you. He uses his own experience, his knowledge, even his scars, to mark out the potholes on the road ahead, to open windows you might never have reached on your own. When you're afraid of how hard life is, he uses his experience to tell you, 'Don't be scared, there's a way.' When some sudden joy goes to your head, he reminds you, 'Steady — this is only one stop.' Maybe one of his greatest social values is exactly this: he can lift his partner to his own level of understanding, so she can see the complicated face of the world."
"But," she said, the conversation turning, a supple strength entering her voice, "very few people will also tell you the other truth at the same time — the scenery you see from someone else's shoulders, and the scenery you take in after climbing to the summit yourself, one step at a time, are two completely different experiences. The first is a gift, a guiding hand, something to be grateful for. The second is what it means to truly own it — a strength and a vision carved into your very bones."
More resonance and reflection began to appear in the comments:
[Two kinds of scenery! So well put!]
[Being carried up, and climbing up yourself — the confidence is completely different!]
[Wanwan, you're talking about your own changes over these years, aren't you?]
"Let me share something from the first few years of my marriage." Lin Wan leaned back slightly, gentle folds gathering in her linen robe, her gaze growing distant. "It was around the fourth year. Qiang took me to a very important industry gathering. It was my first time stepping into that circle — a room full of accomplished entrepreneurs and investors. I wore what I thought was my most appropriate dress, tried hard to keep smiling, and listened to all that unfamiliar jargon and verbal fencing, feeling like an outsider who'd wandered into a banquet by mistake. Lost, out of place."
"On the way home, I felt a little low. But Qiang was happy. He said, 'See? This is how the real world works. You have to adapt.'"
"That night I couldn't sleep. Not because of him, but because of a deep sense of weightlessness — I realized that the 'world' I'd understood and lived in all along, and the 'world' he moved through with such ease, were separated by a wide, deep canyon. And our marriage was quietly pushing me toward the edge of that canyon, demanding that I find some way to cross it."
She took a sip of water and went on, her tone calm but charged with strength:
"The crossing was not graceful. I was like a primary-school student, learning from scratch how to read a financial statement, how to grasp business logic, learning to catch the useful information buried in those seemingly ordinary dinner-table conversations, practicing how to respond without showing fear, without ruining things. Sometimes Qiang would offer me a pointer or two, but more often he taught me simply by existing — letting me witness with my own eyes how people calculate before their own interests, feel the cold mercilessness of real-world rules, see clearly the cruel, efficient way resources and networks of connection move."
"And in that process, I heard the one sentence from him that stung me the most — and yet, in the end, woke me up completely." Lin Wan lowered her voice, but every word was crystal clear, striking at the hearts of her audience. "It was once when he'd been drinking. We were arguing over some small thing, and he said, with irritation and a kind of 'why won't you live up to your potential' tone, 'Sometimes I think showing you all this is like handling a lotus root. If you want it to taste crisp, you have to dig it out of the mud that looks so comfortable and soak it in icy, bone-chilling water. That little bit of pride in you, that streak in your eyes that can't stand a speck of filth — if you don't grind it away, you'll never get a feel for the real workings of this world. You'll never make anything of yourself.'"
The comments exploded in an instant, full of anger and ache:
[That's so hurtful! What gives him the right to say that!]
[A lotus root?? What a terrible metaphor!]
[He's way too controlling! Wanwan, how could you stand it!]
[This is PUA! Naked belittling!]
Lin Wan watched the comments race up the screen in calm silence. There was no grievance on her face, and no anger — only the clarity and peace of someone who has been through it.
"At the time, I didn't argue with anything," she said slowly. "But that 'lotus root' metaphor was like a thin, tough thorn, driven into the deepest part of my heart. It took me a long time to digest that sentence, to sort out the emotion in it, the motive, and the cruel truth wrapped so crudely inside — a truth that maybe even he wasn't aware of."
"Later, I slowly came to understand two things."
"First, it wasn't that he was controlling me. It was that the 'highly sensitive' me, the one chasing a flawless purity, had placed far too much of her attention on his judgment. I wanted so badly to be the 'right person' in his eyes, I was so afraid of being dirtied by the mud, that I ended up trapping myself."
"Second," her gaze grew clear, even touched with a wise compassion, "I have to admit that, in that stage, from a certain extremely practical, even crude angle, he was right. At least in that stage, he was right. Only later did I read it correctly — that seemingly cold 'control' of his, that way of pushing me again and again into the 'cold water' and the 'mud,' was actually him trying, in the way he knew best — a way full of rough survival wisdom — to make me 'stronger.' As a child he'd clawed his way out of the mud and the cold water himself. In his understanding, that was the only effective 'path to growth' there was."
The anger in the comments gradually settled, replaced by deep thought and complicated feeling:
[My god... this angle...]
[It's still hard to accept, but I think I can understand that clumsy, cruel kind of 'for your own good' now.]
[Wanwan's reflection is so profound.]
[Marriage really is a discipline mixed with pain.]
"In these eight years with Qiang, I've had countless moments where I felt stifled, wronged, where I wanted to turn and run." Lin Wan's voice was soft, yet it carried enormous weight. "But it was also these eight years that let me see, more and more clearly: this walled city of marriage — for most women who choose to enter it, maybe it was never simply about coming in to enjoy shelter and sweetness. It's more like the most realistic, the least forgiving training ground there is."
"Here, you're forced to train a pair of eyes that can pierce through the glamour and the sweet talk, to see the interwoven light and dark at the bottom of human nature. You have to learn to stay clear-headed in the cold water called 'life,' to anchor your own roots in the complicated 'mud of reality' without being completely drowned or assimilated. The hardest lesson of all is this: you have to find your way, step by step, to walk out a path of your own inside rules other people made."
The comments surged with powerful emotional resonance:
[A training ground... that word is so precise.]
[The process of cultivation really does hurt.]
[But it seems like only this way can you truly grow up, truly have strength.]
"This long process of 'cultivation,' from unaware to aware, took me nearly eight years." There was no resentment in Lin Wan's voice — only a stillness and strength forged together by time and experience. "Eight years of quiet observation, endurance, adaptation, digestion; eight years of learning to stay clear-headed under the sting of all that 'cold water,' of practicing how to root myself inside the 'mud.' And then, I don't know which day it started, I suddenly realized that the course this family was steering — in so many small but crucial places — had quietly, without anyone noticing, passed to me at the helm."
She reached out and, with the lightest touch of her fingertip, brushed the green, vigorous stalk of bamboo standing in the porcelain vase.
"That old friend said something else later: 'Wanwan, you were meant to be such a beautiful flower, and now you've been forced into growing into a stalk of bamboo.'"
Lin Wan smiled, and in that smile there was release, a pride that comes after weathering storms, and a faint, mature woman's blend of worldly wear and wisdom.
"I answered her: 'Yes — but bamboo is good too. Its roots strike deeper; they can drive through hard rock. Its frame is more supple; when wind and rain come, it knows how to bend. Maybe its vision is higher, too, because what it's reaching for is the wider expanse of the sky.'"
"From a flower to a stalk of bamboo. That's probably the deepest shaping my marriage gave me." Her voice was gentle and firm, holding an inner strength. "I lost the flower's delicacy, its quickness to wilt, and that dependence on being kept in a greenhouse. But I gained the bamboo's toughness, its suppleness, its unbroken will to keep growing. I no longer need a constant warm room; my roots have learned to find water on their own. I no longer fear the unpredictable wind and rain, because every swaying, every burden borne, only adds to the strength and resilience of my life."
"So, back to what my friend said at the very start." Lin Wan's gaze turned clear toward the camera, her eyes glinting with points of light bright and steady as stars. "Yes — at a certain stage, I really did stand on his shoulders to look at the world. For that, I'll always hold a complicated gratitude. But later, I stepped down from those shoulders, steadily, on my own."
"With the clarity I kept in the cold water over those eight years, and the strength of the roots I clawed out of the mud, I laid my own higher foundation, brick by brick. Now, we stand side by side, and what we see is the scenery you can only take in together — at the place where two separate climbs, two separate journeys, meet at a certain height. In that scenery there's the vastness he brought, and the depth I opened up with my own hands."
The background music of the livestream rose slowly — a minimal, ethereal piano melody, like raindrops falling on bamboo leaves, like the far-off, clear echo of wind passing through a bamboo grove.
"When a marriage gets this far, maybe there's no better state than this." Lin Wan's voice melted into the music, like a final summing-up, and like a tender word of parting. "We were once a flight of stairs for each other on some stretch of the way, and in the end we became the steady horizon in each other's lives. In his world you learned strength and clarity; in your presence, perhaps he slowly came to know softness, and another kind of strength. From a flower that needed cherishing, and a tree that thought it could shield from wind and rain, the two of you grew together into this bamboo grove — each upright and independent, yet roots deeply joined beneath the ground, sending out a harmonious, resilient echo through the storms of the years."
"That's all for tonight. If you're also struggling in the 'cold water' and the 'mud' of marriage, if you also feel yourself turning from a 'flower' into some other kind of plant, you're welcome to leave how you feel in the comments. The process of transformation is always lonely, but knowing someone else is walking a similar road is, in itself, a kind of strength."
"Good night. May we all, in this most cruel training ground called life, master our own gentle and powerful kung fu. See you next time."
The livestream picture slowly dimmed. The study returned to quiet, with only the bamboo stalk casting its lean, slender shadow under the light. And Lin Wan's words, like the wind among bamboo leaves, lingered long in the heart of every listener.
In the living room, Shuai Hongqiang had been standing at the floor-to-ceiling window ever since he came back from the Guangu Hall, looking out at the city lights beyond the glass. The cigarette in his hand had burned halfway down, and for a long time he hadn't taken a drag.
When Lin Wan came out, this was the back she saw. She didn't speak. She walked over, wrapped her arms gently around him from behind, and pressed her face against his broad back.
Shuai Hongqiang's body stiffened slightly, then relaxed. He raised a hand and laid it over hers where it circled his waist. They stood like that in silence, neither of them saying a word.
On the windowpane, their leaning figures were reflected, and beyond them that vast, complicated, real grown-up world.
After a long while, Shuai Hongqiang spoke, his voice hoarse, so low it was almost inaudible:
"...That thing about the lotus root... I'm sorry."
Behind him, Lin Wan shook her head gently.
"You don't have to be sorry." Her voice was muffled in his clothes, but it was clear. "Without that pool of cold water, I might... have stayed nothing but a flower that needs a vase, forever."
She paused, then held him a little tighter.
"And besides — aren't you, now... finally willing to soak yourself in the sun, and loosen up your own soil a little?"
Shuai Hongqiang's throat worked, and in the end he only gripped her hand more tightly.
The bamboo stalk stood quietly in the porcelain vase in the study. It knew it came from a patch of mud, and that only after soaking in cold water did it come to have its cool, clear bones. And in this moment, toward the light, toward a wider imagining, it was growing unseen, upward-reaching joints.
