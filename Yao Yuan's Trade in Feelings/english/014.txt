Chapter 14 — Lin Wan, "The Wife with the Face of a Virtuous Lady"
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640261.html

When Shuai Hongqiang pushed open the front door, the grandfather clock in the living room was just striking the second chime of one-thirty in the afternoon. The tone was deep and heavy — an antique he'd had shipped up from Guangzhou specially when they married. Lin Wan had said at the time that it was "a bit too grand," but in the end she'd let it take up residence in the living room anyway.

The house was very quiet. Their son, Shuai Wenyao, had eaten lunch and was down for his nap; the door of the children's room stood ajar, leaking the faint white noise of a humidifier. On the kitchen counter, a few white porcelain bowls and dishes sat covered — his lunch, kept for him. Braised pork ribs, stir-fried baby bok choy, tomato and egg soup, all warming on a heating mat, kept at exactly the right temperature for the first bite.

Everything was perfectly seen to. Lin Wan fulfilled every duty of a "wife" flawlessly — even more flawlessly than before.

In the blink of an eye, they'd been married more than seven years. People talk about the "seven-year itch"; whether such an itch existed, Shuai Hongqiang couldn't quite say. He only knew, plainly, that Lin Wan was no longer the way she'd been as a newlywed — when everything had revolved around him, when she'd poured all her care and love onto him without holding anything back.

The Lin Wan who had once made him feel "seen," the Lin Wan who treated him as a living, complicated person worth exploring rather than merely a "husband" —

the Lin Wan whose eyes and brows used to light up with tenderness whenever she mentioned him — was quietly making her exit, day by day, inside all this perfection.

And yet, in every small detail of daily life, Lin Wan still arranged everything beautifully, with not a single fault to find. What they shared now was only a carefully maintained, constant "appropriateness," and a calm blueprint concerning their child's future.

Shuai Hongqiang walked to the study door. It wasn't shut all the way, left open a hand's width.

Lin Wan was inside.

She had her back to the door, seated at the broad solid-wood desk. The afternoon sun sliced in at an angle through the gaps in the blinds, plating a fuzzy gold edge onto her crisp short hair and her pale-gray cashmere cardigan. Wearing those thin-rimmed blue-light glasses, she was studying three monitors lined up side by side. The left one held a dense table of data; the middle, a mind map of a livestream script; the right, the page of some women's personal-growth forum.

She wasn't speaking, and she wasn't operating anything — just looking, quietly. Now and then she'd tap the trackpad lightly with a fingertip, scrolling the page, or make a small mark on one line of text. The whole motion was smooth, precise, without a trace of excess hesitation or emotional ripple.

In that moment she looked exactly like the educated woman from years ago at the redevelopment symposium, sitting upright in the audience, calmly recording and dissecting everything.

Shuai Hongqiang was a grassroots man who had clawed his way out of a deep mountain gully with his life.

The first half of his life was written full of the weight of reality. The year he was in middle school, his mother fell gravely ill, and it crushed the family flat — crushed his schoolbooks along with it. He'd ground himself down into the dust, fist and foot, and built — with nothing but a wild, scrappy ferocity — the thing other people called "success." Maybe that was exactly why those halls he'd never entered, those books he'd never finished, became a stubborn point of brightness deep in his heart. He had an almost instinctive admiration and longing for women who were highly educated, cultured. At the dinner table, in the world of business, the shrewdness he'd forged was enough to handle most situations. But when such women happened to be talking — when the name of some unfamiliar author, a stretch of jargon, a viewpoint beyond the range of his experience drifted past — though he couldn't catch it, his heart would stir faintly. That bit of not understanding, far from diminishing anything, instead wrapped them in a halo that both soothed and fascinated him — that was art, that was civilization, that was the other world he'd missed along the way. Yao Yuan was that light, and so was Lin Wan.

Yao Yuan's beauty was a beauty that flaunted itself and knew it.

It was concrete, with an aggressive edge. Like a bared blade — bright, dazzling, gleaming hot — cleaving open all the stale air to come straight at you. Hers was a wanton beauty, a swaggering, triumphant beauty, and she knew full well the power of that beauty. She wasn't just beautiful; she had the intelligence to match it. Standing beside her satisfied more than a man's vanity — it was an inflated masculine pride, the kind that came from conquering the most brilliant quarry of all. Shuai Hongqiang had loved her — perhaps, in his meager emotional history, the one burning that came closest to that thing called "love." But the flame was too fierce; it scorched him into panic. Marry her? That took an enormous kind of courage. He'd been too young then; his fists had earned him money, but not enough nerve — they hadn't polished away the dread of being "uncultured" that lived in his very marrow. He was afraid — afraid this fire was something he'd never be able to contain, afraid that his nouveau-riche foundation couldn't hold up under the dazzle of her beauty and the sharp life-force in her. So he hesitated, he shrank back, he stayed silent, and watched her cool down inside all that expectation. In the end, heart gone cold, she left — taking with her a whole bank of fire-clouds, like a sunset burning out at the edge of the sky.

Lin Wan walked into his world in that cold winter, the very winter Yao Yuan left.

Lin Wan's beauty was a beauty unaware of itself.

Not dazzling, not arresting — like a smooth, warm piece of jade, like a pool of still water, utterly unconscious of itself. She had a face naturally drawn by traditional taste into the very picture of "virtue and grace." A full forehead, smooth brow-bone, a nose-bridge straight but never sharp, lips whose curve carried a faint, gentle smile by nature. Without any makeup, she was a well-bred young lady stepped out of an old magazine illustration; with a light touch of powder, she gave off the composed dignity of the lady of the house. Her fans jokingly called this Lin Wan's classic "matriarch face" — not meaning she looked old, but that warm quality, settled by years, able to take in all hardship and all stories.

Lin Wan's eyes were very clear, like an autumn lake, the irises a pale brown, holding a strange, compassionate insight when she looked at people. Crisp short hair, three centimeters above the ear, not a strand out of place. Her figure was tall and straight — none of the frailty of deliberate dieting, but a suppleness and strength sculpted by years of disciplined exercise.

This beauty didn't flaunt itself, yet it carried its own weight.

She'd been sitting just like this the first time he saw her too, back straight as a young poplar. On stage the experts held forth at length; down below she took rapid notes, occasionally lifting her eyes — and in those pale-brown eyes there was not a trace of worship or excitement, only a calm scrutiny and analysis. It was that very gaze, in a hall full of slickness and anxiety and calculation, that stood out fresh and self-possessed.

Late winter, 2017: a symposium hosted by the municipal planning bureau, "Organic Renewal and Community Revitalization of Old Industrial Districts." The venue chosen was a creative park converted from an old textile mill, where rough concrete pillars stood in jarring contrast with sleek spotlights.

Twenty-eight-year-old Lin Wan attended as a young-scholar delegate, in her capacity as a researcher and doctoral candidate at a top university's Center for Urban and Social Development, prepared to present a report based on big-data analysis: "Class Mobility and Emotional Cost in Spatial Restructuring — A Study of Three Redevelopment Zones."

That day she wore a well-tailored pale-gray skirt suit, her short hair crisp, her makeup light. Sitting in the third row, a notebook stamped with the university crest open on her lap, an extra-fine black pen in her hand, she jotted down keywords in the gaps between speakers. Her posture was upright, her expression focused — a sharp contrast to some of the attendees around her who whispered to one another and scrolled their phones. In the interval while she waited to go up, she even took an English monograph dotted with sticky notes from her bag and read it, undistracted.

Thirty-eight-year-old Shuai Hongqiang, meanwhile, had been "invited" by the organizers under the double billing of "local entrepreneur" and "potential investor" — to fill out the room and to hear the policy talk. He wore an expensive but slightly dated dark-blue suit, the top button of his shirt undone, his tie a little crooked. He sat in the back row by the door, handy for taking calls and slipping out early. He had a notebook open in front of him too, but on it was no text — it was a crude floor plan of a warehouse scrawled in thick marker, with a few rows of figures. He was calculating how much compensation floor-area his logistics warehouse — about to fall within the redevelopment zone — could be traded for, or what loopholes in the policy he might exploit.

Past the halfway mark, the symposium moved into the Q&A. A planning-institute expert on stage was holding forth on "humanistic care" and "community warmth," his language abstract and lovely.

Shuai Hongqiang had grown impatient. The instant the expert finished, he was the first to raise his hand. He didn't wait for a staffer to pass him a microphone; he just stood up, his voice booming, carrying the blunt, looming pressure peculiar to a grassroots businessman:

"You speak very well, sir, but I want to ask something concrete. I'm the owner of that old freight-station plot in such-and-such district. You talk about 'revitalization,' you talk about 'sentiment' — well, what happens to my warehouse? There are thirty-odd workers in there counting on it to eat. The government's compensation plan only counts building floor-area; it doesn't count the paved ground and the security systems I've put in over the years. Is that reasonable? Can all that 'warmth' of yours help me pay my workers' wages?"

The hall went quiet for a moment. The expert, interrupted mid-lyric, was a little embarrassed, and tried to answer with "the macro view" and "long-term interests."

That was when Lin Wan raised her hand. She took the microphone, her voice clear and steady — not loud, but enough for the whole room to hear:

"This gentleman's question is very specific, and very representative. As it happens, I've studied the very district you mention." She turned to a particular page of her notebook, her pace even, yet brimming with solid data to back it. "Cross-referencing business-registration and social-insurance records from 2014 to 2017, the logistics cluster where your warehouse sits actually contains seventeen active small-and-medium logistics firms, employing roughly two hundred and eighty people directly — not the thirty-odd you mentioned. The average survival cycle of these firms is 2.7 years, with an annual staff-turnover rate of 35 percent. Which means that even if the warehouse stayed untouched, the employment of these workers would itself be extremely unstable."

Shuai Hongqiang was startled. He narrowed his eyes and, for the first time, looked carefully at the young woman who had risen in the front rows.

Lin Wan continued, turning her calm gaze toward Shuai Hongqiang, then to the expert on stage: "So the problem may need to be broken down. First, there's the technical question of property-rights compensation, which involves valuation standards. I've noticed that Article 13 of the latest 'Implementation Rules (Draft for Comment)' leaves a gray area on the compensation of special structures — that might serve as an opening for negotiation." She stated the article number precisely.

"Second, there's the question of the social cost of resettling the workers. This is exactly where the 'humanistic care' in the plan runs into difficulty on the ground. My team has run the numbers: if you adopt a package of 'compensation payment + targeted skills training + priority entry into the newly built industrial park,' the government's short-term cost rises by about 8 percent, but it lowers the potential unemployment-relief outlays and social-instability risk over the next five years. In the long run, it's economical. There's a detailed model later in my report."

When she finished, she gave a slight nod and sat down. No emotion — only analysis and a plan.

At the tea break, Shuai Hongqiang, carrying a cup of instant coffee, walked over of his own accord to Lin Wan, who stood alone by the window going over her documents.

"Dr. Lin?" he said, his tone a little softer than on stage, but still with an appraising edge.

"You're the gentleman who asked the question just now?" Lin Wan turned, her expression composed.

"Name's Shuai. Shuai Hongqiang." He handed over a business card, the title "General Manager, such-and-such Logistics Co., Ltd.," exquisitely designed. "Those figures you gave just now — where'd they come from? Are they accurate?"

"My research requires going through a great deal of policy text, local archives, and case precedents. Cross-validation of public data, plus a limited number of sample interviews. The accuracy rate is around 92 percent." Lin Wan took the card, glanced at it, and drew an understated card of her own from an inside jacket pocket to hand back. "My research needs field verification. If you don't mind, Boss Shuai, perhaps I could come look at your warehouse — some of the data may need checking."

Shuai Hongqiang laughed, the amused laugh of a man with a streetwise edge. "Research? Research how we get our buildings torn down? And after the research is done? Write a paper, win some award? Not a damn bit of use to us. Aren't you afraid I'll think you're meddling?" The words were blunt, full of the grassroots distrust — and faint hostility — toward intellectuals who fought their battles on paper.

Lin Wan's tone stayed calm, as if she were stating a theorem. "Research is for understanding. Understanding why things turn out this way, understanding where your predicament sits within the whole system, understanding which strategies of resistance are effective and which are useless or even harmful. For instance —" she shifted, pointing to the paving Shuai Hongqiang had mentioned earlier, "the heavy-truck load-bearing pavement you spoke of does have corresponding provisions in the classification standards for urban infrastructure and in the compensation rules. But in the district baseline land-price system updated in 2013, its weighting was lowered. If you can produce the special construction-acceptance certificate from that year, you might be able to invoke an earlier local-regulation clause as grounds in the negotiation."

Her pace was even, her terms professional, every sentence landing squarely at the core of the practical problems Shuai Hongqiang cared about. The contempt on his face gradually withdrew, replaced by a look of surprise and reassessment.

Lin Wan then asked back, "Are you the owners' representative for this district? I've noticed that the success rate of scattered, individual resistance is very low, but once a small alliance forms, the negotiating leverage increases exponentially. Does your current alliance have a shared bottom-line plan? Or is everyone out for themselves?"

Shuai Hongqiang didn't answer the question; he only studied the young woman before him again, carefully. She wasn't spouting empty theory — she really seemed to "get it," and to get it to a level that could hold a conversation with his streetwise experience. He looked again at the thick monograph in her hands, full of charts and English annotations. A strange feeling welled up in him. He'd seen many women — beautiful ones, gentle ones, shrewd ones — but never one like this: like a precise, beautiful instrument whose operating manual he couldn't read at all. She even... seemed to be treating him as an interesting research sample.

"There's people at the warehouse on weekends," Shuai Hongqiang said, draining his coffee and crushing the paper cup flat. "Come by — call ahead. Let me see for myself how you top-rank scholars 'research' rough types like us."

Leaving the venue that day, Lin Wan wrote in her notebook:

"New investigative contact: grassroots entrepreneur, Shuai Hongqiang. Typical sample, sensitive to policy, pragmatism above all, possesses a strategy of resistance, emotional expression direct and rough. Worth observing in depth: 1. risk-decision pattern; 2. mode of interaction with grassroots bureaucracy; 3. complex attitude toward 'knowledge' (both dismissive and curious). Potential research value."

Shuai Hongqiang, meanwhile, sat back in his Land Rover but didn't start it right away. He took out Lin Wan's card and looked at it again. The sunlight fell through the car window onto those words — "Doctoral Candidate in Sociology."

"Doctor..." he said aloud, and shook his head — yet he tucked the card carefully into a sleeve of his wallet, rather than tossing it casually into the glove compartment.

He thought of the clear-eyed look she'd worn when she spoke, and that calm expression that said "everything can be analyzed." Interesting. Completely different from the women in his world, who either clung or calculated. She seemed to live by another set of rules — a set made of numbers, models, and "logic."

And Shuai Hongqiang's creed in life was this: all rules could, in the end, be exploited, negotiated, or broken.

Maybe this was a new domain of "rules" worth getting to know a little.

Outside the car window, the chimney of the old factory stood silent in the setting sun.

Their meeting began in a stretch of old factory grounds about to be torn down and remade — the analytical framework of a sociologist colliding, in that moment, with the survival wisdom of a grassroots entrepreneur, striking the first sparks. Neither of them imagined that those sparks would also open a long contest — over rules, power, and self-definition — that would in time spread to every corner of their lives.

Maybe this is the most interesting thing between a man and a woman. Shuai Hongqiang was drawn to Lin Wan — the highly educated, cerebral woman he himself had never possessed — entranced by that calm, clear light she carried from another order of the world. And Lin Wan had been raised, from childhood, disciplined by the rules and rituals of propriety and academia. She admired the survival wisdom Shuai Hongqiang had honed in the wilds, that free-and-easy, streetwise loyalty of a man unbound by rules. They say love is finding your own reflection in another person — those familiar traits, those shared interests, like finding another self in the vast sea of people, fitting together, drawing each other in; but really it's more like finding in the other the parts of yourself that are missing — the qualities, the courage, or the dreams we long for but have never had.

"You're back?"

At some point Lin Wan had already turned her head, looking at him through the crack in the door. She'd taken off her glasses, and on her face was the gentle, gracious smile he knew so well — the corners of her mouth curved to perfection, her eyes holding exactly the right measure of inquiry.

"Your food's in the kitchen, the temperature should be just right. Wenyao has art class at four; I'll wake him at two-fifty. If you've got nothing on this afternoon, you take him to class."

Her voice, like her smile, was gentle, clear, methodical.

"All right." Shuai Hongqiang heard himself answer, his own voice as usual.

He turned and walked to the kitchen, lifting that bowl of soup at exactly the right temperature. The soup was savory, the ribs stewed tender to the bone, the greens a vivid green.

He took a sip, then another.

His stomach was warm, and it settled a little of the alarm of having learned the secret of the bronze mirror.

But some place in his heart still felt wrong, and uneasy. He knew that some things were like this bowl of soup at exactly the right temperature — you couldn't find a single fault with it. And yet you also knew, clearly, that the first boiling, churning, scalding heat had been left behind forever in some moment of the past.

He'd married her, certainly, because she carried everything "respectable" and "high-class" that he longed for, giving him — a middle-school dropout from the grassroots — a secret sense of conquest and of making up for what he lacked. Her cerebral nature, her composure, that warm temperament of hers that seemed able to take in everything and dissolve everything, made him feel safe, made him feel a satisfaction of being "elevated." But even more, it was because he'd thought that light could be his own, could illuminate the dark corners of his world — the ones left from all the years he'd fought on by instinct and fists, corners even he couldn't quite name. That it could let him truly say goodbye to that Shuai Hongqiang who had walked barefoot out of the mountain gully, covered in dust and dread.

The indicator light on the kitchen heating mat went from red to green and from green to red again, cycling soundlessly.

At four in the afternoon, after dropping off the boy, Shuai Hongqiang didn't go home. He drove two aimless loops around the area, then came back to the foot of the art-school building, sat in the car, and watched the daylight slowly slant west through the window, until five o'clock. He picked up Wenyao, the boy chattering away about the fun things from class, and he answered with vague mm-hmms.

Back home, the house was filled with the smell of cooking. Lin Wan was just carrying out the last dish from the kitchen, steamed sea bass. She'd changed into loungewear, but her hair was still neat, and on her face was the usual, comforting smile.

"Mama! Look at my starry sky! Teacher said the purple I painted looks really good!" Wenyao swooped toward the dining table like a little bird, his small hands still smudged with unwashed watercolor, holding up his painting in high spirits.

Smiling, Lin Wan brought out egg tarts from the oven, baked to just the right point with edges lightly browned, golden and glistening. "Wow, that's beautiful! Go wash your hands quick, then come taste the Portuguese tarts Mama just made — a reward for our little artist." She turned and gave Shuai Hongqiang a natural little wink. "He's been going on about it all afternoon — said the smell drifting over from the bakery next to the art classroom was just too tempting."

Two dishes and a soup were already laid out on the table. Hot oil had been poured sizzling over the steamed sea bass, garnished prettily with slivers of scallion and red chili. The garlic broccoli was a crisp, vivid green, and the soup was a milky-white fish-head-and-tofu soup, its steam curling up wreathed in fragrance.

"The sea bass turned out well today — taste it, see if it's soaked up more flavor than last time?" Lin Wan said, tying a napkin around Wenyao while nodding her chin toward the fish, a light note of fishing-for-praise in her voice.

Shuai Hongqiang picked up a piece — fresh and juicy, the timing indeed just right. "Mm, delicious." He nodded, then picked up another piece and set it in Wenyao's bowl. "Eat plenty of meat, son, so you grow tall."

Wenyao took a bite of egg tart, flakes of pastry crumbling down, and squinted in satisfaction, then rushed to show off his painting again: "Papa, look, this is the Milky Way! And here — I drew the three of us, our whole family, lying on the grass watching the stars! It's just... it's just that Mama's face came out a little round..."

Lin Wan burst out laughing and pulled a tissue to wipe the crumbs from her son's mouth: "How is Mama that chubby? Tomorrow I'll be your model so you can paint it again."

"Papa's face is square too!" Wenyao "told on" him.

Shuai Hongqiang deliberately put on a stern face and pretended to jab his chopsticks at his son: "Eat! Look at your painting after you've finished."

The lights were warm, the food was good, the boy chattered away in his childish prattle, and his wife wore an easy smile, now and then exchanging a glance with him about their child. The air was filled with the smell of food, a faint whiff of acrylic paint, and the homey, reassuring sound of conversation.

Shuai Hongqiang ate slowly, watching his son's waving chopsticks, feeling that everything in this moment was good. Warm lights, good food, wife and child at his side. Life was showing its plainest and most joyful face.

He knew that in a little while, when the hands of the clock reached seven, this woman who was now picking out food for him and asking whether there was enough soup would walk into that already-prepared study, and under the carefully arranged lighting, would tell countless strangers about a life of another temperature. She would face the livestream camera and, in this same gentle, clear, methodical voice — only with an added professional penetration — dissect, for countless unfamiliar usernames, a specimen called "marriage." Inside that specimen was their life, his shadow, refined and expounded, turned into "content" to be watched, consumed, even used to heal others. Though he knew perfectly well that the words in the livestream, for the sake of traffic and effect, inevitably involved performance and exaggeration. But the skeleton, the texture of it, were after all dismantled out of their real life.

Lin Wan had started doing livestreams four years ago. At first it was just sharing book reflections; somehow the topic slid toward marriage and managing a relationship, toward the dynamics between men and women, and she began to talk about how the two of them lived together — those details, warm or frictional. He'd objected, thinking how could private matters be laid out for outsiders to see, especially when they weren't short of money at the time. But Lin Wan was dead set on it, and so the livestream carried on. To his surprise, it really took off; the fans grew, and in those two or three years when his business was struggling and they were most strapped, it was the income from that livestream room that steadily held up the sinking foundation of this family. He could no longer bring himself to voice his objections — only the feeling stayed, complicated as a wad of water-soaked cotton, lodged heavy in his chest.

By ten to seven in the evening, dinner had been eaten in quiet, the bowls and chopsticks washed and put back in place. Wenyao was doing his homework in his own room. Shuai Hongqiang sat on the living-room sofa, the TV screen dark, his phone in his hand though he hadn't opened a single app.

The study door opened. Lin Wan came out and went straight to the bathroom. A few minutes later she appeared again, already changed. Not loungewear, and not the formal wear for going out, but a soft-textured oatmeal-colored knit that set off the warmth of her complexion, her hair falling smoothly over her shoulders, her face done in light, refined makeup — makeup that cleverly brought out the softness of her features and the clarity of her gaze, making her look both approachable and trustworthy, impeccable.

She gave him a faint smile, no different from the one in the daytime — gentle, gracious. "Qiang-ge, I start at seven, about two and a half hours." Her voice was steady as ever, only with a barely perceptible focus, the concentration of being in work mode.

From the time Lin Wan and Shuai Hongqiang first met, she had at first dutifully called him "Boss Shuai." Later, as the two of them dated and then stepped into marriage, her way of addressing him settled permanently into "Qiang-ge," and never changed. Perhaps it was because Shuai Hongqiang was ten years her senior, carrying the appeal of a mature man and the warmth of an elder brother; or perhaps, in this relationship, such a form of address conveyed her dependence and love just right. And Shuai Hongqiang quite relished the name "Qiang-ge." After all, in this world, there is probably no man who can resist the sweetly called "ge" from the woman he loves — that simple syllable, brimming with deep feeling and admiration, like a warm current that flows straight into a man's heart.

"All right." Shuai Hongqiang heard his own reply, equally steady.

She turned and walked into the study, gently shutting the door. A soft click — the faint sound of the lock turning: during the livestream, absolute quiet was needed, with no disturbances.
