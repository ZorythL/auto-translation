The apartment elevator mirror: traveling back to Hushi, the origin of a boss's betrayal.
Consciousness rose out of chaos, the way a diver drifts slowly back to the surface.
The first thing to return was her body's awareness of "existing"—she was standing, with something solid beneath her feet. Yao Yuan opened her eyes. Before her stretched an endless, flawless field of pure white. There was no light source, yet the light was even and soft; there was no direction, yet gravity still held her. She was back here again, in this "transitional space" that lay between her several journeys through time—impossible to understand, impossible to control.
She steadied herself and wasted no time probing the physical makeup of all this white. Her first three trips had taught her that doing so was pointless. She focused her mind on her own state and ran a quick review. A moment ago, she had still been in the elevator on the way up to her penthouse with its view of the Jinshi night, staring blankly into the mirrored wall. Then the familiar dizziness and sense of being peeled loose had washed over her. When she opened her eyes again, she was here.
Which meant another "rewind" was about to begin, or was already underway. The bronze mirror's "rules" remained unpredictable, but they seemed to trigger whenever her thoughts converged on some pivotal stretch of the past or some node of intense emotion. This time… something stirred in her mind, and she felt a faint premonition of what she was about to face.
Sure enough, in the very instant that thought flickered past, the pure white space ahead began to ripple in a familiar way. Like a stone dropped into a still lake, the ripples spread outward in rings, and the scene at the center gradually warped, sharpened, and finally settled into a moving image—carrying with it the low murmur, the soft clink of glasses, and the dazzling, almost unreal glow refracted from crystal chandeliers, all unmistakably the sounds of a gala that even passing through space had not dulled.
The image rushed at her. The moment she made it out clearly, Yao Yuan's breath caught for one fleeting instant.
The Bund. The banquet hall of a top-tier hotel.
Beyond the floor-to-ceiling windows lay a textbook view of Lujiazui by night: the silhouettes of the Oriental Pearl Tower and the Jin Mao Tower glowing against the dark, the waters of the Huangpu River mirroring the neon, rippling like scattered gold. Inside, the room glittered with perfume and elegance, a gathering of the elite.
Her gaze locked, almost at once, onto two people in the scene.
One was a young woman in a white gown, standing half a step behind and to the side of a composed, dignified middle-aged man. That gown… in the white space, Yao Yuan's fingertips curled in unconsciously. A single-shoulder strap studded with tiny crystals, a high slit on the left running all the way up the thigh, so that with every step her bare white legs made a silent declaration. The cut hugged her perfectly, tracing a slender yet ample figure, and the back plunged into a deep V that bared a broad expanse of smooth skin. Her hair was swept up without a strand out of place, baring a long neck and a pair of dazzling diamond earrings. Standing there, she was like a white rose blooming to its absolute peak at midnight, beautiful to the point of aggression—vivid, scorching, instantly the secret focus of many converging glances in the room.
That was Yao Yuan at thirty. A look that belonged to that age, that stage—flaunting ambition and allure without the slightest restraint. Even as an "assistant," it had never once occurred to her to dim her own light.
The middle-aged man beside her was Ding Shitong. A navy bespoke suit of no discernible brand but superb cut, no tie, the top button of his shirt collar left casually undone, a understated Patek Philippe at his wrist. There was nothing extra on him; he simply stood there, leaning in slightly to listen to another magnate, a faint, disarming smile at the corner of his mouth, nodding now and then, his gaze calm and sharp, able to seize the heart of whatever the other man said in an instant. That aura—deep as a still pool, towering as a mountain, in control of everything—came through undiminished even across the screen of time and space.
In the pure white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan was like the sole, and cruelest, audience seated before a giant IMAX screen. She watched that moving image set into the void, watched the "self" in it whose brows and eyes still held a flicker of vitality and hope not yet ground away by reality, watched the man she had once looked up to, been infatuated with, and who had, in the end, pushed her into the abyss.
There was no violent surge of emotion. Only a cold, detached scrutiny, like a doctor examining an imaging file labeled "textbook case."
The image flowed on.
Ding Shitong always seemed able to sense Yao Yuan's slightest unease. When yet another unfamiliar executive came over with a glass in hand to make small talk, his gaze lingering half a second too long on the overly dazzling gown, Ding Shitong had already stepped forward half a pace without a trace of effort, using half his body to shield her more naturally behind him. He raised his glass in greeting, and in two or three sentences steered the topic toward recent macroeconomic trends—defusing the possible scrutiny and awkwardness while completing the necessary social courtesy. His hand would occasionally rest, very lightly, against her back to signal her—guiding her on whom to greet next, or when to hand over a business card. That touch, through the thin fabric of the gown, was brief and restrained, yet carried an unmistakable warmth and a note of command.
Yao Yuan watched, and she understood. Back then, that sense of being protected and guided, mixed with the powerful aura and the charm of his experience, had been a fatal catalyst for her. He was her boss, a heavyweight in the Hushi business world, eighteen years her senior, at the very peak where a man's bearing, experience, and energy fused just right. That age gap naturally lent him a complex authority—mentor, father, and friend all at once—and cloaked all his attentions in a layer that seemed reasonable, even worthy of gratitude.
She remembered the details: at drinking sessions, he would quietly fend off ill-meant toasts on her behalf, his tone gentle but brooking no argument: "Little Yao has a bad stomach, I'll take this one for her." When he came back from business trips, he would "casually" hand her a velvet box holding a limited-edition Cartier bracelet or a Tiffany diamond brooch, brushing it off lightly: "A client gave it to me, I have no use for it, you girls can wear it for fun." Or, even more "thoughtfully": "I was picking out a gift for my wife and thought this one suited you too, so I grabbed it." Those gifts were expensive, exquisite, and quietly matched her taste. The ambiguity was like a carefully blended perfume, drifting through every exchanged glance, every "casual" act of care. He never crossed the line, but that ever-present control of detail stirred her heart far more than any blunt confession could, and made her mistake it for a singular favor meant only for her.
In the image, Ding Shitong was talking with someone, his gaze drifting now and then to Yao Yuan at his side. Seeing her lift her chin slightly, trading easy pleasantries with a well-known producer, something seemed to flicker deep in his eyes—a very faint, hard-to-catch glint, a mix of appreciation and some deeper satisfaction that belonged to a man in control.
"Little Yao, come." Ding Shitong's voice was not loud, but in the noisy background it came through clear and steady, like a safe anchor point. He led her toward a relatively quiet lounge area on one side of the banquet hall, where a middle-aged man stood whose bearing was a touch unruly but whose eyes were unusually bright and keen.
"Boss Duan, long time no see. This is my special assistant, Yao Yuan." Ding Shitong introduced her, his tone familiar yet carrying a barely perceptible note of gravity.
Duan Yanqing. In the pure white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan silently mouthed the name. Her heart gave no extra flutter—only a cold, dead stillness.
In the image, Duan Yanqing looked Yao Yuan over, his gaze resting on her for several seconds. It was not an ordinary man's wonder or greed, but the focused appraisal of a top-tier predator sighting rare prey—deep as the sea, every trace of desire and certainty perfectly tucked behind a calm, even faintly admiring surface. He raised his glass toward Ding Shitong, his smile full of meaning: "Boss Ding really does have hidden talents around him. Miss Yao, a pleasure."
"Hello, Boss Duan, I've long admired your name." The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan smiled with composure and handed over her card. She could feel that this man was different, could feel the weight Ding Shitong placed on him.
What followed was a conversation that left a very deep impression in Yao Yuan's memory. Ding and Duan seemed to share a long-standing rapport; the topic skipped straight past any feeling-out and cut into a vast, cutting-edge blueprint.
"The age of content as king hasn't changed, but the medium and the way we consume it have been turned upside down," Duan Yanqing said, swirling the red wine in his glass. "Short dramas, interactive dramas, AI-generated scripts, virtual-idol livestreams… a gold mine buried in fragments of spare time. But what we need is a platform that doesn't just make content—one that can intelligently distribute it, even intelligently co-create it."
Ding Shitong nodded and picked up the thread: "Exactly. After the traffic gateway comes ecosystem stickiness. Smart homes, in-car systems, even the personal terminals of the next era—they're all living scenarios where content can seamlessly plug in. Imagine telling your smart screen in the kitchen, 'Give me a short drama to go with dinner,' and the AI recommends, or even generates, a clip based on your history, your mood at that moment, even what's in your fridge; the background sound while your robot vacuum works could be a customized knowledge podcast or a finance breakdown."
The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan listened quietly off to the side, inwardly stunned. She had followed Ding Shitong for a year and witnessed his command of strategy, his deep mastery of manufacturing and traditional investment, but the sparks struck now between him and Duan Yanqing were about the future—a far more ambitious building of an ecosystem. From AI screenwriting algorithms, vertical short-drama brand matrices, and immersive podcast experiences, to smart appliances (ambient lighting that could sync with a plot, scene-based voice interaction for robot vacuums), the exclusive content ecosystem of next-generation smart cockpits, even discussions of investing in or developing lightweight AR devices as the medium for future immersive short dramas… the breadth of coverage and depth of integration left her dazzled.
And as Ding Shitong laid all this out, he would occasionally turn to her and explain a little using analogies she could grasp: "It's like that community-retail model we analyzed before—at its core it's still a closed loop of terminal and service, only now we've swapped in 'content plus AI plus smart hardware.'" In moments like these, he always made her feel that she was not just a bystander but someone he was deliberately drawing into this grand narrative. That sense of being needed, of being led, of touching the future—mixed with her admiration for his intelligence and vision—fermented wildly in her young heart.
Duan Yanqing was clearly moved too by the clarity of Ding Shitong's vision. The two clinked glasses, and a preliminary intent had already landed amid the easy talk and laughter. "Let the teams gnaw on the details; with the direction set, all that's left is execution. Boss Ding, I can see Miss Yao here is very capable—we can have her coordinate more when the time comes."
The gala went on, and Ding Shitong took Yao Yuan to meet several more key figures. He always found a clever way to create opportunities for her to shine—having her briefly lay out the data on some project she had been tracking, or supplement an industry update at just the right moment. When she finished, he would cast her a faint, approving glance, a look that quickened her heartbeat half a beat more than any praise could.
There had been too many such moments over the past year. He brought her into private clubs known only to the topmost circles and taught her the lore behind appreciating wine and cigars (though she rarely indulged); when she was a little nervous at her first massive financing negotiation, he gave the back of her hand a light pat under the table and then, with perfect steadiness, took command of the whole room; he even, on her birthday, under the name of "rewarding an outstanding assistant," gave her a small sum of seed capital, guided her through her first stock-market investment, and patiently explained market logic and risk control—an investment that, under her careful handling, turned a decent profit. What he taught her was not just business skills but a poised way of facing a complicated world.
Toward him she felt the respect owed a teacher, the trust owed a friend, but deep down there was also a woman's longing and attraction toward an utterly superior man. She was captivated by the slight knit of his brow when he thought, the unhurried confidence with which he negotiated, the occasional flashes of singular insight into art or literature, even the faint, steady woody scent on him. It was a refined halo tempered together by experience, success, and rigorous self-discipline, and it held a fatal pull for her.
And yet Ding Shitong's boundaries were always clear. His care was the care of an elder, his protection that of a superior. He would remember her bad stomach and remind her to go easy on cold drinks, yet when he draped a coat over her he never overstepped; he would let his aura turn cold in an instant over the slightest slight against her, yet in private he never spoke or acted toward her with any intimacy. He was like a warm, solid fortress, allowing her to rest and grow within it, yet never once opening, of his own accord, that inner door leading into the realm of private feeling. That care, present yet never declared, that distance held with perfect measure, made the feelings in Yao Yuan's heart rise and fall quietly like the tide under moonlight—never finding a shore to break against.
By now the gala was thinning out. Ding Shitong had the driver take Yao Yuan home first. The car was hushed inside, soft classical music flowing through it.
"How did today feel?" He loosened his collar and leaned back against the seat, looking a little tired, the side of his face especially deep-set in the light and shadow streaming past the window.
"There was so much to take in—I learned a great deal. Especially the ecosystem vision you and Boss Duan discussed; it showed me a completely different scale of things." Yao Yuan spoke from the heart, her fingertips absently stroking her necklace.
Ding Shitong gave a small smile, a faint relief in it. "You've grown fast. If this project gets off the ground, I'll hand you some very important coordination work. The pressure won't be small, but I believe you can do it."
His words were trust, a charge entrusted, still strictly framed within the working relationship. Yet that trust itself felt somehow like a singular intimacy.
"I'll give it everything—I won't let you down." Yao Yuan said softly. The thread of tenderness born from the weariness he occasionally let slip wove together with that suppressed longing, a tangle of mixed feelings.
The car stopped at the foot of Yao Yuan's apartment building. As always, Ding Shitong instructed the driver to confirm she had safely entered the building before leaving.
"Boss Ding," Yao Yuan said before getting out, unable to help looking back, "you should rest early too."
Ding Shitong nodded, his gaze lingering on her face for a moment, that look especially deep and unreadable in the dim light, until at last it became only a gentle: "Goodnight, little Yuan."
The car door closed, and the black sedan eased into the night.
The black sedan drove into the night. The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan stood where she was, the night wind brushing past, the diamonds at her ears swaying faintly, her heart still steeped in that special "little Yuan," secretly delighted.
In the pure white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan watched, with absolute calm, the thirty-year-old self in her memory—the one thrown into turmoil by a single "little Yuan," standing alone in the night wind, secretly pleased.
Foolish?
No, not quite foolish. It was the inevitable result of limited understanding. The thirty-year-old self was like a carefully trained experimental subject who had begun to master a complex instrument, preening over the occasional approving glance from her creator, utterly unaware that she herself was part of the experiment—perhaps even a control group. The golden cage Ding Shitong had built, out of a halo blending a mentor's authority, a man's charm, and the shelter of top-tier resources, was too gorgeous; the "knowledge bait" and "emotional value" he fed her were too precise. For a young woman in whom ambition and loneliness coexisted, who hungered to be recognized on a higher plane, it was an almost impossible formula to resist. Everything he gave—exposure, opportunity, meticulous care, an affirmation kept just out of reach—landed precisely on her points of need and her vanity. He did not love her; this was high-order domestication. He was savoring the sense of omnipotence and the subtle satisfaction of raising and taming a superior, intelligent creature. And the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan had mistaken domestication for favor, the bait for a feast meant for her alone.
She knew what would come next.
The "pursuit" Duan Yanqing would unfold, aimed at the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan—if that precisely calculated torrent of resources and access, that dimension-flattening strike, could even be called a pursuit.
It was not the floral and jewelry bombardment of an ordinary rich heir, nor the romantic verse of a literary youth. Duan Yanqing's "methods" were built on a precise psychological profile of women like Yao Yuan. He saw straight through the vanity beneath her gorgeous gown—the near-starvation for "the high-end" and "recognition" that came from clawing her way up from the bottom—and saw through, too, the yearning and unease, hidden beneath her clever ambition, about truly stepping into "that class." What he meant to do was not to satisfy her, but to redefine the threshold of her desire—to make even the air she was used to feel cheap, to make the world of Ding Shitong she had once looked up to seem narrow and small.
The first wave: class access and the illusion of identity.
On the third day after the gala, Yao Yuan received an elegantly worded invitation, sealed in wax and stamped with the crest of a top Swiss private cigar club. The venue was a non-public old mansion in Bund Source. Duan Yanqing's assistant called, his tone deferential yet leaving no room for refusal: "Mr. Duan is hosting a small cigar tasting next week—everyone coming is a friend. He remembers Miss Yao has a knowledge of Cuban coffee and thought you might be interested in the cigar culture of the same growing region, so he asked me specially to invite you. Dress casually."
The result of "dress casually" was that, when Yao Yuan stepped into that hidden chamber thick with aged cedar and leather, she found the so-called "friends" were two internet new-money tycoons who often graced the covers of finance magazines, a director who had just brought home a Golden Lion from the Venice Film Festival, and an heir whose surname carried the heavy flavor of an old Hong Kong family. They smoked limited-edition Cohibas said to be worth more than gold, yet what they discussed was not business—it was the starry skies of some undisclosed Himalayan trekking route, the latest findings at an Antarctic emperor-penguin observation station, a tribal secret rite about to be held in some small African nation, open to only twenty guests. The way Duan Yanqing drew her into the conversation was utterly natural: "Yao Yuan has real insight into shifts in coffee-bean growing regions—we were just talking about how climate affects a crop's flavor, and it has a curious parallel with cigars." With a single sentence, he lifted her from "Ding Shitong's pretty assistant" to a position where she could hold some kind of "equal" conversation with these figures. He personally selected a mild cigar for her and demonstrated cutting, toasting, and lighting it, his fingers never once touching her—yet that atmosphere of drawing her into a private ritual was more invasive than any physical contact. That night she smoked only a few puffs, but every breath carried the mellow scent of money and power settled into something rich. As she left, Duan Yanqing merely gave a faint nod: "Try the Davidoff annual limited edition next time—you'll like it." As if they already had many a "next time."
The second wave: scarce experiences and sensory conquest.
Then came a "friends' gathering"—aboard a seventy-eight-meter superyacht in the Mediterranean. A helicopter ferried her over from a helipad in Monaco. Apart from a crew so well-trained and good-looking they rivaled models, there was only Duan Yanqing and two of his "childhood buddies" (only later did Yao Yuan learn that one was a silent shareholder in some old-line European luxury house, and the other's family controlled several key shipping lanes). There were no bikini-clad beauties or champagne revelry as she had imagined; by day they went deep-sea fishing, and the bluefin tuna they caught were prepared on the spot by a three-Michelin-star chef sailing with them; at dusk they watched the sunset on deck while the speakers played an unreleased private recording of some jazz master, transferred from vinyl; deep in the night, they lay under the starry sky on deck and talked of the scent of locust blossoms at the mouth of a childhood alley, the cold of sleeping in a basement after a first failed venture, the lifelong regrets of some elder now passed. Duan Yanqing rarely let slip a little "realness": pointing at the stars, he spoke of the loneliness of surveying the Gobi desert in his youth, of how back then the stars had seemed like cold diamonds, and only now seemed like "distant campfires." Then, very naturally, he draped a cashmere shawl gently over the shoulders of Yao Yuan, who wore only a silk slip dress: "The wind's harsh at night—don't catch a chill." That care broke free of Ding Shitong's elder-style fussing; it felt more like the consideration between equals, between two of a kind. In that moment, Yao Yuan vaguely felt she had touched a thread of genuine warmth beneath this man's hard shell. This "privileged sharing of something real" was more lethal than the yacht and the stars combined.
The third wave: resource empowerment and value recognition.
Duan Yanqing began to "use" her—not the way Ding Shitong assigned concrete tasks, but treating her as an "insider" who could take part in certain core affairs. He would have his assistant copy her on business briefings that were non-core but meaningful, then "offhandedly" ask her opinion. Once, it was even a letter of investment intent regarding an emerging consumer brand; after hearing her analysis based on user data, he nodded and told his assistant: "Note down the community-spread risk point Miss Yao raised—have the post-investment department run a stress test." Her opinion was actually adopted. He would also take her to view some extremely private collections—not to show off, but to discuss: "What do you think—this Northern Song Ru-ware piece, is there any way to translate its aesthetic value into contemporary commercial brand-building?" He was earnestly seeking her view, as if her taste and commercial instinct were assets worth treasuring. Once she happened to mention an interest in a certain niche avant-garde designer, and the very next week he arranged a private dinner with only her, the designer, and the brand's chief—over the course of which a preliminary cooperation intent for that brand's entry into the Chinese market was reached. Duan Yanqing said to her lightly: "You've got good taste—this label has potential. If you're interested, you could put in a little pocket money, call it a seed round." This was not gift-giving; this was granting her the power to invest and to turn stone into gold. He made her feel she was not a vase clinging to him, but a partner who could create value alongside him. This "value recognition" and "resource empowerment" was a fatal temptation for a woman like Yao Yuan—proud and hungry to prove her own ability. Ding Shitong cultivated her, but it was always "superior to subordinate"; Duan Yanqing, within a seemingly equal partnership, let her taste what it was to be a "ruler."
The fourth wave: erosion of detail and the colonization of life.
His infiltration was seamless yet never made her uncomfortable. She offhandedly mentioned liking some niche fragrance brand's scent of earth after rain, and before long the hotel suite where she usually stayed, even the inside of her own car, had quietly been switched to the same fragrance. She mentioned she had been sleeping poorly lately, and the next time they met he "happened" to bring a small tin of an ancient-recipe herbal tea said to come from some hidden Himalayan village, produced in tiny quantities each year: "A friend gave it to me—good for calming the nerves, give it a try." She found that he remembered all her dietary preferences (no scallion or ginger, a liking for sour and spicy), and once, at a business lunch, he naturally swapped onto her plate the restaurant's signature dessert that she had glanced at twice—the motion so smooth it was as if he had done it a thousand times. He never sent roses or jewelry that obviously symbolized romantic love; the things he gave were either out-of-print art books that "happened" to relate to her professional interests, or abstract prints he had "bid one extra copy of at a friend's auction," or a lifetime membership at some top equestrian club for her to "experience." None of these things touched on lust, yet they slotted into her life and her taste with uncanny precision, accustoming her to his presence, to his "understanding," to the feeling of being "cared for" at such a high concentration and such high taste. Her vanity was not merely a vanity for material things, but a deep addiction to being "treated so attentively by someone of such a level." It made her feel she really was "special"—special enough that this man standing at the summit was willing to put in such thought.
In the pure white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan calmly dissected the thirty-year-old self's wavering, which had deepened by the day. She watched that young self study, over and over in the dead of night, the messages Duan Yanqing sent—worded never to overstep, yet heavy with meaning; watched her practice an ever more composed bearing before the mirror after each "high-end experience"; watched her begin, without realizing it, to compare the "guidance" and "gifts" Ding Shitong had given her—once treasured beyond price—with the "platform" and "possibilities" Duan Yanqing offered. Ding Shitong was good, was steady, was a reliable ladder—but what Duan Yanqing showed her was the vast sky above the ladder, another way of living she had never imagined, where freedom and power existed side by side.
Her vanity was fed until it flourished. She relished the faintly probing, courteous looks now cast at her by people she had once had to look up to; relished her invisible status in Duan Yanqing's circle, where no one dared overlook her even in her silence; relished that thrill—mingling danger with utter satisfaction—of "being pursued with full force by the most cunning hunter in the world."
Of course she was wary. But the high art of Duan Yanqing's hunting lay in this: he never truly forced her, always granted her the (seeming) power of choice, always kept a distance pitched just right. His "pursuit" was more like a lavish cultivation game tailored to her alone—letting her watch with her own eyes as her vision, her scale, her quality of life shot upward, accustoming her to this "high-end" until she could no longer endure the "ordinary." What he domesticated was not her body, but her taste, her thresholds, her desires.
The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan knew that, had it not been for the betrayal that came later, the thirty-year-old self might very well have truly fallen—fallen not for love, but for that vast illusion of being wrapped in top-tier resources, affirmed by overwhelming power, her own worth seemingly magnified without limit. That vain satisfaction was, to the Yao Yuan of that time—who longed to reach the summit, longed to prove she deserved it all—a kind of ▢▢.
And it was precisely because she had once truly wavered like that, come so close to being seduced, that the betrayal represented by Ding Shitong's later soft "Mm" was rendered all the more cruel and absolute. It shattered not merely the fantasy of one man, but the illusory understanding of her own allure and worth that she had built up through Duan Yanqing. She woke with a jolt: so in Ding Shitong's eyes, all this worth that Duan Yanqing had carefully "cultivated" and "courted" was, in the end, nothing more than a chip with a clear price tag, to be traded away! So the "specialness" and "being chosen" she had believed in were, on the higher chessboard of interests, nothing at all!
It was exactly this agony—of being slammed from the clouds of vanity into the mire of reality—that made the later Yao Yuan strip away every shred of emotional naivety. She saw clearly that in the world of Duan Yanqing and Ding Shitong, everything was appraisal and exchange. The so-called appreciation, pursuit, painstaking cultivation—all of it revolved around the core of "value." She no longer believed in any "kindness" without cause, no longer held any fantasy of "singular favor." She tempered the experience of this betrayal and seduction into a new law of survival: since everything is value exchange, then she would make herself the party with the highest value—and the one who firmly held the power to set the price. Depend on no one's "giving," fantasize about no "exception," believe only in quantifiable chips and controllable bindings of interest. Feeling is redundancy, softheartedness is weakness; only by ceaselessly raising her own value, calculating gain and loss with precision, and executing transactions coldly is there any way to survive.
This, then, was the starting point of all the logic of the later "Godmother of Feelings," Yao Yuan—and the original, and most painful, furnace in which that cold, hard bedrock of her character was forged.
