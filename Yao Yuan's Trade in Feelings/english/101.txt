Chapter 101 — Coffee in the Warm Sun: Telling Zhao Yiming the Secret of the Mirror Pact

Back at the riverside apartment, the afternoon sun was at its fullest.

The two of them stepped inside and changed their shoes. The soft click of the latch in the entryway shut out the last of the lingering bustle from the world outside. Yao Yuan told Zhao Yiming to make himself comfortable, then went straight to the kitchen, where she brewed two cups of espresso in a moka pot. Carrying the dark-brown coffee with its rich, oily aroma, she invited him to come into the study with her.

To Yao Yuan, the study felt like the right place to lay out every absurd secret and confess everything that had come before.

The north side of the study was one whole wall of floor-to-ceiling glass. The warm northern winter sun poured in without restraint, splitting the room into two interwoven blocks of light and shadow. Beyond the window the Yellow River surged on without pause; under the clear sky its rippling waters were crushed by the sunlight into a sky full of leaping golden scales, rising and falling slowly with the current.

They sat facing each other across the desk, bathed in the cascade of light, each cradling a warm porcelain cup, the long, bitter fragrance of coffee drifting quietly through the still air. For a while neither of them spoke. There was only the faint trickle of the water heater, which made the room feel all the more peaceful—and quietly set the stage for a confession that would upend everything he thought he knew.

After a long moment, Yao Yuan gently set down her cup, drew her fingers in, and pressed down the last trace of unease in her heart. She leaned forward and pulled open the bottommost drawer of the desk. Inside there were no cluttered files or odds and ends—only a dark-brown box of old sandalwood, lying quietly. The box had been handled over and over through the years; the patina on its surface was warm and thick, glowing with a calm, deep light.

She set the box down between them and slowly lifted the lid. There, cushioned in soft dark velvet, a plain, palm-sized bronze mirror lay quietly dormant, steeped in years and in unknown mystery.

Yao Yuan pinched the wrapping velvet between her fingers, lifted the bronze mirror out steadily, and held it across to Zhao Yiming. "Take a look at this."

Zhao Yiming raised his hand and took it. A bone-deep chill spread across his palm in an instant, as though the old mirror carried its own cold, sealed off from all the warmth around it. The mirror face was only seven or eight centimeters across, its edges polished smooth and round, fine to the touch as jade—nothing like ordinary metal. The body of the mirror was a deep, brooding gray-green, its mottled verdigris scattered across the surface—not messy or grimy, but layered, holding a dark, subtle texture all its own.

At the center of the mirror's back was a small, delicate bridge-shaped knob, ringed by an intricate, finely cast relief of Kui-dragons and cloud-and-thunder patterns, interlacing and coiling in layer upon layer. The slanting sunlight swept across the body of the mirror; the grooves of the pattern sank into shadow, while the raised ridges refracted fine threads of gold. At the dragon's eyes especially, the lines ran in a strange, secretive direction—stare at them long enough and a dizzying illusion would rise, the sense of being watched from somewhere hidden.

He turned the mirror over. Its face was a dim, dull yellow; the reflection it gave back was blurred and warped, sketching out only the faint outline of his features, like looking at himself through a thick fog. As an object alone, it was a finely crafted, well-preserved old bronze mirror of considerable collector's value—and beyond that, nothing unusual at all.

Zhao Yiming lifted his eyes to Yao Yuan, a clear question in his gaze, waiting quietly for her to go on.

Yao Yuan met his clear, rational gaze, drew in a soft breath, and for a moment had no idea where to begin. The man across from her had spent his whole life believing in mathematical logic, biological law, AI modeling, and objective principles—a lifetime spent deconstructing everything in the world through science. To explain to him an old mirror, time-reversal, the borrowing of fortune, a fated pact—absurd things that fell entirely outside the modern scientific framework—was far harder than dissecting human nature or playing out a negotiation.

She needed a real, concrete time-anchor that they had both witnessed, something to give all these bizarre accounts a place to stand.

"Do you remember that winter two years ago, the night we first met for dinner?" she said softly, her voice unusually clear in the quiet study.

Zhao Yiming paused, and the memory rose up sharp and clear at once; a faint, gentle warmth flickered in his eyes. "I remember it well. You wore a red dress under a white sable coat, and when you pushed the door open and walked in, you were like a fierce rose blooming straight out of the flames—dazzling and bold."

He paused, his tone still even but carrying a hint of amused recollection. "And the very first thing you said was loaded with provocation. You showed no mercy at all, peeling open my scars right away—bringing up my first-generation 'Genesis Engine' model, how it burned through several hundred million and finally blew up my own server room."

At that, the corners of Yao Yuan's lips curved up into a faint smile.

Back then she had come in probing step by step, with the goal of sizing up a partnership, her bearing aggressive and her words sharp, set on seizing the upper hand and taking control of the negotiation. And he had given no ground either—rational, incisive, his whole presence on full display. Neither of them could have foreseen that that needle-sharp, swords-drawn first meeting would ultimately lead to the deep bond of souls and shared fate they had now.

That smile quietly diluted the heaviness of the room, which was about to face its secret head-on; the taut atmosphere loosened a little.

"It was that day." Yao Yuan reined in her smile and returned to the point, her words clear and steady, landing solidly. "Before that dinner, Shuai Hongqiang came to my company to discuss a project. This bronze mirror was the gift he gave me that day—our first meeting in eight years."

She instinctively studied Zhao Yiming's expression, then went on candidly: "You should remember—at the Pretty Big One AI launch, he came to offer his congratulations. Shuai Hongqiang is an old friend I've known for years in Jinshi, and also... my ex-boyfriend."

Not a ripple crossed Zhao Yiming's face. No surprise, no probing—he simply listened quietly and waited for the rest. That utterly steady acceptance gave Yao Yuan the courage to go on.

"After that dinner, once I got home, the strange things started happening." Yao Yuan slowed her pace, recounting this most absurd and inexplicable experience in the calmest, most objective tone she could manage. "Without any warning, my consciousness was forcibly pulled out and sent straight back to the point in my life when I was twenty-two. It was like being an immersed bystander to a hologram of the past—watching old events with my own eyes, even able to talk to the version of myself in that other time and space."

Zhao Yiming's brow shifted almost imperceptibly, but he didn't interrupt; the fingers gripping his cup only tightened slightly.

"At first I thought it was a hallucination, a fantasy born of sheer exhaustion." Yao Yuan owned her initial struggle frankly. "But after it happened again and again, I had to admit this was a real anomaly. Later, out of guilt over the 'borrowing of fortune,' Shuai Hongqiang confessed the secret of the bronze mirror and took me to see Old Chen, a highly respected antiques appraiser here in the city."

"That was when I learned this is no ordinary relic. It's said to have come from the hands of a Tang-dynasty alchemist, Lingxuzi, with secret arts hidden inside it. And my fate is unusual—I bear the Great Fortune of the Nine-Purple Departing Fire, and under specific emotions and states of mind, I accidentally activated the mirror's time-tracing mechanism."

Yao Yuan chose her words carefully, trying to avoid anything too mystical, but the core facts couldn't be sidestepped. "Put simply, it can briefly draw my consciousness back to certain key moments in my past life. It isn't the body traveling through time—it's pure consciousness tracing back, watching the past, connecting with the version of myself in another time and space."

"Briefly drawn back? Sent back?" Zhao Yiming seized on the key words with precision, his questions cutting straight to the heart of it, carrying a researcher's particular caution. "Frequency? Duration? Trigger conditions?"

"There's no fixed pattern to the triggers. Most of them are tied to swings in my emotions, major life decisions, shifts at the turning points of fate." Yao Yuan carefully reviewed every experience and answered precisely. "The perceived duration drifts—sometimes it feels as long as several hours, sometimes it's gone in a flash. But measured against real time, the longest is under an hour, the shortest only a few minutes. Take that ninth time-tracing just now in the mirror room: it felt like I spent at least half an hour in that other time and space, but in reality less than ten minutes had passed."

Zhao Yiming was silent for a moment, his gaze falling back to the bronze mirror in his palm, his fingertips tracing the cool Kui-dragon pattern. In an instant he strung all the clues together. "So when you suddenly fainted in the mirror room just now, it wasn't weakness or a daze—it was the normal drain after your ninth tracing-back ended, your spirit briefly spent?"

"Yes." Yao Yuan nodded gently, and felt something in her chest ease.

The greatest fortune of confiding in someone supremely clever and transparently rational is that there's no need to belabor the causes, no need to lay groundwork or explain—they always piece the fragments together in an instant, smooth out the logic, and strike straight to the core.

Now that the most crucial opening had been made, everything that followed flowed effortlessly. Yao Yuan went along the timeline, slowly recounting every secret: from her self-doubt and search for proof after the first time-travel, to the confirmation and fear of the second and third; to how Shuai Hongqiang, jittery day and night, unable to bear the karmic pressure of being the bearer of the Pact, came forward to confess the bronze mirror's origins and the Mirror Pact's karmic bond between the two of them; from how the two of them tracked down the descendants of the mirror-maker, to finally finding the old Mr. Wang Baoguo, the mirror-maker's descendant, and the method for breaking this uncanny pact.

Throughout, her tone stayed even, with no subjective coloring, no emotional filter—like reviewing a complex, precise project, clear in its order and complete in its logic. The bronze mirror, the Mirror Pact, the nine tracings-back, the backlash from borrowing fortune, the conditions for breaking the pact—she laid them all out, one by one, in full.

When the last word landed, the study sank into a long stillness.

Beyond the window the Yellow River rolled on without end, its ancient, ceaseless sound faintly reaching the ear, making the silence inside feel all the deeper.

Yao Yuan picked up her now-cooled coffee and took a sip. The bitterness washed over the tip of her tongue, yet her heart felt lighter and looser than ever before. For an entire year this absurd, heavy secret had pressed on her heart like solid ice—no one to tell, no one who could understand, not even a word of it shared with her own mother. Now that it lay fully bared before the person she trusted most, more than half the thousand-pound weight she had borne alone had quietly lifted.

Zhao Yiming stayed silent for a long while.

He raised the mirror to the light again, studying every patch of verdigris, every line of pattern, every inch of texture. On the physical level—the material structure, the marks of craftsmanship—this was simply a beautiful, ancient mirror from long ago. But the supernatural qualities of "time and space," "pact," "tracing back," "borrowing fortune"—none of them could be captured by any instrument or corroborated by any law.

And yet he chose to believe completely.

He didn't believe in empty mysticism or fated destiny, but he believed absolutely in Yao Yuan's mind and character. She had always been rational and clear-eyed, steady and restrained, rigorous in her logic; she never indulged in baseless fantasy, and she certainly wouldn't fabricate an absurd story at the expense of her own fate and safety. What's more, every separation, every tracing-back, every detail she described was real and finely textured, echoing layer upon layer—nothing that could be conjured up out of thin air.

That science couldn't explain it didn't mean it didn't exist. The beauty of mathematics lay in accepting that certain axioms need no proof; the complexity of biology lay in the countless unsolved mysteries life itself still held. He studied AI, exploring the boundaries of consciousness and intelligence; he understood better than most that humanity's grasp of the world, of itself, of time and existence, might not have touched even the tip of the iceberg.

After a long while, he set the bronze mirror down, raised his eyes to Yao Yuan with a clear, sharp gaze, and cut straight into the most practical risk analysis. "Let me lay it out. To break this 'Mirror Pact,' you need fingertip blood from three people—you, Shuai Hongqiang, and that old Mr. Wang. It has to be done after a number of time-travels that is three, or a multiple of three. You wrap the mirror in nine layers of specially made cinnabar-red cloth, and sink it to the riverbed at a specific stretch of the Yellow River, at a specific hour. Is that right?"

"That's right," Yao Yuan answered.

"Before you found old Mr. Wang, you'd already completed seven tracings-back, missing the third and sixth marks, so you could only wait for the ninth." Zhao Yiming's mind raced, swiftly locking onto the core risk. "Today you've just completed the ninth—it's the safest window right now, the one with the fewest variables. If you delay, there's a very high chance of triggering a tenth or eleventh tracing-back, missing the mark again, and then you'd have to wait until the twelfth. In the meantime, all the unknown backlash, all the twists of fate—we'd have no way to predict any of it."

"So we have to break the pact as soon as possible."

"Right." Watching him so calm and composed, with everything grounded and accounted for, the last trace of unease in Yao Yuan's heart dissolved completely, replaced by a deep sense of recognition and warmth.

He hadn't questioned whether the story was true, hadn't pressed her on the mystical details, hadn't shown any morbid curiosity or fear, and certainly hadn't fixated on the fact that she'd kept it from him. Instead, he'd swiftly grasped the heart of the problem: risk control and a solution. That utterly "Zhao Yiming"–style response was exactly the security she most needed.

She let herself relax a little and deliberately blinked, taking on a teasing tone. "I've just told you all these bizarre, eerie things—aren't you going to think I'm making up a story? Or... blame me for keeping it from you all this time?"

Looking at the rare, faintly innocent contrast in her expression, a very faint, gentle smile crossed Zhao Yiming's eyes, though he kept his face deliberately serious. "I do blame you for one thing."

Yao Yuan arched a brow slightly, waiting for the rest.

"For being too stubborn, for carrying this alone for so long." His tone softened in an instant, his gaze sincere and grave. "A secret this absurd and inexplicable, with no one to share it—anyone in your place wouldn't dare show it lightly to another soul. That you were willing to tell me means you trust me completely. I'm grateful for that, and I cherish it."

He paused, then added a line of his usual cool humor: "At the very least, now I don't have to worry that my fiancée might suddenly be swept off by some unknown consciousness one day, or be invited by some special agency to 'assist with research.'"

At that, Yao Yuan finally let go entirely, her eyes curving as she laughed.

None of the heaviness, doubt, confusion, estrangement, or argument she'd braced for appeared. In their place was total acceptance, transparent understanding, and the tacit alliance of two people standing side by side to face the storm. The moment the secret was laid bare, every barrier melted away, and the bond between them grew deeper and firmer than before.

"The urgent thing now is to coordinate with Shuai Hongqiang and old Mr. Wang—nail down the time, the place, the list of materials." Zhao Yiming reined in his smile and returned to his practical mode, arranging things crisply. "The sooner it's done, the better. Cut out every variable. What do you need me to help with?"

"I'll contact Shuai Hongqiang first." Yao Yuan picked up her phone. "He's more anxious than I am. The Mirror Pact, the bearing of fortune, the karmic backlash—it could fall on him. He's very frightened."

When the call connected, Shuai Hongqiang was in a hospital ward. The cast on his daughter Shuai Wenrui's leg was a glaring white. This sudden injury had long since driven his fear of the Mirror Pact's karmic backlash to its peak, leaving him uneasy day and night. Thankfully his daughter was in much better spirits now, propped up at the head of the bed watching a show on a tablet. Seeing that the call was from Yao Yuan, Shuai Hongqiang's heart gave an inexplicable jump. He signaled to his daughter and stepped out into the corridor before answering.

"Shuai Hongqiang, it's me." Yao Yuan's voice came through the receiver, calm and steady, carrying the certainty of dust finally settling. "The ninth time-travel... just ended. The mark for breaking the Mirror Pact is set."

Shuai Hongqiang's knuckles tightened abruptly around the phone, the veins standing out faintly, his throat dry and tight, both thrilled and afraid. "Good! Good! We've finally made it!" he said, the words tumbling out.

Out of the corner of his eye he glanced through the gap in the ward door at his recovering daughter inside, and the fear in his heart grew thicker still—he both longed to break the pact as soon as possible and dreaded what karmic backlash he, as the bearer of the Mirror Pact, might still suffer before it was fully broken, after yet another time-travel of hers. "Let's set it for tomorrow! The sooner the better! Delay breeds trouble. I'll contact old Mr. Wang right now, nail down the hour, the stretch of river, and the full list of everything we need, and I'll tell you the moment it's settled!"

"Then you contact old Mr. Wang, settle the time and place, and let me know." Yao Yuan understood his urgency. "And go over the list of things we need to prepare with him once more."

"Understood. I'll call right away. Stay in touch." Shuai Hongqiang hung up in a rush and immediately pulled up Wang Baoguo's number from his contacts. His hands were shaking a little; he took two deep breaths before pressing the dial key.

Listening to the busy tone on the line, Yao Yuan put down her phone and looked at Zhao Yiming. "Shuai Hongqiang's gone to make the call. We wait for word."

Zhao Yiming nodded gently, rose, and walked over to stand before her. He reached out naturally, pulled her up from the chair, and drew her steadily into his arms.

It was a purely comforting, supporting embrace—not a trace of desire in it, only complete acceptance, protection, and the certainty of standing side by side.

"It'll go smoothly," he said by her ear, his voice steady and strong.

Yao Yuan buried her face against his shoulder, breathing in the reassuring scent of him, and gave a soft "Mm."

Outside the window the daylight slanted west, the sky full of warm sun gradually shedding its fierce glare, dyeing the rolling Yellow River a gorgeous orange-red before it slowly settled into a calm purple-gray. In the changeover between day and night, all things lay silent and still.

The secret was now shared. The road ahead was still uncertain; the breaking of the pact at the Yellow River held fortunes good or ill no one could foretell, and the karmic backlash was not yet over. But from now on, she was no longer alone in fighting an absurd fate.

Their embracing silhouettes formed a quiet, solid outline in the lengthening light and shadow of the study.

The countdown to breaking the pact had begun. And all the tangled fate, the shackles of the Mirror Pact, the old fears of the past would, before long, be handed over to that great river that had flowed in silence for millions of years.
