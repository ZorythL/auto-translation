Chapter 72 — The Truth About Marriage: Kill the Old Self, and Survive the Game Together

Eight in the evening.
A high-summer night. Beyond the window lay Jinshi's scattered lights against the dark, the faint drone of cicadas. The lighting in the studio had been turned a softer, warmer gold than usual, like the last glow of a setting sun. Lin Wan wore a pale apricot linen short-sleeved shirt, the fabric crisp yet casual, the top two buttons undone to show the clean line of her collarbones. Her short hair caught a healthy sheen under the lights, the ends tucked neatly behind her ears, making her neck look long and slender. Her makeup was light and tasteful; only her lips wore a soft mauve-pink that lifted her color without ever shouting for attention. There was no elaborate tea set in front of her, just a glass of plain hot water sending up thin curls of steam. In the background, that potted Pollux tree—her symbol of protection—stood quietly in one corner, and beside it someone had added a small bundle of dried eucalyptus, its gray-green leaves curling, giving off a cool, calming herbal scent that brought a touch of visual coolness to the summer night.
She looked into the camera, her gaze settled and calm, the kind that comes from a life lived through—like the surface of a windless lake at midnight, clearly mirroring the sky and the stars, yet fathomless beneath.
"Good evening, and welcome to 'A Gentle Crossing.'" Her voice was warm, carrying a power that made people go still. "Tonight, we'll talk about something real, and something less tangible too. We'll talk about marriage—the bindings, the trades, the games of strategy you can see—and the thing you can't see, the thing that may decide everything."
[The scrolling comments drifted by softly, with the slow rhythm that belongs to nighttime:]
[Wanwan looks great tonight—fresh and gentle.]
[The seen and the unseen? Now I'm curious.]
[This feels like it'll be a deep one. Getting my notebook ready.]
"Lately I've gotten a lot of messages on the backend, especially in my private inbox—so many women describing a kind of deep exhaustion inside their marriages." Lin Wan spoke at an unhurried pace, as if talking with a friend late at night. "They say they're working so hard to grow stronger, grinding to make money, never daring to ease up, signing up for course after course to better themselves, terrified that if they stop they'll be called 'a dependent,' afraid of losing their voice in the relationship, afraid of being looked down on. I understand that effort completely, and I respect it absolutely. Of course we should grow. We should have the ability to stand in society and create value. That's our footing as we move through the world, and the bedrock of keeping our dignity and our whole selves intact in any relationship."
She paused for a moment, her eyes sweeping across the screen, as if she could see the figures of all those women quietly digesting their stress and laboring on alone, deep in the night.
"I understand the pain behind that feeling all too well." A barely perceptible note of lament entered her voice—the understanding of someone who has been there. "People who can face the wounds of their family of origin head-on, who can look straight at the uglier scars inside a marriage, tend to live more deeply, more clearly. But the ones willing and able to truly 'look' are always the minority."
"For far too many people, their values were set in stone very early on: find a good man, marry him, and you've solved life. The best ending for a woman who marries seems to be never having to work, being provided for, hiding behind a man for the rest of her days, sheltering from the storms of life." She shook her head lightly—no judgment in the motion, only clear sight. "But only those who've actually walked this road, or watched it up close, know that this puts the cart before the horse, confuses what matters with what doesn't. Any woman who pins her entire hope for survival and happiness on the foundation of 'being provided for by a man' will, more often than not, find no escape from a fate of being manipulated, looked down on, even bullied. I've seen that road. I've tasted what's on it. I see it far too clearly."
[The comments began to fill with recognition and self-reflection:]
[This is the truth. My mom raised me exactly this way, and now my life is anything but good.]
["Being provided for" sounds beautiful, but it's really like walking on thin ice.]
[Has Wanwan been through something? There's more in these words than she's saying.]
"Outsiders looking at a marriage rarely see clearly—they only see the surface." Lin Wan's tone was plain, as if stating an objective fact. "They see that I seem to have a marriage, a man at my side, no worries about material life, and they might slap a 'lucky woman' label on me without a second thought. No one knows—or rather, no one cares—that I may have been trapped inside this besieged fortress of a marriage for many years, that I've long since become a seasoned old prisoner here."
She gave a small laugh, something complicated in it—not bitter, more like the self-mockery of someone who has risen above it: "It's not that I live some fairytale happiness. It's just that I understand the survival rules of this 'fortress' too well—I know how to disguise, to play along, to keep up appearances within its rules, and, in the process, to find the ways that make me relatively comfortable and strong. That's all."
She picked up the glass of water, took a small sip, and went on: "People often ask me in the studio, very bluntly: 'Wanwan, between you and Brother Qiang—are the two of you completely transparent about money?' My answer is just as blunt: of course not. If two people lay their account books fully open, every cent in and out crystal clear, you only fall into endless calculation and tug-of-war—you tallying whether my income's gotten too high, me tallying whether your spending's unreasonable, both of us guarding against each other at every turn, running little schemes in our heads, never able to fight side by side with a clear mind against the bigger world out there. It's precisely because there's a certain gray zone around money—because each of us has a space we can fully control, and the footing that comes with it, because we both understand how hard it is for an adult to live in this world and live with dignity—that we can actually set down a lot of pointless suspicion. We can be like trusted partners, streaming together, facing society together, building a business and an income together. This is a higher kind of trust, grounded in reality and an understanding of human nature."
[The comments split between agreement and dispute:]
[The honest truth! My husband and I split everything down to the line item, and we fight about money all the time.]
[Not transparent? Isn't that just two people each hiding their own agenda?]
[I think she has a point. Total transparency makes it feel like flatmates splitting rent, not a family.]
[The key word is "footing"—you have to be able to earn money yourself before you've earned the right to not be transparent.]
"Some people also say," Lin Wan said, looking at another kind of comment scrolling past, speaking it openly, "that you can tell at a glance: in this relationship, the woman loves the man more, and the man doesn't love the woman as much."
She faced the camera, her gaze clear, not the faintest flinch: "I can openly admit that too. If you measure us by that worldly notion of pure, fantasy-soaked, you-and-no-one-else 'deep romantic love,' then between us—maybe there isn't any. But we're both greedy for money and pleasure, partners in mischief, each taking what we need, binding each other tight. We've twisted reality's interests, the tangled web of human relations, and the most basic logic of survival together like strands of hemp rope—wound into a single body of shared interest, hard to pull apart. There's none of that fairytale, untouched-by-the-mortal-world devotion, only the unspoken symbiosis between two adults; no impractical, naive love, only the tacit understanding and the checks and balances that come after seeing through the complexity of human nature."
"I sit here, in front of the camera, playing out the kind of 'happiness' and 'wisdom' the world's gaze demands, while at the same time seeing through the essence of it all more clearly than anyone. What outsiders see is the traffic, the dignity, the safe harbor; only I know that I'm one of the most clear-eyed players in this game. I've seen through the essence of marriage as a social institution, through the light and dark of human nature, through the illusion and the reality of the online world, and through a certain fate that perhaps no woman of this era can fully escape—and, beneath that fate, the limited but important choices an individual can still make."
Her words were like a scalpel, calmly cutting open the shell of warmth to reveal the complicated, true grain inside. The studio was very quiet; even the comments seemed to slow, everyone digesting the weight of what she'd said.
"And the thing that once tormented me most—and in the end woke me up completely," Lin Wan's tone dropped, drawing on something more personal, "was the way Brother Qiang, deep in his bones, holds an almost religious faith in the power of money. He built everything from nothing, earned a considerable fortune, and he truly believes money is all-powerful, that it can solve the vast majority of problems. In his eyes, marriage is, by its nature, a clear exchange of value and a partnership—nothing low about it, but stripped of any romantic filter, full of reality's cold logic. He never hid this view. At certain moments he'd even tell me, plainly: 'My money can buy you, but it can also buy other women. You aren't the only one. I have enough wealth, which means I can have you and, at the same time, keep other options open.'"
[The comments exploded:]
[That's just too cruel a thing to say!]
[Who could put up with that? Wanwan, how did you bear it?]
[So realistic, so brutal...]
[So do rich men really all think this way?]
"The me of the past, hearing words like that," Lin Wan's voice was very calm, as if telling someone else's story—but beneath the calm lay enormous strength, "would tremble all over with anger, would feel wronged to the core, hurt, certain my feelings had been thoroughly turned into a tool, never cherished. That heart of mine, so full of longing for emotion and connection, was pierced full of holes by words like those. I'd lie awake all night, sinking into deep self-doubt: was it that I wasn't good enough? Had I lost my dignity completely in this relationship, lost my independent self? Back then, even wrapped in luxury goods every day, getting the most expensive cosmetic work done to keep up my looks—glossy on the outside—inside I was always anxious and unsettled, unable to find a single place where I could rest in peace."
She drew a small breath, her eyes turning utterly clear—the clarity left behind when the fog lifts: "I, too, was lost and struggling for a long time. I watched plenty of people around me envy me my comfort, my well-off, stable life; only I knew how much I'd lost in spirit for that 'stability,' into what corner the real 'self' had been pressed. But after the pain reached its limit, what came instead was total clarity. With my own hands I 'killed' that old self—the one so sensitive and fragile, so desperate for emotional value, who hung her happiness on the other person's attitude."
"I began to truly see through it: the way Brother Qiang builds and maintains relationships—intimate ones above all—the way he knows best, and is almost the only thing he's good at, is power control and value exchange. That's how his mind works; it's a major source of his sense of security; and to some degree, it's a baseline of his human nature, a limitation he can't change." Lin Wan analyzed it as coolly as a psychologist. "And me—I stopped fixating on demanding his 'favoritism' and his 'tenderness,' stopped grinding myself raw over some vague 'emotional value' he probably couldn't give in the first place. Instead, I began to deliberately train myself, until I mastered the ability to 'give' emotional value freely, at will. This isn't fawning. It's a clear-eyed skill."
"I pinpointed what I most fundamentally wanted from this relationship—a stable alliance for living, shared interests in growing together, and a 'shell' to house my worldly life and my feelings. Then everything I said and did began to revolve around that core goal, maintaining and running the relationship in the most low-energy, least self-depleting way. I learned to show up in the posture of 'the one being controlled,' deferring to his opinions in all outward things, giving him all the superiority and the sense of control he wanted. When he let that wealth-based arrogance and need for control show again, I no longer got angry, no longer argued. Instead I'd let my eyes curve into a smile, lean in gently, let him kiss my cheek, and then sweetly, half-joking, play along: 'I know, I know, my big boss. I'll work hard today and earn this much to show you—how's that?'"
[The comments split into two poles of interpretation:]
[This... isn't this just groveling? So lowly!]
[Why does this strike me as top-tier wisdom? Conquering hardness with softness.]
["The ability to give emotional value"—I've learned something. The point isn't to "ask," it's to "give."]
[As long as you can stand on your own. Otherwise it really is just lowly.]
"Others may see this as lowly, as caving." Lin Wan's gaze sharpened—the light that belongs to the one in control. "But only I know clearly that this was never dependence, and certainly never flattery. This is the active choice and command of a clear-eyed strong person. I saw through his hunger for the feeling of power, and I saw clearly the essence of marriage as a partnership institution—a dynamic balance of interests and feelings where each takes what they need. He's satisfied with the feeling of power that comes from being looked up to, being relied on; I get to house my life, my feelings, and my growth. At the deepest level, our needs happen to fit. On the surface it looks like he leads everything, but in fact it's me, steadily holding the rhythm and the direction of this relationship—becoming the true controller of emotion and the one who sets the pace in this marriage."
"This little household is like a miniature society. Our days look plain and ordinary, but they're full of invisible contests and adjustments at the level of the mind. Yet I'm long past panicking over it, past fearing it. I've shed the rawness, the sentimentality, and the unnecessary pride of my younger years, and transformed into someone stronger, clearer, who knows how to hold her own in this complicated reality and get what she needs from it."
She paused, then delivered what was perhaps the sharpest line of the night: "Some say that a good marriage, or any deep growth, often comes with 'killing' the self you used to be. I agree completely. I 'killed' that old self who was always anxious about gaining and losing, who kept losing her dignity and her power in the relationship. Inside this imperfect, even somewhat cold marital contract, Brother Qiang and I work each other into shape—and, to a degree, make something of each other. We no longer have those impractical illusions of love; what we have is the clear-eyed understanding that comes after seeing through reality's rules, and the solid, symbiotic relationship built on it. In the end, we've both grown into versions of ourselves that fit the present better, that move through it more at ease—at least, I have."
