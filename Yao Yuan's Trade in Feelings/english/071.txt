Chapter 71 — Reviewing Five Trips Through Time
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640318.html

Back in the apartment she hadn't seen for days, the familiar surroundings and scent finally let her taut nerves go slack. She didn't touch her work right away; she walked straight to the bathroom.
She filled the tub with hot water, dropped in a few drops of soothing essential oil, and sank herself completely into the warmth. The hot water wrapped around her weary limbs, and it seemed to wash away too what clung to her soul — the wind and sand of the Gobi, the cold, murky chill that time-travel left behind. Her skin, baked for days in the dry desert air, drank in the moisture now and let out a satisfied sigh.
But her thoughts couldn't relax the way her body did.
Leaning against the rim of the tub, with the steam blurring her vision, the images in her mind only grew sharper and more chaotic — and with them came a stubborn, almost obsessive urge to sort them into order.
Fate really was capricious to the point of absurdity.
Time and again she had traveled back into the past, trying to change things, or unwittingly influencing her younger self. Some of those changes looked like they'd corrected certain mistakes, spared her certain pain.
The first time, for instance, she'd reminded her younger self to see clearly the gulf in social standing between Jiang Hai and his family, sparing her the violent agony of sinking deep into love only to be betrayed, humiliated, her dignity trampled. That kind of pain wasn't just the loss of a relationship; it was a profound doubting and denial of her own worth, one of the roots of her sense of "not being good enough" — something she had instinctively suppressed and forgotten, yet which became a thorn buried deep in her heart, finally awakened by the Mirror Pact. Her younger self had pulled out early; there might still have been pain, but nothing that cut to the bone or shadowed a whole life.
But was the "good" that came from changing things really, entirely, good? Her younger self had broken up early, gone abroad to study, and come back — yet still hadn't managed to build a career in the capital. Because of her mother Zhang Fengxia's sudden, serious illness (an illness that, in the present Yao Yuan's memory, her mother had never had), she'd chosen to return to Jinshi. And she seemed not to have stayed with the dance career she'd once loved, turning instead — like Yao Yuan herself — to the gamesmanship of business.
"If fate grants a gift, in secret it takes something away." The line surfaced in her heart like a cold incantation. One love wound avoided — and yet still she'd missed the chance to stay on a larger stage, given up the field she adored, and even made her mother suffer an illness (one that didn't exist in the original timeline). How was one supposed to weigh that ledger of gains and losses? She didn't know which version of a life was truly "better," and which was "worse."
The second time, she'd warned Shuai Hongqiang before his low self-esteem and traditional notions made him flinch away, and her younger self chose to lay her cards on the table and marry. That spared her the old tragedy of aborting the child and fleeing, heartbroken, to Hangzhou. But the ending? The marriage lasted less than a year before it ended in the loss of a six-month-old child, the trauma of possibly never being able to conceive again, and finally divorce all the same.
Was that change better than before? It spared her the physical and emotional agony of the operating table and the humiliation of fleeing far from home — but it brought instead the immense blow of hope in pregnancy and sudden loss, and a brief, failed marriage. The shame-laden thorn lodged deep in the present Yao Yuan's heart — the one named "chosen again, scrutinized again, and in the end discarded by some invisible rule" — perhaps the pain had only changed its shape; it hadn't gone away.
The third time, faced with that scorching but doomed-to-burn-out relationship with Zhuge Jinye, she had made no intervention at all. Her younger self walked the same path she once had, all over again. It looked like nothing changed — but that reckless love, the one that burned everything to ash and scorched them both, was branded just as deeply into the soul on every timeline, an indelible part of her. Whether she changed it or not seemed to make no difference to the nature, or the ending, of that relationship.
The fourth time, she'd warned her younger self before Ding Shitong could treat her as a bargaining chip. The young Yao Yuan made an active choice, using the subtle tug-of-war between Ding Shitong and Duan Yanqing — and their interest in her — to win herself real, tangible advantages and room to maneuver. That change had no doubt spared her younger self the deepest humiliation and spiritual castration the present Yao Yuan had endured: being appraised, traded, and discarded like merchandise, and the spiritual torment of "do I really not deserve a man's pure, true love?"
That was, without question, a positive, major change. And so far, no "bad fruit" had grown from it.
The fifth time, she'd appeared directly in a scene her own life had never passed through — her slightly younger self (at thirty-three) inspecting Zhao Yiming's Western World Technology, which was itself a chain reaction of the change wrought by the fourth trip. Of this "unknown" territory she had no way to warn her, and only at the last moment, on an instinctive wariness about Yu Hao's surname, had she tried to give a warning — without finishing her words. And that timeline's young Yao Yuan seemed not to have suffered any obvious "ill-luck" backlash from the earlier changes; her life still ran along some kind of "normal" track.
And this time, the sixth trip. What she had witnessed was the far greater divergence produced when the fourth and fifth changes stacked on top of each other — her younger self (at thirty-five) had never gone through that "friends-with-benefits" arrangement with Yu Hao, never endured the cold cruelty and hurt of "I only want a child, I don't want marriage," never tasted the disappointment of a fragile bond built on calculated interest snapping all at once. Instead, she'd met a more "ideal" version of Yu Hao — young, ardent, sincere, holding marriage up as his highest commitment — and, with friends and family as witnesses, had happily accepted his proposal.
Seen as this single "present" slice, the change looked flawless, beautiful as a fairy tale. The young Yao Yuan was spared yet another round of the spiritual torment of being "chosen, weighed in the balance" that she herself had lived through, and was striding straight toward the hall of marriage, toward worldly "fulfillment."
So her trips through time really could change some things. That change sometimes triggered unforeseeable side effects (like her mother's illness), sometimes merely swapped one form of pain for another (like the marriage to Shuai Hongqiang), but sometimes it really did dodge some truly fatal spiritual wounds (like escaping being traded away by Ding Shitong) — and might even, possibly, lead toward a seemingly smoother, "happier" path in life (like the engagement to Yu Hao).
Then what about her own pain, all the struggle and trauma and humiliation and scheming and loneliness she'd lived through on this "unchanged," or "not changed enough," timeline? Was it a necessary price? A wrong turn down a blind alley? Or was it simply that, out of countless parallel versions across countless timelines, she just happened to be living in the comparatively "unlucky" one?
And that "Mirror Pact" — the curse that bound her so eerily to Shuai Hongqiang, draining her very spirit to buy fortune for him — on which "version" of her was it founded? On this "original," trauma-soaked timeline? If her younger self were changed enough, were turned onto a wholly different, happier path, then would this "Yao Yuan's" very "origin" change too? Would the Mirror Pact loosen along with it — even vanish?
The more she thought, the more her thoughts tangled like a knotted skein, no thread to grasp. Logic and feeling, hope and emptiness, warred fiercely in her mind. Not even the warm water could drive off the deep bewilderment and chill spreading out from the bottom of her heart.
Whether from too great a drain on her spirit or from water too comfortably warm, an utter exhaustion swallowed her like a rising tide. Her thoughts slowly stalled, and her consciousness sank into a murky darkness.
She had a dream.
The space in the dream was a boundless, ice-cold expanse of pure white, like the most advanced laboratory, like an unknown void.
She "stood" there, and saw, not far off, the backs of six men, gathered in a circle, all facing some huge surface ahead — a mirror, or a display screen, like a movie screen — watching something intently. Blurred light and streams of data flowed across that "screen"; she couldn't quite make out what they showed.
A strong, inexplicable curiosity and unease rose in her, and without meaning to she crept softly closer.
Closer and closer... she could make out their clothes. They all wore identical, spotless white lab coats, like the most elite researchers.
Then she saw their profiles, and several of them turned their heads —
Jiang Hai. His expression held its usual gentle refinement, but his gaze carried the cool, examining scrutiny of a researcher.
Shuai Hongqiang. A merchant's shrewdness in his brow, with a lingering trace of gloom, his eyes complicated.
Zhuge Jinye. The line of his profile sharp, his gaze fervent yet with an obsessive focus, as if staring at his own work upon a stage.
Duan Yanqing. His bearing steady and composed, a faint, almost imperceptible smile of total control at the corner of his mouth, his eyes deep and unreadable.
Yu Hao. On his young, handsome face, an expression mixing curiosity, appraisal, and a faint, hard-to-detect coldness.
Zhao Yiming. He stood a little off to the side, his expression the most focused and calm of all, the eyes behind his lenses sharp as a scalpel, rapidly analyzing the data flowing across the "screen," his fingers tracing unconsciously through empty air as if modeling something.
Then Yao Yuan's gaze went past their shoulders and landed on that huge "screen."
What it showed was, unmistakably, the scene of this very moment in reality — herself, eyes closed, soaking in the warm water of the tub, steam rising, her face weary. It was an image like a live security feed.
A vast horror, the chill of being spied upon, drowned her dream in an instant like ice water!
"Aah—!"
Yao Yuan jolted awake out of the tub, her heart pounding so hard it nearly burst from her chest. The warm water splashed out with her violent movement and wet the floor. She gasped for breath in great heaving gulps and looked around — it was her familiar bathroom, and she was alone. Curling steam, a silent room.
No pure-white laboratory. No six men in lab coats. No giant surveillance screen.
Just a dream. A grotesque, lurid nightmare born of overthinking and crushing mental strain.
But that terror of being watched, being analyzed, as if she were nothing but a set of data or a test subject, lingered with absolute reality, leaving her cold all over — a chill that even soaking in hot water couldn't drive away.
She wrapped her arms around herself. The hot water slowly went cold.
Beyond the window, the night was thick as ink.
And the fog in her heart was deeper, heavier, than the night.
