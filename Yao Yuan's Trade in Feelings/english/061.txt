A Stirring of the Heart
The business was, for now, settled. The dishes arrived right on cue—refined, light, exactly the sort of thing the Yunting kept to. The mood between them eased of its own accord, sliding from the sharp, focused strategy session into the looser, easier chatter of a shared meal.
Yao Yuan cut into the tender perch on her plate and, recalling an earlier thread of conversation, asked offhandedly, "By the way—you mentioned before that the model fusion had a kind of 'rejection response.' Any progress lately? Our 'Emotional Super-Advisor' prototype is still waiting on that stronger 'heart.'"
Zhao Yiming nodded. "I've had a conceptual breakthrough. The key may be that you can't just crudely stitch things together—you have to design dedicated reasoning modules at a deeper level, in the 'cognitive architecture' itself, for highly complex, multi-objective problems like emotional decision-making. It's like…" He thought for a moment, then found his metaphor. "It's not draping a general-purpose robot in a layer of psychology. It's building it, from the very first design choice, as a specialist in handling 'relationships' and 'choices.' That takes more data like the 'Pretty Big One' case library—full of contradictions, trade-offs, and real consequences—to feed it and to validate it. So," he raised his glass toward her, "our partnership matters enormously."
Yao Yuan caught his meaning and tapped her glass lightly against his. "To an AI that understands the human heart a little better." She took a sip, then said, as if in passing, "My brother, Yifan—since he got back from Phuket last time, he keeps talking about how well the two of you clicked. Says he got a lot out of those conversations. This year he applied to a joint-training program at MIT, focusing on early biomarkers for neurodegenerative disease—which I think overlaps with some of the foundational research you did?"
Zhao Yiming's eyes lit up. "Yifan is exceptional. His thinking really cuts through to the core. I know the person who runs that MIT program—if it would help, I can write him a recommendation. And if he ever wants to explore the cutting-edge intersections of brain-computer interfaces or computational neuroscience, the labs and clinical-collaboration network at Western World are open to him anytime, to come exchange ideas and learn."
That easy, genuine concern for Zhou Yifan's future—and the real, concrete help behind it—warmed something in Yao Yuan. He was always like this: steady, considerate, offering warmth in the places you least expected it.
Dessert was a delicate almond tofu, served in little crackle-glazed bowls. The conversation between them slipped, naturally, into more personal territory.
Yao Yuan stirred the gleaming white custard with a small silver spoon, then looked up, seemingly at random, at Zhao Yiming, whose expression had softened across the table. The room's lamplight was warm, catching the clean lines of his profile. She thought of the unfinished conversation on the yacht in Phuket, and something in her stirred.
"Zhao Yiming," she said, setting down the spoon—the first time all evening she had used his full name in such an informal moment. Her voice was low, carrying a pure, purely personal curiosity. "I remember in Phuket you mentioned that relationship of yours at MIT. Since then… have you ever met a 'system' you felt was 'compatible'?"
The question was a touch private, but her tone was candid and her gaze clear, as if she were merely continuing the high-caliber meeting of minds from over dinner—only shifting the topic from business strategy to human nature itself.
Zhao Yiming clearly hadn't expected the conversation to turn here. He blinked, briefly caught off guard, then relaxed and leaned back into his chair, the tip of one finger tapping unconsciously against the smooth tabletop, as if entering some command for thought. He didn't dodge the question; if anything, because it came from Yao Yuan, he treated it with a special seriousness.
"Since then…" He mulled it over for a moment, then shook his head, a trace of self-mockery in his smile. "The first few years of the startup, I was all in on the 'Genesis Engine.' The lab and the Gobi were my entire world—no time, and no inclination, to think about 'compatibility.' Later, once the company found its footing and I met more people, but…" He paused, searching for the precise words. "It seemed to get harder."
He looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze frank, carrying the cool detachment of a researcher dissecting a phenomenon—yet strangely mixed with a faint, hard-to-name wistfulness. "You start to realize that when your world turns more and more 'hardcore'—revolving around algorithms, neuroscience, hyperscale data centers—the people who can truly understand what you're doing, why it excites you, why it makes you anxious, are themselves very few. And among those few, to then find someone who not only understands but resonates, who can even… make you feel, beyond that cold rational code and those models, a completely different dimension of 'attraction' and 'challenge'…"
He stopped without finishing, but the unspoken meaning was already clear. It was the loneliness of standing where the air is too thin at the heights—and an utterly clear-eyed grasp of his own inner needs.
Yao Yuan listened quietly, not interrupting. She understood the feeling. In her own way, in her own field—dissecting emotion, the games of human nature, the workings of capital—she had reached a similar place. Many understood her; few resonated. And the people who could truly make her feel something was "interesting," "a challenge," who could even illuminate certain blind spots in her thinking—those were rarer still.
"So," she said softly, steering the conversation, "your standard now—or rather, what you're hoping for—is it still the same as what you described in Phuket? Someone who can explore the unknown alongside you, who isn't afraid of chaos, who can even create order within the chaos?"
Zhao Yiming's eyes met hers. The absolute rationality from their earlier business talk was gone now, replaced by something deeper, harder to parse. He nodded slowly.
"Yes. But maybe… a little more specific than back then, and a little more… greedy." He leaned slightly forward, and the distance between them seemed, in the soft light, to close invisibly. "After going through some things, seeing some of the world—including a few conversations with you—I'm more and more convinced that what I need may not be a 'compatible' system, but a 'catalyst' that sets off a beneficial mutation. Or a 'collaborator' who can help build out entirely new emergent properties."
His phrasing still carried the flavor of his field, but the emotional core was almost ready to break the surface.
"This person needs to be smart and resilient enough to understand—even take part in—the building of my world, someone who won't shrink back in the face of complexity and difficulty. But at the same time, she has to possess an inner world that's wholly foreign to me, maybe one I could never fully simulate with algorithms—rich, alive. A world with its own operating logic, its own emotional temperature, its own aesthetic system. One that can offer unexpected perspectives and inspiration when I'm too lost in rational deduction; that can remind me, when I'm surrounded by cold code and data, of the parts of human nature that can't be quantified and yet matter most—things like trust, like intuition, like a certain kind of… attraction that goes beyond any utilitarian calculation."
By the end his voice had dropped low, but his gaze stayed locked on Yao Yuan's face—as if, through her bright makeup, her dazzling jewels, even that suit of armor that stood for "Boss Yao," he were examining the soul beneath: complex, powerful, full of contradictory allure, and possessing an "inner world" he was endlessly curious about.
Yao Yuan felt the heart-guarding jade against her chest grow faintly warm for an instant. She held his gaze without flinching, and at the corner of her red lips a smile spread—very faint, but heavy with meaning.
"Sounds like your bar is high," she said, in a tone you couldn't tell was teasing or simply stating fact. "You want both a rational mind to fight shoulder to shoulder with you and a soul-deep emotional warmth that resonates; you want someone who can move with ease inside your rules, and yet possess an 'inner world' you can't fully control… It's almost a paradox."
"It is. A high-difficulty, multi-objective-optimization paradox." Zhao Yiming admitted it, but there wasn't the slightest retreat in his eyes—instead there was that light a person gets facing a top-tier problem, a mix of excitement and certainty. "Which is exactly why I say it's hard. But precisely because it's hard, if I ever truly meet that person, I shouldn't let her slip away lightly. She's worth the greatest sincerity and patience—worth trying to solve this 'paradox,' to see what finally 'emerges.'"
He never said "you are that person," but every word of his description was like a carefully ground prism, and the light it refracted fell, squarely and unmistakably, on Yao Yuan's own qualities. This kind of "expression"—no blunt confession, yet brimming with intellectual admiration and deep inquiry—was more penetrating than any sweet talk, and far better at striking the heartstrings of a woman at Yao Yuan's level.
The room fell quiet for a few seconds, with only the faint sound of wind beyond the window. The air was thick with words left unsaid and a tension growing quietly. It was a most refined ambiguity, built on the highest order of intellectual recognition and respect for character—not loud, not frivolous, yet pressing heavily into every breath, full of infinite possibility and dangerous appeal.
Yao Yuan lowered her lashes, looking at her own blurred reflection in the bowl, then glanced at the warm jade against her chest. On one side, an invitation full of challenge, standing for rational exploration and future possibility; on the other, a curse to be broken, a symbol of fated entanglement and the shadows of the past. And she stood in the middle, pulled both ways.
At last she raised her head and gave him a bright smile—one that put back on a little of the "Boss Yao" social mask, though the faint ripples deep in her eyes had not entirely stilled.
"A very impressive 'user-needs portrait,' Boss Zhao," she said, with a hint of playfulness, deftly using the language of business to pull the nearly overflowing private mood back into the safe zone. "It seems you're not only good at mapping out technical roadmaps—you've got your expectations for a 'partner' charted out just as clearly."
Zhao Yiming laughed too, accepting the line she had just drawn. He understood the measure of advance and retreat.
"Just a deduction grounded in facts." He picked the thread back up easily, lifting his teacup, as if that soul-deep exchange a moment ago had been nothing more than another high-caliber meeting of minds. "And I know the best 'partnership' is never one-sided need being met—it's mutual fit and mutual spark. My 'portrait' is clear, but whether a 'match' is finally possible takes both sides' 'data' to speak, and… time and shared experience to verify."
There was meaning beneath his words, and he left ample room for it, and for the imagination.
Yao Yuan didn't press the topic further. She turned instead to a recent hot-button event in the AI industry, and Zhao Yiming picked it up in perfect step, bringing up the latest energy-optimization figures from the Gobi data center.
And yet, once certain things are said, once certain hopes are laid bare, the air is already different. The emotional undercurrent surging beneath that rational "white paper," woven together with the tacit tension now between them, lent this meeting—originally one with a clear purpose—an entirely different, heart-quickening color.
By the time they left, the night had grown deep, bamboo shadows swaying.
Yao Yuan pulled her cream-white trench coat tighter, the heart-guarding jade at her throat appearing and vanishing at the collar, its warm touch unbroken.
Zhao Yiming walked her to the car and, quite naturally, opened the door for her. As she settled into the driver's seat, he rested his arm lightly against the roof, leaned down, and said gently, "Drive safe. Take your time with the draft—there's no rush."
"All right." Yao Yuan looked up and smiled at him. In the night her eyes shone especially bright under the makeup and the light. "We talked it through thoroughly tonight. Thank you."
Zhao Yiming nodded and stepped back, watching her. "Good night, Yao Yuan."
"Good night." Yao Yuan answered, and started the car.

As she pulled onto the main road, the city's neon flowed by like a river. On the passenger seat, the "Preliminary Risk Assessment and Benefit Analysis White Paper on the 'System Interaction Protocol' (Draft)" lay quietly.
A rational blueprint toward a future business empire and a place in the industry; a warm piece of old jade guarding the mind, holding off an uncanny fate.
Her world was still split between these two stark poles. But tonight, that blueprint was so clear and so tempting, the man who'd drafted it so steady and dependable, that even as she struggled against a chaotic destiny, she saw—more clearly than ever before—a bright road she could open up by her own wits and her own strength.
The shadow of the Mirror Pact was still there. But ahead, it seemed, a stronger light and a more solid ally were beginning to appear.
Her phone screen lit up—a message from Zhao Yiming: "Home now. Any thoughts on the draft, we can talk anytime." Brief, steady, just like the man himself.
Yao Yuan looked at that line of text, glanced again at the folder beside her, and a complicated but genuine smile broke across her bright face. She replied: "Will do. Talk soon."
At that moment, Zhao Yiming's last words about "mutual fit" and "verification over time," and the focused, burning gaze he'd worn while describing that "high-difficulty paradox" of a partner, were printed clearly in her mind.
Rationality and feeling, future and past, a clear protocol and a heart in turmoil… all the contradictions and pulls reached a new peak in this moment. And the direction of the story, because of this dinner—woven through with a top-tier clash of intellect and a deep probing of emotion—grew all the more elusive, and all the more compelling.
Spring was gone; high summer was on its way. Some entanglements may be hard to break free of, but a new chapter, along with this weighty "white paper," had now been solemnly lifted at one corner.
