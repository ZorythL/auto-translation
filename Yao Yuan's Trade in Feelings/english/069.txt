Chapter 69 — Three Days in the Gobi, a Warmth Growing Tender
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640316.html

They stayed in the Gobi for three full days.
For those three days, stripped of every role and obligation the city had imposed on them, time seemed to be stretched and loosened by the desert wind. By day, they went out with the team, immersing themselves in the vastness and desolation of this land.
In the deepest dark before dawn, wrapped in heavy blankets, they climbed a dune to wait for the desert sunrise. They watched a thin line of crab-shell green seep out of the inky black at the horizon, then deepen into orange-red and gold dust, until at last a round, blazing red sun tore itself free of the horizon and flung its boundless light and heat across the rolling sea of sand. In that moment the world fell solemn and still, and all things woke; the golden grains of sand seemed to catch fire, glittering with hundreds of millions of tiny points of light. Quite naturally, Zhao Yiming took Yao Yuan's hand, and the two of them stood shoulder to shoulder. Neither said a word, only quietly took in this primal, surging burst of life.
At dusk they went again, this time to watch the long river and the setting sun. They drove to a high, open vantage point; below them lay a dry ancient riverbed, winding away like the skeleton of some giant dragon. The clouds in the western sky had burned into a boundless ocean of fire, soaking the sinking sun in a light both tender and tragically grand. "Lone smoke rises straight in the great desert; round sets the sun over the long river," Zhao Yiming murmured the old line of verse. Yao Yuan followed his gaze. In the distance, a single ruler-straight column of smoke—she could not tell whether it was cooking smoke or a pillar of dust—rose silently into the crimson sky, and together with that huge, perfect, blood-red sun slowly slipping behind the ochre mountains on the horizon, it formed a picture that matched the poem exactly, a scene that stilled the very soul. The afterglow of the setting sun warmed the fair skin of her cheeks too, and Zhao Yiming turned his head to look at her, feeling that the woman before him was more captivating than this timeless landscape.
They tasted the local honeydew melons and ginseng-fruit, sweet as honey, brimming with juice and carrying the strong, sun-soaked scent of the place. They rode gentle camels too, following the camel-herders deep into the heart of the sand sea, listening to the camel bells jingle and watching the curves of the dunes shift into soft, graceful ripples under the light. When the wind passed, fine sand slid down the ridges like flowing water, with the faintest hiss, as if the earth itself were breathing.
At night, they still often went to look at the stars. Sometimes right by the camp, sometimes Zhao Yiming would drive her somewhere farther from the light pollution. The starry sky was the same every night, and yet brand new every night. What they talked about, too, drifted from the technology and business of those first conversations into far broader country—funny stories from childhood, the hardships of their student years, vague hopes for the future, even certain fragile moments buried deep in the heart and never spoken of to anyone. Beneath an absolutely silent sky of stars, beside the warmth of a body growing slowly familiar, both speaking and listening became remarkably natural. Catalyzed by the magnificent scenery and the small daily sharings, their feelings warmed quickly and grew sweeter by the day. A single glance, an accidental touch, each carried an unspoken intimacy. Yet they both held to their original "agreement," never letting this private closeness bleed into any work discussion; in front of the team, they remained the professional, reliable "Boss Yao" and "Boss Zhao."
On the morning of the fourth day, the team set out to return to Jinshi.
The convoy drove home along the expressway that cut dead straight across the Gobi. The novelty and anticipation of the journey out had turned, on the way back, into contentment and a faint weariness. Yao Yuan and Zhao Yiming rode in the same car; she sat in the front passenger seat while he drove himself. In the back sat one of Yao Yuan's assistants and one of Zhao Yiming's engineers. At first the four of them chatted about all they had seen these past days, but the talk gradually died away, and the assistant and the engineer began to doze with their eyes closed.
Yao Yuan was a little tired too, though still alert enough. She leaned back into the comfortable seat, watching the seemingly endless Gobi landscape rush backward past the window, replaying fragments of these past days in her mind—the cold database, the blazing campfire, the magnificent starry sky, Zhao Yiming's focused profile, the dry warmth of his palm, and those moments under the stars that made her face flush and her heart race… Without realizing it, the corners of her mouth curved into the faintest, gentlest arc.
The car began to enter the mountains, and the road grew winding. Ahead lay the famous Wushaoling tunnel cluster, one long tunnel after another bored through the body of the mountains, slicing the journey into alternating stretches of light and dark.
The air conditioning was on inside the car, and the light dimmed abruptly as they entered a tunnel, leaving only the dashboard and the central screen giving off a faint blue glow. A wave of drowsiness washed over Yao Yuan too; she shifted her position, ready to take a short rest. Just as she closed her eyes and her awareness was about to sink into a haze, the car drove into yet another very long tunnel.
The lights inside the tunnel were a monotonous warm yellow, streaming swiftly backward and forming a regular flow of light and shadow inside the car. For some reason, she had not fallen fully asleep; half-dreaming, half-awake, she instinctively tilted her head slightly, her gaze drifting absently across the rearview mirror on the right.
In the mirror, the image that should have reflected the cars behind and the tunnel walls suddenly twisted and rippled—as if a huge boulder had been thrown into a calm surface of water!
Yao Yuan's breath caught sharply; all her drowsiness vanished.
An instant later, that mirror surface seemed to become a bottomless whirlpool, and a familiar, soul-trembling, overwhelming pull surged in without the slightest warning!
She wanted to grab hold of something, but her fingers were limp and weak. The heart-guarding jade at her breast blazed scorching hot in an instant, as if it would sear her skin, and a gentle yet powerful force tried to drag her back—
The pulling lasted only a brief second or two, yet to Yao Yuan it felt as long as a century.
She felt the whirlpool abruptly swallow her whole! The scorching heat of the heart-guarding jade was instantly cut off, receding far away. Zhao Yiming, the echo of the engine inside the tunnel, the hum of the car's air conditioning… every real sound and sensation was rapidly drawn away and dissolved.
She lost consciousness and sank into a faint.
