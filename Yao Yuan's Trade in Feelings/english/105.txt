Breaking the Mirror Pact at White Horse Rapids; Sinking the Mirror in the Yellow River to Sever Cause and Effect
The Year of the Snake, the third day of the twelfth lunar month, the hour of the Rooster — five o'clock in the afternoon.
Yao Yuan and Zhao Yiming set out early, driving to the Guangu Hall to pick up Old Chen. The three of them headed west along the riverside road. One by one the city lights fell away behind them, the houses thinned out, and the cold, desolate winter countryside spread open all around. The sky hung low and leaden, and the mighty Yellow River ran quietly below their eyes — like a faded old length of silk, wrapped in the harsh, dull chill unique to a northern twelfth month, running silently across heaven and earth.
They were the first to reach White Horse Rapids.
The parking spot was still a hundred-odd meters of dirt track from the riverbank. The moment they pushed the doors open and climbed out, a bitter north wind poured into their collars. The wind by the Yellow River was unlike the wind in the city. It came sweeping down all the way from the upstream gorges, carrying fine shards of ice and spray off the river's surface, finding every gap, piercing through layer after layer of clothing like countless needles of ice driven into the skin. Yao Yuan tugged the hood of her fur coat down further, until her whole face was nearly buried in the thick, plush collar, leaving only her eyes. In the dim daylight those eyes looked unusually clear, carrying the stillness of someone keeping an appointment.
Zhao Yiming wore a long black down coat, his hood likewise drawn up. He first carefully tightened Old Chen's scarf against the wind, then turned to Yao Yuan and reached out to take her gloved hand in a steady grip. The warmth of his palm came through the leather, calm and certain.
Old Chen stood by the car in a navy, dark-patterned Chinese-style padded gown with a buttoned front, a black camel-down vest over it, a fleece-lined cotton cap pressed down to his brow, his scarf half-covering his jaw. This seventy-year-old appraiser of antiquities looked like a man who had walked straight out of an older age — heavily wrapped, yet standing perfectly straight. His gaze went toward the not-distant Yellow River, and in his eyes lay the settled depth of one long used to watching the years roll by.
"Let's go," Old Chen said.
The three of them set off, treading across the frozen, rock-hard earth toward the riverbank. The ground beneath their feet was cracked and uneven, and across the whole stretch the withered grass had been frozen by the deep cold into brittle, charred black stalks. With every step came a small, crisp crunch. White Horse Rapids in the dead of the twelfth month was all bleakness and lifeless silence. Here the hills on both banks drew suddenly inward, brute-forcing the wide channel into a narrow, treacherous gorge. The hills were a gray-brown drained of all life — bare rock, not a single blade of grass, desolation taken to its extreme. The whole riverbank was a sloping jumble of stone, blue-gray boulders of every size strewn from the bank down to the waterline, their surfaces glazed with thin ice that gave off a hard, cold gleam in the dim dusk.
Here the Yellow River had shed every trace of its gentle, placid temper and bared its full violence. Where the channel narrowed the current turned suddenly fierce; the muddy yellow water carried ice floes that had not yet frozen solid and slammed them against the reefs packed thick along the riverbed. The roar of it never let up, setting the whole valley humming. The waves struck the rocks hard, bursting into spray ten feet high, sending mist flying across the sky that fell again as fine flecks of ice, soaking the entire bank in a bone-deep wet cold.
The three of them stood alone on the frozen riverside, like three stumps of dead wood lost in the open wilderness, keeping watch over a vast emptiness amid all the cold.
Before long, from the dirt track behind them, footsteps came again.
It was Shuai Hongqiang and his wife Lin Wan.
The two of them had kept vigil outside the ICU for a full day and night, worn out in body and mind. Only around noon that day, after the hospital director had taken the lead and specialists from several departments had held a joint consultation, did they barely manage to stabilize Shuai Wenyao's vital signs and pull him temporarily out of the most dangerous stage — only stabilized, with recovery still nowhere in sight. Lin Wan had asked her parents to rush to the hospital to keep watch in the meantime, then hurried off with Shuai Hongqiang to make the appointment.
Neither of them had had a chance to change clothes; they were still in what they'd worn when the accident happened the day before, the fabric badly creased, smeared with dried blood and dust. Shuai Hongqiang's hair was a wreck, heavy shadows hung beneath his eyes, ragged stubble had sprouted along his jaw, and the whole spirit of the man had collapsed — he radiated utter exhaustion and ruin.
Lin Wan's face was just as pale and haggard, yet she had forced her hair into some kind of order, revealing a thin, cool face. Her once gentle, clear eyes were deeply sunken and red-rimmed, brimming with the weariness of a sleepless night and a grief cut to the bone — and yet, deep in those eyes, there was a clarity unlike anyone else's. It was the instinctive self-possession wrung out of her after extreme pain had ground over her, the lucid sanity a mother holds onto by the sheer conviction that she cannot fall, cannot break.
Yao Yuan was the first to step forward to meet them. Her gaze rested briefly on Shuai Hongqiang's haggard, disheveled face, then turned to Lin Wan. Her tone was gentle and low, carrying over the howling wind: "How is the child?"
Shuai Hongqiang's throat trembled. He opened his mouth, but not a single sound came out. Lin Wan answered, her voice hoarse and dry but unusually steady: "Stable for now. Still under observation in the ICU."
A single short sentence, with nothing more added. The brutal injuries, the night-long ordeal, the gnawing dread — all of it had long passed beyond what language could describe. Extreme pain has never needed dressing up. Yao Yuan nodded gently and did not pile on superfluous words of comfort. She knew well that, in the depths of despair, all pity and pleasantries were pale and useless.
This was the first time Yao Yuan and Lin Wan had formally met. Two years earlier, she had once happened to glimpse Shuai Hongqiang and his family of three at a shopping mall: the Shuai Hongqiang of that time was gentle and considerate, the woman at his side soft and serene, the young child lively and bright. In that moment a small ripple had stirred in her heart — so the home he had finally chosen was the world's standard picture of a virtuous wife and good mother, gentle and steady, the kind suited to making a home, the complete opposite of her own flashy, sharp-edged, never-back-down self.
Now, face to face up close, Yao Yuan finally saw Lin Wan clearly. She was slight and small, and even wrapped in a heavy down coat she still gave off an air of fragility. Her face was full of exhaustion, pain hidden in her eyes, yet none of it could mask the refined, scholarly grace in her bones. Even in such a wretched, desperate state, even after being struck so hard, the decorum and sense of order she had drilled into herself over many years was still carved into her bones, and had not collapsed.
A complicated, hard-to-name tide of feeling churned in Yao Yuan's heart. Half of it was the subtle comparison between women. In their younger years, the verdict Shuai Hongqiang had implied — "you're too beautiful, too bright, too headstrong; bring you home and there'd be no holding you down" — echoed softly now, after all these years. And the woman before her was gentle, soft, like a quiet plant. That old wound of being weighed, picked over, and finally cast aside as "not the kind you marry" was lightly touched at this most inopportune of moments. Very lightly — but it was real.
The other half was a deeper empathy, woman for woman, a sense of kinship. Watching the tense line of Lin Wan's shoulders, the fingertips she clenched and released over and over, the way she choked down her grief, Yao Yuan understood this gritted-teeth strength all too well. A truly strong woman has only ever been able to plant her own feet by force of will, to steady her spirit atop the ruins and face the storm head-on.
At the same time, Lin Wan was quietly sizing up Yao Yuan too.
Until now, Yao Yuan had been, to her, merely a hugely controversial symbol in the online world of relationship content — a wildly divisive, sharply distinctive emotional livestreamer adored by countless people and reviled by just as many. She had watched clips of her broadcasts: calm, sharp, every word drawing blood, with a cold beauty to the way she dissected love and laid it out for display. She admired her clarity, and had privately studied her techniques of expression. Between peers in the same field, there had always been this complicated kind of attention.
But now, seeing her in the flesh, this woman who lived inside the lens of the internet was real, vivid, wrapped in costly fur, beautiful in a way that was almost aggressive. The exquisite face, the noble dress — even set down in the coarse, barren surroundings of a Yellow River bank, her presence was still extraordinary. And she was, of all things, Shuai Hongqiang's ex-girlfriend. The other party to the pact made over that uncanny bronze mirror. The woman who, these past two years, had been bound tight to her own husband's fate, sharing the most secret of alliances.
In Lin Wan's heart there was none of the jealousy, hostility, or resentment she might have expected. After a night of extreme torment she was already drained, body and soul, with no strength left to summon up those "wife" emotions. All that remained was an endless sense of absurdity — fate was like a net woven at random, dragging utterly unrelated people into the same whirlpool. And this woman, in a certain sense, was one of the net's victims too.
"This is Zhao Yiming, my fiancé." Yao Yuan spoke up at the right moment, breaking the silence, turning slightly to introduce the man at her side, her tone natural and open.
Zhao Yiming gave a slight nod. His gaze was steady and restrained; he did not let himself study the two of them freely. His voice was gentle and measured: "Take care of yourselves. The child will be all right."
Shuai Hongqiang nodded numbly, his mind in turmoil. But Lin Wan started slightly — fiancé.
Learning that Yao Yuan had long since found a home of her own, that thin, inexplicable comparison and grudge in her heart quietly dissolved. So it was that everyone drifts and sinks through this mortal world, each enduring their own storms, each chasing their own peace; no one is spared, no one has it easy.
Yao Yuan's gaze fell back on Lin Wan's face, and a flicker of recognition crossed her eyes. She suddenly asked, "Haven't I seen you somewhere? You wouldn't happen to be Lin Wan, the host of the 'Gentle Awakening' livestream room?"
Lin Wan was a little surprised. She hadn't expected Yao Yuan to recognize her, even if they were both in relationship content. But on second thought, it made sense. People who are genuinely good at this always come onto their peers' radar. She nodded, though the weariness on her face and the grief in her eyes were still heavy: "I see you online often too — I watch your content. You're very good."
That remark held no pleasantries, no rivalry; it was pure recognition and appreciation between peers.
Hearing it, the corner of Yao Yuan's mouth curved up faintly. "So are you."
Three simple words — recognition, understanding, the confirmation that comes in the instant of glimpsing one's own kind amid this vast human world, this rolling muddy current. There on the Yellow River bank, with the cold wind howling, two women who had never met yet whose fates had been woven together by the same man and the same absurd catastrophe held no hostility, no contest — only the subtle kinship of like souls who, standing in dire straits and knowing well how hard life is, read each other clearly. Very faint — but real.
Just then, from the dirt track in the distance, came slow footsteps again.
Everyone turned toward the sound, and saw old Mr. Wang Baoguo, descendant of the mirror masters, being carefully supported as he walked slowly forward by a middle-aged man. As the two drew near, Yao Yuan, Zhao Yiming, Shuai Hongqiang, and Lin Wan all froze — the one supporting Old Wang was, of all people, that banker friend they all knew, Wang Qian.
Wang Qian wore a heavy mountaineering down jacket and a knit cap, his face flushed red by the cold wind. Facing the four pairs of eyes turned in unison upon him, his expression was a little awkward, but he still spoke up of his own accord, his voice tight in the wind: "Wang Baoguo is my father. I... didn't mean to hide it from you."
The riverbank fell suddenly silent.
Only the roar of the surging Yellow River filled the emptiness of the whole valley.
Yao Yuan looked at Wang Qian and remembered how strange his manner had been at their last few gatherings — words half-spoken, then swallowed. So there had been signs of it all along. He had recognized the heart-guarding jade that Old Chen had given her, knew she was searching so painstakingly for a descendant of the mirror masters, and yet had said nothing, only watching coldly from the side.
Behind his glasses, Zhao Yiming's gaze was calm; he was swiftly reconstructing every detail of his past dealings with Wang Qian.
Shuai Hongqiang, for his part, went stiff all over and stared at Wang Qian in chilled astonishment. So his desperate turnaround two years ago, that single greedy, obsessive impulse, the calamity that had wrecked his home — from beginning to end, this friend he had known for years had known and stood by watching, had even egged it on, pushed it forward.
And Lin Wan watched the scene in silence, a deeper, heavier chill welling up inside her — so everything her family had suffered had been seeded years ago, and they, like chess pieces, had walked all unknowing to this day.
No one spoke up to demand answers; no one raised a voice to accuse. Yet, intangibly, the old friendship, trust, and unspoken understanding had already shattered with a crash. Some concealments, once exposed, can never be repaired; from then on, whenever they meet, a gulf will forever lie in their hearts — "you knew all along, and stood by watching."
Wang Qian read the distance and disappointment in everyone's silence. His lips moved; he wanted to explain, but in the end swallowed it all. The crack had already formed; words were pale, and no amount of argument could mend anything.
Old Chen stepped forward, and he and Wang Baoguo nodded to each other in greeting. One was an appraiser who knew old objects through and through and glimpsed their hidden workings; the other was a descendant of the mirror masters who carried on a secret art and guarded its taboos. The two old men, reunited on the bleak Yellow River bank, met each other's eyes, and in both lay the heavy fatedness of "so it has come to this after all."
"Old Wang, can the pact-breaking rite begin now?" Shuai Hongqiang could hold back no longer; an anxious tremor showed in his voice. He didn't want to wait a single second more, as if a moment's delay would let that unseen backlash snatch away the last spark of life from his son in the ICU.
Wang Baoguo raised his eyes to him. His murky but piercing gaze swept lightly over the man's worn-out, panicked face, and his brow knit slightly: "Something happened at home?"
The question was like a needle, piercing the composure Shuai Hongqiang had forced himself to hold. His shoulders dropped abruptly; he buried his face in his hands, and from between his fingers spilled a broken, sob-choked voice: "My son... Wenyao... last night he was setting off fireworks, and got hit point-blank by a Gatling firework... severe head and face injuries, ruptured eyeball, intracranial bleeding... he's still in the ICU, still not out of danger... and my daughter, a while back she broke her leg skiing..."
He was incoherent, but his meaning was clear. Old Chen and Wang Baoguo, who hadn't known, exchanged a glance, and a heavy "just as we thought" showed in their eyes. The backlash of the Mirror Pact never misses.
Wang Qian drew a sharp breath, his face going white in an instant, his heart full of guilt. He opened his mouth, wanting to offer a few words of comfort, only to find that any language was pale and powerless at this moment. He could only give Shuai Hongqiang's shoulder a heavy pat.
Yao Yuan and Zhao Yiming had already learned of it the night before, but hearing Shuai Hongqiang recount it again in such a broken state still sent a jolt of unease through them. It was not only sympathy for what a child had suffered, but a stark dread before the word "cause-and-effect" made flesh. It was formless and without substance, yet precise and cold, always striking hardest where you are most fragile.
Through it all, only Lin Wan stood in silence. She watched her broken-down husband, watched the others with their varied expressions, like a bystander calm to the point of cruelty. But Yao Yuan understood: that was not indifference. It was a mother, after the most extreme pain, drawing all her life-force inward into an energy-saving state, spending it only on "holding onto her clarity" and "solving the problem." Crying out, complaining, collapsing — all of these cost energy, and right now she could not waste a shred of it. It all had to be saved for the son lying in the hospital, for the pact-breaking rite before her that bore on the whole future.
A surge of respect for Lin Wan rose in Yao Yuan's heart. True strength has never been about never breaking down — it is about staying clear-headed atop the ruins and still doing what must be done.
Wang Baoguo drew back his gaze, asked nothing more, and lifted his eyes to the horizon.
The hour of the Rooster had arrived in full. The last ray of the setting sun sank behind the western hills, leaving on the horizon only a thick, murky layer of orange-gray, like congealed old blood. The river valley darkened swiftly; lead-colored clouds pressed low over the peaks; the cold wind grew ever more biting, rolling ice flecks off the river's surface as it raged across the wilderness.
"The hour has come. Set the array and break the pact." The old man pronounced it in a deep voice, motioning for Yao Yuan to bring out the bronze mirror.
Yao Yuan did as told, taking from her handbag a dark cloth pouch. She untied the knot and lifted out the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror. In the dim dusk its face glowed a deep, dull yellow; on its back, the Kui-dragon relief coiled and wound, still clear and sharp after a hundred years of erosion, faintly exuding an eerie solemnity. The bronze mirror was icy and heavy in her hands, like holding a slab of time and fate frozen for a century.
Wang Baoguo took from his own cloth pouch a slender silver needle wrapped in red cloth, and produced a small porcelain dish. He looked first at Yao Yuan: "Left index finger."
Yao Yuan pulled off her glove and held out her left hand. Wang Baoguo gripped her fingertip, and the silver needle pricked it in a flash. A bead of crimson blood welled up and dripped into the porcelain dish. Then it was Shuai Hongqiang's turn; his fingers were shaking badly, so Wang Baoguo held them firmly and pricked his finger the same way. Last, the old man pricked his own fingertip.
Three drops of blood, belonging to the three parties of the Mirror Pact: Shuai Hongqiang, who had taken on the "shifted fortune"; Yao Yuan, the one who "traveled back through time"; and Wang Baoguo, descendant of the pact's maker and executor of its breaking. Three threads of crimson blended faintly in the white porcelain dish, mirroring the dim sky, glaring and stark.
Wang Baoguo dipped the silver needle into the merged drop of blood and dabbed it carefully into the sunken hollows of the Kui-dragon's eyes on the mirror's back. The warm blood slowly soaked along the ancient patterns, spreading, seeping into the fine pores of the object. The blood-stained patterns gave off a moist, dark red glow in the gloom, like a wound just scabbed over, silently sealing away two years of entangled cause and effect.
Once the blood had congealed slightly, Wang Baoguo brought out a square of coarse crimson cloth. The color was deep and blazing; on a riverbank all gray and yellow and bleak, it was like a leaping tongue of bright flame. The cloth was thick and rough, steeped through with cinnabar by ancient method and aired in the sun again and again, giving off on the wind a faint, bitter medicinal scent.
The old man laid the bronze mirror in the center of the red cloth and began, layer by layer, to wrap it carefully. His movements were slow and steady; with each layer he smoothed the cloth flat, pressed the corners tight, left not the slightest gap. Layer after layer of red folded over it, and the outline of the bronze mirror gradually vanished, until at last it became a thick, heavy, perfectly square red bundle — as if this alone could shut out all the uncanny aura and the entanglements of fate.
Nine layers. Nine is the number of the utmost, signifying sealing and severing. It was an ending, a cutting-off of every connection.
Wang Baoguo cradled that heavy mass of red and walked toward the river. The others followed in silence. The spot the old man had chosen was where White Horse Rapids ran swiftest, where the whirlpools were most pronounced. The stones underfoot were jagged, glazed with slick thin ice. The Yellow River churned and roared before them, its yellow waves smashing against the reefs, shattering into a sky full of spray laced with ice flecks that struck their faces, cold to the bone.
At the water's edge Wang Baoguo set out a few simple offerings of dried fruit and pastries and lit three slender sticks of incense. The pale smoke was no sooner rising than the violent wind tore it apart and scattered it. The old man bowed three times toward the Yellow River, his mouth chanting an ancient incantation of strange syllables and desolate cadence — a language no one could understand, like a secret tongue that had conversed for a thousand years with this land, this river, these jagged stones.
The chant finished, he carefully bound the red bundle to a fist-sized blue-gray stone and tied a dead knot. Then the old man stepped back two paces and motioned to Shuai Hongqiang: "You do it. It began with you; it ends with you."
A great shock ran through Shuai Hongqiang's heart, and he understood at once. This absurd disaster had begun with one greedy, obsessive impulse of his, and it was only right that he bring it to a close with his own hands, sever it with his own hands.
He stepped forward two paces and took the heavy red bundle steadily in both hands. The bitterness of cinnabar mingled with the raw cold of the river water rushed into his face, weighing down on his chest. He moved onto the great boulder at the very edge of the water; beneath his feet the waves struck the rock, trembling faintly, the roar of the water flooding both his ears.
He looked back once. Lin Wan stood a few steps away, watching him in silence, no expression on her face, only a bottomless weariness and calm. That gaze sent a sharp pain through his chest, yet strangely it also gave him a last shred of strength. He turned back, faced the surging Yellow River, drew a deep breath of the cold, fishy-smelling air, and with all his might hurled the red bundle in his hands toward the center of the river, to where the whirlpool ran fastest.
Shuai Hongqiang drew his gaze back, turned to face the endlessly surging Yellow River, breathed in a lungful of the icy, bone-piercing spray, gathered his strength and raised his arm, and flung the red bundle in his hands fiercely into the swiftest whirlpool at the center of the channel.
The bright red bundle flashed once in the muddy yellow waves, was swiftly swallowed by the churning water, and sank into the dark depths, drawn into the unseen undercurrents. Only the bound stone dragged it down, plunging toward the deeps of the Yellow River, toward those secrets buried under silt for a thousand years.
The wind paused for an instant; the howl of the waves went on.
Shuai Hongqiang stood frozen at the bank, staring blankly at the surging water, his mind in a daze. As if he could not believe that the nightmare that had entangled him for two years, the uncanny pact for which he had finally paid such a brutal price, had just like that, so simply and so completely, been swallowed whole by the rolling Yellow River.
Just like that? Just like that... it was over?
And then, as everyone's thoughts rose and sank in private reflection, the change struck without warning.
Yao Yuan, who had been leaning against Zhao Yiming's side, watching that streak of red vanish into the Yellow River's whirlpool, went suddenly rigid. She felt a pair of huge hands seize her soul and clench down hard, and a familiar wrenching-away followed with no warning at all — that sensation more violent, more complete than all nine times before, as if something were being torn up by the roots from the very bottom of her life as that bronze mirror sank.
The Yellow River, the jumbled stones, the figures of the people in her sight all began to twist, stretch, and spin, like a scroll wantonly crumpled by a giant hand. Zhao Yiming's frantic cry receded layer by layer in her ears, growing faint and unreal: "Yuanyuan?!"
The last shred of consciousness she had left was of falling into a solid, warm embrace, with that familiar, reassuring scent lingering at the tip of her nose. And then darkness swallowed all awareness completely.
The Yellow River still surged on without end, its waves roaring, the valley vast and dim, as if nothing at all had happened.
Only the few people standing at the bank, and that woman gone limp in her fiancé's arms, as if she had sunk into sleep.
The old pact has sunk; is cause and effect severed? No one knows.
The mighty river says nothing, only carries a thousand years of silt and flows silently east, hiding away every unknown and every answer.
