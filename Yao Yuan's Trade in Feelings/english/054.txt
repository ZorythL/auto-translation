But the equity structure of this new company, my share of the profits, my decision-making power in the future… none of these specifics has he ever discussed clearly. He just says, 'Let's get it going first, I won't shortchange you,' and 'Talking about this stuff between us would only hurt our feelings.' Teacher Yuanyuan, should I agree? Spend three years using 'the debt of training' and 'a secret affair'—inputs of almost zero economic cost—to lock down a capable, loyal subordinate-slash-lover who's emotionally dependent on him. The value you create far exceeds your salary, but he writes it off with 'feelings' and 'the future.'"
"Phase two: risk transfer and deep binding. The new business is risky? Make you the legal rep.
What is a legal representative? It's the company's legal 'face,' and when something goes wrong, the first-in-line 'shield'—even the 'scapegoat.' Tax problems, debt disputes, lawsuits—the legal rep is the first party held responsible."
"And what price did he pay to make you the legal rep? One feather-light word, 'trust,' and a blank check called 'I won't shortchange you,' deferred indefinitely. Equity? The profit split? Decision-making power? An exit mechanism? Risk protection? All of these—the core terms that make up a 'partnership agreement'—are completely blank!"
"This isn't 'entrusting you with an important role' at all—this is an invitation to a 'contract of indenture' that carries enormous risk and grants zero rights. What he wants you to do is use your entire personal credit and future legal exposure to guarantee the development of his new business, for free. If the business succeeds, how much you get is whatever he decides; if the business fails, or anything goes wrong, you're the one standing at the very front taking the blow. And you don't even have a piece of paper that says 'how much you'll be compensated after taking the blow.'"
"Phase three—and this is the point to be most wary of," Yao Yuan's eyes turned suddenly sharp, "he may take the initiative to suggest that you have a child for him."

"So, you ask whether you should agree?" Yao Yuan sat up straight, the V-neck of her wine-red shirt cutting a sharp line under the cold light. "My answer is: absolutely do not accept the current terms! This isn't a question of whether to cooperate—it's a question of being roped, with 'feelings' and 'trust,' and pushed toward the edge of a cliff."
"But this may well be your opportunity too." Her tone shifted, her eyes sharp as a blade. "This is the first time he's put the matter of 'needing you to carry a critical risk' out in the open. "Now, what I'm giving you isn't a 'what to do'—it's a 'three-step negotiation and self-rescue plan' you must execute:"
"Step one: a revolution in mindset—from 'a subordinate-and-lover waiting to be placed' to 'a potential partner entering with core assets.'
Stop all the inner churn of 'will he shortchange me or not,' immediately. Your value isn't decided by his conscience—it's decided by your irreplaceable business ability, the resources you may control, and the legal-rep risk you're about to take on for him. This negotiation isn't begging for charity; it's bargaining over the terms of a business partnership."
"Step two: prepare your 'partnership offer'—turn every vague promise into ironclad legal text.
Go and talk to him, but it must be in a formal, private setting, in a fully businesslike tone. You can put it like this:
'Boss [X], I've thought carefully about the matter of being the new company's legal rep. Thank you for your trust—this is both a responsibility and a major personal risk for me. So that we can build this business steadily over the long term, there are some foundational terms of cooperation that need to be settled before the company is registered. That's how we look out for each other, too.'
Then lay out your list of conditions directly (be sure to consult a lawyer on the framework first):
1. Equity and profit-sharing: nail down your shareholding percentage in the new company (set according to your business contribution and the risk you bear—don't be shy), and write it into the company's articles of association. Nail down your right to dividends and your decision-making power (at minimum, a veto on major matters).
2. Compensation and incentives: beyond a base salary, you must agree on a clear, transparent profit-sharing scheme tied to performance.
3. Risk isolation: for the extra legal liability that may arise from serving as legal rep, the company or he personally must provide a corresponding guarantee or compensation mechanism (for example, signing a compensation agreement, buying directors' liability insurance, and so on).
4. Going public with the relationship, and security (optional but important): if you want to resolve the uncertainty in the relationship, this is the best moment. You can propose: 'So we can strive together more steadily, could we consider making the relationship public and making some plans for the future?' Watch his reaction. If he dodges, you should give up entirely on the 'emotional partnership' part and focus on negotiating the business side well."
"Step three: set your bottom line, and be fully prepared to 'walk if it falls apart.'
During the negotiation, watch his reactions. If he flies into a rage at your list of terms, brushes it off, or keeps making empty promises, then the answer is crystal clear: he has never seen you as an equal partner. To him you're just a useful, cheap 'tool' he can keep squeezing.

1. Inventory your core abilities and resources: the business experience, client relationships, and industry insight you've built up over these six years are your greatest wealth.
