Marriage Washes Off the Gloss; The Rest of Your Life Takes Root in Yourself
"Good evening, everyone. Welcome to 'The Gentle Crossing.' I'm Lin Wan."
Tonight's stream felt different. The ring light had been dimmed a touch. Lin Wan wasn't wearing her usual silk blouse or knit sweater, but a loose, off-white linen robe, her short hair soft and tousled. There was no delicate teacup in front of her, only a glass of plain water. On the bookshelf behind her, the potted paulownia tree had been moved to the edge of the frame. In its place stood a short sprig of tender bamboo in a plain white porcelain vase, and a single white banksia rose just beginning to wilt, its petals curling faintly at the edges.
The comments scrolled by gently, tinged with curiosity and a quiet calm:
[Wanwan looks so plain and elegant tonight]
[This mood feels like she's about to tell a really deep story]
[The bamboo and the flower — do they mean something?]
"Tonight, we won't talk tactics, and we won't analyze power structures." Lin Wan's voice was even calmer than usual, like a stream slipping over pebbles late at night — unhurried, yet carrying its own quiet strength. "I want to talk with you about what marriage, that long and arduous journey, ultimately leaves behind in us — about the shape life reworks us into."
She paused for a moment, her gaze drifting tenderly over that sprig of green bamboo, then settling on the wilting flower.
"Let me start with a story from my own life." Lin Wan's voice was very soft, as if she were sharing a secret. "Brother Qiang has a business partner he's known for many years — they're close, so our two families spend a lot of time together. His wife — I've always called her Sister Man — just turned forty this year. In Sister Man's dressing room, one whole wall is hung with haute couture gowns for every occasion. In her jewelry box lie diamonds and jade that many women only dream of. On her vanity, everything is top-tier skincare — the kind you have to book an appointment for, none of it cheap. Just last month, she finished the latest anti-aging facial treatment. Her skin is firm and luminous, in such good condition it makes you envious."
"Whenever there's an important dinner, Sister Man slips into the newest dress, spritzes on a limited-edition perfume, and wears that flawless, perfectly measured smile, gracefully working the room beside her husband. She's the acknowledged 'perfect wife' of that circle — elegant, polished, as if time and worry had never touched her."
"So many people tell her, 'Sister Man, you're a real winner at life. At forty you're living more vividly than you did at twenty. You married well, you'll never have a care in the world — how enviable.' Sister Man always smiles, raises her glass with grace, and answers in a light, teasing tone, 'Is that so? Take a look at me, then — am I still like a young girl?'"
At this point Lin Wan let out a soft sigh — a sigh that held no judgment, only deep understanding.
"But perhaps only those who've gotten close enough can glimpse, through the cracks in that perfect smile, a hint of something else. It's the kind of homeless panic that quietly wells up only in the dead of night, when she's taken off all her makeup and faces a bedroom that's gorgeous yet empty."
Her tone grew calmer still, as if she were tracing the tangled threads behind an exquisite piece of embroidery:
"This is the fifteenth year of Sister Man's marriage. She has gotten, by the world's standards, much of what a woman might dream of in a marriage: a life of comfort beyond most people's reach, the freedom of never having to scramble for a living, more luxury goods than she could ever wear, an endless supply of the most cutting-edge care. She lives in a vast floor-through apartment overlooking the city's night skyline, has a driver when she goes out and a housekeeper for the chores — she's become the very template of what so many people imagine 'marrying well' to be."
"But what she lost — or rather, what was traded away and worn down in the process of getting all this — is like a soft splinter buried in her heart that won't come out. Not fatal, but always there, a reminder of some emptiness."
"She lost the independence that lets a person stand firm on her own. After they married, her husband, aching at the thought that she 'shouldn't have to work so hard,' coaxed her — in the name of love — to quit the design career she'd already built some modest success in. That had been work she loved, work that gave her a real sense of worth and an income of her own. From then on, her world gradually shrank, its center shifting to her husband, her children, and all the socializing that kept up the family's public image. Her emotions, her sense of worth, wound themselves ever more tightly around her husband's moods and the family's 'respectability.' When her husband was in a good mood, the house was sunny; when he was glum, she softened even her breathing."
"She lost a certain footing for speaking as an equal. After fifteen years together, a pattern had hardened into place. Her husband was used to making the decisions, used to her gentle compliance. She couldn't hold too many 'different' opinions, especially about 'matters out in the world.' Even when she felt wronged, most of the time she could only swallow it, wrapping her feelings up in that flawless social etiquette, keeping the shell of the marriage bright and gleaming. She didn't dare truly lose her temper, didn't dare show 'unseemly' fragility — didn't even dare, anymore, to be the real self she once was: a little willful, with clear likes and dislikes. She had become exactly the kind of 'wife' her husband and their social web most needed, while losing track of where she'd dropped the girl called 'herself.'"
"Her deepest loss, perhaps, was that solid inner steadiness and sense of belonging. The house is huge, lavish, but often so quiet that the only sound is her own footsteps. Her husband is very busy; even when he's home, they're often each absorbed in a phone or a computer, exchanging only what's necessary about the chores and the children. She fights gravity with the most expensive skincare and wraps her body in the most splendid clothes, but deep down there always lingers a thread of fear — fear of youth slipping away day by day, fear that this seemingly solid life, in truth wholly dependent on someone else, might collapse, fear of the self growing ever more hollow beneath that polished shell."
Lin Wan's gaze turned distant. "She came to possess material abundance most people can't imagine, yet without realizing it, she pawned off her independent soul, her right to be respected as an equal, and that reassurance of 'no matter what, I can always stand on my own.' Late at night, when all is quiet, she might sit alone in the spacious living room, looking at a home ablaze with light, and feel nothing but a vast loneliness. Outward brilliance can't fill the hollow inside. No amount of jewelry can buy a single conversation in which the soul is truly seen."
"She slowly came to understand that the ledger of gains and losses in a marriage is never simple addition and subtraction. She gained an easily won comfort, but lost the strength of character that can only be forged through struggle and growth, the baseline of being respected, and the anchor of her soul. Surrounded by every splendor, her heart was like rootless duckweed — feeling, inside that exquisite golden cage, a hidden and unending pain and bewilderment."
She paused and looked into the camera, her eyes full of compassion. "This is, perhaps, a not-unfamiliar portrait of many women trapped in similar circumstances: they've attained the 'completeness' society defines, yet through day after day of adapting, compromising, even talking themselves around, they've gradually ceded the boundaries of their dignity, the wholeness of their character, and that vivid, outspoken, living true self. They wear an ever-thicker 'perfect' mask, but their hearts have nowhere to rest — left only with the panic and bitterness they can't speak of, beneath the polished makeup."
"So," Lin Wan asked softly, as if asking the audience, and also asking herself, "tell me — what has fifteen years of marriage shaped Sister Man into? An artificial flower, never to wilt, carefully kept in an expensive vase? Or a strand of dodder that looks lush and leafy but really lives off the tree it clings to?"
The comments fell into a brief, thoughtful silence, then began to scroll:
[So real, it's like I'm watching my own aunt...]
[Gain and loss have always come bundled together.]
[An independent character is the priceless treasure.]
[Wanwan, you're making me want to cry — am I slowly turning into this?]
"Sister Man's story," Lin Wan paused, her gaze sweeping gently across the camera as if meeting the eyes of every listener, "or rather, the state she's in, moves us — even quietly aches in us — because it touches the very core of the struggle so many women face in marriage: the dense thicket of social expectation against the inner self that longs to grow."
The comments started sliding past:
[Honestly, it's like I'm seeing myself ten years from now. So scary.]
[That suffocating feeling is too real.]
[It's not just marriage — it's what society as a whole demands of women.]
She leaned slightly forward, her tone growing calmer still, as if sharing notes she'd long been turning over:
"I came to think later that the way marriage shapes a woman is truly like a many-edged blade — extremely complex. It can offer a harbor, but it can also become, without anyone meaning it to, a gentle set of shackles."
The comments:
[A many-edged blade! That metaphor is perfect!]
[Gentle shackles... I'm crying.]
[Finally someone says marriage isn't all good.]
"The challenges and the toll it brings are sometimes silent." Lin Wan tapped the desk lightly with her fingertips. "For instance, it can lead us, without realizing it, to outsource our worth — pinning it on whether our looks stay young enough, whether we keep house perfectly enough, whether we can forever be the 'presentable' partner. We pour enormous effort into upholding these standards, just like the shell Sister Man so carefully maintains with cosmetic procedures and luxury goods. But where the real 'I' inside should live grows blurry instead — and so we feel we have 'nowhere to dock.'"
The comments:
[I got that word 'outsource' instantly! I've always lived for other people's eyes!]
[Living myself to death just to earn a 'your wife's so great.']
[With no home inside, the bigger the house outside, the emptier it is.]
"For another, there's the invisible emotional labor. In a relationship, the woman is often the one who first senses a shift in mood, who actively smooths the atmosphere, who looks after everyone's feelings. This work has no KPI, no salary — day after day, yet it genuinely drains the spirit. Over time, it's easy to feel a deep exhaustion, to feel that the smile on your face is like a mask worn too long: it won't come off, but you can't breathe under it either."
The comments surged in an instant:
[Sobbing! That's me! The whole family's emotional trash can!]
[The mask! Every day before I step in the door after work I have to take a deep breath and put on the mask!]
[The phrase 'emotional labor' is so good — finally there's language for my exhaustion!]
[No pay, no thanks, only 'it's your job.']
"And again, there's the quiet shrinking of life's center of gravity. To run the household well, we may retreat bit by bit within the boundaries of home. Our old circle of friends, our own hobbies, the room we might have to stretch in our careers — all of it gives way before we know it. The world grows smaller, and so all our emotional needs and our sense of security naturally tie themselves more deeply to this one relationship and this one person. That 'panic' of fearing loss — its root usually lies right here."
The comments: [That's me. After marriage I had fewer and fewer friends, and the only topics left were my husband and kids.]
[I used to paint. Now I can't even find a brush.]
[The world shrinks, the fear grows — it really does happen like this.]
[Without your own social support network, you're just too fragile.]
"There's also the fact that, even amid material abundance, if the roots of your own development slowly wither inside the relationship, a deep unease about your ability to stand on your own surfaces. Take Sister Man — she has so much, but she's afraid too. Afraid that if the foundation of it all isn't something she tamped down herself, is it really steady? That unease has nothing to do with how much jewelry you own. It's about the deepest part of the heart — that footing of 'can I stand firm on my own?'"
The comments: [So true! Even when I have every right to ask for money, holding out my hand still leaves me feeling hollow inside.]
[Without financial independence, it's hard to be independent in character.]
[That's why girls absolutely must have their own careers and their own money!]
[Security is something only you can give yourself.]
Then her tone shifted, and a brighter, more hopeful strength entered her voice:
"But let's not forget — marriage can just as well bring nourishment and growth. A good relationship should be a profound alliance. It can offer a kind of emotional support and understanding found nowhere else in the world, becoming the most solid bedrock in our hearts when we face the storms outside. Those frictions, those adjustments — painful as they are — are also a precious training ground where we come to know ourselves, practice love, and learn to make room for another. Two people pulling together should be able to share the pressure, lift each other up, and make one plus one greater than two."
The comments: [Tearing up — I still long for a healthy intimate relationship.]
[Yes, yes, you can't write everyone off at once!]
[A good marriage really does heal both people.]
[That phrase 'training ground' — painful, but you grow.]
"So you see, marriage itself is rarely a black-or-white answer. It's more like a complex mixture, where positive nourishment and negative depletion are often woven together." Lin Wan's eyes grew utterly clear and firm. "And the key to telling it all apart, the way I see it, usually isn't the form of 'married' or 'not married,' but the very nature of the relationship we find ourselves in."
She spoke each word clearly:
"Is this relationship built on equality and respect? Are both people's independent characters cherished together, or is one side silently compromising and sacrificing?"
The comments scrolled quickly:
[A question for the soul!]
[Equality is so hard, but we have to chase it.]
[Always compromising, until I got used to it...]
"In this relationship, is the woman an 'object' to be endlessly examined and demanded of, or a 'subject' who can help write the future together? Are her voice, her needs, her dreams as an independent individual seen and valued — or ignored and set aside?"
The comments:
[Subject! I want to be a subject!]
[Ignored for so long, I'd even forgotten I had dreams too.]
[Always meeting his needs — what about mine?]
"Finally, does this relationship widen each other's worlds, making both people freer and richer? Or does it compress each other's territory, leaving everyone feeling a kind of hemmed-in exhaustion?"
The comments:
[Widen or compress? I'm going to lose sleep tonight.]
[My husband and I widen each other — it feels so good.]
[I think I've been getting compressed all along...]
Lin Wan paused for a long while, letting these questions sink into her listeners' hearts.
"Sister Man's plight — and that of so many women who suffer as she does — its root may lie right here." She summed up softly at the end, with deep compassion and understanding. "The environment or the relationship they're in has failed to nourish their strength as 'subjects.' Their sense of worth has to be confirmed again and again through outside approval — 'like a twenty-year-old,' 'has so much' — yet they can't draw real peace and strength from a stable, self-sufficient, vigorously growing core within. This isn't just the problem of one marriage. What it reflects is a far broader, deeper gaze that reduces a woman's worth to a single thing and turns her into an object."
The comments welled up in that moment:
[I've got goosebumps.]
[Wanwan, you see right through it.]
[We need more clear-eyed voices like this!]
[Starting today, I'm learning to be the subject of my own life.]
"And our learning to be clear-eyed, our learning to grow — perhaps it's all so that we can recognize that gaze, and within it, no matter what, guard the roots of our own inner bamboo, and reach with all our might toward our own sky."
The comments flooded the screen:
[Guard your own roots!]
[Grow toward your own sky!]
[Good night, Wanwan — thank you.]
[Another night of being healed and empowered.]
The music in the stream rose up gently.
