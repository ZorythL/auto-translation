Shuai Hongqiang Unravels the Riddle of the Bronze Mirror for Yao Yuan — The Mirror Pact
On Shuai Hongqiang's side, the air had a different texture altogether.
The eight-million bank loan hung over his head like a ticking clock, every second piling on more weight. The fifteen million in project payments the city government still owed him was, right now, the only thing that could turn the situation around. Last Saturday Ji Wenshan had brought in an unexpected windfall of five hundred thousand, but against an eight-million bank loan, that was a drop in the bucket.
For this lifeline of cash, he had set up a lunch first thing this morning. The other party was a middleman who supposedly "had some connections," someone who could grease the wheels with the government and chase down that bad debt.
Right now he was driving to the meeting spot.
His phone buzzed once on the passenger seat. WeChat. He grabbed it at a red light and glanced down.
It was from Lin Wan, brief and to the point: "Wenyao's parent-teacher meeting tonight, six thirty, don't forget." A classroom number followed.
His thumb moved, and he sent back a single word: "Got it."
He scrolled up. There were a few more unread messages. One was a repayment reminder from the bank app. One was from the boss of a materials supplier he'd worked with before, fishing in a roundabout way: "Any movement on those project payments the government owes you?" And one was a debt-collection form letter, "politely" reminding him that his debt was overdue, along with the relevant legal consequences.
He swiped them all away without opening a single one.
He pushed the car door open and walked into the elevator. The mirror in the car gleamed bright enough to see himself in: a navy cashmere overcoat over a crisp, well-pressed shirt, hair neatly trimmed, chin cleanly shaved. This was the "basic image management" Lin Wan demanded. She'd told him, "However hard things get, a man can't let his appearance fall apart first." He'd gone along with it, even if sometimes it felt like painting the outer walls of a house already hollowed out by termites inside.
The elevator opened straight onto the ground-floor lobby. The ceiling was nearly ten meters high, the cold marble floor polished smooth as a mirror, a huge abstract art installation hanging down from above. They'd booked a private room at a Japanese restaurant on the second floor. A place like this was quiet, private, convenient for talking business — and a single meal cost a small fortune.
A waiter led him into the private room. The other party hadn't arrived yet. He took off his coat, sat down, and looked out the window. From this angle he could just see the office tower across the Yellow River where Yao Yuan worked. In the winter afternoon sun, its glass curtain wall reflected a cold, sharp light, like a vast, unfeeling slab of blue ice. The Yellow River, winding through the city, was on this clear afternoon nothing but a quiet, gray-yellow ribbon, without a glimmer.
He thought of the song he'd heard while parking just now. The lyrics surfaced like a ghost: "...the old dog dares not bark, sleepless and tireless, working for a mouthful of broth..."
He gave a self-mocking tug at the corner of his mouth. Wasn't he, right now, running himself ragged for a mouthful of "broth"? Begging people, forcing smiles, cashing in the last of his connections and his pride for a sliver of hope at getting that debt back. That fifteen million had become the lifeline he pinned his entire comeback on — and also the boulder pinning him down in this swamp.
While he waited, the numbers churned through his head unbidden:
Owed to the bank: 8 million (the penalty interest snowballing every month)
Owed to him: 15 million (overdue nearly three years, with no end in sight)
Fixed assets (what's left): one apartment he lived in (worth about 4 million when bought, now down by half, in Lin Wan's name, unmortgaged), one old BMW X6.
Household monthly cash flow: mainly from Lin Wan's livestreaming and product-sales income (about thirty to fifty thousand, unstable), covering household expenses, the child's education, basic socializing. His own income had all but dried up.
Personal account: usable funds kept at a few tens of thousands year-round, a hedge against the risk of being frozen at any moment.
This was the whole of his economic map. A dangerous, extremely fragile balance. The bank's collection was the open spear, the government's arrears the hidden arrow. Lin Wan's income was the cloth that kept this household looking calm on the surface, covering the riddled, holey truth beneath.
He had it far better than someone truly at the bottom — a roof, hot meals, his child's future not yet ruined. But he had also lost forever the bold sweep he once had, waking every day to calculation and anxiety, falling asleep with his dignity worn raw again and again. Like an insect trapped in amber, frozen in the posture of flight, yet long since unable to move.
"Boss Shuai! So sorry to keep you waiting, so sorry!"
The private-room door slid open and a middle-aged man in a suit, his face heaped with smiles, walked in, his booming voice shattering Shuai Hongqiang's thoughts.
Shuai Hongqiang stood up at once, his face already shifting in an instant into a flawless, faintly warm smile, hand extended.
"Director Li, don't mention it, I just got here myself. Please, sit."
Pleasantries, sitting down, ordering. The sake was warmed, the sashimi platter arranged exquisite as a painting. The man talked at length, thumping his chest, spinning the same old loops about "people up top," "knowing the process," "just needing some funds to smooth things over."
Shuai Hongqiang smiled, played along, raised his cup in toast. But the ledger in his heart coldly turned to another page: this round of "smoothing things over" would be another sizable outlay, and whether it would come to anything was still anyone's guess. Right now he was like a gambler nearly out of chips — even at the glimpse of the faintest flicker of light, he had to push in what little remained, betting on a comeback as insubstantial as smoke. Sunlight came through the fine-latticed wooden window of the Japanese restaurant, casting crisscrossing patterns of light over the back of his hand. Half in the bright warm sun, half in deep shadow.
He raised the small sake cup, gestured to the "Director Li" across from him, and drank it down in one. The liquor was warm and smooth as it slid down his throat, yet he couldn't taste a thing.
Just as Shuai Hongqiang was propping up his composure, carefully maneuvering around "Director Li," his phone chimed. His heart sank; he instinctively assumed it was another debt-collection message or some other junk text. Still, he set down his chopsticks, picked up the phone, and ran his eyes across the screen —
His movement froze in an instant.
It was a deposit notice from the bank. The Municipal Finance Budget Settlement Center had transferred it in at 11 a.m., amount: 10 million. Because it was a large transaction, the credit had been delayed, and only now had it appeared before his eyes.
A barely perceptible tremor ran through Shuai Hongqiang. A tangle of feelings he couldn't name surged up — absurdity, irony, an unbelievable lightness, and a deeper bewilderment. Here he was, treading carefully, burning money, trying to pry open that tightly shut door — while behind that door, the money had already leaked out, more than half of it, without a sound.
Then a thought slammed into his mind: the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror. Could this be no accident, but rather Yao Yuan once again traveling back through time, the good fortune transformed from the Primordial Purple Energy she'd brought back?
The ringing of the phone pulled him back from his churning thoughts. The name "Yao Yuan" flashed on the screen. He answered, and her voice came through, asking to meet him at her office this afternoon, to talk about the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror he'd given her.
After hanging up, Shuai Hongqiang looked back at the still-gushing Director Li, his frame of mind quietly altered. He no longer eagerly echoed and pushed things along; his manner stayed polite but turned noticeably cooler. The meal was eaten, the wine drunk, and this thread couldn't be cut off entirely — after all, there were still five million to go. He kept up the basic appearances and finally, with a string of empty, going-through-the-motions courtesies, brought this costly lunch to a close.
Having seen Director Li and his group off, Shuai Hongqiang didn't go home. He got straight into the car, started it, and drove across the bridge spanning the Yellow River. Outside the window the murky river water rolled eastward, while in his mind that plain, ancient bronze mirror seemed to cast a faint, elusive light up from the riverbed.
The car came to a stop below Yao Yuan's company building, tires crunching over the withered yellow plane-tree leaves with a fine, brittle sound. Shuai Hongqiang pushed the door open and got out; a cold wind carrying the dry, biting chill peculiar to a northern November rushed at his face. He looked up at the towering glass-curtain-walled building. The gray-white sky spread across the sharp-edged façade, the shadows of a few bare branches slicing over the cold-toned glass surface.
The elevator rose smoothly to the 38th floor. Unlike last time's inquiries and waiting, Yao Yuan seemed to have given instructions ahead. This time, the moment the receptionist heard "here to see Boss Yao," she put on a professional smile and led him deep down the corridor — to Yao Yuan's office. He pushed open the well-soundproofed, light-gray grained wood door — Yao Yuan stood with her back to the door, before the full wall of floor-to-ceiling windows. The winter afternoon sun poured in, gilding her silhouette with a faint gold. Hearing the sound, she turned around.
Her gaze first landed on his face, lingered a moment, then moved away — drifting naturally to the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror at the center of the desk.
The office was modern minimalist, in tones of black, white, and gray, the heating running soundlessly, the room as warm as spring. And that bronze mirror sat quietly on the matte black tempered-glass desktop, its dark blue-green patina dim, the Kui-dragon cloud pattern winding under the ceiling light, as if carrying its own veil of ancient, unshakeable mist. Two eras stood facing off here, out of joint.
Yao Yuan looked at him for a good while.
The memories brought back by yesterday's time-travel hadn't yet receded; those emotions — fierce, tender, aching, scalding — surged anew through her veins. She looked at him, the forty-six-year-old Shuai Hongqiang, in his navy overcoat, the shirt collar at the neckline of his sweater clean and flat. For a hazy moment she couldn't tell whether the man before her was the one who, years ago, had unwound his scarf in the snow to wrap her up, cherishing and doting on her — or the one who, just four days ago, had reappeared after eight years seeking a business partnership.
Time was clearly the present, but her heart was sunk in the past.
"Yuan—" Shuai Hongqiang spoke first. A "Yuan'er" nearly slipped out; he caught himself, paused, and changed it. "Yao Yuan. These past few days — have you been having very strange dreams?"
Yao Yuan gathered herself, her gaze returning to scrutinize his face.
"Shuai Hongqiang," she said, her voice calm yet thin as a blade's edge, "the years we were together, I can honestly say I never did you wrong. After we broke up, I never clung on either. Eight years later you came to me to talk business — that was work, and I took it. Now—"
She walked to the desk, her fingers lightly brushing the mirror's cold edge.
"Be honest. What exactly is strange about this mirror? Why did you give it to me?" She raised her eyes, looking straight at him. "To harm me?"
"No! Absolutely not!" Shuai Hongqiang stepped quickly forward, then forced himself to a hard stop two paces from her, his voice edged with agitation. "How could I possibly want to harm you? I... didn't even know the mirror's secret myself, at first."
He drew a deep breath, his gaze also falling on the bronze mirror.
"After I gave you the mirror that day, I went home and didn't sleep all night. Suddenly I remembered something the old appraiser at the auction house had said back then — at the time I just thought he was being deliberately mysterious, and didn't take it to heart." Shuai Hongqiang knit his brows, sinking into memory. "He said the mirror's age was hard to pin down, its aura was 'heavy,' a little uncanny. They couldn't test out a specific year. If you kept it for yourself, no harm; but if you gave it to someone, especially someone close to you, you'd have to... think it over. Something about 'the collector must be sincere of heart; all things have a spirit.'"
Shuai Hongqiang did not mention the faint, opportunistic hope of changing his luck that had stirred in him when he gave her the mirror.
Yao Yuan listened quietly, her fingertip sliding unconsciously along the rim.
"The more I thought about it, the more wrong it felt. I was afraid there really was some taboo, something bad for you. First thing the next morning I went to the antiques market to find that old master." Shuai Hongqiang looked at her, his eyes complicated. "And that's when I got the mirror's origin out of him."
"What origin?" Yao Yuan pressed.
"The master said this mirror was forged by a Yin-Yang adept of the Tang dynasty, who refined a single strand of Primordial Purple Energy from the moment heaven and earth first opened, using a secret method. Hidden within the Kui-dragon cloud-and-thunder pattern on the back is a 'Time-Reversal Talisman.'" Shuai Hongqiang spoke each word distinctly. "And so, it can let a person — travel back through time."
The air went abruptly still.
The sound of the wind outside was sealed off by the double glazing into a vague background. Yao Yuan's fingers stopped on the bronze mirror, tightening slightly.
"...Time-travel?" she repeated, her voice very soft.
"Yes. But there are conditions." Shuai Hongqiang paused. "Not just anyone holding the mirror can use it. Take me — I kept it in hand for eight years, and to me it was never anything but an ordinary old mirror."
"What conditions?"
"Do you remember?" Shuai Hongqiang looked at her. "Many years ago, at the antiques market, there was an old Daoist who chased after you, insisting on reading your fate, saying you carried the great destiny of the 'Nine Purple Departing Fire'?"
A flicker stirred in Yao Yuan's eyes. How could she not remember — hadn't she, just yesterday, watched the whole thing again with her own eyes: the twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan chased down by an old Daoist for a fortune-telling? At the thought, she couldn't help a low laugh.
"What he said was true." Shuai Hongqiang lowered his voice, as if afraid of disturbing something. "The one who can activate this bronze mirror must be a person carrying great fortune. Only such a person can forge a 'Mirror Pact' with the mirror. And at certain specific times... trigger the time-travel."
He got this far, then suddenly stopped.
There was a flash of hesitation. Extremely brief, so brief it was nearly impossible to notice. But Yao Yuan saw it — his eyes wavered for a moment, his throat moved slightly, as if he had swallowed back some words.
(He's hiding something.)
The thought surfaced, clear and cold. Yao Yuan didn't press right away; she only lowered her eyes to the bronze mirror. Its surface was dim and clouded, casting no clear reflection, only the faint blur of her own outline, and behind it, the face of Shuai Hongqiang on the verge of speaking and stopping.
The office was so still you could hear the faint sound of the heating vents.
"So," Yao Yuan slowly raised her eyes, her gaze placid and unrippled, "I wasn't dreaming. I really did... go back?"
"Yes." Shuai Hongqiang nodded, then added, "But the master also said time-travel isn't without a price. Reversing time stirs up one's fate, and all the more so for someone carrying great fortune. Each trip drains the spirit — you need to be careful to rest and recover."
He said it earnestly, with genuine concern in his eyes.
Yet that flicker of wrongness in Yao Yuan's heart wouldn't go away.
(If it's only draining the spirit, what was he hesitating over just now?)
(What is it he doesn't dare to say?)
She walked to the window, her back to him, looking down at the pedestrians on the street below, bundled tight in their coats. Withered leaves spun in the wind.
"Why did you give me this mirror?" she asked, her voice floating in the glass's reflection.
Behind her, silence for a moment.
"The first time I saw it, all those years ago, I just felt you'd like it." Shuai Hongqiang's voice came rough. "Later... we parted, and I kept it put away. When we met again, giving it to you felt like returning it to its rightful owner. I just never expected—"
He stopped.
Yao Yuan turned around. Backlit by the cold, indifferent winter light outside the window, her expression couldn't quite be made out.
"Shuai Hongqiang," she said, "some things — once they're past, they're past. I'll keep the mirror, since it's fated to be with me. As for the time-travel..."
She paused, a faint curve, of an indefinable meaning, tugging at the corner of her lips.
"Let's just call it a very vivid dream. From now on, I won't touch it again."
Shuai Hongqiang opened his mouth, as if he wanted to say something, but in the end only nodded. "...All right. As long as you're safe."
He stood there a while longer, his gaze lingering on her face for a moment, sweeping over the bronze mirror, before he said in a low voice, "Then I'll go now. You... take good care of yourself."
The door closed softly, the latch giving a faint click.
Yao Yuan still stood at the window, watching the distant Yellow River flow soundlessly by.
She turned, walked back to the desk, and bent her head to gaze at the bronze mirror.
Her fingertip brushed across the cold surface, the Kui-dragon cloud-and-thunder pattern winding and rising under her touch. For a hazy moment, she seemed to be back at the mouth of that little Jiangnan alley she'd once passed through on a trip — the plum-rain season, the damp air, the bluestone-paved road, that man standing beneath the camphor tree, smiling and beckoning to her.
(Shuai Hongqiang, what exactly were you about to say just now that you didn't say?)
(Behind this "Time-Reversal Talisman" — is it really as simple as just "draining the spirit"?)
(The part you hid... what is it?)
She pulled open the drawer, took out the wooden box, and carefully placed the bronze mirror inside, face down, the ancient patterns on its back looking all the deeper against the dark velvet.
Just as she was about to close the lid, her hand suddenly stopped.
(That's not right.)
(He said "only one carrying great fortune can forge a Mirror Pact.")
(So if I've already forged a Mirror Pact...)
(How is this pact undone?)
(Why didn't he mention it?)
Yao Yuan's fingers hovered above the box, her fingertips going faintly cold. She looked at the bronze mirror again, and those winding patterns no longer seemed mere quaint ornament — more like some silent incantation, some pact binding her together with the unknown.
The heating in the office was strong, yet she suddenly felt a little cold.
Downstairs, Shuai Hongqiang got into the driver's seat and shut the car door, but didn't start the engine right away.
He gripped the steering wheel with both hands, the backs of his hands going white with the force, veins faintly bulging. The warmth from the air-conditioning still lingered in the car, yet he felt a chill crawl up his spine.
(I didn't tell her everything.)
(The master said what's hidden in the Kui-dragon cloud-and-thunder pattern isn't only the "Time-Reversal Talisman.")
(There's also a "Fortune-Turning Talisman" — that's the second talisman, hidden in the reverse-scale pattern. Each time one carrying great fortune travels back and stirs up their own fate, a portion of that fortune flows through the Mirror Pact, silently... blessing the one who gave the mirror.)
(The giver of the mirror bears its fortune; the traveler drains their spirit. One waxes as the other wanes — it's called "borrowing fortune.")
Shuai Hongqiang squeezed his eyes shut and pressed his forehead against the steering wheel.
That day in the dim back room of the antiques city, the old master's voice had been aged and heavy: "This mirror is uncanny precisely because it recognizes both its owner and its pact. You gave it to her — that forged a pact of giving. If she uses the mirror to reverse time, that forges a pact of fortune-turning. The former is a bond of fate; the latter is a debt. She loses spirit and lifespan; you gain fortune and opportunity. This debt... can never be repaid."
"Is there a way to undo it?" he'd asked then, his voice dry.
The old master shook his head. "The Mirror Pact is already forged. To forcibly take it back or destroy it may bring disaster. Especially for the one holding the mirror — if her 'spirit-mind' is in the midst of a reversal, rashly moving the mirror could leave her consciousness unable to return properly to its place. Unless she perceives it herself and finds a way to break the pact — but that method was lost to time long ago. For now, you might first watch quietly how things unfold, keep a close eye on her state. If she mentions 'reversal' more and more often, or shows any mental abnormality, you must find her at once and bring her to see me. Perhaps... there may be some other remedy, but it will need long and careful thought."
Shuai Hongqiang opened his eyes, the rims red.
(I didn't mean to harm her.)
(I just... didn't tell her everything.)
(Maybe she won't use the mirror again. The old master said, as long as the reversal isn't triggered, the Fortune-Turning Talisman won't activate, and she won't keep draining herself...)
The phone suddenly buzzed; his wife's name lit up on the screen. He stared at those two characters for a few seconds, and in the end pressed the call away and started the car.
The sound of the engine was especially jarring in the quiet winter parking lot.
He pulled out of the garage. In the rearview mirror, that glass tower grew smaller and smaller. Behind some window on the 38th floor, Yao Yuan was probably still there, with that mirror.
And he chose to drive the car in another direction — to the Bank of Communications, where there was still an eight-million loan to repay.
Human nature can't withstand the test. Especially for a man who has once grabbed a vine at the cliff's edge — it's very hard to talk himself into letting go.
In the office on the 38th floor, Yao Yuan had not closed that quaint old wooden box.
She took the bronze mirror out again, lifted it before her eyes, and under the ceiling light studied the Kui-dragon cloud-and-thunder pattern closely. The cloud-and-thunder pattern coiled, the dragon's body wound, and at one turning point the pattern seemed especially dense — was that the position of the reverse scale?
Her fingertip brushed across that area; the texture seemed a touch warmer and smoother than the rest, as if it had been rubbed by a hand over many years.
(If there really is a "Time-Reversal Talisman"... where is it hidden?)
(And this inexplicable exhaustion and palpitation these past two days — is it really just the normal aftereffect of time-travel?)
She set the mirror down, walked to the bookshelf, and pulled out a copy of A Study of Tang Dynasty Patterns. She turned to the chapter on the Kui-dragon pattern, those ancient designs stretching across the page, the annotation reading: "The Kui-dragon, a divine beast of antiquity, shaped like an ox, gray-bodied and hornless, with a single foot. Wherever it enters or leaves the water, great wind and rain arise; its light is like the sun and moon, its voice like thunder. It governs time and space, and fortune..."
The phone rang.
It was her assistant. "Boss Yao, the four o'clock meeting with Mingke Capital — would you like to head over now?"
Yao Yuan glanced at the clock on the wall. Three forty-five in the afternoon.
"Push it back half an hour for me," she said. "Also, look someone up for me — eight years ago, the old master in charge of appraising the bronze-mirror category at Jinshi's winter auction. Surname... possibly Chen, very old. Find his contact details and send them to me."
She hung up, took one last look at the bronze mirror, placed it back into the box, and locked it in the drawer.
But the moment the drawer closed, she suddenly felt an inexplicable palpitation, as if something had been tugged at, ever so lightly.
The mirror lay in its box, silent as a riddle.
And the pact was already forged; the threads of fate, quietly winding tight.
