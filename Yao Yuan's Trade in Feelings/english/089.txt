The Bloody Coming-of-Age of the Gifted: Making Up the Required Course in People Skills
[Call connected.]
The caller (a woman, 28, her voice carrying the crisp logical edge typical of an elite Top-2-university graduate—but beneath that logic was a string pulled taut, about to snap): "Teacher Yuanyuan, I need help. I think I might have a... terminal illness when it comes to workplace relationships." She got straight to the point, almost with the composure of someone presenting a research topic, but the falter at the end of her sentence gave away a trace of trembling.
"Describe the symptoms." Yao Yuan's reply was as concise as a doctor taking down a chief complaint.
"I'm 28. Bachelor's and master's from a Top-2 school, in finance. Graduated first in my class, joined the top-tier investment bank where I work now. It's been a year and three months. My performance speaks in data: of the three deals I've handled, my error rate is the lowest in the department, my reports get used as templates, and my quarterly reviews are all S-grade. My direct supervisor and the MD—the managing director—have both made it clear they're betting on my professional potential." Lin Wei spoke fast, as if fending something off. "But my relationships with my colleagues are a disaster. No one teams up with me for group projects. I always eat lunch alone. Any thread in the work chat goes dead the moment I reply. When I need to collaborate, all I ever get is a robotic 'sure' and the bare minimum of cooperation. Information always reaches me last and least complete. And I even... I overheard them in the break room saying my 'nose is stuck in the air,' that all I know how to do is 'perform for the boss.'"
She paused, and the string drew tighter still: "I don't understand. I genuinely mean no one any harm. Everything I do, I do to push the work to perfection. I don't gossip, I don't waste time on idle chat—is being efficient, focused, and a perfectionist somehow wrong? Why would they rather huddle together with people who are mediocre at the job but who'll grumble together and order milk tea, and yet isolate the one person who can actually get things done? This environment... it suffocates me. Every day I walk into that office, it's like walking into a silent cage full of hostility. I can't make sense of it—is being too good, in itself, a crime?"
The scrolling comments started to split:
[So textbook. Top-student brain slamming into the wall of real society.]
[Even hearing it is suffocating, but I feel like she's offended people without realizing it.]
[The workplace isn't an exam hall, sis. Reading the room is a required course too.]
[I feel a little bad for her, but isolation like this doesn't happen for no reason.]
[Is it just me who thinks she's probably stepped on toes without knowing?]
Yao Yuan listened quietly. No sympathy surfaced on her face, no surprise either. She just leaned back slightly into her chair, the fingertips of both hands meeting to form a steady triangle propped against her chin. The posture didn't look like someone listening to a personal woe; it looked more like an architect surveying a complex system riddled with conflicting code.
Yao Yuan heard her out patiently, then spoke mildly, cutting straight to the heart of it: "Sweetheart, let me give you one very practical line first: no one will dislike you for being excellent. They'll only dislike you for being excellent and convinced of your own superiority."
She spoke again, her voice low but slicing the silence like an ice pick:
"Your problem is not a 'terminal illness in human relationships.' Your problem is that you're running an absolutely rational 'excellence operating system,' one cultivated in a sterile lab, and trying to run it inside a primitive jungle called the 'workplace'—a place teeming with viruses, bacteria, tangled ecosystems, and the law of the dark forest. There are no absolute right answers here. It's overrun with viral, complicated human nature, with bacterial growths of competing interests, and with the dark-forest law of the strong devouring the weak. The system isn't compatible, and it triggers a violent rejection response."
A faint, sharply indrawn breath came through from the caller's end.
"You think the workplace is just an upgraded version of campus—harder questions, richer rewards, but with the same core rule: highest score takes all?" A faint, almost cold curve crossed the corner of Yao Yuan's mouth. "Wrong. Especially in the finance circle you're in, which runs on collaboration and connections, the underlying logic was never about 'scores.' It's about trading resources, contests of power, and alliances for survival. Your professional skill is only one of the resources you hold—and it's a high-risk, high-threat kind of specialized resource. When you merely display that resource and lean on it, when you unconsciously use it to crush everyone around you, then in your colleagues' perception system you are not a 'respectable, excellent partner.' You are a dangerous intruder who has barged into an established tribal territory, brandishing a gleaming blade, eyes full of arrogance, yet utterly ignorant of the tribe's secret codes and taboos. The tribespeople won't worship your sharp weapon. They'll instinctively band together to reject you, to wall you off—until you're forced out, or slowly assimilated. Or until you learn to sheath the blade while mastering the tribe's language and rules, and maybe even become the new chieftain."
The comments froze for a beat, then erupted:
[That metaphor is so precise! Every cut draws blood!]
['High-risk resource'—got it. Being too capable isn't necessarily a good thing.]
['Tribal secret codes'—oh my god, I'm learning those every single day!]
"I... I never wanted to crush anyone." The caller's voice dropped, carrying the awkwardness and frailty of someone who'd just been seen straight through.
"'Intent' and 'impact' are two separate systems." Yao Yuan cut her off, her tone as calm as a scalpel cutting cleanly. "You're immersed in a mental temple you've built out of reason and efficiency, chasing the ultimate in perfection and purity. To you, idle chat is entropy increasing, and accommodating the 'mediocre' drags efficiency down. The overall aura you give off, without meaning to, is this: 'I don't belong to the same tier as you people. My time is worth more. My methodology is superior.' This isn't the content of your words. It's the hidden signal continuously broadcast by every quick turn you make to end a non-work conversation, every look in your eyes as you toss out the 'optimal solution' as if it brooks no question, every report so flawless it makes those beside you feel diminished. There is a vigilance rooted deep in human nature, planted by the survival instinct: we naturally grow wary of, distance ourselves from, even band together to resist anyone who continually makes us feel inferior, who highlights our own incompetence, or who threatens our standing in the group—especially when that person is completely oblivious to it, or simply doesn't care. Your 'brilliance,' lacking the necessary 'lubricant of empathy' and 'buffer of relationship,' is in itself the sharpest offense and the heaviest source of pressure."
Yao Yuan's gaze seemed to drift past the camera, toward some dim corner of her own memory suddenly lit up. Her tone took on a far-off, certain lament:
"I understand you completely, sweetheart. Because many years ago, I too lived inside that bubble of 'excellence' defined by a single standard. I studied dance, and after dancing long enough, my whole world shrank down to the millimeter precision of a movement, the intensity of an emotional release, and the score on the judges' table. I thought the partners I trained with were dead weight. I thought the audience didn't grasp the depth in my choreography. I believed my world was the mirrors of the practice room and the spotlight on the stage—rules clear, rewards and punishments plain. Until my second year of senior high, when reality slapped me awake with a blow that pulled no punches. I discovered that the 'star figure' I thought I was could, in the wider arena of human relationships, be nothing more than a 'misfit who doesn't belong.' The 'excellence' I prided myself on was not only worthless in everyday settings that called for warmth and cooperation—it had become the very root of my isolation and the misunderstandings about me. That early stretch of my life—where I was smug about my good grades and good looks, got disliked by the whole school, then through painful adjustment learned to notice other people's strengths, to praise them sincerely, and finally won people over—taught me a lesson carved to the bone: if you don't understand 'people,' if you can't read the subtle, complicated grain of human nature, if you can't handle those seemingly useless 'relationships,' then 'excellence' on any axis is just a gorgeous, fragile island—unable to connect to real land, let alone form an ecosystem that nourishes a life."
She drew her gaze back, refocusing on the present, and her fingertip—that touch of "rust red"—tapped lightly on the cold, hard desk, giving off a firm, decisive sound:
"This society, especially the society of adults, runs on rules that are bloody and crude. It doesn't care how pure your heart is, how self-consistent your logic, how noble your goals. It only recognizes results—and that 'result' is usually decided by the combined force that emerges after countless complex relationships have played out against each other. You can call it vulgar, call it ugly, call it a place that buries genius. But this is the rule. Either you grow strong enough to ignore the rules, to make the rules—or you must bow your head and learn them, dance within them, even use them."
She paused for a long while, letting that heavy, precious realization settle in the air.
"That experience is the most original, and the most profound, source code behind everything I later came to understand about people, every insight I have into feelings—even behind why I do this livestream." Yao Yuan's voice turned unusually clear and forceful. "It made me understand early, at the price of pain: human nature is subtle and complex, far harder to crack than any dance movement or math formula. The essence of relationships is the flow of energy and a delicate balance of power—not a simple matter of right and wrong, win and lose. A person's worth lies never merely in being 'excellent' on some single axis, but in whether they can build healthy, nourishing, sustainable connections with this world and with other people."
"Sweetheart, the loneliness and rejection you feel in the workplace now—that's the amplified, adult version of what I felt back in my classroom. The rules are more hidden, the price more immediate. But the core hasn't changed. Society won't hand you a 'free pass on the human-relations exam' just because your professional scores are high. On the contrary, when your excellence sails without the escort of emotional intelligence and people-wisdom, it becomes your 'Achilles' heel' all the more easily."
"Change is painful, of course—as painful as scraping poison off the bone. But it's the only road to becoming truly strong. While keeping your core competitiveness, you have to forge yourself a suit of 'social armor' that can adapt to a complex environment, and learn those 'people skills' that look 'useless' but are absolutely vital. This isn't a betrayal of your profession. It's building a 'highway' for your outstanding professional ability—one that lets it reach its goal smoothly, and even amplifies its effect. When you can both solve hard problems and make the people who work with you feel as if they're basking in spring breeze, that's when you become irreplaceable. Otherwise, you're just a useful 'tool' that people keep at arm's length—one that could be swapped out at any moment for a more 'useful' or 'cheaper' tool."
"Teacher Yuanyuan, what exactly should I do?" The caller's voice was dry, but she seemed to have caught hold of something.
"First, run a cognitive reformat. Quarantine that 'ability-is-everything' system in your head for now. Tell yourself that over the next three months, 'building trust' and 'fitting into the environment' are more important KPIs than 'completing tasks flawlessly.' Treat your professional ability as the Swiss Army knife in your toolbox, not the assault rifle you're holding up at all times.
Second, launch a 'micro social investment' plan. Force yourself to make one 'useless' social move every day: bring a colleague a coffee (and remember how they like it), say a sincere 'Nice work, that was fast' when someone finishes a small task, ask 'What does everyone think?' first in a group discussion even when you already know the answer. Shift part of your intelligence from solving math problems to solving the problem of 'people.' Observe who's close to whom, what the department's sensitive topics are, what kind of asking-for-help won't irritate people but instead draws you closer.
Third, learn to expose 'harmless weaknesses.' This isn't about genuinely playing weak; it's about strategically showing a bit of 'imperfection.' For example: 'This Excel formula has actually had me stuck for ages—can you take a look?' or 'Ugh, I stayed up late on the report last night and even coffee isn't working today. Got any secret tricks to stay alert?' Let people feel you're a living human who 'also runs into problems and also needs help,' rather than a flawless AI. This dramatically lowers how threatening and distant you seem.
Fourth, redefine your 'enemies' and your 'allies.' Your enemy isn't those 'mediocre' colleagues; it's the environment that keeps you from playing to your strengths and traps you in internal friction. Those colleagues could be potential allies in changing that environment. Try to discover the value they hold beyond professional skill—who has the best-connected grapevine, who's best at smoothing over conflicts, who has the boss's ear. Replace a competitive mindset with a cooperative one. Even if the cooperation is only on the surface.
Fifth, prepare yourself mentally for a long dormancy—even for the possibility of failure. Turning an impression around takes time, possibly a long time. Along the way you may still feel lonely, awkward, like you're 'not yourself.' Remember, this isn't betraying yourself; it's expanding the dimensions of your personality, learning a new language tied to survival and growth. The process will hurt, like bone growing a second time. But once you get through it, you'll gain a tougher, more three-dimensional, more powerful capacity to survive—the ability to both reach the summit in your professional field and move with ease through complex human relationships. That's closer to real strength than any 'excellence' in the academic sense."
At the end, Yao Yuan looked into the camera, her gaze like the deep water of a cold pool—icy, yet reflecting a light that saw through everything: "Sweetheart, the world won't roll out a red carpet for your excellence. More often, it sets up roadblocks for your excellence. Tearing down a roadblock sometimes takes more than a sharper axe—it takes knowing how to go around it, or finding people willing to help you lift it together. Sweetheart, come down from the lone peak. Walk into the open wilderness of the human world. You'll find that true strength isn't being absolutely correct in a vacuum. It's deeply understanding and mastering both human nature and the rules, and still being able to move forward clear-eyed—until, in the end, the world has no choice but to make way for you. What you have to do now is make up this bloody, necessary lesson. This is the class I was forced to start in my second year of senior high, and I'm still taking the advanced course to this day. You should start too. Good luck."
Yao Yuan's last words rang out like the heavy toll of a bell, echoing through the livestream room.
[Call time is up. The feed cuts off amid an atmosphere heavy with the pressure of reality.]
The comments didn't flood in right away. There was a brief, wide-open blank, as if countless viewers in front of their screens had fallen into a silent storm of thought, digesting this cruel and clear-eyed 'guide to surviving the jungle.' Then, slowly, long comments full of admiration and self-reflection began to scroll like a tide.
Yao Yuan didn't move. She still held that posture, both hands propped against her chin, gazing out the window at the leaden-gray sky and the eternally turbid Yellow River below. The youthful past about dance, about lonely arrogance, about having her bubble torn open by force by reality—the actual blood and tears behind it had long since been dried by time. But the lesson it had hardened into still carried a biting chill in this moment, even as it had been deeply fused into the underlying logic by which she saw the world and answered it.
Excellence is talent; it's effort. But how to make that excellence survive, grow, and even reign in a world that is not always kind—a world often full of envy and calculation—that is a far longer and far crueler campaign. It takes wisdom, it takes resilience, and above all it takes a bone-deep understanding of, and reverence for, the gray zones of human nature. It was precisely that one 'system reinstall' that laid the foundation for her later ability to dismantle emotional tangles and see through the contests of human nature with such a cool, penetrating eye.
She slowly let out a breath, leaving a small, fleeting patch of fog on the cold glass.
"Next." Her voice was steady and professional again, every flicker of emotion reined back in. The operating table was still brightly lit, waiting for the next case to be dissected—a case about growth, and about survival.
