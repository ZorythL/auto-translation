A New Year's Hotpot and Family Talk: Mother and Daughter Open Their Hearts and Recall the Road They Came By
The last livestream of the year ended on a perfect, settled note.
The blazing studio lights dimmed one bank at a time, and the buzz of voices slowly ebbed away. The team, worn out from a full day's work, shrugged off their fatigue, wished one another a happy and healthy New Year, packed up the equipment, double-checked the closing numbers, and set off with light steps toward their own New Year's celebrations. In an instant the vast studio hall fell silent, leaving only the faint warmth of lights gone dark, a quiet that settled over all the heat and bustle of the day.
Yao Yuan glanced at her phone. On the screen, invitation after invitation to New Year's gatherings popped up, lively and bright, wrapped in all the grand ceremony of a worldly holiday. She swiped past them with a smile, not lingering.
Yao Yuan glanced at her phone. A few invitations from friends' gatherings jumped up; she swiped past them with a smile, not lingering. She opened the pinned conversation. Zhao Yiming's message from noon was still sitting there: "The Pretty Big One finished its third round of model runs. The numbers are beautiful. Come by the company tomorrow morning? We should sit down and really talk through the pricing model."
She wrote back a simple "Sounds good," and the corners of her mouth curved up without her meaning them to. They hadn't made plans for tonight, but her heart felt steady. There seemed to be an understanding between them already: toward those holidays that get hyped up until you feel you must "do something," they both kept an ordinary, unbothered attitude. Save your energy for what actually matters — like the model they'd nail down tomorrow, the one that decided the future of the "Pretty Big One." Building out a business map side by side, walking toward a future you could actually see land — that was the steadiest, most lasting thing to run toward.
The other message was from Zhang Fengxia, timestamped half an hour ago: "Come over when you're done streaming. Your Uncle Zhou saved the best cucumber-cut and lamb shoulder for you. We're waiting."
No extra words, and yet it warmed her heart more than any lively invitation. She replied "On my way," grabbed her coat, and headed out.
Driving through the deepening dusk, she passed shops on both sides of the street all decked out in festive cheer. Stopped at a red light, she saw a father by the roadside carrying a little girl on his shoulders; the girl was waving a glittering balloon, her laughter ringing out. Yao Yuan watched and found herself smiling softly too. These simple, lively little fragments of life didn't feel noisy to her just then — they had a vivid, lovable quality instead.
The three-story building of "Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot" blazed with light along the street, a warm magnet drawing in everyone who came looking for a good meal. Before she'd even parked, she could see through the fogged-up first-floor windows the shifting silhouettes of people inside, the place packed and bustling. She pushed open the door and a wave of warmth rolled out to meet her — rich sesame paste, fresh lamb, the sweet sharpness of sugar garlic, carried on a tide of clamoring voices — and the cold lifted off her in an instant.
"Aiyo, Yuanyuan, you made it!" Zhou Lufei was standing by the counter chatting with a regular, and the moment he spotted her his whole face broke into a grin. He had on a brand-new dark-red sweater today that brought out his color and made him look especially spry. "Was the traffic bad? Come on up, your seat's been saved a while now — your mom's asked after you twice already."
He led Yao Yuan toward the second floor; even the stairs had been hung with festive little lanterns. As they climbed he couldn't help sharing his joy, his voice low but every word brimming with happiness. "Guess what? Just like you taught me — no reciting a script on the stream, just chatting like family. These past few months the set menus online have been selling like crazy! Revenue's up by this much over last month!" He spread out five fingers and gave them a shake, the crow's-feet at his eyes crinkling up in a smile. "Lots of young customers order online and then bring their friends down to eat in. So lively! The restaurant business is tough these days, but this old place of ours, plus your new idea — we've steadied things, and we're doing even better than before!"
Yao Yuan listened, her heart brightening too. "It's because you've got a solid foundation here, Uncle Zhou — honest meat, a hard-earned reputation. All I did was help 'pass' your good word along."
"It's not as simple as passing a word along," Zhou Lufei said earnestly. "You taught me how to 'open up' to friends I've never even met. Honest talk — it works better than any fancy advertisement!"
The little window booth on the second floor was quiet, with a good view. Zhang Fengxia was already sitting there, a small copper pot in front of her bubbling with clear broth, a few red dates and goji berries bobbing up and down. When she saw them come in, she lifted her eyes. "You're here? Pour yourself some pear soup, it's still warm." Her tone was as flat as ever, but her hands had already picked up the serving chopsticks, fishing a heaping spoonful of tender bean sprouts and golden fried tofu puffs out of the big simmering pot beside her and setting them in the dipping bowl in front of Yao Yuan.
"Your mom timed it just right to stew that. She knows streaming wears out your throat, says this is the most soothing thing." Zhou Lufei sat down cheerfully. A server quickly and deftly brought over glossy, fragrant sesame paste, freshly fried chili oil, and a few plates of crisp cold dishes.
Yao Yuan sat and took a sip of the warm pear soup; its clean sweetness slid all the way from her throat to her stomach, and the faint dryness from an afternoon of talking was smoothed away at once. She picked up a chopstickful of bean sprouts coated in sesame paste and put it in her mouth — the flavor was wonderful.
"Everything still going smoothly at the company?" Zhang Fengxia asked, even as she slipped hand-cut fresh lamb into the rolling clear broth. The lamb was sliced paper-thin, its red-and-white grain like plum blossoms fallen on snow; it cooked the instant it touched the broth, and she scooped it up with practiced ease, sliding most of it onto Yao Yuan's plate.
"Smooth. It's going well." Yao Yuan ate the meat her mother had served her. The lamb was so tender it hardly needed chewing, and the rich aroma of sesame paste melting in her mouth was a solid, genuine satisfaction.
"As long as it's smooth." Zhang Fengxia nodded and asked nothing more, then used a strainer to swish a couple of slices of tripe in the spicy pot beside her and dropped them in Zhou Lufei's bowl. "You eat too, don't just keep talking."
Zhou Lufei chuckled and picked up a chopstickful of his proudest sugar garlic for Yao Yuan. "Try this. It's your mom's new recipe this year — sweet-and-sour, crisp, and unbeatable for cutting the grease."
A few plates of meat and vegetables down, warmth spread through them, and the conversation opened up more. Zhou Lufei talked eagerly about his plans for spring. "I'm thinking I'll fix up that little rooftop terrace on the third floor, put up a grape trellis, and in summer do some cold dishes and beer. The young folks are sure to love it."
"You and your ideas." Zhang Fengxia shot him a glance, but for all she said it, she didn't argue, and she fished out another piece of frozen tofu soaked full of broth for Yao Yuan.
"Business is like living your life — you've got to keep pushing forward, keep something to look toward." Zhou Lufei took a satisfied sip of barley tea and looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes gentle. "Oh, and your mom's sugar garlic — lots of customers ask if they can buy some to take home after they've tasted it. After the New Year, I want to find a reliable factory to talk to, see if we can make it into small packages, so the folks who love that flavor can have it at home too."
Yao Yuan listened, watching the hope for the future in Uncle Zhou's eyes and the quiet, capable care her mother put into everything, and the thoughts that had been circling in her mind — over the stream, over the Mirror Pact, over all the unresolved things — were gradually softened and smoothed flat by this honest, steaming warmth. This was the most solid foundation of life.
When they'd nearly finished eating, Zhang Fengxia wiped her hands with a napkin and spoke as if in passing. "You and Yu Hao — you've split for good?"
"And now — you're with Zhao Yiming?" Zhang Fengxia asked it directly, no roundabout way. Hearing this, Zhou Lufei lowered his head and concentrated on his hotpot, as though he'd suddenly developed a great interest in the doneness of that slice of lamb.
Yao Yuan finished chewing slowly, lifted her eyes, and met her mother's gaze. There was probing in Zhang Fengxia's eyes, and concern, but more than that a kind of calm appraisal — not gossip, but a mother's assessment of an important choice in her daughter's life.
"Yes, we're together." Yao Yuan's voice was steady. "But Mom, don't read too much into it. We get along well, and the rest will take its course. The most important thing for you and Uncle Zhou now is to take care of your health, not to worry about my affairs."
"Take its course?" Zhang Fengxia repeated the phrase and gave a soft snort through her nose. "Back when you were with Yu Hao, you said it would take its course too. And what came of it? A few years together and still just 'buddies.'" She used the trendy internet word, her tone subtle.
"That's different." Yao Yuan set down her chopsticks, her tone still even but carrying more weight now. "Yu Hao is Yu Hao, and Zhao Yiming is Zhao Yiming. I know what I'm doing."
"As long as you know." Zhang Fengxia didn't press further; instead she served her a chopstickful of greens, her voice softening a little. "After today you'll be thirty-eight. I just hope there's someone steady by your side, someone who knows when you're warm and when you're cold." It was only then that Zhou Lufei cut in, smoothing things over. "Fengxia, let the kids handle their own affairs. People in tech are steady and reliable. Yuanyuan has good judgment and a mind of her own — you can rest easy. Here, tea instead of wine, let's clink glasses. May next year be good for all of us!"
Three teacups touched lightly together with a clear, pleasant ring. In that simple little ritual was packed the plainest of wishes for the year to come.
The mood at the table eased again. But Zhang Fengxia's words were like a small stone tossed into the lake of Yao Yuan's heart, rippling out faintly. She thought of her appointment with Zhao Yiming tomorrow — Western World, the pricing model for the Pretty Big One AI... Between the two of them, beyond the tangled, unresolved riddles of the Mirror Pact and time and space, it really had reached the stage where some practical matters needed to be "made real."
The conversation drifted, somehow, to longer ago. Perhaps it was this warm reunion that made one think of the road already traveled.
"Remember little Xiaoping next door, back when we lived in the Liu Lane alley?" Zhang Fengxia asked suddenly.
Yao Yuan's memory stirred. Blurry images surfaced: a cluttered courtyard always hung with the smell of cheap cigarettes and leftover food. The woman of that household wore heavy makeup and often came home late at night; the man loafed about doing nothing. Their little girl was called Xiaoping — eight or nine years old, yet never seen at school, running wild in the alley all day with a pack of half-grown kids, her grubby little face lit by startlingly bright eyes.
"I remember." Yao Yuan nodded. "You never let me play with her back then."
"That's right, I didn't." Zhang Fengxia's tone was firm and absolute, even carrying a trace of the old hardness. "Your dad passed early, and the two of us were crammed into a ten-square-meter rental. Times were hard. But however hard it got, I had to tell you — there are some roads you don't take, some people you don't get close to. I understood the lesson of Mencius's mother moving three times. I didn't have the means to move us out of that place right away, but I could keep you from running around with that child."
She looked at Yao Yuan, her gaze the same as always. "You were small then. You thought I was unkind, you thought Xiaoping was pitiful — no school, no one looking after her. But I saw it clearly: the way that whole family lived was rootless duckweed, getting through one day at a time and calling it good enough. Her parents drifted through their days, and the world the child saw was a frightened one. What that kid took in with her eyes, heard with her ears, turned over in her heart from the time she was little — how could it ever be the same as yours? You play one day in the mud with her, and what she learns is how to snatch other kids' marbles, how to swear without repeating herself; you come home and read one more page of a book, write one more character, and that gain goes right into your own bones."
Yao Yuan listened quietly. The small grievances and confusion she'd felt as a child over being forbidden to play were, in this moment, re-decoded by her grown-up mind. Now, at thirty-eight, sitting in the settled life she'd fought to build, listening to her mother speak of those long-ago choices in that calm tone, what she felt was not accusation but a late-arriving, deep understanding.
The white steam rising off the hotpot blurred her mother's face, yet let her see clearly how that mother — once just as young, stripped of any support, struggling at the edge of survival — had, in her own lonely, brave, clumsy way, fought to draw the first strokes about "the bottom line" and "direction" onto the canvas of her daughter's life.
She suddenly thought of those desolate women in Eileen Chang's writing, swallowed by their circumstances. She thought of the heroines in Yi Shu's novels, the ones who no matter what would struggle to keep their dignity, to haul themselves out of a wretched situation. And her mother, Zhang Fengxia — a woman who'd come up out of a mountain gully, with little schooling — had, by a kind of instinctive, plain, and tenacious survival wisdom, lived out the same sort of principles.
She didn't understand any "theory of family of origin" or "social class stratification," but by a mother's instinct and a beast-like vigilance, she knew she had to "draw a line" for her child. She might not have been able to give Yao Yuan a privileged start, but she did everything she could to keep her daughter's character from being eroded too soon by a bad environment. She used the most direct method — forbidding — to mark out a safety line. Behind that line was a faint but stubborn conviction: "however hard it is, we climb upward."
This kind of upbringing shaped certain hard cores in Yao Yuan's character: a wariness of one's surroundings, a deliberate distance from forces that "drag you down," an obsession with self-building, and that clear-eyed knowledge that "in the end you can only rely on yourself." It wasn't warm — it even hurt — but it worked. Like cutting a path through a thicket of thorns: the process is bound to leave you scratched, but in the end it lets you cross the mire and reach more open ground.
"Thinking about it now," Yao Yuan said, her voice very soft, "you were right back then."
"Mom," Yao Yuan said, her voice very soft but carrying a smile and a kind of release, "I understand now. Back then... you were right."
The hand holding Zhang Fengxia's chopsticks paused slightly. She lowered her eyes and, as if to cover it, poked at the long-cold leaves of vegetable in her bowl. Several seconds passed before she said, in a voice even softer than before, "...As long as you understand. Hurry and eat, the meat's getting cold."
But Yao Yuan saw the corners of her mother's eyes flush red for a quick instant. Then she stood, saying she'd go get more greens, and as she turned she raised a hand and wiped her cheek, very fast.
Zhou Lufei took it all in and said nothing, only smiled and served Yao Yuan another piece of sesame flatbread. "The chive-flower paste your mom pickled goes perfect with this. Unbeatable."
The meal lasted until nearly nine at night, host and guests all content. Going downstairs, the first floor was still packed full, laughter and chatter everywhere. Zhou Lufei walked Yao Yuan all the way to the door and pressed a heavy eco-bag into her hands. "Take it! There's freshly mixed sesame paste inside, your mom's new sugar garlic and chive-flower paste, and two pieces of braised beef shank — slice it up any day you don't feel like cooking."
Zhang Fengxia carefully tucked her scarf in for her. The night wind was cold; she gripped her daughter's hand a little tighter and murmured, "Drive slow. Send a message when you get home. With Zhao Yiming... be good to each other."
"I know, Mom. Uncle Zhou, you two turn in early too, you've worked hard all day." Yao Yuan opened her arms and hugged her mother gently. This time Zhang Fengxia didn't go stiff; she raised her arms and hugged her back, then quickly let go and urged her on. "Off you go now, the roads are busy."
Settled into the car and driving for a good stretch, she could still see in the rearview mirror those two figures standing side by side at the shop door, looking after her, especially warm against the cold night. She cracked the window a little and the crisp air poured in, carrying the faint smell of fireworks, while her heart felt full to the brim.
On the drive home to her apartment, the city glittered with light. Many shops along the way were still open, getting ready to greet the turning of the old year into the new. But Yao Yuan's heart was quiet, all that clamor as if behind a pane of glass. Her mother's words, her childhood memories, and those sharp dissections of human nature, love, and survival from the livestream wove together, collided, and settled in her mind without a sound.
Back at her riverside apartment, the rooms were silent and serene. She didn't switch on the main light, only lit a single floor lamp beside the sofa — a gentle halo of light circling out a room's worth of solitary peace.
Beyond the floor-to-ceiling windows, the city's neon spread out like a sea, the traffic threading through like broken streams of light, sketching out the splendor of the metropolis. In the darkness the Yellow River ran on as it had since ancient times, flowing silently through year after year, witness to every rise and fall, every parting and reunion in the human world.
She suddenly remembered this day last year.
The same New Year's Eve, by Yellow River Tower, fireworks filling the sky, old friends at her side. In the span of just one short year, people and circumstances had shifted, and her state of mind had transformed. The people once around her had each gone their own way, each carrying the sweetness and the weight of their own lives, while she had long since shed her youthful restlessness and empty obsessions, and had grown into someone self-sufficient, settled, with her own quiet ballast.
Now, standing alone at the window, she felt not the slightest chill of "ringing in the New Year by herself." Instead she was filled with a full, ripe calm and a faint, quiet anticipation. It was the steadiness of knowing where you draw your strength from, and where you are running toward.
Up in the night sky, a soft crack — a single firework shot up, burst open with a hiss, brilliant in gold, and in a blink sank back into the silent dark.
She stood here, the silent river before her, but the secret of the Mirror Pact, the countdown to the ninth crossing through time, the daily push and pull of a present life tangled up with Zhao Yiming, the road ahead for commercializing the Pretty Big One AI, her mother's "make it real" circling in her mind...
"Bang—!"
Another point of golden light rose not far off, leaping hard, bursting open with a snap at its highest point, shattering into a handful of glittering stars that came drifting down like a tiny golden rain.
The beauty of fireworks lies in the instant of extinction after the most extreme burning. In knowing it must turn to ash, and holding your breath for that moment of splendor all the same. How much it resembles the human longing for love, for glory, for all things brief and beautiful — the infatuation and the mourning of it.
Eileen Chang saw through to the desolation and emptiness behind such beauty. Yi Shu chose to admire the bustle outside the window only now and then, after she'd secured her own steady "study." And the multitude of ordinary souls mostly struggle back and forth between Qiong Yao's dreamlike romance and hard reality, trying to grasp at a little light to warm themselves.
And what about her, then?
A thunderous boom — another giant firework soared up and burst at the very top of the night sky, a thousand streams of gold pouring down, scattering into a sky-wide rain of stars, drifting down. The dazzling light and shadow fell into her jet-black pupils, bright one instant, extinguished the next.
The utmost splendor, in the end, hurries to a close.
What moves us most about fireworks was never their lasting forever, but the way they pour out everything in a moment's burning, ardent and unguarded. Just like the love and glory of the human world, every kind of beauty — splendor wrapped around emptiness, ardor hiding desolation.
The fireworks spent, the night sky went quiet again, and the faint scent of gunpowder drifted in through the evening wind to the window.
The old year had drawn to a close; the new year was opening its first page.
Outside the window the Yellow River ran on as it had for ten thousand ages, silent witness to the turning of time, holding within it all the splendor and loneliness of the world, all the clear-eyed waking and the sinking under, all the beginnings and the goodbyes.
Yao Yuan slowly turned and walked toward the study.
On the desk the plain old bronze mirror lay quietly on its side, its surface deep and still, reflecting neither the sky full of fireworks outside nor the look in her eyes, the feeling in her heart, just now.
But the countdown to the ninth crossing through time, deep in her mind, silently ticked off its numbers.
The new year drawing near, the fireworks ordinary, the road ahead vast, the fate not yet at rest.
