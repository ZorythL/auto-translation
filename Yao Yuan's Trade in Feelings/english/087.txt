In the few minutes before the livestream began, Yao Yuan let her mind go blank, the way she always did. Her thoughts drifted like the thin winter clouds outside the window, aimless. The matter of the Mirror Pact had finally taken shape. She still had to wait until after the ninth time-travel, but at last there was a definite method and a definite deadline. The worry that had hung over her for so long actually settled now that it had something solid to rest on — since there was no other way, she would simply wait. And in the waiting days, life kept to its own track; it did not let you stand still.

Her days were full. The content of the stream needed constant refining. After "Pretty Big One Emotional AI" went live, user feedback poured in, and there was no time to lose in iterating and optimizing. Friends' gatherings, industry exchanges — her schedule was packed solid. Her relationship with Zhao Yiming, built on shared creation and a rational understanding between them, had gradually settled into something stable and grounded, like an instrument running in harmony after careful calibration. Now and then she thought this life really wasn't bad. Her career was a clear path climbing upward; at her side was a partner more than capable and able to understand her. A grounded sense of security, built on clear-eyed choices and real strength, wrapped around her. Perhaps this was the most ideal form of life she had clawed and scrapped her way into earning for herself.

She drew her thoughts back. The timing was exactly right.

Wednesday, two in the afternoon, not a second off.

【Livestream Status】

The ring of fill lights flared on without a sound, spilling a sterile, operating-room white that cloaked the central area without a single shadow. Yao Yuan sat poised in the middle of it, like a carefully displayed work of art, and also like an attending surgeon about to take up the scalpel.

Today she had chosen a caramel-brown velvet suit. The heavy, particular texture of the velvet and its restrained sheen fit her upright figure perfectly, lending a note of unmistakable luxury to her composure. Beneath it she wore a silk V-neck blouse in the same shade but a touch lighter; the soft luster of the silk played against the matte texture of the velvet in an exquisite layering. She had swept all her hair back into a sleek, flawless high ponytail, leaving only a few deliberately placed wisps of soft curl at her temples and hairline to soften the clean lines of her face and add a touch of unstudied liveliness. At her ears, a pair of minimalist diamond studs caught and cut the light into cold points of starlight with her every small movement. On her wrist was a platinum watch of clean lines, and on her ring finger a geometrically cut black agate ring shifted with a dark, unreadable gleam under the cold light.

The backdrop was that eternal, vast floor-to-ceiling window. Outside, the winter afternoon light was a cold, indifferent gray-white, and under it the muddy surface of the Yellow River shimmered with a metallic, almost dull gray cold — silent, enduring, pausing for no one's joys or sorrows, only surging slow and stubborn toward the horizon. This carefully constructed space — the precise light, the symbolic backdrop, the eternal chaos beyond the glass, together with the exquisitely dressed, calm-eyed woman within it — formed a single instrument, a precise instrument for dissecting the human heart, weighing emotion, taking desire apart.

The dark "rust red" polish on Yao Yuan's fingertips slid lightly across the smooth surface of the tablet as she prepared to take the first caller. On the screen, the title was clean and sharp, like a honed scalpel pointed straight at some common, aching emotional knot: #Phantom High Ground? Don't "Act Out" Your Love into an Abandoned Construction Site#

Yao Yuan adjusted the tiny mic clipped at her collar, raised her eyes, and gazed calmly into the depths of the lens. In that gaze there was no warming up, no small talk — only the cool, focused attention of someone who saw through everything, and the professional steadiness of someone ready at any moment to begin a "clinical dissection."

"All right," her voice came through top-grade equipment, clear, steady, free of any excess emotion, like the confirmation before a surgery begins. "Let's connect this friend."

【Call Connected】

Caller (a woman's voice, 26, the words edged with a panic she was fighting to hide): "Teacher Yuanyuan… I, I really don't know what to do anymore. My ex-boyfriend… this time it seems he really means it. He doesn't want me anymore." She drew a deep breath and rushed on, as if afraid a single pause would break her. "We were together two years. He was so good to me, that kind of really good. Holiday gifts, anniversary gifts, never half-hearted — always expensive, always thoughtful. If I said in the middle of the night I wanted the congee from the east side of town, he'd drive half an hour to go buy it. When I hit trouble at work, he'd pull strings to smooth things over for me. Sometimes my temper would flare and I'd say awful things, and he'd just take it, then come and coax me out of it…"

"So what was the reason for the breakup?" Yao Yuan asked, her tone calm, offering no comfort, like a doctor taking a history.

"I…" the caller's voice dropped, full of confusion and regret. "Maybe… I 'acted out' too much. I always figured that since he loved me so much, he definitely couldn't do without me. The moment I was unhappy, or felt he'd fallen short somewhere — even just answering a message a little slowly — I'd bring up breaking up. At first he'd get so anxious, all kinds of apologies, trying to win me back, buying me bags and flowers, waiting downstairs for hours. Later… I did it so many times he seemed to get used to it, but every time he'd still come and coax me. Until last month, over something especially tiny again — he'd worked overtime two days straight, he was a little tired, and during our video call that night he yawned. I decided he was impatient, that he didn't value me, and I argued with him on the phone, and said again, 'Let's break up. I'm tired, there's no point.'"

She broke off, her voice thicker with tears, threaded with disbelief: "I thought he'd be like before — that he'd come apologize the next morning after he woke up, beg me not to make a scene. But no… A whole day passed, and all he sent me on WeChat was one line: 'Okay. I respect your decision. Let's break up. Take care.' And then he never reached out to me again! He won't answer calls, he blocked me on WeChat. I went to wait downstairs at his company, and when he saw me, his eyes were like he was looking at a stranger — he just walked right past me… Teacher, I didn't actually want to break up! I just wanted him to care about me a little more, wanted him to fret over me the way he used to! I'm dying of regret now, I can't get anything done, I'll never find anyone better than him… Have I really lost him for good?"

The scrolling comments started racing past:

[Classic "Boy Who Cried Wolf," live on air.]

[The guy gave so much up front — he'd warm up a block of ice — but nobody can take cold water thrown at them every single day.]

[Sis, people's patience and love do run out.]

["Phantom high ground" is too precise a term — when you're acting out you think you're a queen, then you find out the city's been empty for ages.]

Yao Yuan listened quietly, no sympathy or reproach on her face, only a deep concentration. She waited until the girl's stifled sobbing eased a little before she spoke, slowly, not loudly, but every word clear, landing in the silent studio with a force that struck at the heart:

"Young lady, let's first step outside words like 'love or not,' 'care or not.' Let's take it apart using a logic you might understand better — say, project investment and risk management."

On the other end the caller seemed to freeze for a moment.

"Your ex-boyfriend was like an angel investor — huge early commitment, full sincerity, almost no bet-against clauses. What he put in were scarce resources: time, money, top-tier emotional value, and a beautiful set of expectations about the future. And you? You were the promising 'core project' he'd bet on. Early on, the partnership looked smooth, the investor was diligent and conscientious, and the project showed a decent growth curve."

Yao Yuan's tone was as cool as if she were reviewing a business case: "But you, the 'project side,' made a fatal strategic error. You took that unlimited 'credit overdraft' privilege the investor extended to you out of trust and expectation, and treated it as capital you could squander at will. And you turned 'threatening to liquidate and pull out' — bringing up a breakup — into your routine 'management tool' and 'mood regulator.' The moment you felt your expectations weren't met, your risk appetite wobbled — bad mood — or you simply wanted to test the investor's 'loyalty and risk tolerance,' you triggered the 'liquidation threat' program. Once, twice — out of long-term faith in the project and the enormous sunk cost already paid, the investor chose to add investment and raise his risk tolerance: coaxing you, winning you back. But every single 'liquidation threat' violently burned through the investor's already limited reserve of trust and capital of patience, and sharply pushed up the 'risk rating' you held in his mind."

She leaned in slightly, those calm eyes seeming to pierce through the screen: "Until last time, when you once again triggered the Nth 'liquidation threat' over a trivial 'expectation-management error' — he was tired and yawned. This time the investor likely ran a thorough 'post-investment review' and 'risk assessment.' He found that this project's 'core management' — you — lacked basic emotional stability and any awareness of risk control, and was itself the single greatest source of risk. The project's future cash flow — the happiness and sustainability of the relationship — had become wildly uncertain in the internal drain and stalls caused by one 'liquidation alarm' after another, possibly even negative. And so he made a rational final decision — one perhaps also carrying enormous disappointment — to cut his losses in time and execute the liquidation. When he replied 'I respect your decision,' it wasn't sulking. It was a calm, business-final verdict based on a complete assessment."

The comments:

[That analogy is unreal! You get it instantly!]

["Emotion is the single greatest source of risk" — too true!]

["Liquidation verdict" — so cruel but so real. The guy really made up his mind.]

From the caller's end came stifled, rapid breathing.

"So your pain right now doesn't come only from losing him. At a deeper level —" Yao Yuan spoke word by word, her tone cutting, "you've suddenly fallen from the imagined 'phantom high ground' where you thought you could command the wind and rain, down into a brutal reality where you have to face 'project bankruptcy, investor withdrawal' — and you've discovered you hold not a single bargaining chip, and your posture is a mess. That fall destroys a person's self-image far more than you imagine."

"Then can I… can I still win him back? What should I do?" the caller pressed, urgent, like clutching at the last straw.

Yao Yuan was silent for two seconds, then said clearly and firmly: "My advice may be the opposite of what you're hoping for. Not only do I not advise you to 'win him back' now — I'll tell you that in your current state, any so-called 'winning back' is just continuing to make the mistake, pushing yourself into an even lower pit."

"Why?" The caller's voice shook.

"Because we have to clarify a core question right now: what is true 'class' in love, and what is utterly, thoroughly 'low.'" Yao Yuan's gaze was sharp as a knife, as if to cleave through every fog.

"Young lady, listen well. A woman's true class is never shown in the lofty, swaggering pose she strikes while she's being loved and carefully cherished. That isn't control — that's stupidity and a lack of breeding. Taking someone's heart-and-soul devotion as capital you can overdraw without limit and treat as worthless; viewing the other person's tolerance and forbearance as a bottom line you can probe at will and trample on again and again; even 'not treating him as a human being' in the relationship, only as a tool that must obey absolutely and satisfy your every emotional demand on command — this is the lowest sort of behavior. What it exposes isn't your power and charm, but the extreme poverty inside you, your ingratitude and selfishness. You're feeding your forever-bottomless black hole of insecurity by trampling on another person's dignity, and that face you wear is hideous."

Her tone eased a little, but the core grew harder: "Truly high-end togetherness is cherishing, is reverence. Cherish the person who is good to you with nothing held back; tuck his goodness away carefully and repay it with equal respect, consideration, and shared effort. It's giving clear praise and sweet feedback when the other does well; it's consciously managing yourself when your own emotional storm hits, using clear-headed communication instead of wounding attacks, voicing your needs sincerely instead of threatening a 'breakup.' The confidence of 'not being afraid to lose' shouldn't become a dagger you use to hurt the one who loves you. It should become the backbone that keeps you sincere in the relationship — neither groveling nor arrogant, never swallowing your own grievances to keep the peace."

"So when should you draw the 'breakup' card without a moment's hesitation?" Yao Yuan's eyes turned utterly clear, carrying a flat, absolute resolve. "Only when the other person truly, repeatedly, and without any remorse crosses your principles and your bottom line. Deception, betrayal, infidelity, violence of any kind, or an irreconcilable conflict in core values. A breakup at that point is self-protection, is defending your dignity, is the clear-eyed recognition that this relationship has gone bad and that staying will only keep draining and even destroying you. That kind of breakup is a calm decision, a never-look-back cutting of losses. The posture may be tragic, but it is absolutely beautiful — because you're protecting something more important: your own integrity and bottom line as an independent individual."

"But your 'breakup'?" She looked straight into the lens, as if straight into the girl's eyes, her words hitting the mark in one stroke. "It was a weapon, a test, a chip for extracting emotional value, a threat to force the other person to submit. You took the most precious contract of the relationship and gambled it on the tiniest emotional fluctuation, turning 'dissolving the contract' into an upgraded version of your daily wheedling. And then one day, the way the boy who cried wolf even convinced himself, the other person went from initial alarm and exhaustion, to final numbness — and then to total clarity. The game is over, and only then do you discover you've overplayed it: the table is gone, the house has walked away."

Yao Yuan's tone carried an almost cold-blooded clarity, but beneath that coldness was a far deeper guidance:

"Once you've reached this point, you have to own the loss. Admit, openly and squarely, that you overplayed it — admit that you wrongly and repeatedly deployed 'nuclear deterrence,' and that you finally brought about a 'nuclear winter' in the relationship, irreversible. For you to go apologizing now, trying to win him back, lowering yourself — all of it, at its root, declares to him and to yourself: 'See, all my earlier arrogance, my willfulness, my treating him as less than human — it was all a bluffing paper tiger. I simply can't bear the cost of truly losing you. My earlier 'high ground' was all fake.' That look is even more degrading, even more contemptible, than when you were 'acting out.' Fairies, remember this: when someone is good to you, you should not swagger. Your worth does not need to be proven by pressing on the bottom line of the person who loves you."

"So why do I firmly oppose your trying to win him back now?" Yao Yuan closed the logic into a complete loop. "Because your present 'winning back' is paying an extra shame-tax for all your earlier 'low' behavior, begging for a scrap of pity in an even 'lower' posture. It won't make you look more worthy of love — it will only doubly confirm how correct his decision to leave was: you really are a 'high-risk project' that can't manage its own emotions, can't take responsibility for an intimate relationship, and lacks basic self-respect."

"The only correct road you can walk right now — the only one — is to fully own this loss." Her voice carried an unquestionable force. "Stop all the futile pursuit, the weeping, the self-pity that will only make you more wretched. Take every bit of your effort, your gaze, your energy, and pull it all — completely, cleanly — back off the person you yourself pushed away and who has shut the door on you for good. And then slam it, a hundred percent of it, into yourself!"

"Go and carry out the 'bankruptcy restructuring' from my analogy. First — accept reality, bear the consequences. This finest of investments is the one you acted out of existence. Own it, take responsibility, make no excuses for your mistake. That is an adult's accountability, and also the starting point of a new life. Cut off contact entirely; give each other a final dignity and space. Your not disturbing him is the only — and the last — respect you can offer right now.

"Second — review deeply, scrape the poison from the bone. Take up the scalpel and cut yourself open: why do you need to keep threatening breakups to gain security, to prove you're loved? Behind it is extreme inferiority, a severe uncertainty about your own worth, a profound distrust of relationships — or simply a giant-baby mindset that takes another person's goodness as a tribute owed to you by nature. Go to a professional counselor, read, study, and make up the key lessons missing from your character: emotional management, a sense of boundaries, and empathy. You have to rebuild yourself, with real difficulty, from an 'emotional black hole' and a 'relationship-destroyer' into an emotionally stable, independent individual who carries security and a sense of worth within. This is the rewriting of the core 'competitiveness' and 'underlying code' of your 'life project.'

"Third — rebuild your life, and live out the substance of 'class.' Turn every second you'd spend dwelling on him, missing him, regretting losing him, into action that invests in yourself. Eat every meal properly, sleep every night well, make your work shine, pick up the hobbies you abandoned, tend your friendships with care, raise your understanding and your skills. Your goal must absolutely not be to 'get better so he regrets it' — that still makes him your reference point. Your goal is this: my life was always meant to be lived full, confident, vibrant, magnetic in itself. When you no longer parasite your self-worth on another person's attention and love, when you can become a warm, stable, powerful source of energy on your own, only then do you truly touch the core of 'class.'"

Yao Yuan slowed her voice, but the force in her words built layer upon layer: "So-called 'class' is not playing the imperious queen in a relationship — that is exactly the outer armor of an inwardly weak heart. True class is cherishing every piece of sincerity you meet, respecting every bit of another's giving; it's holding gratitude when you're loved, not growing arrogant on favor; it's putting everything into the relationship while you have it, not lightly giving up; it's leaving decisively, without dragging your feet, when your principles are crossed; it's calmly owning the loss when your own mistake causes it, not blaming heaven or others, and then pouring all your strength into rebuilding a better self. It's being able to run your own life vividly and luminously, self-sufficient — whether or not there's a partner at your side."

"Young lady," she finally called the girl by name, her tone the last bit of warmth after the severity. "Someone being good to you is a gift of fate, a blessing. Your task is to catch it, be grateful for it, and nourish it with the same goodness. Don't take a blessing and 'act' it, with your willfulness and ignorance, into a wretched, unbearable bond in both your lives. Your very existence should make those who come close to you feel warm, grounded, nourished — feel 'it's worth it' — not feel exhausted, afraid, drained. If one day you have to leave a relationship, let it be because the other person isn't worthy, not because you 'don't know how.'"

"Before you learn to 'cherish,' before you possess the capacity for 'high-end' love, please first give it everything you have, and make yourself worthy of that 'goodness' you once held without realizing it — and only mourn now that it's lost. Your journey was never the road back to that man; it's the glorious road of your own rebirth, your character leveling up. First live yourself into someone worthy of being loved, and who knows how to love. As for the rest — heaven keeps its own timetable."

【Call time up; the picture cuts off amid a long, powerful resonance】

For a few seconds the studio was completely silent, and then the comments came surging in like a tide, full of shock, recognition, and self-examination.

Yao Yuan did not immediately look at the churning comments. She leaned back slightly, settling into the chair, picked up the lukewarm water at her hand, and took a light sip. Beyond the window, the Yellow River under the dim winter light still flowed silent, powerful, eastward, pausing for no one.

She set the cup down, and the black agate ring on her finger flashed a faint dark gleam under the lights. Another "clear-eyed operation" complete. She had cut open the surface of "acting out" to expose the core lesions inside — about self-respect, cherishing, character, and growth. Painful, but necessary.
