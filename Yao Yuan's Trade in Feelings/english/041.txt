Chapter 41 — A New Year's Eve Banquet

Time slipped by fast, steeped in city neon and alcohol. The calendar turned page after page, and before she knew it deep winter had arrived. November's cold winds stripped the last withered leaf from the treetops, and December's first snow quietly blanketed the whole city one predawn morning. Through those two months, Yao Yuan's life moved along its fixed rhythm—livestreams, business, socializing—and the time-travel incidents had not recurred since the last one, the strangeness now feeling like nothing more than a dream. She met Yu Hao now and then, keeping up that easy, neither-deep-nor-shallow "buddy" arrangement. As for Zhao Yiming's "Genesis Engine," in mid-December it had once again attempted a deep fusion with "the Pretty Big One's core framework," and once again the attempt ended in failure. The technical team's diagnostic report was calm and obscure: "Our assessment remains that some segment of 'non-standard emotional mapping' in the external high-dimensional data stream, when attempting deep coupling with the 'Pretty Big One' core model, triggered a chain reaction of conflict in the underlying logic—similar to 'immune rejection,' but expressing itself with particular violence at the data layer." The physical circuitry and base architecture checked out fine, but some of the emotional weight parameters in the core model needed recalibration and reinforcement, and a stricter "emotional firewall" protocol had to be established.

Zhao Yiming's voice on the phone gave nothing away emotionally—only the focus peculiar to a technical man facing an unsolved problem. "Something in your emotional data is making my model 'allergic.'" Yao Yuan listened and only returned a flat line: "Then it means it isn't 'robust' enough yet."

Not robust enough. The phrase circled in Yao Yuan's mind for a few days, finally dissolving into a barely audible little laugh. People or AI, it made no difference—not robust enough, and you deserved to come out behind in the game.

In the blink of an eye it was New Year's Day.

On the first day of the new year, as dusk gathered, Jin Lan's "Yellow River Tower" blazed with light. The building, known as "the First Tower of the Ten-Thousand-Mile Yellow River," stood at the highest point of the city's riverside scenic belt: 93.39 meters tall, sixteen floors above ground and one below, a tower structure of seven visible tiers and six hidden ones that, in the night, looked like a glowing pagoda of glazed tile. Its mock-antique design in the Han and Tang styles layered flying eaves and bracket sets one atop another, while vermilion railings and jade-green tiles, lit up, glowed with a lustre where the weight of history fused with modern luxury. Standing before the floor-to-ceiling windows of the top-floor private room, you could look down on the entire winding course of the Yellow River through the city, the skyline on the far bank as brilliant as a galaxy hung upside down.

Yao Yuan was the third to arrive. Tonight she wore a breathtaking gold gown. Not a gaudy, glaring gold, but an expensive silk somewhere between champagne and molten sunset, woven with a faintly visible dark pattern that, when the light swept across it, rippled with a flowing, water-like sheen. The dress was a one-shoulder mermaid cut, tracing the smooth line from collarbone to ankle to perfection, cinched very tight at the waist, the hem unfurling slowly from below the knee and swaying gently with her steps—like a lazy, dazzling goldfish drifting through the night. Her jewelry was just as striking: from her ears hung two gold tassel earrings, minimal in design but heavy in presence, each segment linked by tiny diamonds that threw off scattered starlight when they swung; at her throat lay a matching collarbone chain, set at its center with a teardrop yellow diamond. Under the room's warm amber light, that gold was deep and rich, as if a small sliver of sunlight had been imprisoned forever against her skin. Her long hair was loosely coiled at the back of her head, a few carefully arranged loose strands falling at the side of her neck, making that neck look all the longer and fairer. She wore no fur, only a sharply tailored black cashmere coat—now handed to the attendant—and she leaned against the window, watching the lights reflected on the river's surface, her whole figure like a golden work of art that had been meticulously forged and then granted life.

"Yuanyuan!" Jin Lan came in from the door, in a sapphire-blue embroidered qipao with a white fur stole over it, makeup exquisite, beaming. "Happy New Year! We were just waiting on you—oh wait, no, there are still a few who haven't shown." Her gaze lingered on Yao Yuan for a few seconds, and she said with genuine admiration, "God, this outfit of yours… tonight you've clearly made up your mind to make sure no one can take their eyes off you."

Yao Yuan turned and embraced her with a smile, the tassels at her ears tapping together with a very faint, crisp little sound. "Happy New Year, Jin Lan. You chose this place well—the view is incredible."

"Of course. My own turf—the best private room naturally goes to my own people." Jin Lan took her by the arm and led her to her seat. The room was enormous, with a round redwood table at its center big enough for fifteen, already set with delicate blue-and-white porcelain and a glass lazy Susan. All around stood mock-antique screens and curio shelves displaying porcelain pieces, the overall style classical and refined with an understated luxury running through it.

People arrived one after another.

Wang Qian came in with his wife. Mrs. Wang was thirty-five or thirty-six, round-faced, with gentle features, dressed in a cream knit dress under a light-gray coat, her makeup soft and understated—a curious contrast to Wang Qian's smooth, all-things-to-all-people air. The moment she stepped in, her eyes locked onto Yao Yuan, and they instantly lit up; almost reflexively she pressed a hand to her mouth.

Zhao Yiming came alone, in a dark-gray suit with no tie, his usual faintly distant little smile on his face. He greeted Jin Lan first, then turned his gaze to Yao Yuan, pausing on her half a second longer than on anyone else. Behind the lenses his eyes showed no ripple; he only gave an almost imperceptible nod, which counted as a greeting.

Chen Ye and Liu Qingzhuo arrived together. This made Yao Yuan raise an eyebrow slightly. Chen Ye still wore a shirt of fine material, though this time light gray, with a navy cashmere cardigan over it that made him look more cultured. Liu Qingzhuo wore a wine-red velvet jumpsuit, her makeup vivid, her hand resting quite naturally in the crook of Chen Ye's arm. As they came through the door they were murmuring something to each other, Chen Ye tilting his head slightly to listen, a gentle smile at the corner of his mouth, nodding now and then—an expression utterly different from the startled helplessness when he'd been passively kissed in the KTV last time, now carrying a few degrees more of natural intimacy.

So that kiss in the game last time really had become the spark, Yao Yuan thought, the corners of her lips curving into a faintly amused arc. Good. Liu Qingzhuo was decisive, Chen Ye steady, their family backgrounds well matched—a decent trade, all things considered.

Gao Jieyuan was the second-to-last to arrive, in a flashy wine-red velvet suit, his hair gelled into perfect order. He flung his arms open dramatically the moment he came in. "Happy New Year, everyone! Ah, sorry I'm late, the roads were jammed like crazy!" His gaze swept the whole room like radar and jolted to a stop on Yao Yuan; his throat seemed to bob, his smile deepened, and his voice rose a few notches. "President Yao, tonight you've… put the sun on? You're so dazzling I can barely keep my eyes open."

Yao Yuan only smiled faintly and raised her glass in acknowledgment, the yellow diamond at her throat flashing a sharp glint as she moved.

The last to arrive was an unfamiliar woman. About twenty-seven or twenty-eight, tall, in a minimally tailored black suit over a white silk blouse, her long hair tied into a low ponytail at the back of her head, her makeup light, wearing fine-rimmed glasses—an altogether intellectual, crisp air about her. She carried a dark-brown briefcase. Coming in, she first nodded in greeting to Jin Lan, then her gaze settled on Yao Yuan, carrying a clear curiosity and scrutiny—and within that scrutiny was mixed the focus of a researcher facing a precious specimen.

"Let me introduce everyone," Jin Lan said, taking the woman's hand. "Jiang Tiantian, a friend I've recently met. Her father is Jiang Shuhai—Teacher Jiang—yes, that well-known author. Tiantian herself mainly works as a director and in film investment, focusing on women's subject matter. She's seen Yuanyuan's livestreams and the audience comments, found her especially interesting, and kept saying she wanted to meet her."

Jiang Tiantian stepped forward, gave the group a slight nod, her bearing composed, then fixed her gaze firmly on Yao Yuan and extended her hand. "Teacher Yao, I've long admired you. I'm a deep consumer of your content, and a researcher of your methodology."

Yao Yuan rose and shook her hand, her gold skirt sweeping a graceful arc, a fine gold bracelet sliding out from her sleeve, its pendant a tiny, abstractly shaped lock. "Miss Jiang, you're too kind. I've read Teacher Jiang's books as well—penetrating, powerful writing." Her hand was dry and warm, her grip well measured, neither overly eager nor coldly distant.

"My father would be delighted to know you've read his books." Jiang Tiantian's tone was sincere, the eyes behind her lenses bright and sparkling, that glow rational yet ardent. "Actually, my coming uninvited today, aside from wanting to meet you, has a very specific purpose—I've recently been preparing a documentary series about contemporary urban women's survival strategies, and I'm also drafting a novel on a related theme. As 'the McKinsey of Feelings,' your entire logical system—how you analyze men and women, teach women to gain precisely in love and marriage, with everything centered on real interest—I find tremendously valuable for research and highly representative of our era. I wonder if there might be a chance later to have a systematic, in-depth conversation with you?"

Yao Yuan looked at the undisguised inquisitiveness in her eyes, and that sense of kinship in placing emotion, too, under a rational analytical framework, and the little pleasure of being understood by one of her own kind rippled gently open inside her. She liked being treated this way—as a "methodology supplier," a "strategist," rather than simply an "emotional mentor" or a "beautiful woman." "Of course," she smiled, that smile a precisely calculated blend of propriety and openness. "I look forward to talking with you too, Miss Jiang. After all, helping women live with more clarity and lose out less—that's a good thing."

Everyone took their seats. As host, Jin Lan took the head of the table, Yao Yuan to her right, and Jiang Tiantian sat down quite naturally beside Yao Yuan. Zhao Yiming sat to Jin Lan's left, followed by the Wang couple and Gao Jieyuan; Chen Ye and Liu Qingzhuo sat on the window side, their seats very close together.

The cold dishes were already on the table. On the delicate platters were brine-cured duck liver, drunken shrimp, osmanthus-sweet lotus root, cold-dressed jellyfish head, and four-happiness baked gluten—elegantly plated, enticing in color. Attendants came forward to pour the drinks; tonight Moutai had been laid out, along with a white Burgundy, and freshly squeezed juice.

Jin Lan raised her glass and stood. "Come, everyone—on the first day of the new year, the fact that we can gather here is fate itself. I drink a toast to you all. Happy New Year, and may everything go your way!"

Everyone rose, glasses clinking, the crisp sounds running together into one. "Happy New Year!" "Wishing Sister Lan's business roaring success!" "Good health to all!"

The first glass down, the mood at the table came alive at once.

Wang Qian was forever the best at warming a room. He filled his second glass, stood, and toasted Jin Lan. "Boss Jin, this Yellow River Tower just gets grander and grander! I hear last month you hosted several foreign delegations? Amazing! I drink to you—may the new year bring you wealth rolling in, and may you turn this 'First Tower of the Ten-Thousand-Mile Yellow River' into the 'First Tower of China'!"

Jin Lan laughed and drained her glass. "No one talks like you!"

Wang Qian turned to Yao Yuan, his smile carrying a few degrees more mischief, but a genuine admiration deep in his eyes. "Yuanyuan, this glass I have to drink to you alone. That dance you did last time at the Crimson Night Palace—after my wife saw the little clip I secretly filmed, she went on about it for days, said she absolutely had to meet you in person. Today she's finally got her chance." He turned to pull his wife alongside him. "Come on, dear, aren't you Teacher Yao's diehard fan? Quick, say something."

Mrs. Wang's face went slightly red. A little embarrassed, she stood, holding a glass of juice—she'd said earlier she was driving, so no alcohol. She looked at Yao Yuan, eyes bright and shining, her tone sincere to the point of being endearing, even carrying a touch of the nervousness of meeting an idol. "Teacher Yao, I really am an old fan of yours. From your very first livestreams, I've followed every episode, and I've bought all your courses. I—I especially love your whole system about the 'marriage-partner mindset' and 'emotional cost-benefit accounting'—it simply opened the door to a new world for me! I always used to think talking feelings hurts your wallet and talking money hurts your feelings, but after listening to you I finally understood: feelings that don't account for interest are castles in the air!" The more she spoke the more worked up she got, cheeks flushing. "And there's what you said—'a woman should be a shareholder in her marriage, not an employee.' I got so many of my girlfriends to come listen, and now we've got a group where we discuss every day how to apply your methods…"

Everyone at the table laughed, the laughter carrying different shades of meaning. Wang Qian clutched his chest theatrically. "So, dear, you've been treating Teacher Yao's words as a war manual—taking them home to use against me?"

"That's called jointly building a happy home and optimizing our cooperation model," Mrs. Wang shot back, glaring at him, righteous, then turned to Yao Yuan with worshipful eyes. "Teacher Yao, I really think you're brilliant. You're not the kind who teaches women to give foolishly, or to be relentlessly independent—you teach how to win smartly within a relationship, so both people benefit, while making sure you don't lose out yourself. That's what's truly high-level. This glass is for you! I'll use tea in place of wine—please, as you like!"

Yao Yuan was amused by this sincere yet precise "fan confession," a flicker of warmth at being understood rising in her heart. She lifted her glass and looked earnestly at Mrs. Wang. "Thank you for understanding it so thoroughly. That you can apply the theory in your own life and form a healthy dynamic with your partner—that in itself proves the method works. This glass is for you—and for every smart woman who knows how to plan shrewdly for herself, and how to run a win-win relationship too."

The two drank to each other. As Mrs. Wang sat down, an excited flush still on her face, she secretly tugged Wang Qian's sleeve and whispered, "I drank with my idol! And she even praised me!"

Wang Qian patted her hand indulgently, leaned to her ear and said something. Mrs. Wang gave him a light punch, and the two exchanged a smile. The scene was warm and natural, and watching it, a faint, almost imperceptible satisfaction crossed the depths of Yao Yuan's eyes. See—her "methodology" was flowering and bearing fruit in the real world, creating a template of "happiness" that looked traditional but was brand-new at its core.

The hot dishes began to come out. Yellow River Tower's signature dishes were brought up one by one: steamed Yellow River carp, braised pork knuckle, crabmeat lion's-head meatballs, Wensi tofu soup, "boiled-water" cabbage… each one exquisite and meticulous, the aromas spreading everywhere.

The toasting and exchanging of cups went on around the table, lively beyond measure. Gao Jieyuan made an energetic round of toasts, and when he reached Yao Yuan he deliberately poured himself a full glass of Moutai, eyes blazing as they fixed on the tassels swaying at her ears. "President Yao, new year, new beginnings—this glass I'll drain, and you, as you like. I wish you a new year of growing ever more beautiful, your career climbing another level, and you carrying on as the… guiding light in all our hearts!" With that he tipped his head back and drank it dry, showing off the empty bottom of his glass.

Yao Yuan smiled and took a small sip. "Thank you, Young Boss Gao. 'Guiding light' is too much—I just share a few takeaways, that's all."

"Hey, that won't do—I drained mine, how can you go as you like?" Gao Jieyuan wouldn't let it slide and moved to pour her more.

"Jieyuan, Yuanyuan doesn't hold her liquor well—the gesture's enough." Jin Lan spoke up, her tone smiling, though it sounded more like fanning the flames.

But Yao Yuan picked up the decanter herself, the gold bracelet sliding down, and filled her own glass to seven-tenths. She raised it toward Gao Jieyuan, the yellow diamond glittering at her throat. "Since Young Boss Gao is giving me such face, of course I'll keep you company for one. But 'guiding light' is too heavy—I prefer the position of 'strategy supplier.'" With that, under everyone's watching eyes, she drank the white liquor down steadily. From throat to stomach ran a line of heat, but her expression was beautifully controlled—only the flush on her cheeks deepened a little, the gleam in her eyes growing more limpid, reflecting the room's lights and the sheen of her gold gown, with a kind of heart-stopping splendor.

"Bravo!" Wang Qian applauded.

Gao Jieyuan's eyes lit up further, and he was about to say something more when Yao Yuan had already turned to Jiang Tiantian beside her, raising her glass. "Miss Jiang, this is for you. Thank you, and thank your father for his work, and thank you for your attention to this field of 'emotional strategy.'"

Jiang Tiantian hurriedly raised her glass, the eyes behind her lenses flashing a sharp light. "Teacher Yao, this is exactly what I want to ask you about in depth. You encourage marriage, you encourage having children—in the current climate of a certain 'radical singlehood' or 'fear of marriage and childbearing,' that seems like a 'clear stream' running against the current. And yet your core is entirely rational calculation and the safeguarding of interest. How do you grasp the balance point between these? Or rather, how do you explain this 'positive pragmatism' to your audience?"

The question thrown out, the table went quiet for an instant. Even Chen Ye, who was ladling soup for Liu Qingzhuo, stopped moving, and Zhao Yiming set down his chopsticks too, turning a calm gaze toward Yao Yuan, as if he too were waiting for an answer outside any algorithm.

Yao Yuan gently swirled the amber liquor in her glass, the gold polish on her fingertips mirrored against it. She mused a moment before speaking, her voice clear and steady, carrying that tone peculiar to her livestreams—soothing and intensely persuasive at once.

"I encourage marriage and childbearing because, under the existing social structure and biological reality, for the vast majority of ordinary women, this is still a path of relatively lower resistance, with potentially higher overall returns—emotional support, mutual economic aid, shared risk, the realization of reproductive value, and so on. But that absolutely does not mean letting women walk into it blindly, sacrificing themselves." She paused, her gaze sweeping over those at the table before landing back on Jiang Tiantian's intent face. "My core is this: you should go in, but not go in blind; you should give, but not give for nothing; you should pursue a long-term relationship, but you must make sure the terms of that relationship favor you. Marriage is a special kind of partnership project; childbearing is a major joint investment. What a woman needs to do is run that project with a partner's clear eyes, and evaluate that investment with an investor's caution. Use feeling as the lubricant; use law and practical means as the safeguard. Not losing out in the middle is the precondition for this game being playable at all. If a relationship is destined to leave you worse off, then it should never have begun in the first place."

Jiang Tiantian was recording rapidly in her phone's memo, fingers flying, and asked without lifting her head, "And love? Pure emotional attraction—how much weight does it carry in your model?"

"Emotional attraction is the ticket in, the start-up capital." Yao Yuan smiled, that smile rational to the point of coldness. "But it isn't the capital for ongoing operations. A long-term partnership relies on matched value, clear rules, and the steady accumulation of shared interest. Love matters very much—it makes all the hardship sweet to swallow—but it can't be used to pay the bills, and it can't serve as compensation when the other party breaches. So I teach women to cherish love, but not to be superstitious about it; to wield love, but not to be held hostage by it."

The table fell silent for a few seconds, only the soft bubbling of the lamb hotpot base.

"Brilliant." Jin Lan was the first to applaud, her eyes full of admiration. "Couldn't be clearer! I used to be far too ruled by my feelings!"

Mrs. Wang nodded over and over too, looking deeply moved.

Gao Jieyuan stroked his chin, his smile a little playful. "President Yao, this theory of yours sends a chill down us men's backs. So from now on, to start a relationship, we have to pass your 'risk assessment' first?"

"A quality candidate like Young Boss Gao would naturally pass the highest standard of assessment." Yao Yuan deflected the force with ease, raising her glass toward him, her smile flawless.

Jiang Tiantian let out a long breath, lifted her head, her eyes ardent. "Teacher Yao, this system of yours is more… real, more powerful, than any women's narrative I've encountered before. It doesn't shout empty slogans—it provides an actionable action map. I absolutely must find time to do an in-depth interview with you and lay it all out systematically."

"Welcome anytime." Yao Yuan smiled and lightly touched her glass to Jiang Tiantian's, the gold earring tassels swaying.

The topic was ignited. The women began discussing specific "strategies": how to negotiate a prenuptial agreement effectively without hurting feelings; how to structure family assets to safeguard women's interests; how to secure a partner's substantive support rather than verbal concern before and after childbirth; even how to identify a "high cost-performance" partner. The men joined in too—Wang Qian shared his takeaways as an "object of assessment," Chen Ye spoke gently about "building an equal, contractual intimate relationship," and Gao Jieyuan half-jokingly complained that "these days you have to hand over a business plan just to court a girl."

Amid this conversation woven of reason and revelry, Chen Ye and Liu Qingzhuo's interactions looked especially natural and sweet, yet faintly carried a "deal closed" understanding. Chen Ye would attentively spin around the dish Liu Qingzhuo wanted to eat, listen seriously when she spoke, occasionally adding a quiet line or two that often cut right to the point. When Liu Qingzhuo got animated, she'd unconsciously pat Chen Ye's arm, and Chen Ye would only smile and let her, his eyes holding the indulgence of someone holding the winning hand. Once, when Liu Qingzhuo's glass was nearly empty, Chen Ye quite naturally took up the bottle and filled it for her, his fingers lightly brushing the back of her hand; Liu Qingzhuo lifted her eyes to him, and the two exchanged a smile—a smile that, beyond affection, seemed to hold a mutual, knowing grasp of each other's terms, pace, and progress.

"That's enough out of you two," Gao Jieyuan said sourly. "How long have you even been together, and you're already this in sync? Could you spare a thought for those of us still single, stuck at the 'due diligence' stage?"

Liu Qingzhuo arched a brow, threw an arm around Chen Ye's, lifted her chin, her smile vivid and crisp. "What, jealous? If you're jealous, go find someone yourself—just like Teacher Yao says, assess properly and strike precisely."

Held by her, the tips of Chen Ye's ears went slightly red, but his smile stayed gentle and tolerant. He said nothing, only patting the back of her hand lightly—everything understood without a word.

"Find someone how? Haven't all the good women already evolved by your standards?" Gao Jieyuan said, his gaze drifting toward Yao Yuan, faintly, with a challenging edge.

Yao Yuan was sipping soup in small mouthfuls with a silver spoon, as if she hadn't heard. Only a very faint curve at the corner of her lips showed she'd taken it all in.

After three rounds of wine, the mood at the table grew warmer still. Zhao Yiming, rarely, took the initiative to raise his glass, toasting Jin Lan and Wang Qian, then turning to Yao Yuan. His voice stayed steady and clear amid the noise. "President Yao, I'll drink to you too. Thank you for the data you provided, and… the line of thinking."

Yao Yuan knew he meant the AI model and their last conversation. She raised her glass and touched it to his, the rims meeting with a crisp ring. "You're welcome. Any progress?"

"Sort of, and sort of not." Zhao Yiming drained his glass, set it down without changing expression, and pushed up his glasses. "On the technical side, we rebuilt the emotional firewall and adjusted the weight parameters, trying to frame emotional responses with a stricter 'contract logic.' But the problem persists." He paused, seeming to search for a way a layperson could understand. "Your data—or rather, the kind of highly rational, interest-driven emotional decision-making mode you represent—produces a strong rejection reaction when fused with the more 'humanized' empathy model we're trying to build. It's like…" He thought for a moment. "Like an extremely precise business logic trying to understand a poem. It can analyze the poem's meter, word frequency, the probability of an image appearing—but it can't understand why the combination of these elements moves people. Your data lacks that 'overflow that can't be computed.'"

Yao Yuan swirled her glass, the ice bobbing in the golden liquid. She turned it over thoughtfully. "Maybe that 'overflow' is unnecessary noise to begin with—even harmful. At least in an emotional model that pursues stable returns. A person is moved by a poem, and then what? After the emotion, life still has to go on, the bills still have to be paid, the relationship still has to be run. If your AI truly wants to simulate 'successful' emotional interaction, perhaps it should focus on learning how to efficiently reach cooperative goals and create positive experiences—not on simulating those useless 'moments of being moved' that lower decision-making efficiency."

Zhao Yiming looked at her, the eyes behind his lenses deep, as if observing some complex algorithm through her. "But your livestream data shows that the cases you consider 'successful'—those women who gained stable relationships and satisfying lives—weren't entirely without that 'overflow' along the way. They too had irrational impulses, uneconomical concessions, even foolish trust."

"That's part of the strategy." Yao Yuan smiled, the smile against the foil of her gold gown taking on a metallic quality. "Precise calculation needs a little smoke screen of sentiment. Complete rationality puts people on guard; a measured dose of 'irrationality' is what lowers the other party's defenses and brings the cooperation about. It's called the 'emotional lubricant'—the dosage and the timing matter. What your AI needs to learn isn't to 'possess' emotion, but to 'simulate' and 'wield' it, just like wielding any other tool."

Zhao Yiming was silent for a few seconds, then suddenly gave a very faint laugh, and that smile looked oddly out of place on his usually calm face. "Interesting. To instrumentalize and parameterize emotion as well. That actually happens to align with my latest cross-disciplinary line of thinking. I've noted it down."

Their conversation was cut off by Wang Qian's booming voice. "What are you two on about, so deep? AI this, emotion that—today's New Year's Day, no talking work allowed! Drink! President Zhao, I drink to you—thank you for leaving us men a way to survive, for not turning us into AIs too!"

Everyone burst out laughing. Yao Yuan and Zhao Yiming exchanged a glance and raised their glasses to join the next lively round of toasts. No one noticed that, as Zhao Yiming tipped his head back to drink, his gaze again flicked swiftly across the glittering yellow diamond at Yao Yuan's throat, a deeply focused light in his eyes—the kind a scientist holds when facing an unsolved riddle—flashing past in an instant.

This meal went on for nearly three hours. By the end the table was a wreck of cups and plates, several wine bottles emptied. Everyone had drunk a fair amount, faces flushed with the heat of alcohol, laughter loud, eyes hazy, the carefully maintained social masks of ordinary days loosening before the alcohol and familiar friends.

When they stepped out of Yellow River Tower, it was already past nine. The winter night's cold wind was knife-keen, striking their faces and instantly sweeping away the stifling heat pent up in the room, jolting their dazed heads alert. Yellow River Tower glowed from top to bottom in the night, the strings of red lanterns hung from its upturned eaves swinging violently in the wind, casting a warm, shifting halo that, in everyone's tipsy eyes, ran together into a flowing river of light. The distant river surface was ink-black, reflecting the cold bands of light from the high-rises on the far bank, glittering and rippling like a handful of crushed diamonds scattered into a bottomless abyss.

Suddenly, a thunderous "boom" tore through the cold night sky.

"It's the municipal New Year's fireworks show," Jin Lan explained with a smile, pulling her stole tighter, her face especially vivid amid the flickering brilliance. "Every year, set off right across the river. This spot of ours is the perfect viewing position."

Everyone stopped, lifted their heads, and fell silent. Even the noisiest one, Gao Jieyuan, shut his mouth for the moment. Riotous colors burst, dissolved, and burst again in everyone's pupils. Yao Yuan tilted her neck back slightly, that beautiful line fully exposed to the shifting sky-light. The tassels at her ears and the yellow diamond at her throat flared with each explosion's flash into one sharp, fleeting glitter, then sank back into darkness, waiting for the next illumination. A corner of her gold skirt was lifted by the river wind, then settled. The fireworks' splendor flowed across her face, dyeing her meticulously drawn makeup with dreamlike colors, and those eyes—always clear, always calculating, always scrutinizing—now reflected a sky full of fleeting, magnificent nothingness, showing for once a rare, almost bewildered absorption. She was watching the fireworks; and what Gao Jieyuan, quietly lifting his phone, froze in his frame was her radiance set off against the fireworks—a luxurious, fragile beauty.

Zhao Yiming stood a little apart, looking neither at the fireworks nor at Yao Yuan. He looked at the place across the river where the fireworks rose, at those points of light that turned to ash the instant after lighting the sky, pushed up his glasses, and no one knew what he was thinking. Chen Ye lightly held Liu Qingzhuo's shoulder, and Liu Qingzhuo leaned into his arms, head tipped back to the sky, a smile of pure appreciation on her face. Jiang Tiantian narrowed her eyes slightly, her lenses catching the light; what she watched seemed to be not the fireworks themselves but this crowd watching them, this scene, this atmosphere—like a cool-headed field researcher.

Ten minutes later, the last enormous, all-color umbrella-shaped firework blazed at the highest point, then wilted soundlessly. The night sky returned to a settled black, leaving only a faint, somewhat acrid smell of gunpowder smoke drifting in thin wisps through the bitter wind—proof that the splendid dream just past had not been a collective hallucination.

Wang Qian glanced at his watch, drew his wife's shoulder close, his voice carrying the hoarseness and warmth of drink. "Well, we have to head off first. The little ancestor at home is still waiting—they've called several times already."

Mrs. Wang was reluctant to part, glancing again at Yao Yuan, her cheeks ruddy from the wind. "Teacher Yao, I'm so sorry—let's gather again when there's a chance. C-can I take another photo with you? Just one!"

Yao Yuan nodded with a smile. "Of course."

Mrs. Wang immediately stood at Yao Yuan's side, and Wang Qian took out his phone, its screen light illuminating two differently smiling faces—one the excitement and contentment of meeting an idol, the other the usual, perfect, well-mannered smile. The gold skirt and the cream knit dress pressed together, like two utterly different worlds curiously sharing one frame.

"All right, all right, off you go—the child comes first," Jin Lan urged.

The Wang couple said their goodbyes several times over, then walked off arm in arm toward the parking lot, their figures soon vanishing into the night.

Chen Ye and Liu Qingzhuo came over too. Liu Qingzhuo still had the excited afterglow of the fireworks on her face, the tip of her nose a little red from the cold, which only added to her charm. Clutching Chen Ye's arm tightly, she said to the group with a smile, "We're off too. Chen Ye says he'll take me to that new warm-pavilion lounge by the river for a cup of mulled wine, clear off the drink, and along the way… talk over some plans for next year." As she spoke she lifted her eyes to Chen Ye, who returned a gentle smile carrying the ease of someone with everything in hand.

"Look at you two, moving fast," Gao Jieyuan teased.

"Efficiency is the primary productive force." Liu Qingzhuo arched a brow, her smile crisp—a double meaning in every word.

Chen Ye nodded to the group. "Happy New Year. We'll go on ahead."

The two left leaning close together, their figures stretched long in the mingled light of the streetlamps and distant neon—sweet, intimate, and carrying the solidity of a strong-meets-strong alliance.

Five remained: Yao Yuan, Jin Lan, Zhao Yiming, Gao Jieyuan, and Jiang Tiantian.

The cold wind seemed stronger now, scraping across their burning faces, bringing a stinging kind of clarity. The alcohol still burned inside them, driving off the chill and stirring a restlessness that wanted to prolong the revelry, to resist the lonely cold of being alone after the party broke up. Jin Lan rubbed her hands, pulled her fur stole tighter, and her eyes, amid the lingering smoke of the fireworks and the buzz of drink, turned and lit up. "Breaking up now is no fun. It's only just past nine—nightlife is just getting started. I know a new place, Liuyue Palace, supposedly fantastic—atmosphere, music, drinks, all top-tier. How about it, move the party there?"

Gao Jieyuan was the first to respond, almost impatiently. "We absolutely have to go! Anything Sister Lan recommends is sure to be good! On a freezing night like this, we've got to find somewhere lively to keep warm!" His gaze drifted to Yao Yuan again, that heat more scorching than the drink.

Zhao Yiming said nothing, only turning his gaze to Yao Yuan, as if waiting for her decision—or perhaps it made no difference to him where they went or what they did; he was simply going with the flow.

Jiang Tiantian looked at Yao Yuan too, pushing up her glasses, a clear research drive in her eyes—she had little interest in the club itself, but she was deeply interested in the subject of "the behavioral patterns of Yao Yuan, the Godmother of Feelings, in a nightclub social setting." It was an excellent field site for close observation.

Yao Yuan felt the night wind passing through her hair, brushing across the cold metal at her ears; the slightly tipsy warmth of the alcohol inside her and the restlessness of blood racing faster; that complex, brimming feeling in her chest of having been excited, praised, watched, briefly shaken by the fireworks, then jolted awake by the cold wind. She liked control, liked being surrounded, liked to observe, and also liked, now and then, to throw herself into a sensory current that required no excessive thought. She curved her red lips, and that smile, in the city night settling over them once more, was as brilliant as the diamond at her throat.

"Let's go."
