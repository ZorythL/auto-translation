Spring Festival: A Team-Building Reward in Phuket
The twenty-third day of the twelfth lunar month, Little New Year. The fine snow over Jinshi hadn't yet settled on the ground before the heat rising off the airport runway steamed it into a thin mist.
Zhou Yifan's flight back from Shanghai landed at dusk. While waiting for his luggage, he switched on his phone, and Yao Yuan's message had already popped up in the family group chat: "@everyone This Spring Festival we're relocating to Phuket — company team-building plus family trip, seven days, six nights. Ten a.m. on the 28th, meet at the airport."
Below it was attached a minimalist itinerary and a list of notes. The per-person budget was clearly marked: thirty thousand yuan. A chartered direct flight, five-star the whole way, the schedule paced with a good mix of tight and loose — the first two days for free exploration, then sailing trips, a Thai cooking class, and Thai massage woven through the middle, and a complete switch-off at the end.
The group chat was quickly buried under cheering emojis from the staff. Zhou Yifan looked at the screen, the corner of his mouth curving up. This was so very Yao Yuan — make the reward tangible, make the experience top-tier. She wanted everyone who followed her to see it: effort had a price, and the price was clear.
He wheeled his suitcase out into the arrivals hall and spotted Yao Yuan at a glance among the crowd waiting to greet passengers. A cream-white cashmere coat, her long hair loosely pinned up, she was looking down at her phone. In the cold white airport lights, her profile had a quiet sharpness to it. Two years apart, and the sense of control she carried had settled into something like an aura.
"Sis." Zhou Yifan walked over.
Yao Yuan looked up, her eyes brightening for a moment, and she naturally took a small bag from his hand. "You've lost weight. Is the lab wearing you down?"
"It's worth it." Zhou Yifan looked at her. At twenty-two, the young man already stood much taller than she did, and the rigorous training of medical school had given him an unconscious clinical eye when he watched people. "The dark circles under your eyes — foundation can't hide them. Up all night staring at data again?"
Yao Yuan raised an eyebrow. "You don't even have your medical license yet, and you've already learned to diagnose by looking?"
The two of them headed toward the parking lot. The car was warm inside, and as Yao Yuan drove out of the airport she said, "The company beat its targets by thirty-eight percent this year. Time to let the team see something of the world. Thirty-odd people plus our family — I chartered a business jet. Mom and Dad are all set; it's just Uncle Zhou—"
She paused, a little exasperated. "He's fretting over the shop's Spring Festival business and doesn't want to go."
"And then you talked him into it?" Zhou Yifan fastened his seatbelt.
"Of course." Yao Yuan flicked the turn signal. "I told him to hand the shop over to the manager and do a video reconciliation of the books every day. This year's team-building theme is 'how to spend the money once you've earned it.' You're going to be a doctor — all the more reason to see how this world actually runs. It's not just operating rooms and labs; there are far more complicated lesions and compensation mechanisms inside the human heart."
She said it calmly, the streaming lights outside the window sweeping across her face. Zhou Yifan suddenly felt that his sister was opening another window onto the human world for him, in her own particular way — not a window of pathology, but of the dynamics of human nature.
Talking Zhou Lufei around had indeed taken some doing.
"Seven days! The seven best days for business!" In the back kitchen of Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot, Zhou Lufei's knife flashed through the lamb almost too fast to see. "And Yuanyuan, how much is this going to cost? Thirty thousand a head — that's a hundred and twenty thousand for the four of us! How much does this shop of mine make, slaving away dawn to dusk for half a year?"
Yao Yuan leaned against the counter, unhurried. "Uncle Zhou, what was the shop's net profit last year?"
"Around a hundred and eighty thousand." His hands never stopped, and there was a solid pride in his tone, earned over the better part of a lifetime. From the little eight-table place it once was, to this three-story new-Chinese-style building today — "Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot" in gold letters on black was a name to reckon with along the whole restaurant street. It was the most tangible achievement of his sixty-five years.
"My company's after-tax net profit last year was this much." Yao Yuan made a gesture.
Zhou Lufei's knife froze. He turned to look at her, eyes wide, and even forgot to set down the handsome lamb hindquarter in his hand.
"Money can never all be earned." Yao Yuan's voice softened. "But you're sixty-five, Uncle Zhou. You and my mom have been married twenty-five years. You raised me, and then you had Yifan. What's the farthest place the two of you have ever been? Beijing? Or Hangzhou, the year I graduated and took you there?"
Zhou Lufei fell silent, the rhythm of his blade against the cutting board slowing.
"Money is only living water when it moves. Sitting in a bank card, it's just a number. Spent on experiences and on broadening your horizons, it becomes life." Yao Yuan went on. "This trip isn't only for fun. I had my assistant contact two high-end Thai restaurant groups. They have hotpot brands under them that are doing quite well, and we can go take a look at their back kitchens, have a chat. Don't you want to know how a Thai hotpot supply chain is managed, how the customer experience is designed?"
That hit Zhou Lufei right where it itched. He set down the knife and wiped his hands. "We can really see other people's kitchens?"
"We can." Yao Yuan smiled. "I've arranged it all. And besides, it's about time my mom got out and saw the world too. In her whole life, she's never once stepped outside the country."
That last line finally broke down Zhou Lufei's resistance. He rubbed his hands and gave a slightly sheepish laugh. "All right then, I'll go along with you. I'll keep your mom company too, get out and have a look around... Oh, right, your sister's side—" he meant the daughter left to him by his late first wife, five years older than Yao Yuan, now working at the provincial Commerce Department, long since married with a child of her own. "I've already told her. They'll spend their own holiday with their little family this year and not worry about us."
The twenty-eighth day of the twelfth lunar month, the VIP departure lounge at Jinshi Airport.
More than thirty core staff from Yao Yuan's company, plus her own family — a team of thirty-eight — had assembled. The younger employees talked in low voices, unable to hold back their excitement. Several managers gathered around Yao Yuan, confirming the last details.
Zhou Yifan sat quietly beside his parents. Zhang Fengxia wore a newly bought crimson wool sweater, her hair carefully permed, looking bright and lively. Zhou Lufei, by contrast, was a little stiff, forever straightening the jacket he so rarely wore.
"Nervous?" Zhou Yifan asked quietly.
"A bit." Zhou Lufei admitted it honestly. "Such a grand setup... your sister, these days — she really is something."
Zhang Fengxia shot him a glance. "Your daughter's done well for herself, and you still can't relax?"
"It's not that I can't relax..." Zhou Lufei scratched his head. "I just feel like I'm dreaming."
Yao Yuan came over and crouched in front of Zhang Fengxia, helping her arrange her scarf. "Mom, when the plane climbs and descends, if your ears bother you, chew on these." She handed over a box of mints.
"I know, go on, go do your thing." Zhang Fengxia patted her hand, pride she couldn't hide in her eyes.
The Thai Airways charter landed in Phuket in the early afternoon. The tropical wind, wrapped in a salty dampness, rushed at their faces, and in an instant the northern winter was thrown far behind them.
Three luxury coaches took the team straight to the five-star resort on Kata Beach. Yao Yuan had chosen the hotel herself — standalone villas and pool suites scattered just so, the balance between privacy and sociability struck perfectly. The staff stayed two to a small villa; Yao Yuan's family had the four-bedroom villa with a private pool, tucked away in the deepest part of the grounds.
After check-in, there was a brief gathering. Yao Yuan stood in the open-plan lobby, facing more than thirty young faces.
"Each room has an itinerary and an envelope. Inside the envelope is fifty thousand baht of spending money. For these seven days, there are only three rules. First, stay safe, travel in pairs. Second, put down your work and relax completely. Third—"
She paused, her gaze sweeping over every face. "Experience it, fully. Experience all of this that you earned with your own abilities. Remember how it feels to stay in a good hotel, eat fine ingredients, be surrounded by excellent service. Grow the numbers another thirty percent next year, and we'll go to Japan. The year after, Europe. I want you to know that following me, Yao Yuan, what you earn isn't just money — it's the standing to choose how you live."
The applause was warm. A few of the young women had reddened eyes.
Zhou Yifan stood at the edge of the crowd, watching his sister. In that moment she was a leader, a person who made dreams come true, telling her followers in the most direct way possible: the good things in this world — you deserve them, and you can have them.
The schedule was loose and lavish.
The first day was free time, and the staff went off in pairs to the beach or the spa. Yao Yuan strolled around the resort with her family. Zhou Lufei was especially keen on the hotel kitchen and the restaurant's design, pulling Yao Yuan aside to ask about this and that. Zhang Fengxia and Zhou Yifan fell behind, walking slowly along a path through the coconut palms.
"Your sister's gone all out this time." Zhang Fengxia looked at Yao Yuan's back up ahead and said softly.
"She deserves it," Zhou Yifan said. "And she's willing to give it."
Zhang Fengxia turned her head to look at him. At twenty-two her son had grown fully into a man — in his brows and eyes there was her own stubbornness from her younger days, Zhou Lufei's gentle warmth, and, more than either, a quiet keenness all his own.
"Things going smoothly in Shanghai?"
"Smoothly. My advisor thinks highly of me, and the research is making progress." Zhou Yifan answered, then added, "Just busy. I don't get back often."
"Busy is good." Zhang Fengxia paused. "When your sister was your age... she went through a lot of hardship."
The words were light, but Zhou Yifan understood them. He knew some of his sister's past — not the details, but enough to piece together the outline: those fragments of betrayal, struggle, and rebirth.
"She's doing well now," he said.
"Yes." Zhang Fengxia watched Yao Yuan's upright figure. "She's made herself into a beam of light."
In the evening, the whole family had dinner at a seaside restaurant. The sun set in molten gold, the surf rolling in wave after wave. Zhou Lufei had a little of the local beer and grew talkative.
"This place really is something — good air, and the food's so fresh. I just keep thinking about the shop... Spring Festival is the busiest time for business."
"Didn't you watch the video? Everything's running like clockwork." Zhang Fengxia placed some grilled fish in his bowl. "Just stay put and relax."
Yao Yuan watched them with a smile. Her phone buzzed. A message from Yu Hao: "Happy Spring Festival."
It had been more than a month since their last conversation about "children and marriage." She didn't reply, and locked the screen.
"Sis," Zhou Yifan asked, "the sailing trip tomorrow — did you bring seasickness pills?"
"I did." Yao Yuan put her thoughts away. "Relax, little Doctor Zhou."
After dinner, Zhou Lufei said he wanted a walk along the beach to settle his stomach. Zhang Fengxia hadn't meant to go, but Yao Yuan gave her a gentle nudge. "Mom, keep Uncle Zhou company. The night view is lovely."
The two of them walked slowly along the sand. The night sea was deep blue, the surf gentle. After a while, Zhou Lufei suddenly spoke.
"Fengxia, thank you."
Zhang Fengxia turned to look at him. The streetlamps were dim, his expression a little blurred.
"Thank me for what?"
"A lot of things." Zhou Lufei's voice was a little hoarse. "Thank you for not minding, back then, that I was a man whose wife had died and who came with a daughter. Thank you for being so good to my daughter — she isn't your own flesh and blood, but no one could fault how you've treated her. Thank you for being willing... to give me Yifan."
Zhang Fengxia's steps slowed.
"I'm clumsy with words, I can't say pretty things." Zhou Lufei went on, like words pent up a long time had finally found their way out. "I know I'm not good enough for you. You're beautiful, capable, fiery. When you married me, plenty of people said you'd gotten the short end of it. I just thought — I have to be good to you, good to your daughter, can't ever let you regret it."
The sea breeze drifted by, carrying its salty damp.
"That temper of yours is actually a good thing." Zhou Lufei suddenly laughed. "Straight to the point, nothing hidden, nothing held back. My first one... she was soft, kept everything bottled up inside, and in the end it made her sick. You're the kind who lets it out — it's a relief. When I do something wrong, you scold me straight out, and once you're done, we go on living the way we always did. You don't hold a grudge."
Zhang Fengxia stopped. In the moonlight, the corners of her eyes glistened.
"Old Zhou," she said, very softly, "I have to thank you too."
Zhou Lufei was taken aback.
"Thank you for not minding that I came with Yuanyuan, that 'extra baggage.' Thank you for loving her like your own real daughter — when she was little, if you'd ever raised a hand to her, I'd have come at you, but you never once struck her. The most you ever did was sigh. Thank you... for not abandoning the two of us, mother and daughter, in those hardest years."
She drew a deep breath. "We were both on our second marriage, both carrying people and things from before in our hearts. But for these twenty-five years, you've lived this life with me honestly, no crooked thoughts, no hidden selfishness. Every cent the shop earned, you handed to me. To Yuanyuan you never said a single harsh word. When she went to university, when she started her business, you cared even more than I did. I've kept all of this in mind."
Zhou Lufei's eyes reddened. He turned his face away and blinked hard.
"We're an old married couple — what's the point of saying all this..." he muttered.
"What ought to be said has to be said." Zhang Fengxia took his arm. The gesture made Zhou Lufei stiffen all over — in twenty-five years, she had rarely reached for him like this. "Come on, let's head back. The children will be worrying."
The two of them walked slowly back the way they'd come, their shadows stretching long on the sand, now and then overlapping.
Chinese couples who marry midway through life rarely speak of love. Their affection is tucked into the meals made each day, into the cups of tea carried over when one of them is ill, into answering when the other's child calls them "Dad" or "Mom," into the time earned cent by cent and saved up together. There is nothing grand about it, only the steady, long-flowing burden and companionship.
And this, perhaps, is the most solid kind of deep devotion there is in this ordinary, fire-and-smoke human world.
