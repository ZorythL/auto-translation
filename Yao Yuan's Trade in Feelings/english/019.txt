Chapter 19 — Traveling Through Time Again, Back to Twenty-Six and the Height of Her Romance with Shuai Hongqiang

Yao Yuan got up and sat back down at her desk, her gaze landing on the file at the top of the pile — Shuai Hongqiang's project proposal. Until she had a clear picture of his finances and credit record over the past few years, she would give him no business answer at all. But her thoughts drifted, floating back to that "dream" that had felt at once real and unreal. Could it be... that it really had something to do with that mirror?
She pulled open the drawer and took out the bronze mirror once more.
Yao Yuan examined it closely, turning it over in her hands. Apart from its age, she could find nothing unusual. The body of the mirror was seven or eight centimeters wide, its edges not sharp but warm and smooth as old jade, glazed with a dark blue-brown patina of copper rust. At the center of the back was only a small bridge-shaped knob, ringed by coiling cloud-and-thunder patterns, with a Kui-dragon carved in low relief lying quietly among them.
In the sunlight spilling through the floor-to-ceiling glass, it took on a kind of muted refinement. She studied it again and again, and the pad of her finger, without thinking, brushed across the faintly concave surface of the mirror —
And in that instant, the light in the room seemed to give a soundless twist.
A faint, thin dragon's cry, like something rising from the bottom of a very deep well of years, swept past her ear. Yao Yuan's breath caught, and she flipped the bronze mirror over in a flash. The Kui-dragon on the back came suddenly alive in the shadow, and those carved eyes seemed to glint with a ghostly light —
Before she could even feel afraid, a vast, irresistible pull — eerily familiar — seized her soul and wrenched it hard.
"Ngh..."
A stifled groan caught in her throat. Everything in her field of vision — the desk, the screen, the daylight outside the window — was instantly stretched into warped streams of color, then in the next second shattered into glittering fragments, sucked into a silently howling vortex. Her body lost all weight; her consciousness kept plunging and tumbling through the chaos...
She had no idea how long it lasted.
Stillness.
A stillness absolute and complete.
Beneath her was a hard flat surface, smooth, icy, without a trace of warmth.
Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open.
White. The ceiling was white, the ground beneath her was white, and on every side, endlessly, was the same even, soft white — yet a white so cold it held no life at all. There was no source of light, yet light was everywhere. She slowly sat up, the feathers on her black knit dress trembling faintly — still the outfit she'd worn for the livestream.
This was not a dream.
This was not a dream. If it were a dream, then she had been here once before, in her last dream.
"Hello? Hello? Is anyone there?" Still, she tried calling out a few times. The sound came from her, but there was no echo and no answer, as though it had been swallowed whole by this pure white.
She stood and looked around. As she had last time, she ventured forward, testing. After a few steps, the calm white "air" ahead of her suddenly rippled outward —
A complete, vivid scene rose soundlessly out of the void.
It was a street of antique shops. The afternoon sun, sliding toward the west, lay warm over the gray flagstones, and the air carried that particular faint warmth and languor of the turn from spring to summer. A man stood with his back to the "camera," before an antique shop hung with an old plaque, his head turned slightly as he talked with the owner behind the counter. His bearing was tall and straight. He wore a pure cotton green shirt, crisp with a touch of American ruggedness, the sleeves casually rolled up to his forearms, baring a length of firm wrist. Around that wrist, loosely wound, was a pale yellow bracelet.
The strand he'd carelessly wound around his wrist was a star-and-moon bodhi mala of superb quality. The beads were not the harsh white of fresh seeds, but had been worked to a warm, creamy honey base — even and mellow in tone, with a soft matte sheen. On each bead, the "moon" eye was clear and well placed, the "star" points fine as scattered ink, sparse and well spaced — exactly the "bright moon, sparse stars" quality that collectors prize. The surface had already formed a thin, glossy, glass-like patina that flowed with a restrained luminescence in the warm afternoon sun, looking at once elegant and full of life.
What drew the eye most were the accent beads worked in among them. A round bead of high-porcelain, high-blue turquoise served as the head bead, its color a deep blue like a frozen lakeheart, its texture pure and dense; against the honey-colored star-and-moon beads it looked all the more cool and noble, like a fleck of icy soul dropped into a warm current. On either side were spacer slices of persimmon-red nanhong agate to set it off — a red full and lustrous, vivid without being garish, like ripe cherries or refined cinnabar, brimming with a warm, auspicious quality. The "cold blue" of the turquoise and the "warm red" of the nanhong agate, against the mellow base of the star-and-moon bodhi, struck a clever clash and balance. They held each other in check and yet completed each other — the cold of the turquoise quietly tempered by the warmth of the nanhong, the ardor of the nanhong gently pressed down by the calm of the turquoise — together stringing up a visual rhythm like a "song of ice and fire," striking and arresting yet never losing its overall harmony and high taste. It showed that its owner had spent not only on the materials but also a great deal of thought on the aesthetics of the pairing.
The man seemed quite pleased with the bodhi beads, his fingertips absently rolling one bead, though his gaze had already turned to another pair of objects on the counter. "These walnuts... the grain's solid enough, and the matching's passable, but I have my doubts about your 'Mentougou muffled-tip lionhead' story." His voice was low, carrying the unhurried fussiness only an insider has. "The base navel isn't quite straight, and the tips are muffled in a way that feels a bit deliberate. Old-tree fruit? Looks more to me like a later, man-doctored 'official-cap' lineage. The skin's a touch stiff — it doesn't have that natural, aged richness."
The shop owner was a lean, middle-aged man. He took no offense at this, just gave a "heh heh" laugh, baring teeth stained yellow by smoke. "You've got a sharp eye. But these days, where are you going to find old-tree muffled-tips that are so 'flawless'? This pair — the body shape, the grain, the weight — they're all rare for this price range. If you're a serious player, I'll give you an honest price..."
The man neither agreed nor refused. He gave the pair of deep-red, oily-bright walnuts a light tap with his fingertips, drawing out a crisp "click," as though still weighing it.
Just then, the brass bell hung on the antique shop's carved door — patterned with interlocking lotus vines — gave a light "ding-a-ling."
A young woman walked out from inside the shop, and the warm golden afternoon sun wrapped around her in an instant, as if plating her in a flowing honeyed light. The moment she appeared, even the somewhat worn street scene around her seemed to brighten a few shades. She wore a true-red silk diagonal-collared off-shoulder top — that red, an utterly pure, utterly intense cinnabar red, set off the bare skin of her shoulders, neck, and arms, white as frost and outshining snow. The top was cut to fit perfectly, tracing the slender, supple line of her waist and her full curves; the collar and front were embroidered in matching silk thread with intricate hidden patterns of interlocking vines that shimmered faintly in the light, classical yet possessed of an aggressively striking beauty.
Below she wore black high-waisted, slightly flared suit trousers with a superb drape, flowing smoothly from her waist down to the tops of her shoes, lengthening her figure to perfection; as she walked, her steps stirred the air with an unwitting, swaying allure. On her feet was a pair of simple black thin-strap heels, her bare ankles slender and fair. Her long hair was not carelessly tied up but coiled into a low bun, slightly loose yet brimming with charm, a few lazy curls falling at her neck. At her temple was pinned a pearl-tassel hairpin of vintage design that swayed with her movements, all glimmer and shine. Her makeup was exquisite, her brows and eyes drawn especially clear, her lips a vintage true-red echoing her top. The whole of her was like a moving flame, bright and scorching, in stark contrast to the plain, old-fashioned surroundings — and yet strangely drawing every eye.
In the pure white void, Yao Yuan's breath suddenly stopped.
That face, those brows and eyes, that bearing...
It was unmistakably herself at twenty-seven.
Younger, the face flush with the fullness of youth, the brows and eyes still holding a trace of green softness not yet tempered by the dust of the world, interwoven with that powerfully striking beauty — a beauty uniquely bold and dazzling.
She held a small brocade pouch in her hand and looked up naturally toward the man at the door, the smile spreading at the corners of her mouth bright and bold, alive with the cherished, fearless ease of someone who is loved — plainly intimate and at ease with him.
And now, the Yao Yuan in the pure white space also saw clearly the profile of the man standing at the door — it was Shuai Hongqiang. Shuai Hongqiang at thirty-seven, carved by time to just the right point: the brashness and rawness of a young man shed, his brows and eyes settled into the composure and certainty of a man whose career had taken hold; and yet still far from the age when worldliness soaks through and shows as weariness or oiliness. His features were distinct, his jawline clean and crisp, the bridge of his nose high, a faint not-quite-smile curving at his lips. It was the charm of someone at the full, rising peak of life's arc — a blend of the steadiness experience confers, the confidence success nourishes, and a near-genuine vigor not yet worn down by the grind of living. The star-and-moon bodhi mala on his wrist caught and threw back a warm glow with his movements.
But back then, his "taste" and "hobbies" tended to carry distinct marks of imitation and social climbing. That superb-quality star-and-moon bodhi on his wrist, his fussiness over walnut grain, skin, and provenance, even the slightly fussy, relaxed insider's posture he struck now, talking walnuts with the owner at the antique shop door — the source of it all was perhaps some "business big brother" whose circle he was desperately trying to attach himself to, or at least to enter.
Yao Yuan dimly remembered that, during that period, he had seemed especially keen on attending private tea gatherings and small auctions, and would come back carrying a mingled scent of agarwood and Pu'er tea, dropping words like "patina," "crackle glaze," and "old-age feel" into conversation without thinking. He was clumsily and eagerly tracing the language and symbols of that circle.
Obsession? Perhaps there was a little. But for the Shuai Hongqiang of that time, what he was obsessed with was perhaps not the settled time within the antiques themselves, but what owning, appraising, and discussing them symbolized — a higher-grade badge of identity, closer to the circle of "the successful," a "refined hobby" that served as a knock at the door, setting him apart from the nouveau riche. He was obsessed with anything that could swiftly proclaim "I have style": the oily, glossy strand on his wrist, the walnuts he turned clicking in his palm, that pricey Tao inkstone in his study... All of it, together with his rapidly accumulated wealth and his just-right gentlemanly manner, had successfully "equipped" him into a man of substance, taste, and worldliness. Back then Yao Yuan had wandered with him through every antique market — local and nearby, and even in the cities they traveled to.
When the brass bell rang "ding-a-ling," in the instant he saw Yao Yuan step out the door, an undisguised flash of dazzlement and possessiveness crossed his eyes, and the fussy, appraising look on his face melted at once, replaced by a fervent, proud tenderness. He reached out his hand, utterly naturally, his fingertips lingering as they brushed a strand of hair fallen at her shoulder — a gesture intimate and full of admiration. "Done looking? Find anything you like?" His gaze lingered over her, plainly very pleased with this eye-catching outfit of hers. As if this bold, beautiful woman were one of the "collections" he found most worth showing off, and most proud of, at that moment.
Twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's smile grew, and with a little flush of pride she gave the pouch in her hand a shake. "Just picked up a few cheap little things. You two finished talking?" Her voice was a touch clearer and brighter than it would later become, carrying the vivid spirit particular to that age.
"Just about." Shuai Hongqiang lifted the pair of walnuts in his hand and glanced at the owner again. "I'll think the price over." With that, he reached out, utterly natural, his palm settling steadily against the bare, smooth side of her waist, drawing her gently to his side — a gesture full of possessiveness and protectiveness, and faintly of display too. Yao Yuan leaned into it, her posture relaxed and trusting, even carrying a touch of taking-it-for-granted enjoyment.
And in that very moment, the twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan, held in Shuai Hongqiang's arm, let her gaze sweep absently toward some direction down the antique street. Then, as if sensing some far-off, indistinct presence, she suddenly lifted her head, her line of sight passing over Shuai Hongqiang's shoulder, over the clamorous backdrop of the marketplace, even over the barrier of time — and in those eyes drawn so exquisitely and seductively flickered a barely perceptible flash of sharp, dreamlike bewilderment, utterly out of place with the bright mood around her, like someone just waking from a dream — looking, straight and unobstructed, into the pure white void, at the future self who was watching all of this!
The eyes of the two met, in the turbulent current of time!
Though she knew full well the other could not possibly truly see her, that instant of meeting eyes sent a sudden jolt through the very depths of Yao Yuan's (present) soul!
"Boom——!"
The mind of the Yao Yuan in the pure white space went blank. That red dress, that bold beauty, that intimate posture of being held in a lover's arms... all of it had once been a vivid, warm part of her memory.
"In my last dream... I clearly warned her — the twenty-two-year-old her..."
Thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan stood in the void, murmuring to herself, her voice sounding hollow in the absolute silence. In an earlier, more chaotic and blurred "dream" — one she had once taken for nothing more than a dream — she had, in a vague, frantic form, screamed with all her might at that twenty-two-year-old self deep in her romance with Jiang Hai, trying to turn fate aside — "Leave Jiang Hai, his family will make him give you up! Stay in the capital! Or go abroad to study! Don't go back to Jinshi!"
If the twenty-two-year-old Yao Yuan had heeded her warning, then the track of her life should have changed. At twenty-seven she should have been in the capital or abroad — so how had she still ended up back in Jinshi? Still wearing the red dress Shuai Hongqiang loved best, smiling like a flower in the crook of his arm?
Destiny — could it really be a net already woven, impossible to break free of? No matter how the threads in between trembled, would it all in the end be drawn together toward the same single knot?
If every struggle — even a struggle from the "future" — was futile, then this eerie thing that sent her, the thirty-six-year-old, back into the past, that dragged her time and again into this pure white cage to watch some node of her own life — what was its purpose? Was it the malicious mockery of some god, or some crueler punishment, forcing her to tear open her own healed scars with her own hands?
No, that wasn't right. A cold voice rose from the depths of her mind. If it were only repetition, why would there be a "warning"? And if the "warning" was doomed to be useless, then what meaning did the very existence of the "warning" have?
Carrying that doubt, the black dress thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan had prepared for the livestream felt heavy now as a suit of armor. Powerlessly she watched everything before her, like someone who already knew the entire script, watching a film she had starred in herself yet could not switch off.
She watched them stroll, talk, and laugh on that antique street at the turn of spring into summer in 2016, the very picture of a couple in love. Twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan, bright and bold before thirty-seven-year-old Shuai Hongqiang, like the most dazzling of flowers. As if rewinding a tape, she watched the feng shui master who had read her a fortune of "Nine-Purple Fire Great Luck" appear, as expected, from the other end of the street, doggedly chasing after the twenty-seven-year-old her to tell her fortune; watched her make a fuss, and watched him — the one holding her safe in his arms — laugh. In those days, each of them mirrored the other in their eyes, and they too had once given each other what they believed was their "true heart."
Even if, beneath that true heart, fate had long since planted the foreshadowing that flowed toward different ends.
