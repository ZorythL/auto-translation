Chapter 16 — Crossing Paths, Interwoven Fates

Sunday morning, 9:30.
The clamor of the city flooded in like a rising tide, yet it was deftly sealed out by a carefully maintained quiet. Shuai Hongqiang held the steering wheel steady with both hands, his gaze fixed straight ahead on the snarl of traffic, as if it pierced through the press of cars and looked off toward some unknown distance. In the passenger seat, Lin Wan was bent over her phone, firing off replies to work messages, her nails tapping the screen with a steady click-click, like the monotonous beat of a drum keeping time alone in the silent car. In the back seat, their son Shuai Wenyao had his headphones on, completely lost in his own cartoon world. The cheerful images danced in his innocent eyes, and he was utterly oblivious to the faint pressure of conflict hidden beneath the harmony between his parents up front.
Last night's livestream, those heart-baring words of Lin Wan's, seemed sealed away in the past twelve hours. There was no sharp interrogation, no eruption of argument, not even a single glance to hint at the matter. Like well-trained actors, they played the part of a "loving couple" in perfect, unspoken coordination: he handled drop-offs and pickups, she rushed off to work. Only, beneath this seemingly affectionate surface, things were like a riverbed just scoured by ice water—bare and hard—and every step was taken with care, each one keenly aware of the wariness hidden beneath the calm.
The car stopped in front of a glass-curtained office building near the RT-Mart. "Right here, thanks." Lin Wan's tone was flat as she unbuckled her seatbelt, picked up her handbag, her movements smooth. "Wenyao will call me when class lets out. If I'm in a meeting, just take him straight home."
"Got it." Shuai Hongqiang answered without looking at her. Only after she shut the car door and strode crisply toward the revolving door in her low-heeled ankle boots did he glance at her retreating figure. The oatmeal-colored coat was tailored to fit, making her back look straight and poised. She didn't look back.
He stepped on the gas and merged into traffic. That nameless, stifled feeling welled up in his chest again. She really was busy now—negotiating partnerships, meeting with brands, propping up the "everyday" of this household in real, tangible ways, by the very methods he had once dismissed. And he, who had once been the absolute provider, was now more like a... driver, and a business partner who needed to be informed of the schedule.
Meanwhile, elsewhere.
Yao Yuan drifted awake from a deep, dreamless sleep. Sunlight slipped through the thin gauze curtains, scattering softly over the bed like golden threads, spreading a warm glow across half of it. That afternoon yesterday at her mother's house, the two of them talking honestly, had felt like lifting a great stone that had pressed on her heart for so long; even her breathing had turned light and easy. First she ran a comfortable hot bath, letting the warm water soothe every inch of her skin and chase away the lingering fatigue. Then came a simple but cozy breakfast, followed by a calming yoga session along with a video—as if every crease pent up in her body and mind had been ironed smooth, one by one.
Only in this thoroughly relaxed state did she pick up her phone and begin methodically working through the messages that had piled up. Replies from business partners needed careful weighing, invitations from her girlfriends made her smile, and her colleagues' requests awaited her sign-off... As she scrolled through them one by one, that text from Shuai Hongqiang leapt into view.
The message had been sent at noon the day before, brief but oddly worded: "Yao Yuan, that mirror I delivered yesterday—if you feel anything's off about it, or... if you have any strange dreams, tell me anytime."
Her fingertip hovered above the screen for a few seconds. The chaos and terror she had lived through on the day she traveled through time seeped out again from deep in her memory, thread by thread, bringing a wave of cold vertigo. That plain old bronze mirror... could it really be more than a coincidence? But the night she traveled through time, the mirror had been in a drawer in her office, clearly not with her. So then—was the thing that triggered all these strange events not the physical mirror itself, but some invisible, intangible... mysterious connection?
She shook her head hard, trying to drive these unsettling associations out of her mind. Outside the window, the sun was perfect, the road thick with traffic, a scene of bustling prosperity. On the tablet at her side was the latest data-analysis report on the emotional AI model that Yu Hao had just sent over; the complex charts and code gave off a cold, rational light, as if to remind her that she lived in a world full of science and logic. She was someone exploring the frontier of that solid scientific field—how could she let herself be so easily seized by these groundless, vaporous metaphysical guesses? The idea was simply too absurd.
Right—it had to be a hallucination, surely just too much stress lately causing a brief cognitive scramble. She thought it with conviction; her current vigorous, well-rested state gave her plenty of backbone to trust her own judgment. As for Shuai Hongqiang... this text was probably just a pretext to show concern, when really it was a roundabout reminder, nudging her not to forget to look at the project proposal he'd given her. She didn't reply, and set the phone aside, like brushing away a speck of dust.
At noon she kept a lunch date with a close girlfriend from the "Sisters' League," heading to a newly opened Yunnan restaurant on the sixth floor of the RT-Mart. She drove herself, savoring the freedom of being alone.
After reaching the mall, she parked and took the elevator straight to the sixth floor. The dining floor buzzed with voices, the aromas of food all mingling together. On her way toward the appointed restaurant, her gaze happened to skim past the floor-to-ceiling window of a family restaurant next door, one built around kids' meals.
Her steps faltered, almost imperceptibly.
Inside, at a spot near the aisle, sat Shuai Hongqiang and his family of three. Lin Wan was leaning slightly forward, head bowed as she fastened a bib on their son Shuai Wenyao, her profile gentle and serene. Shuai Hongqiang sat across from them, carefully nudging a cup of juice toward the child, a look of... a doting father's tenderness on his face—an expression Yao Yuan had never seen on him before. It looked a little clumsy, yet was tender beyond measure and utterly focused, as if the whole world had narrowed to this lively, adorable child in front of him. The little guy held up a dinosaur toy, waving it around in excitement, gesturing animatedly as he said something to his dad, his innocent smile as brilliant as sunshine.
It was the most ordinary of scenes, yet an utterly harmonious one—a family of three eating out on the weekend.
Shuai Hongqiang didn't see her, and Yao Yuan made no move to greet him. Her steps didn't stop; she walked calmly past. Only, deep down, that picture had stirred her, ever so slightly. The man who in business was by turns forceful, slick, or mercenary—so this was how tender he became as a father. And that child... was indeed adorable.
Without thinking, she touched her own lower belly. There, too, a child had once been—if only... A very faint, indescribable bitterness spread out, only to be quickly pressed back down into her heart. What was past had to be set down completely, buried away. As for other people's lives, they had nothing to do with her.
Over lunch, Yao Yuan and her friend chatted happily; the amusing little moments of daily life and their mutual gossip let her set aside, for a while, all her earlier tangled thoughts. A little over an hour later, they said goodbye at the elevator landing by the mall's parking garage and went their separate ways to fetch their cars.
Yao Yuan's red Bentley sat quietly in a corner off to the side, like an elegant scarlet beast. She got in, started the engine, and the car eased out of its spot, its bright headlights slicing instantly through the garage's dim light. Just as she turned toward the exit lane, an old black BMW X6 was rolling slowly across the intersection ahead, the driver's window half down.
It was Shuai Hongqiang's car. Lin Wan sat in the passenger seat, and in the back the faint outline of a child's safety seat could be made out.
Yao Yuan's eyes weren't fixed on that car in particular; she was focused on watching for traffic behind her, waiting for the right moment to accelerate. Her Bentley, like a nimble red sprite, glided smoothly and steadily—almost soundlessly—past the front of Shuai Hongqiang's car and straight on toward the exit, without pausing for an instant. Naturally, she didn't notice the pair of eyes in the driver's seat that had suddenly turned to look her way.
But Shuai Hongqiang saw her clearly.
That streak of elegant red, carrying an air utterly unlike the sedan he was now driving—remote yet swift, like a phantom from another world—flashed past before his eyes. He even saw clearly Yao Yuan's profile in the driver's seat: calm and refined, her gaze fixed firmly ahead, as if the car beside her carrying a family of three simply didn't exist.
The fingers gripping the steering wheel clenched suddenly, of their own accord, the knuckles whitening faintly with the force.
Lin Wan, in the passenger seat, seemed to sense his brief, subtle stiffening. She turned her head, following his gaze toward the exit, catching only a red taillight just about to vanish from sight. She glanced at Shuai Hongqiang's expressionless profile; a flicker of barely perceptible puzzlement crossed her eyes, but in the end she asked nothing, only pressing her lips together lightly and lowering her head back to her phone.
In the garage, the low growl of the Bentley's engine quickly faded into the distance, leaving only a faint smell of exhaust. Shuai Hongqiang slowly loosened his grip on the wheel, drew a deep breath, stepped on the gas, and followed the flow of traffic toward the exit, easing out into the slightly languid sunlight of a weekend afternoon. That glance just now was like a small stone dropped into an already restless lake, sending out a ring of faint ripples only he could perceive. In the rearview mirror, his son's smile was still bright, his wife still absorbed in her phone—everything exactly as it had been a few minutes ago.
And yet, somehow, something felt different. That red Bentley brushing past was like a brief confirmation of another track of life, reminding him of certain things he was losing, and certain others he had never truly possessed. The quiet inside the car felt heavier now than before.
