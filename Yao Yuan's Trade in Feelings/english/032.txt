Crimson Night, Palace Walls, the Mirror's Crossing: An Old Dream of High Summer — Zhuge Jinye

The third time Yao Yuan woke in that pure-white, soundless space, a strange sense of familiarity rose in her chest.

She waited calmly. Sure enough, before long, a ripple stirred at one spot in the white expanse.

The ripple sharpened, resolving into a professional dance studio.

A tall, open, square room without a single pillar. A floor-to-ceiling wall of dance mirrors, spotless; the tempered glass glowed with a soft, warm sheen, and a hidden light strip along the mirror's edge gathered the glow into a still, gentle wash. Overhead, square ceiling mirrors were set neatly into the matte drop ceiling, mirroring the floor-length glass below so that the space seemed to extend upward without end. The pale-gray dance flooring was laid flat and smooth, and the lower half of the cream-colored walls was wrapped in matching crash padding, every corner rounded off. The solid-wood barre had the fine, mellow gleam of natural timber, and beside it the built-in storage cabinets were shut without a seam.

Beyond the double-paned soundproof glass, every sound from the outside world was cut off. No fussy ornament, no loud color — the whole room was clean, orderly, and held a kind of ultimate professionalism.

It was as quiet here as a dream waiting to be woken.

Just then the door opened gently, and a young man and woman stepped in together, instantly breaking the room's stillness.

The young man wore a fitted black practice suit; the hard lines of his arm muscles swelled faintly beneath the fabric, every fiber radiating power. The young woman wore a white spaghetti-strap dance dress, her tall figure making her waist look slender as a willow; in the fall of her skirt, before a single move began, there was already grace in the air.

The two stood at the center of the room, and the instant their eyes met, the air seemed to freeze. Before the music came, the dance broke loose.

The man's hand rested lightly at the woman's waist, and with a guiding pull he drew her toward him. She arched back with the motion, her spine bending into a lovely curve, her long neck stretching like a swan's. They closed the distance between them in a breath, then parted in an instant — like two clouds weaving together and drifting apart in the wind.

Their movements were fluid and full of tension. He lifted her up, and in the air she unfurled her body, her skirt flying. In the ceiling mirrors countless overlapping figures appeared, light and shadow swaying, dreamlike and unreal.

Then came turns and approaches full of effortless understanding. She spun toward the wall and pressed her back lightly to the mirror. He drew near, his breath close enough to hear. She slid down along the force of him, and in the mirror their shadows nearly merged into one. Her fingertips touched lightly at his shoulder and back, moving as the force passed through them, as if silently answering the same melody.

The music suddenly poured out, the drumbeat like a heartbeat. The two figures crossed and wove — no longer a simple partnering, but a resonance from the depths of the soul. Lifts, turns, leaning, parting… every move fit seamlessly, every glance laced with unspoken feeling. In the ceiling mirrors, light and shadow shifted and flowed with their dancing, as if the whole space had come alive.

At last the music faded. The man drew the woman gently back against him, and her forehead rested lightly on his shoulder. The two leaned quietly together, only the sound of their still-uneven breathing echoing softly in the empty room. The soft light of the ceiling mirrors wrapped around them, as if to freeze this moment of understanding and connection forever in flowing time.

When their breathing eased a little, the pair slowly lifted their heads, eyes meeting, a thousand words seeming to pass between them. The next moment, as if by silent agreement, they leaned forward, stopping at a breath's distance, letting a soundless tremor flow in that small space between them.

Standing in the pure-white space, thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan froze on the spot, caught off guard, her heart suddenly clenching. The next second, memories sealed away for years came roaring back, surging over her. Yes — that entwined pair in the mirror was her at twenty-nine, and Zhuge Jinye.

That past lay deep in the years, glazed over with a faded sugar frosting. In the deep winter of her twenty-eighth year, the north wind swept cold snow across all of Jinshi. Heartbroken and disillusioned with Shuai Hongqiang, Yao Yuan left, with bitter resolve, the city that had trapped her youth and her love, heading all the way south to the soft, humid city of Hangcheng. She went there for one reason only: to seek out the person she had been closest to all her life — An Nanchan.

An Nanchan was the dearest friend etched into her years. From grade school through middle school the two were inseparable, walking the road together; as girls they soaked in the children's dance class together, stretching their legs, arching their backs, polishing the basics over and over, their sweat falling on the same studio floor. They shared all the joys and grievances of those green young years, and all the secrets of girlhood too. An Nanchan's background had always been special — she was, in others' veiled words, an illegitimate child. She never lacked money; her birth father's family was wealthy, and materially she was never shorted a single thing — fine clothes, rich food, comfort without worry. But that abundance could never fill the hollow in her heart. The father's absence from her childhood, the veiled appraising stares of others, a birth without proper standing — all of it pressed long on her, leaving her hard-pressed to find any real happiness. Perhaps it was for this reason that Yao Yuan later especially supported those financially capable, independent women who chose to "keep the child, leave the father" — though she herself had never thought to walk that road. She had seen the hollow in An Nanchan as she grew up; so when Shuai Hongqiang refused to marry her, she gave up that child with bitter resolve. She did not want her own child, one day, to suffer the same kind of spiritual torment.

An Nanchan was by nature meek and solitary, always quiet and reserved. Yao Yuan was the opposite — willful and competitive, vivid and fierce. Two people so utterly different in temperament became each other's best friends, perhaps out of some destined kinship of spirit. Each had a flawed childhood: An Nanchan had a birth father, yet one absent year after year, a father in name only; Yao Yuan lost her own father painfully at seven, and lost her support early. The year she finished middle school, her birth father, far off in Hangcheng, formally came to take her away. She left her homeland and moved to that bustling, indifferent city of the Jiangnan region for high school. But fate's thread was never cut: later the two worked side by side and tested into Beijing Dance Academy together, becoming college classmates once more — in the same city, the same school, carrying on a bond that had grown with them from childhood. They walked four years of college side by side, and after graduation, their life paths quietly forked. An Nanchan grew weary of the clamor of Jingshi and, following her heart, returned to settle in Hangcheng, while Yao Yuan stayed alone to make her way in the capital. Apart in two places, they never grew distant; they kept in touch year after year, each the other's most steadfast refuge.

So when Yao Yuan truly could no longer hold on, the first place she rushed to was Hangcheng, to seek out this one true confidante. But the An Nanchan of that time was trapped in an exquisite cage of her own. Her intensely controlling birth father had forcibly arranged her life, pressing her step by step, ordering her to accept a family marriage alliance — trading her marriage for commercial gain, binding her life firmly onto a chessboard of interests. Beneath the glossy family wealth lay shackles she could not escape; An Nanchan was stifled and oppressed day after day, wrapped all over in a gloom that would not lift.

Yao Yuan's arrival became the one bright spot in her gray life. The two crowded into An Nanchan's waterside apartment, keeping each other company morning and night, comforting one another. At twenty-eight, a dancer's golden years were nearly past. To re-plan her life and find a new way out, Yao Yuan decided to step into the rising internet track, enrolling in a six-month systematic course run by a local professional e-commerce training academy, learning new-media operations, content planning, and on-camera expression from scratch. And it was in that course that she met Zhuge Jinye.

He was three years younger than her, also a formally trained professional dance student — upright in build, fierce in strength, carrying the unique aura and wildness of a dancer. The Yao Yuan of that time had just said goodbye to her past in Jinshi, her heart adrift, her whole self weary; and the young, ardent Zhuge Jinye was like a reckless, scorching beam of light, bursting unguarded into the dull, dry transition of her life. From then on fate entwined them — and laid the groundwork for the love, hate, and parting to come.

And the scene now before her in the mirror — the pair locked in an embrace and a kiss — was the hottest, most ardent page of that old time.

Yao Yuan had craved that sudden warmth that sent her heartbeat out of control. Rather than say they were like dry kindling and raging fire, catching at a single touch, it was truer to say they were two souls of the same frequency, two flames — the instant they met, they merged into a fiercer blaze. They shared the same field, kindred interests, close values, and above all the same identical ideal: to rise above others, to become someone who stood at the top. So alike, and yet so drawn to each other — it was as if she had fallen in love with another self in this world.

Half a year passed, slow as the flowing water of the Jiangnan region, and fast as wind through one's fingers. Yao Yuan and Zhuge Jinye spent their days soaked in the training institute — from livestream scripting and on-camera presence to account operations and content directing, they sat through every class together, their notes crammed full. After class they huddled together to work through practice scripts, correcting each other's expressions and movements in front of the camera. Zhuge Jinye had a professional dancer's command of his body and could always pinpoint exactly what was off in her posture on camera; Yao Yuan, fine and careful in thought, could always help him untangle the complex logic of operations. The two were in perfect sync, becoming the best-matched pair in the class.

Their off-class hours belonged entirely to Hangcheng's everyday warmth and romance. They went out in the morning light to eat scalding soup dumplings and fresh, sweet pian'er chuan noodles from the alleys; in the afternoons they hid in dessert shops gnawing on ice-cold osmanthus cake; at dusk they followed the scents into old lanes for soy-braised duck and Dongpo pork, eating their way through every corner of Hangcheng's food. In their free time they wandered every corner of the city — to Lingyin Temple to watch the curling incense smoke, to Xiaohe Straight Street to browse the lazy little shops, to Prince Bay Park to watch the wind blow over the sea of flowers — and the place they went most often was West Lake.

The West Lake of late spring, its waters rippling bright, its willows trailing low. The two walked slowly along the Su Causeway, the evening wind carrying the lake's damp, brushing tenderly against their faces. Looking up at Leifeng Pagoda in the dusk, dotted with lamplight, Yao Yuan suddenly laughed softly and turned her head toward the tall, upright Zhuge Jinye beside her. "Tell me — Lady White, beneath this Leifeng Pagoda, what do you think she's doing right now?"

Laughter rippled in Zhuge Jinye's eyes, his gaze fixed firmly on her face, and he picked up her words to talk about the Legend of the White Snake. After a moment's silence, he suddenly said, "If it were you, which one of them would you want to be?"

"I'd want to be Xiao Qing." Yao Yuan watched the ripples on the lake, her voice slow and soft. "Bold to love and bold to hate, clear-eyed and clean about it. Not like Lady White, trapped in a pagoda for love, wearing away a whole lifetime."

Zhuge Jinye's fingertips paused. He reached out and gently brushed aside the loose strands the wind had blown across her face, his tone earnest and tender. "Then I'll be Zhang Yutang."

Yao Yuan started and lifted her eyes to him.

"I know," he said, looking into her eyes, his voice low, "Xiao Qing and Zhang Yutang loved each other to the very limit and still couldn't stay together. In the end all they could do was drink the waters of Forgetfulness and forget each other completely. But if it's fated that two can't be together, then to forget utterly, to suffer no more the torment of longing — for both of them, perhaps that too is a kind of perfection."

In that instant a nameless ache rode the lake wind up into Yao Yuan's heart. She wanted to say something, but in the end it became only a soft sigh, buried in the sky-wide dusk. At the time she took it for one of his offhand musings — she never imagined those words would become a prophecy of what came after.

Time slipped by, and the last month of the half-year course arrived; Hangcheng plunged fully into high summer. The blazing sun scorched the whole city, the air thick with a parched, hot wind — and the feeling between Yao Yuan and Zhuge Jinye, like the weather of that high summer, burned into a sky-wide blaze that could no longer be held back.

Young hearts in love need no words. An Nanchan's apartment, the rented dance studio, the night-time shore of West Lake, the small guesthouse where they stayed a while… many places held the traces of their time together. His gaze was always intent and scorching, and in those moments she let down her guard, letting herself sink into that warmth that asked nothing of tomorrow.

Every approach was careful, every locked gaze set her heart pounding like a drum. They craved each other's company, and in that brief summer they gave over their most heartfelt feelings. They knew the parting was at hand, yet neither would say it aloud; they only wanted to seize every moment of the present.

That day after class, they were in Zhuge Jinye's apartment practicing on-camera posture. It was her turn to lead. The air warmed gradually in the focused adjustments, and a soundless tension spread through the stillness.

Something struck some chord in him; Zhuge Jinye's gaze suddenly changed — he gripped her wrist, turned, and in an instant the two were inches apart.

The sofa sank slightly. In his eyes Yao Yuan saw a surging emotion, familiar yet strange. No words, only breaths suddenly crossing, clearer than ever before, carrying an almost desperate resolve, and hiding within it a barely perceptible tremble.

His hand brushed over the hair at her forehead, fingertips slipping into the strands. Yao Yuan closed her eyes, taking in everything around her, and the reassuring scent that came from him — and that stubbornness the two of them shared.

Summer was about to end. They both knew it well.

But in this moment, neither was willing to step back.

A thunderstorm was coming, the air sultry and thick. Beyond the window dark clouds pressed against the city, and the wind drove damp air into the room.

Yao Yuan leaned back into the sofa, her fingertips unconsciously twisting the collar of his practice suit. Zhuge Jinye knelt on one knee before her, close enough to feel the faint heat off each other. Outside the window the muffled thunder rolled, throwing the silence of the room into sharper relief.

He lowered his head, his breath drifting over her lips. The next instant a cool touch came down, gentle but firm. It was a kiss carrying summer's damp and a tangle of feeling, nothing to do with technique — only the most direct passing of emotion. Her fingertips curled slightly, and in the end caught lightly at a corner of his shirt.

This kiss was very light, like a feather brushing the tip of her heart; and very heavy too, carrying too much that went unsaid. A flash of lightning streaked past the window, lighting up for an instant the close-pressed outlines in the room before they sank back into the dim. Raindrops began tapping the glass, sparse then thick, gradually merging into one noisy curtain of rain.

After a long while he drew back a little, his forehead resting against hers, their breaths weaving together, warm and moist. In the sound of the rain the two heartbeats were clearly audible, resonating at the same frequency.

His eyes were damp, reflecting the warm glow of the floor lamp, brimming with deep feeling and reluctance.

"Don't go, all right?" His voice carried a barely perceptible tremor.

Yao Yuan opened her eyes, reached up to wind her arms around his neck, her fingertips gently stroking the sweat-damp skin of his back. She parted her lips, but in the end only shook her head and buried her face in the curve of his neck.

Some answers don't need to be spoken aloud.

That night, they held each other tightly just like that, and in the sound of high summer's downpour they pressed all their love and reluctance into every lingering breath. Beyond the window the lightning still tore the night sky apart, while the warm glow in the bedroom wrapped tenderly around the young man and woman, locking their secret forever in that sweltering summer.
