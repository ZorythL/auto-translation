In the Eyes of the World: What Marriage Gives a Woman
On the other side of the city, Shuai Hongqiang had not known a peaceful moment since the day at the Antiquities Hall, when he met Old Chen, Yao Yuan, and that Mr. Wang Baoguo—a man as lean and serene as an old scholar—and heard the whole story laid bare: the origins of the Mirror Pact, its brutal truth, and the slim hope of escape. Ever since, his chest had felt as if a great stone, soaked through with ice water, were lodged there—heavy, cold, never letting him rest. The more his business prospered in a way that bordered on the uncanny—project approvals waved through one green light after another, payments arriving with unheard-of punctuality, even his stray investments seeming to enjoy the special favor of some god of luck—the more that hidden dread spread and grew inside him. He felt like a thief who had blundered into a vault and made off with a staggering fortune, trembling as he enjoyed everything that came so easily—delicacies, attendants at his beck and call, a life that looked dazzling from the outside. But every compliment, every sum that landed in his account, tolled a silent alarm in his ear: this was stolen, bought by overdrawing someone else's peace—maybe even their very life. He waited, every hour, not knowing when the vault door would burst open, when the owner of all he had taken would appear before him, cold-eyed, not only to claim it all back but to cast him—the greedy little thief—into a bottomless pit of karmic ruin, and worse still... to drag down the family he treasured most.
That fear reached its peak once he learned the possible forms the "backlash" might take. Health, children, family ties... the vague yet crushing "price" Wang Baoguo had spoken of hung over his head like the sword of Damocles, so that when he looked at his wife and son, his gaze took on an unspeakable layer of dread and tenderness.
That day, he wrapped up all his business early, begged off every social engagement, and drove home. Passing a hotel he often visited, he pulled over and went to the back kitchen himself to pack up several of the hearty dishes his wife Lin Wan and their son Shuai Wenyao loved, then stopped at a supermarket to pick out fresh fruit. Back home, he did something rare—he tied on an apron and, clumsily but with unusual care, plated the takeout dishes, washed the fruit, even lit a scented candle on the table for atmosphere. Warm amber light, fine porcelain, the air filled with the smell of food and a faint citrus fragrance—he had deliberately staged everything into a near-perfect, cozy family scene. Beneath that perfection lay a panicked urgency: a need to seize something, to make up for something—or, you might say, to perform some futile, premature rite of mourning for a "loss" that might be coming.
The instant he set down the last pair of chopsticks, the lock turned, and Lin Wan came in, leading their son Shuai Wenyao by the hand. The timing was so neat it seemed calculated.
"Daddy!" Shuai Wenyao came charging over like a little cannonball and wrapped his arms around Shuai Hongqiang's leg. He bent down and lifted his son high, pressing his cheek against the boy's soft, warm hair. That living, breathing scent—milk and sweat mixed together—made his eyes sting for no reason he could name, and his arms tightened, as if he wanted to carve this warm weight into his very bones. "How come you're home so early today? And you made dinner?" Lin Wan set down her bag and glanced, a little surprised, at the table laid out with such obvious care, her gaze lingering a moment on Shuai Hongqiang's face. She caught at once the unusual look in her husband's eyes—a tangle of exhaustion, tenderness, and a flicker of alarm she couldn't read—but she didn't press. She only took off her coat and let a gentle smile show. "Wenyao, go wash your hands. Daddy made all this good food."
Dinner went on in an atmosphere of surface harmony. Shuai Hongqiang was almost too attentive, constantly placing food in Lin Wan's and his son's bowls, asking the boy about funny things at school, laughing along when Lin Wan mentioned an amusing comment from one of her livestream fans. He ate very little, but his eyes seemed glued to his wife and son. He watched Lin Wan carefully pick the bones out of the fish for their son, watched the boy's animated little face as he chattered away, watched this scene of "home" under the lamplight—so familiar it could not be more familiar, yet at this moment making his heart tremble. Every bit of warmth was like a sweet needle pricking at his guilt- and fear-filled heart. He drank in this ordinary warmth greedily, and yet, in the deepest part of himself, he dreaded it—would all of this before his eyes, this seemingly solid harbor, one day be wrecked and stripped away by the tide of "backlash," all because of the "cause" he had planted in a single reckless moment years ago? He could almost picture it: the "retribution" landing on his son, or the family torn apart... The thought had barely risen before it chilled him to the bone, and the hand holding his chopsticks gave an almost imperceptible tremble.
Lin Wan took in every bit of her husband's strangeness. His over-forceful thoughtfulness, the flashes of emptiness and panic in his eyes, and that almost suffocating "tenderness" that seemed to want to wrap her and the child up entirely—all of it drew the wary string in her heart a little tighter. But she didn't do what she would once have done: set down her bowl at once and gently, softly press him—"What's wrong? Is something the matter at the company?" The woman she was now—after the long emotional labor of marriage, after struggling together under financial strain, after the baptism of countless women's stories in her livestream room—had long since built a new, clearer-eyed way of relating. She no longer took it upon herself to absorb and process all his emotions, unless he chose to speak openly. The "emotional value" she gave now came with clear boundaries and a clear trigger. She simply went on eating as usual, answering her son, and when her eyes occasionally met Shuai Hongqiang's, she offered a calm, faintly questioning smile—but she would never be the first to tear the thin veil of "everything is fine" that he was trying to keep up.
Of course she knew of Yao Yuan. Working the same emotional-content lane on the internet, how could Lin Wan not know Yao Yuan and that doctrine of "clear-eyed reckoning"—sharp to the point of coldness—and the enormous controversy that trailed it? At first she had been repelled, even disgusted, by that line of thinking that turned feeling and marriage entirely into "tools" to be "appraised"; she found it too refined, too self-serving, dissolving the warm and beautiful parts of human nature. But over these years, inside this marriage that outsiders called "a good match," she had lived through the whole arc—from being full of longing to watching her illusions shatter, from giving her all to learning to calculate, from craving to be loved to craving even more to be "respected" and to "have a choice." When she looked again at many of Yao Yuan's views, what she felt was utterly different. Though she still couldn't fully agree with some of the more extreme claims, she had to admit that Yao Yuan's naked dissections of the power games, value exchange, and emotional costs in relationships between men and women often worked like a keen scalpel, slicing open with precision the truths that ran silently beneath the tender surface of her own marriage. That was "knowledge born of practice"—the part of the rules she had stumbled through and verified for herself, with her own wounds. Only, she had never connected, across time and space, the dazzling, sharp-tongued "Godmother of Feelings," Yao Yuan, on the other side of the screen, with the husband beside her, Shuai Hongqiang, now wholly absorbed in his role as a middle-aged businessman. She knew nothing of that old romance from eight years ago, still less that a strange ancient mirror was binding her husband—the one she saw as enjoying a "second spring" in his career—together with his deeply guarded past and that distant, formidable "ex-girlfriend," lashing them all to a single chain of cause and effect fraught with unknown risk.
To her, Shuai Hongqiang's recent "good fortune" was merely a long-overdue turn of luck, a case of things at their worst finally turning for the better. And their marriage, after they had weathered the harshest financial crisis together, seemed to have entered a new phase: a stable, mutually supportive alliance with a sense of boundaries, reached by two sufficiently mature adults on the basis of a clear understanding of practical interests and a shared love of and responsibility for their child. There was warmth, but not much; there was care, but in measure. It was more like a joint-venture company—equity clearly divided, responsibilities clearly assigned—the two of them jointly running the "family" project, with the goal of steady operation and giving the next generation the best conditions they could. Love might long since have faded, but the bond of "partners" and the tie of a "community of shared interests" kept this ship moving forward across waters that were, on the whole, calm.
After dinner, Shuai Hongqiang practically rushed to clear away the bowls and chopsticks, a near-penitent urgency in his movements. "Go and get ready for your stream. I'll handle this, and keep Wenyao company with a book—I'll give him his bath in a bit," he said to Lin Wan, in a tone of rare thoughtfulness, almost humble.
Lin Wan looked at him and nodded. "All right—thank you, then." She didn't refuse. She turned and walked toward the study, her other world. Shutting the door, she fixed her hair and clothes in front of the mirror and touched up her lipstick. The woman in the mirror had calm eyes—too calm, even. With practiced ease she switched on her equipment, adjusted the lighting, and slipped on, neatly, the mask of "Wanwan"—the gentle host whose every word brimmed with marital wisdom, teaching women how to manage their relationships "clear-eyed." Outside the study, she could faintly hear Shuai Hongqiang's lowered voice telling their son a story, and the sound of running water rinsing the dishes. One door apart, two worlds, two kinds of "clear-eyed wisdom," running in parallel under the same roof—each understood without a word, yet wound by invisible threads of fate around the edge of a larger, stranger riddle, with no one aware whether a storm was brewing somewhere far off.
[Lin Wan's Livestream Room]
Lin Wan didn't sit down right away. She walked to the window and stood still for a moment, gazing out at the deep night, drawing several long, slow breaths. The gentle smile that belonged to a wife and mother slowly settled away, replaced by something quieter and more focused. Then she turned and walked toward that streaming corner bathed in soft light.
She sat down before the camera. The lighting, carefully calibrated, was bright without glare, framing perfectly her exceptionally approachable face—relaxed brows and eyes, a straight nose, a softly shaped mouth—the warm, gentle features that traditional taste called "virtuous and kind," carrying, even unsmiling, a faint reassuring warmth. Tonight she wore a finely textured cream cashmere turtleneck that set off her jade-smooth complexion, her crisp short hair neatly combed, lending her a long, slender neck and an air of cool, capable reason. She wore no jewelry. Her clothes, like the survival strategy she had chosen, were modern minimalism—stripped of anything superfluous, conveying an undeniable weight and sense of order through restraint. Behind her, a lush umbrella tree glowed green, and a diffuser sent up curling white mist with the calm scent of cedar. The whole frame was warm, intelligent, trustworthy—the "safe house" and "platform for her voice" that she had spent years, across countless nights, painstakingly building for "the host Wanwan."
She adjusted the microphone, looked into the camera, her gaze clear and serene, and let a small, relaxing smile spread at the corners of her mouth—a smile with a settling, steadying power.
"Good evening, and welcome to 'A Gentle Crossing to Waking.' I'm Lin Wan."
"Before the stream, I saw one of you—a sister in our fan group—share a conversation: the 'teachings' an elder of hers, whom we'll just call 'Uncle Liu,' gave her. I found it very telling, and with her permission, I'd like to bring it out and talk it over with all of you."
She picked up a tablet, her eyes settling on the screen, her voice steady and objective as she recounted it, like a scholar quoting material from fieldwork:
"Here's what 'Uncle Liu' said to my sister here: 'A woman has to marry. If a woman doesn't marry, she'll be toyed with by lots of men—how pitiful is that. A woman should trust a man's love, shouldn't fight for power or status, shouldn't work herself to the bone. If your husband truly loves you, then just stay home and enjoy the good life. A man's love—that's marrying you and bringing you home. For a man to marry one woman his whole life, that's righteousness and virtue.'"
Lin Wan set down the tablet, raised her eyes, and looked clear-eyed into the camera:
"My sister here asked two questions on the spot, and 'Uncle Liu's' answers are very much worth hearing together."
She leaned forward slightly, as if back at the scene of the conversation, recreating the exchange in a calm tone:
"My sister asked: So how is a man's love shown?"
"'Uncle Liu' answered quickly, with great certainty: 'By marrying you—this woman—by marrying you, by having children with you. The women out there are just for play; only you are the one he marries. Marriage should last a lifetime.'"

[The retold conversation ends; for a few seconds, the livestream room falls into a heavy silence.]
Lin Wan picked up her teacup and took a small sip. She needed this brief pause—to let those naked words settle in the air, and to give the sisters in front of their screens time to digest the unvarnished shock of those ideas, hitting them full in the face.
Then she set the cup down, her gaze turning clear and focused again—the clarity of someone stepping back from a specific case and returning to the role of analyst.
"This conversation is like an unpolished 'specimen of ideas,'" she said slowly, her voice calm and clear. "It wears no warm disguise. It presents, almost plainly, a 'life script' about marriage and gender that is still running in many places to this day. Let's set aside judgments of right and wrong for now, and first look—in terms we can all understand—at how this 'script' is written."
"Act One is called 'Opening with Fear,'" Lin Wan analyzed in a storytelling tone. "It casts the unmarried woman as a 'weakling' stranded in a jungle, danger on every side, whose only road to survival is to find a male 'protector' and obtain 'safety' through marriage. From the very start, this script denies the possibility that a woman, as an independent individual, can possess dignity, safety, and the right to arrange her own life. Your 'safety' must be redeemed through 'being owned.'"
"Act Two is 'The Hollow Promise,'" she went on dissecting. "When pressed on how 'love' is shown and what the 'benefit' is, this script can't offer anything concrete or redeemable—mutual respect, shared growth, sharing the risks—it can only point, over and over, at the ritual of 'getting married' itself. Which means, in this logic, 'love' and 'benefit' are ultimately reduced to a change in 'ownership status'—from 'unowned' to 'owned.' As for the quality of life after being 'owned,' the inner feelings—the script doesn't write that, or rather, it deems it unimportant."
Lin Wan's gaze grew sharp, going straight to the core:
"Act Three is the leak of the essence and its swift repackaging. 'Only lets one person play with her'—that line accidentally shows the script's hand. Here, the woman is reduced to an object with a 'use function.' Marriage is permissions management, going from 'possibly used by many' to 'fixed, used by one.' When this cold underlying logic is exposed, the script immediately wheels out the gorgeous stage set of 'righteousness and virtue.' It pulls off a clever piece of magic: it stages a relationship based on control and possession as the man's noble 'moral achievement' and 'self-sacrifice' (he marries only you)."
"Finally, when asked about the man's own motive," Lin Wan's tone carried a deep insight, "the script can no longer use the lines of 'use' or 'morality.' It can only switch to an unassailable channel—'the warmth of a family.' That phrase is too beautiful to argue against. But we need to think: this blueprint of 'warmth'—who drew it? Who lights the hearth fire? Who adds the firewood? And who senses and defines the temperature? When the very conception of 'warmth' rests, by default, on one party being expected to provide the bulk of the 'service functions'—childbearing, caregiving, emotional comfort—does the individual inside it really always feel 'warmth'?"
The comments had been guided into deeper reflection:

"I'm not sharing this less-than-comfortable conversation to stir up conflict or manufacture anxiety," Lin Wan's tone eased, taking on a constructive strength again. "Quite the opposite—I believe that clearly seeing the raw source code of certain set 'scripts' is the first step to gaining the ability to write our own life 'screenplay.' When we can tell which words are implanting 'fear,' which promises are actually 'hollow,' and which grand terms are masking a core of 'objectification,' only then can we stay clear-headed inside and keep the initiative in our choices when faced with all kinds of voices."
She thought of her own years of feeling her way through marriage with Shuai Hongqiang, thought of the countless sisters' stories from her livestream room, and a weightier note of understanding entered her voice:
"Seeing clearly some of the cold realities of the structure doesn't mean our only options are rage or escape. On the contrary, it reminds us that, in the present social environment, women all the more need to cultivate a kind of 'clear-eyed wisdom' and 'pragmatic resilience.'"
"This wisdom lies in this: of course we can enter marriage, can pour ourselves deeply into our feelings—but we need to be clear that what we're entering is an 'intimate relationship' that requires building together, not a search for a 'savior' or a 'lifelong employer.'" She used a more accessible analogy: "A relationship is more like jointly running a 'family company' with unlimited liability than signing a one-way, long-term 'labor or gift contract.'"
"The premise of a partnership is that both sides have sustainable 'input' and a clear 'consensus,'" Lin Wan's voice was steady and strong. "Our 'input' is never merely time, childbearing, and housework—it's our emotional intelligence, our management ability, our capacity to learn and adapt, our social resources. And our 'consensus' must never be merely 'gaining a legitimate title,' but building a partnership of mutual respect and shared decision-making—ensuring both parties have room to grow within the relationship, and the right to be informed, to participate, and to fair safeguards over shared resources."
"'Uncle Liu' warned that a woman who fights for power will pay a terrible price. I'll admit it: any attempt to change the established rules of the game, to win more autonomy, will meet resistance," Lin Wan's gaze was gentle and firm. "But there's another price—handing over the controls of your own life, actively or passively, placing the key to your happiness entirely in someone else's hands. From then on, when the road jolts or the direction is lost, you may lose the ability to adjust—you may even forget where you wanted to go in the first place. The first is the challenge of expanding the map of your life; the second is the silent fall of your own territory."
"So, as I see it," she concluded, her voice like still water running deep, calm yet holding power, "true growth is learning to keep cultivating your own inner 'operating principles' even within the established social 'system.' We can be loving partners, and at the same time be clear-eyed observers and strategic builders. Don't treat marriage as life's only answer and its entire wager—treat it as an important, profound journey that takes wisdom to navigate."
"On this journey, we don't only learn how to love and how to give—we also learn how to protect the independence of our own spirit, how to build up the capacity to face change, how to let the roots of our own life sink down deep even in the soil of a relationship, draw nourishment, and grow upward—rather than being merely a clinging vine."
"When we no longer only crave 'being loved' to define our entire worth, but crave to grow, within love, into a more complete and more powerful version of ourselves; when we no longer fear that 'asserting' our rights will destroy the relationship, but understand that a healthy relationship inherently includes the sharing and balancing of rights—then perhaps, amid the complicated realities between men and women, we can find that path where we can pour ourselves in with deep feeling and still not lose ourselves."
[The stream ends; soothing music sets off a warm, strength-filled afterglow.]
Lin Wan took off her headset and rubbed her slightly aching neck. The full, guiding sense of power she felt during the stream slowly receded, and fatigue quietly surfaced. But she felt settled inside. In her own way, she had turned a sharp conversation into a tool of thought—one countless ordinary women could use to examine and reflect on their own situations. Rational yet warm, clear-eyed yet full of hope.
It was quiet outside the study. Shuai Wenyao had probably fallen asleep. She put away her equipment and gently pushed the door open.
In the living room, only a single floor lamp was lit. Shuai Hongqiang sat in the dim halo of light, seemingly lost in thought, turning his head only when he heard the sound. His eyes were complicated, a little blurred in the lamplight—weariness, concern, and what seemed a deeper stirring she couldn't read. He opened his mouth as if to say something, but in the end only pulled out a gentle smile: "Done streaming? You must be tired. I warmed you a cup of milk."
"Thank you." Lin Wan took the warm milk, its soothing heat passing into her fingertips. She looked at Shuai Hongqiang—this man she had shared a bed with for years, who at this moment felt somewhat unfamiliar to her. He didn't know that, just now, behind that door, she had calmly, rationally, all but dissected a certain truth of marriages like theirs. And he sat outside the door, churning inside with another story—of fate, debt, and fear—of which she knew nothing at all.
They stood under the same lamp, bound by the same marriage, yet living inside two utterly different, subtly intertwined narratives. One story was about clear-eyed cooperation within social structures; the other, about the karmic backlash of an ancient pact. The only point where they met was, perhaps, this cup of warm milk right now—and this calm surface called "home" that they ran together and strove so hard to keep up.
The night was still long. The Yellow River flowed on in silence in the distance, carrying away the silt, and covering secrets too. And life went on forward, in this complex balance of each being clear-eyed, and each keeping things from the other.
