Chapter 45 — Yao Yuan Warns Her Thirty-Year-Old Self; the Truth of the Chess Game

At first Ding Shitong paid no attention to these gestures from Duan Yanqing. He even took them as Duan Yanqing showing him respect, grooming him. But before long he sensed the sharp intent beneath that gentle surface.

After one event at the equestrian club, Ding Shitong did something rare: he had his driver take Yao Yuan home early. In the car he stayed silent for a long while, then offered a reminder, as if in passing. "Boss Duan… has a wide circle. But at some functions, if you feel uncomfortable, you don't have to force yourself. Your work, to me, is already more than good enough." He was trying to draw a line, to protect his own territory.

Duan Yanqing's patience, however, had limits, and his chips were heavy enough. When their vast collaboration plan—one massive enough to overturn several industries—entered its most critical phase, Duan Yanqing finally tore away that veneer of courtesy. It happened in a closed-door meeting with just the two of them, a cigar turning between his fingers.

"Shitong, let's not talk in riddles." Duan Yanqing blew out a smoke ring, his smile thinning. "Miss Yao—I'm very interested. It's the first time I've taken such a liking to anyone."

The hand holding Ding Shitong's teacup paused almost imperceptibly, his face betraying nothing. "Xiao Yao is a capable subordinate of mine, the East China regional head I'm grooming for the next stage. Your fondness, Boss Duan, is an honor for her. But she is not a project to be traded."

"Oh?" Duan Yanqing raised an eyebrow and leaned slightly forward, bringing an intangible pressure. "She doesn't seem to be your 'private property' either, does she, Boss Ding? An unmarried man, an unmarried woman—I court her in the normal way, what's wrong with that? Or… does Boss Ding harbor some thoughts of his own, which is why you won't part with her?" Having his thoughts laid bare, Ding Shitong felt a pang of guilt.

At the start, what he felt for Yao Yuan was genuinely the admiration a mentor feels for an outstanding junior. She was smart, decisive, with a startling capacity to learn, and—rarer still—a natural keenness amid the clink of glasses. He poured himself into grooming her, treating her as a future backbone of the company. He took her into every high-end venue, taught her hands-on how to read financial statements, analyze trends, even guided her personal investments. He could feel Yao Yuan's feelings for him: from the initial gratitude and reverence, fermenting day by day through their close contact—nearly sixteen hours a day. But he said nothing, and did nothing. He pressure-free enjoyed the worship and admiration of a beautiful young woman, which greatly flattered the vanity of a middle-aged man and seemed to spur him toward a better display of himself, granting him a new vitality.

And he, too, in the process, seemed to develop an attention toward her that went beyond the scope of "grooming." Perhaps it was the fatigue under her eyes after stretches of overtime, perhaps the slight furrow of her brow when she was made to drink at functions, perhaps simply the new pair of earrings today that better suited her complexion… That protective urge, that faint sense of possessiveness, which a man naturally breeds toward a beautiful, capable woman who "belongs" within his jurisdiction—it quietly spread.

Duan Yanqing deliberately leaned on the phrase "part with her," then softened his tone and threw out the real bait. "Our deal is at the very last step. I know you're fighting for the exclusive content-integration port on that 'Smart Drive Future' new-energy car network system. The approvals and channels—I happen to be able to speed up by at least half a year. My condition is simple." He looked Ding Shitong straight in the eye, spelling it out word by word. "I want Yao Yuan. One dinner, one 'accidental' night, that's all. What happens after, each of us by our own skill. For you, for the deal, this is the optimal solution."

The air froze. Ding Shitong looked at the tea leaves rising and sinking in his cup. Across his mind flashed Yao Yuan's trusting, dependent gaze; flashed the light that flared and was instantly hidden when she received a gift he'd given "casually"; flashed, too, the vast territory and supreme power this deal's success could bring. The struggle was violent. But the scale of his weighing, set against real interests on one side and his own feelings—feelings that could never see the light, that he could never promise—on the other, ultimately tipped. He thought of his wife and children far away in America, thought of the standing and reputation he'd built with bitter effort over years, thought of the higher peaks he could reach once bound to Duan Yanqing. A complicated mix of guilt, pain, and even a trace of relief—at last he need no longer be tormented by this uncontrollable feeling—drowned him.

In the end, he did not look at Duan Yanqing. He only let out an "Mm," so light it was nearly inaudible, as though it were just a small cough at an itch in his throat.

Then came the dinner that changed everything.

Yao Yuan was seated beside Duan Yanqing. Ding Shitong was there too, but seated across from her, no longer looking her way. The drinks came one after another, the reasons for toasting all high-sounding—celebrating the deal, wishing it success. For the first time, Ding Shitong did not block the drinks for her. He even smiled and personally raised a glass to her, saying, "Xiao Yao, you've worked hard these past stretches. Boss Duan thinks very highly of you—give Boss Duan a few more toasts." His gaze was calm, untroubled, even carrying an encouraging smile. In that smile, Yao Yuan's heart sank, bit by bit, turning cold.

She drank a great deal. Before her consciousness blurred, all she remembered was being "supported" out by Duan Yanqing's assistant, while Ding Shitong, his back to her, was chatting and laughing with someone else, never once turning around.

Yao Yuan, in the pure white space, recalled the helplessly struggling self who had been delivered into the presidential suite that night. There was no expression on her face.

The world she woke to was turned upside down. The physical discomfort and humiliation were secondary; when she pieced together, from various channels, the deal that Ding Shitong's feather-light "Mm" had represented, the sense of betrayal—of being pushed into the abyss by her own hand, by the man she most trusted and admired—pierced through every once-warm, once-ambiguous dream like an icicle.

Was she heartbroken? Disappointed? Yes. But just as her present-day theory as "the Godmother of Feelings" dissects it: wallowing in the emotions of a victim is worthless. She lay on the hotel's disheveled bed, gazing at the lavish crystal chandelier on the ceiling, and after her tears ran dry, all that remained in her eyes was a cold clarity and a nearly cruel rationality.

Ding Shitong had chosen profit and betrayed their bond—or perhaps that bond had never existed. Duan Yanqing saw her as a top-grade prey to be traded, determined to have her. Well then. Since the rules were like this, she would play by their rules.

She quickly "accepted" reality and let the current carry her to stay at Duan Yanqing's side. No longer forced, but a deliberate, shrewd choice. She used Duan Yanqing's resources and connections, learning and seizing as if starved. She was no longer the "Xiao Yao" who waited for rewards, but "Miss Yao," the most beautiful—and most clever—woman at Duan Yanqing's side. She knew how to display her charm with just the right touch, and knew better how to whisper in his ear, securing real interests for herself: an independent studio, startup capital, key project opportunities.

She founded her own company, its business cutting precisely into the gaps in the original Ding-Duan collaboration blueprint. She was more nimble than they were, better attuned to emerging markets, better at leveraging her identity as a woman and every tactic she'd learned from these two men. When, with thunderous force, she snatched a core project that Ding Shitong was set on winning—wresting away a key partnership from the tiger's jaws—Ding Shitong stopped her in the corridor after the signing ceremony.

He looked far more worn. With a complicated gaze he regarded the transformed, fiercely commanding woman before him, trying to find some shadow of the past in her eyes. "Xiao Yao… do you—do you hate me?"

Yao Yuan stopped, turned around, an impeccable, commercial smile on her face, but a desert in her eyes, no longer stirred. "You're joking, Boss Ding. The marketplace is a battlefield; each by their own means. I should even thank you and Boss Duan for that year's… 'devoted grooming.'" She deliberately paused a beat on "devoted grooming," and seeing Ding Shitong's pupils contract sharply, felt not a ripple within.

She had already wrung from these two men every usable resource, connection, and experience—down to the lessons they'd given her. Her company's wings were filling out, her capital robust.

Three years later, Yao Yuan stood in the top-floor office of her new company, looking down on the bustling yet cold city of Shanghai at her feet. This place had made her, and had also buried a part of her.

She pressed the intercom key, her voice clear and calm. "Notify management: the final resolution is in. Effective today, the company's headquarters begins the process of relocating to Jinshi. Everything here," she paused, her gaze sweeping the sky outside the window that had borne so much memory and calculation, "should end."

And just like that, she left Shanghai with resolve. A Yao Yuan washed clean of all glamour, stripped of all naivety, believing only in power and chips, carried her capital and ambition off to the next battlefield.

These later memories, like a fast-forwarded film, ran coldly and clearly through the mind of the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan as she watched the closing scene of the banquet. No pain of reliving—only a thorough re-confirmation about human nature and self-interest.

The scene shifted.

In it, the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan finally turned and walked into the apartment lobby. The click of her high heels on the marble floor sounded clear and lonely in the empty night. She pressed the elevator button; the metal doors slid open without a sound.

The elevator's interior was polished like a mirror. She stepped in, turned, and met her own eyes in the reflection. Perhaps it was the lingering warmth of the banquet, perhaps the afterglow of that "Little Yuan"—the woman in the mirror still had, at the corners of her eyes and brows, a trace of un-dissipated softness, touched with a hint of self-satisfaction. Looking at herself like that, she actually tugged gently at her lips and let out a slightly silly, contented smile.

Just as that smile froze on the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan's face—

The elevator's polished mirror, like a pool struck by a pebble, rippled out a silent ring. At the center of the ripples, the figure of another woman emerged from the void into solidity, slowly surfacing.

It was the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan. She appeared in the mirror in a gold gown and a black cashmere coat, her makeup somewhat smudged, with a tempered calm—appearing beside the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan with her exquisite makeup and spring in the corners of her eyes, like a harder reflection dredged up from the depths of time.

The silly smile on the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan's face froze abruptly, her pupils contracting in shock. Even though this was already the "fourth time" she'd seen her future self, each sudden appearance—materializing in this way that defied physical common sense and the rules of cognition—lost not a fraction of its impact and uncanniness.

Inside the mirror and out, the eyes of the two Yao Yuans collided in the void.

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan watched in silence for a few seconds that "past" self with embers still in her eyes. Within her there was neither grief nor joy, only a deep, almost academic understanding. She spoke slowly, her voice penetrating the barrier of mirror and time, calm and unrippled.

"See, you still couldn't hold out. That halo on Ding Shitong—mixing settled power, the crush of experience, and a carefully calibrated 'understanding'—is a targeted drug, precision-delivered to a woman at your stage, of your mental model. You'll get addicted to it. It's not your fault."

The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan snapped back to herself, hastily wiped away the lingering smile, replaced by wariness and urgency. "You… last time you only said 'be careful'! Is it Boss Ding? He means to do me harm?" There was a last struggle in her tone—defending Ding Shitong was almost instinct—but that warning had already lodged like a poison barb into the fabric of her trust.

"Boss Ding?" The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan repeated the title between her teeth, as if chewing some candy long since gone rancid, a faint, temperatureless arc curving at the corner of her mouth. "That title will soon stop meaning protection."

She took half a step forward, and her image in the mirror seemed to press closer too, bringing an intangible pressure.

"Do you know what it meant, that last look Duan Yanqing gave you tonight?" Not waiting for an answer, she went on, her pace steady, each word an icicle. "That was a hunter appraising a prey he's determined to take. And his appraisal was: worth the move, and with a very high certainty of success."

The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan paled slightly, but her reason still struggled. "Duan Yanqing… he does have a strong presence, but I'm just a little assistant. A figure like him…"

"A figure?" The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan cut her off, a cold mockery flashing in her eyes. "In the face of large enough interests, any 'figure' can be quantified and traded. And you, my dear past self, are unlucky enough to have been marked by Duan Yanqing as one of his 'desired attached conditions'—one of the terms he wants thrown in—in that vast collaboration about to unfold, one big enough to reshape the landscape of several industries. And Ding Shitong…"

She paused, her gaze sharp as it pierced the mirror, looking straight into the last tottering trace of trust in the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan's eyes.

"Your Boss Ding, after a brief struggle—perhaps with a sliver of genuine feeling—will make a choice entirely true to his merchant's nature, weighing the territory he prizes, the profit, and his own 'fondness' that can never see the light and can never be promised. He will acquiesce, even… personally bring about this trade. Using you, to exchange for an unobstructed channel of cooperation, for the key chips in Duan Yanqing's hand. At a dinner not long from now, you'll be made drunk, then delivered to Duan Yanqing's suite. And Ding Shitong—he'll sit at the head of the table, smiling, personally toasting you, thanking you for your 'contribution.'"

"No… that's impossible!" The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan blurted out, her body trembling slightly, whether from rage or fear she couldn't tell. "Boss Ding isn't that kind of person! With his own strength and standing, he has no need to use such a base means to win a deal! And the way he treats me…" She choked. Those ambiguous details became, in this moment, unbearably fragile.

"How does he treat you? Grooming you with care? Shielding you at every turn? Giving you gifts that are precious and to your taste? Appearing just when you need him?" The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan's tone was a nearly cruel calm. "That's investment, and it's domestication. Investing in your ability, to create value for him; domesticating your loyalty and fondness, to make sure you—this useful chess piece—stay willingly on his board, and can even be given away as a gift when he needs it. When he finds his domesticated 'pet' has caught the eye of a more powerful hunter, and that handing the 'pet' over can buy him the territory he wants more—what do you think his merchant's nature will choose?"

She looked at the young self in the mirror, face ashen, faith beginning to collapse in her eyes, and slowed her pace, each word like a heavy hammer.

"You overestimated the weight of 'sentiment' on his scale of value, and underestimated the power of interest and ambition to warp human nature. What you took as special care was only part of his controlling the whole board, satisfying his own subtle psychological needs. To him, you were never the irreplaceable 'one and only,' but a quality asset combining appreciation value, use value, and a certain emotional premium. When the 'transfer' of this asset can be exchanged for a higher return, transferring is the most rational business decision."

The air in the elevator seemed to congeal. The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan leaned against the cold mirror, digesting this horrifying "future." The roar of collapsing trust seemed to echo through her mind. It was a long time before she found her voice again, hoarse and taut. "Then I… what should I do? What's your advice?" She stared hard at her future self in the mirror, afraid she would suddenly vanish as in the previous times.

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan seemed to see through her fear, and this time did not vanish at once. Her gaze steady, she gave clear, cold instructions.

"First, immediately stop all unrealistic emotional projection onto Ding Shitong and any fantasy of depending on him. Restore him fully to the pure identities of 'boss,' 'resource provider,' 'a partner to be wary of.' Your emotions and attention are precious resources—don't waste them on an 'investment' destined to run a loss."

"Second, Duan Yanqing's 'interest' is both a crisis and leverage. Don't passively wait to be schemed against. Appraise the resources he can offer—connections, capital, information, a springboard. Then take the initiative to think: how do you convert his 'interest' in you into an 'opportunity' you can control and use? Remember, the initiative must be held in your own hands. To go with him, or not—that must be your proactive choice based on a clear-eyed weighing of pros and cons, not an 'ending' they've arranged for you and forced you to bear."

"Third, raise your hard strength and irreplaceability at top speed. Learn everything in the game's rules at their level like a madwoman—business, law, interpersonal networks, resource integration. Use your current position to draw nutrients with everything you've got, and accumulate your own capital: money, connections, cognition, results. You have to be fast—fast enough that when they want to move against you, they'll find you're no longer the little assistant they can manipulate at will, but a potential rival or partner who already has her own claws and teeth, who makes them weigh the cost."

The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan breathed rapidly, straining to absorb it all. Her future self's words were like a cold scalpel, cutting open the tender, sentimental surface to expose the naked logic of power and exchange beneath. Painful—but a nearly self-flagellating clarity came with it.

"So," she asked hoarsely, "the later me, today's you… you were schemed against, then chose Duan Yanqing, and that's how you got to where you are now—right?" She wanted to verify her guess.

"It was a choice, and it was using him." The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan did not deny it, her tone flat. "After being pushed into the abyss, crying is useless. Either rot in the abyss, or use the abyss as a springboard. I chose the latter. I stayed at his side, wrung from him every usable resource, completed the cruelest and most effective 'primitive accumulation of capital.' Then, when neither he nor Ding Shitong expected it, I struck back with a single blow, seized the key projects, and set up on my own."

She paused, looking at the mix of shock, understanding, and a trace of yearning in her young self's eyes, and added:

"Those three years were three years of shedding a layer of skin. But worth it."

The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan was silent for a long while, then suddenly asked a seemingly unrelated question. "Then… the you now, are you living well? Are you happy? Is this really the life you wanted?"

The question gave the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan a moment's stall. Her image in the mirror even seemed to blur for a tenth of a second. Then she smiled faintly, a smile carrying a complicated, ineffable weariness, though her voice remained steady.

"By worldly, quantifiable standards, very well. Financially free, successful in my career, holding considerable say and influence in a certain vertical field—in some sense, one of the 'people above people.'"

"As for whether it's what I wanted…" She lifted her eyes, her gaze seeming to pierce the mirror and time, looking toward some distant place. "I got the power to choose. To choose how to live, whom to associate with, what career to pursue. That alone is already luckier than too many people. Happiness is a momentary feeling, but a sense of control is the source of lasting security. Now, at least, I can control most of my own life's direction."

"I just didn't expect I'd end up the 'Godmother of Feelings.'" The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan tried to lighten the mood, with a touch of self-mockery.

"Yeah—an influencer, a top-tier streamer, praised and damned in equal measure." The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan picked up, no great emotion audible in her tone. "Selling clarity, puncturing illusions, laying out the gamesmanship, the value exchange, the true colors of human nature in emotional relationships. Plenty of people curse me, say I objectify everything, manufacture anxiety. But the ones who curse hardest are usually the ones most afraid of women understanding this logic. Because once you puncture the packaging of the 'love myth,' a lot of traditional relationship models built on exploitation and self-sacrifice become hard to sustain."

She looked at her young self and, rarely, said a few words more, like a warning, and like an explanation.

"This road stands in the path of the wind, and also on the edge of a blade. You'll be scrutinized, judged, attacked by countless people. You'll be very tired—tired in the heart. If I could, I'd honestly hope you could find another road, not so extreme, not so much under the spotlight and the magnifying glass. But…" She shook her head. "With your character, and the path I've walked, it seems very hard. All we can do is make sure we always hold chips—the backbone to turn and walk away, or to strike back head-on."

The thirty-year-old Yao Yuan fell into thought. The picture her future self painted was glamorous, yet heavy too. But strangely, that fear of fate's trajectory was replaced by a clear sense of "at least I can choose how to fight."

"I understand." She drew a deep breath, her gaze sharpening again. "I'll think carefully about the road ahead. You… do you have any advice on my current direction for starting up? Like, the future's next big thing?"

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan seemed to consider for a moment, then said, "AI. Especially applications deeply combined with vertical industries—that'll be the key to the next cycle. I'm watching and positioning for it now myself…"

Before she could finish, that familiar, overwhelming, irresistible pull surged in again without warning. The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan's image in the mirror suddenly twisted and faded. She had only time to cast her thirty-year-old self a complicated look, her lips moving slightly as if she still wanted to say something—

The next second, the mirror's ripples surged violently, and her figure was wiped away like a smudge of an eraser, vanishing in an instant.

In the elevator, silence returned. Only the thirty-year-old Yao Yuan remained, standing alone before the polished, person-reflecting mirror. In it, only her pale yet utterly changed face was left.

Even with the experience of the previous three times, watching another "self" vanish before her eyes in this way that defied common sense still set her heart pounding wildly with alarm. Uncanny, absurd—yet it had truly changed something.

Facing the empty mirror, she slowly, ever so slowly, exhaled the stale breath she'd been holding in her chest. Her fingertips were cold, but deep in her eyes, something had already thoroughly settled, cooled, and hardened.
