Chapter 5 — The Red Rose and the Binary: Zhao Yiming, Founder of "Western World"
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640252.html

The private room was heated to the point of being stuffy, the air thick with the expensive blend of aloeswood incense and cigars. The glow of the crystal chandelier spilled down onto the rosewood card table, throwing a hard, cold metallic sheen across the chips.

Zhao Yiming was not seated at the head of the table.

He was, in name, tonight's guest of honor — this private club called "Songyun" was itself one of Western World Technology's discreet venues for entertaining top-tier LPs (limited partners) and strategic shareholders. But at the moment he was reclining in the corner, sunk into a huge leather sofa, pinching a small white porcelain teacup between his fingers. The tea had long gone cold, the amber liquid untouched. He wore a dark-gray cashmere sweater, the cuffs rolled back slightly to reveal lean wrists. Amid a room full of Armani suits and Hermès belts, the outfit looked out of place, almost shabby. Yet no one dared underestimate him. The laughter at the card table was loud — three other capital heavyweights feeling one another out — while Zhao Yiming simply listened, those eyes a little unfocused from years spent staring at screens, drifting idly behind his rimless glasses toward the door of the room.

When the heavy solid-wood door was pushed open by a server and a cold draft swept in carrying a crisp note of perfume, the clamor inside seemed to hit pause.

Yao Yuan stood in the doorway like a rose that had been bathed in fire. Her beauty carried an edge of aggression — scorching, blazing — and yet within those burning embers shimmered an allure beyond words. It was the bearing of a woman tempered again and again by the mortal world: she had once shattered, then gathered herself up piece by piece and made herself whole again; she had long since seen through it all, and still chose, of her own free will, to turn back and return to this clamorous battlefield. She gave off a kind of solitary courage, a sharpness that had been mended, a quality impossible to sketch and yet enough to seize the heart.

She wore a white tiger-mink coat, the fur so pure it held not a single off shade, setting off skin whiter than snow. The hem swung with her steps, hinting at a length of red gown beneath; her high heels struck the carpet with a muffled, certain sound.

Wang Qian — the current president of the Jinshi branch of the Bank of Communications, in the middle of shuffling — paused, then broke into a wide smile. "Aiyo, Miss Yao's finally here! This game's been short a God of Wealth — now we're complete!"

Zhao Yiming did not rise.

In the instant he saw Yao Yuan, his pupils contracted, almost imperceptibly. It wasn't dazzlement — it was more like a top-tier programmer, deep in a vast ocean of code, suddenly stumbling on a core function that ran flawlessly, logically self-consistent, and yet was entirely unfamiliar, of unknown origin. An instinctive, highly alert signal.

Yao Yuan's gaze swept the room like radar and finally settled on the one man who hadn't stood to greet her, there in the corner.

Zhao Yiming, founder of Western World Technology, thirty-nine, young and accomplished. His background hid a kind of "cross-disciplinary gene" rare in tech circles. He'd done his undergraduate degree in physics, switched to computer science for his doctorate, and during his postdoc had immersed himself in a Silicon Valley biology lab, studying the intersection of neural networks and computational models. At thirty he'd come home with three top-journal papers and one bare-bones algorithmic model, rented a ten-square-meter office in a high-tech park, and — on three million in angel funding — keyed out the core code for the first generation of intelligent interactive chips.

Yao Yuan peeled off her leather gloves, handed them off to the assistant behind her without looking, and walked straight toward the card table — but stopped as she passed the sofa. She looked down at Zhao Yiming, the corner of her mouth curving into an arc that gave nothing away.

"They all say Director Zhao of Western World is the hardest man to land. Thanks to President Wang here, I finally get to see you in the flesh." Her voice was clear, with a particular trace of languor and edge. "What's this, Director Zhao — not going to play a hand? I hear tech people are the best at calculating odds."

Zhao Yiming set down the teacup his lips had never touched; its base clinked lightly against the marble side table with a crisp "tak." He finally lifted his head, meeting that appraising look with a level gaze.

"I don't gamble, Miss Yao." His tone was flat, without the slightest ripple of feeling. "I only do things that are certain."

"Oh?" Yao Yuan raised a brow and, taking the cue, sat down in the single armchair across from him, crossing her legs. The coat slid off one shoulder, baring the smooth line of it. "But President Wang tells me you're in a rush to find money to plug a hole this time. That's quite a gamble too."

The moment the words were out, the mood in the room froze a degree. The murmuring over by the tea table stopped; the other two at the card table set down their hands as well, watching Zhao Yiming warily.

It was naked provocation, and a probe. She was testing exactly how badly Western World Technology's cash flow had ruptured after the "circuit-breaker incident."

Zhao Yiming's face still showed nothing. He reached up and pushed the glasses up the bridge of his nose — his signature gesture for entering combat mode.

"Western World doesn't need to plug holes. We're rebuilding the foundation." He said it lightly, his voice low but every word distinct. "As for gambling… Miss Yao, you dressed this grandly to come see me — isn't that because you mean to place a bet here far bigger than anything on that card table?"

He didn't dodge the crisis; he threw the question back, precisely.

Yao Yuan blinked, then laughed. She leaned forward slightly, closing the distance. "Director Zhao, what kind of clueless-straight-guy talk is that? This is just how I dress. I plain enjoy dressing up, plain love looking good. I read people and I read myself. What's wrong with that?"

"Since you've decided I'm here to place a bet, why don't I show my hand? That second-gen model of your 'Genesis Engine' — can it actually run all the way through? Don't let it be like last time: burn a few hundred million and end up blowing up your own server room." She deliberately slowed down on those last few words.

"Blowing up the server room" — that was the insiders' nickname for that accident. Wang Qian coughed awkwardly beside them, trying to smooth things over.

Zhao Yiming's expression darkened a shade, but he kept his breathing under control. He looked Yao Yuan straight in the eye, his gaze as sharp as a scalpel.

"Technology never lies. Only people do. My code, my models, all my experimental data — it's right there, ready to face any level, any form of audit at any time. The question is, Miss Yao…"

He stood and walked to the huge floor-to-ceiling window, his back to the gaudy room, looking down at the city lights surging outside, brilliant as a streaming flow of data. The enormous LED screen flickered silently behind him, blue light sliding across his straight spine.

"Do you actually understand it?" He turned, the cold glow of the screen streaming behind him, setting him off as though he stood in another dimension. "Or do you just want to buy the hottest 'ticket' to the future — even if… you have no idea where this ship is headed, and don't even care whether it's seaworthy?"

He walked back toward the sofa, didn't sit, only bent slightly to close the physical distance between himself and Yao Yuan, bringing an intangible pressure with it.

"This is a setup," Zhao Yiming said, looking at her, biting off each word. "But I set it to filter out the speculators whose vision is too short. If you want in, you first have to prove you're not the 'outsider' the algorithm will scrub away."

You could have heard a pin drop in the room — only the low hum of the heating system. Yao Yuan let the smile fade from her face, her eyes turning serious. She knew that this was the true purpose of tonight's dinner.

Just then, the heavy solid-wood door was pushed open by a server again, and another beautiful woman came in. As she opened it, the hallway light fell across her just so — a crisply tailored black velvet suit, the shoulder pads not wide but enough to command the room, a moon-white silk shirt beneath, the collar left casually undone at the top two buttons to reveal clean collarbones: formal, yet carrying a touch of careless ease.

Below she wore matching high-waisted cigarette trousers, the cuffs just reaching the edge of slim-heeled black ankle boots, soundless on the carpet, yet every step steady. Her hair was loosely gathered at the back with an ebony hairpin, a few stray strands falling along her neck, softening the suit's severity. She wore little jewelry — only a single minimalist platinum hoop in her left ear, swaying gently with her movements, glinting with an understated shine in the warm light.

There was not a trace of unease about her. Her eyes swept over everyone present — neither servile nor superior — a faint smile resting at the corner of her mouth, with none of the deliberate warmth of someone trying to please, and none of the stiffness of a stranger either. It was a steadiness that came from within. Like a hibiscus grown wild in open country, both branch and leaf having weathered wind and rain, and so quiet, and so tough, its roots long since driven deep into the unseen.

She was Jin Lan, the helmswoman of Jinshi's dining empire, "Yellow River Tower." The story went that at eighteen she'd left her southwestern hometown alone, plunged headlong into the unfamiliar northwest, and from then on, single-handedly, won this stretch of sky for herself.

"What kept you?" Yao Yuan rose, her tone familiar, walking over to link arms affectionately with Jin Lan and introduce this new arrival to the watching eyes, open and hidden, between the card table and the sofa.

With everyone present, they sorted themselves into host and guests and took their seats.

The round table had been laid. Zhao Yiming took the head without ceremony. To his right was Yao Yuan; beside her was Director Zhang, who worked in new energy and had just closed a deal with Western World on automotive-grade chips. To Zhao Yiming's left was Wang Qian, and beside Wang Qian sat the newly arrived Jin Lan, then in turn Director Liu from a sensor company, Gao Jieyuan — a young partner from the venture-capital world — and two professors from a top university's artificial-intelligence lab.

The cold dishes arrived, crystalline and exquisitely plated. Zhao Yiming didn't pick up his chopsticks. He first lifted the warmed decanter, stood, and personally poured half a cup of amber rice wine for each of the two senior professors.

"Professor Li, Professor Wang, you two are older — drink something mild, it's easy on the stomach." His tone was respectful, the courtesy of a junior toward scholars. Then he scanned the table, raised the cup of clear liquor before him — limpid as water yet hiding a fierce strength. "The rest of you, suit yourselves. No pressing drinks tonight; we're here mainly to talk business. But the first cup — to welcome our two lady bosses, Director Yao and Boss Jin, and to thank everyone for honoring us."

With that he tipped his head back and downed the fiery liquor in one go. The motion was clean and decisive, carrying that stubborn sense of ritual peculiar to tech people. The others raised their cups in answer; the bright clink of glasses rang out, and the first cup slid down warm and burning.

After three rounds, the mood at the table loosened and warmed, visibly.

"Pretty Yao," said Wang Qian, clearly an old acquaintance, teasing with a smile, using the nickname from years ago. "I watched that livestream of yours today. Good grief — your tongue gets sharper by the day. That 'value exchange' theory of feelings of yours has more and more devotees. Even my wife is a die-hard fan. These days I both love and resent her — love that coming home is genuinely cozy, resent that she can't save up a cent of pocket money. With a fan base like yours, you must be raking it in?"

Yao Yuan and Wang Qian went way back — they'd known each other since she was a young girl, back when Wang Qian had only just started work at the Bank of Communications and used to drop by Shuai Hongqiang's office to cadge tea. Through one thing and another they'd gotten acquainted, and since they were close in age, they'd quickly grown familiar. "Pretty Yao" was the nickname he'd given her then.

Yao Yuan's eyes flashed; she pretended to shoot him a sidelong glare, that one look full of flirtation and a familiar edge. "President Wang, you've got the bargain and you're still showing off. Spend a little money, the household's harmonious, the emotional value maxed out, the feelings warming up — as long as you're not getting other ideas, isn't that a good deal? If you want life sweeter still, send your wife to my 'Sisters' Private Class.'" She finished, laughed first herself, then turned to the others to explain. "Actually my fan base isn't as broad as everyone imagines — nothing compared to the huge followings of the top sales-livestreamers. My clients are mostly urban women aged twenty-five to forty, highly educated, high-income. Money, brains, minds of their own — but maybe lacking security in their relationships. What they need isn't fantasy; it's strategy and method. Like our Boss Jin here," she said with a smile, gesturing to Jin Lan beside her. "Wisdom and real strength — that's the best security there is."

"Impressive, impressive!" Gao Jieyuan, sitting opposite, jumped right in, his smile radiant, showing two sharp little canine teeth. That young face, caught between boy and man, carried an unworn-away sincerity. "Sure enough, serving the high-net-worth crowd is the most efficient way to make money. The old saying's right — women's and children's money is the easiest to earn. Better yet, rich women's money."

Yao Yuan lifted her eyes, let her gaze rest on Gao Jieyuan's face for a beat, half a smile, and raised her cup in a slight toast. "Thank you for the appreciation, young Boss Gao." Then, elegantly, she drained what was left in her glass.

At this point Zhao Yiming, at the head of the table, let his gaze travel slowly around the round table and come to rest again on the two professors. "Professor Li, Professor Wang," he said, steering the small talk back on course, "the joint brain-computer interface lab we discussed last week — the site approval came through. Next week I'll have my assistant send over the design drawings; take a look and tell me what needs adjusting."

Professor Li nodded with a smile. "Director Zhao, your efficiency really is beyond reproach. On our end we've done a preliminary screening of the first batch of researchers — all top-notch PhDs in the relevant fields, very clean backgrounds."

The servers began bringing out the hot dishes soundlessly, the fragrance of fine delicacies drifting through the room.

Director Liu of the sensor company seized the chance to speak, his tone carrying a merchant's smoothness and a probing note. "Director Zhao, about… our next-generation smart-home chip — could the power-consumption standard be relaxed, maybe just a touch, two points? The test data on our new materials is honestly running a little tight…"

"Can't relax it." Zhao Yiming cut him off, his tone even but carrying an unshakable certainty, like stating a law of physics. "Users want battery life, not an excuse for us to compromise." He lifted a piece — the most tender part of the steamed reeves shad — and set it naturally onto Professor Wang's plate, while picking up the thread. "Here's what we'll do, Director Liu. Next week, have your CTO bring the core data over for a closed-door meeting with our chip team. Find the room in the underlying algorithm optimization — that's more solid than changing the standard."

He turned to Professor Wang, a different kind of light in his eyes now, the focus particular to talk of technology. "Professor Wang, that neural-signal denoising algorithm you mentioned last time — I had the team reproduce it. The data came out thirty percent better than expected. When are you publishing? Our company would like to sponsor it under our name."

Professor Wang's eyes lit up, color rising in his face. "Final round of revisions — definitely submitting next month! If Director Zhao's willing to support us, the upgrade cost for those few old machines in our lab…"

"Send the budget over." Zhao Yiming answered crisply, then turned the conversation with a negotiator's shrewdness. "But I have one condition: the patent for this technology, we hold jointly. For any later commercialization, Western World gets priority licensing."

Director Zhang of the new-energy firm laughed out loud beside them, pointing at Zhao Yiming. "Director Zhao, oh Director Zhao — that brain of yours is calculating the industry-academia-research conversion rate even at dinner. Doesn't miss a step!"

Zhao Yiming laughed too, and the laugh softened the habitual coldness of his face, showing a glimpse of genuine pleasure. He raised his cup to the table. "It's not calculating — it's responsibility. For those of us in tech, isn't the ultimate goal to turn the formulas and code in the lab into real, durable, usable things in ordinary people's hands? Come — don't just talk, try this matsutake-and-chicken stew. I had the kitchen add bamboo fungus specially. You researchers burn the most mental energy; you need to replenish."

The neon outside slanted through the floor-to-ceiling glass, casting interlaced light and shadow across the side of his face. Those eyes, always mirroring code and data streams, now — amid the rising steam of the food and the faint glints of clinking cups — held a little less of the machine's chill and a little more of a rare, vivid certainty. As if, even in the most worldly bustle of this dinner, he could still see, with perfect clarity, that solid road to the future paved by technology.

By the time the dinner was half over, the mood held a delicate balance in the alcohol and the talk of people each nursing their own designs. They discussed markets, technology, policy, edges hidden in their words, and yet in the round of pouring and drinking it all seemed convivial. Jin Lan spoke little; only when Wang Qian or Director Liu steered the topic her way did she respond briefly, with a fitting smile, on the digitalization of the dining industry or supply-chain finance — precise, watertight. But her gaze drifted now and then, thoughtful, over the laughing, talkative Yao Yuan, and over Zhao Yiming at the head of the table, who took part in the conversation while his eyes stayed clear throughout, as though forever modeling and analyzing in his head.

In the gap before another main course came out, Yao Yuan picked up a hot towel, wiped her hands unhurriedly, then lifted her cup — not toasting the whole table, but turning to Zhao Yiming beside her.

"Director Zhao," she said, not loudly, yet the talk of those nearby naturally dropped lower, "what I said at the door earlier was too harsh. I'll fine myself a cup and apologize." With that, without waiting for him to react, she drank the liquor down. The motion was clean, with the bracing readiness of someone street-smart.

Zhao Yiming looked at the empty cup, his expression unmoved, and only lifted his own to take a small sip. "You speak too gravely, Miss Yao. A technical discussion — about the matter, not the person."

Yao Yuan set down her cup and turned her body slightly toward him, a posture that read as closer, and that also walled off some of the others' eyes. "Then I won't beat around the bush either. Director Zhao, that 'Genesis Engine' of yours aims at the Holy Grail of artificial general intelligence. The scale of the vision, the scope of the ambition — I admire it. But the road to the Grail burns money, burns time, and burns investors' patience even more."

Zhao Yiming's fingers unconsciously rubbed the rim of his cup as he waited for what came next.

"I'm a businessperson. I care about return on investment, and I care about… booking the gains while I can." Yao Yuan tapped a fingertip lightly on the table. "Your large model is the 'operating system' of the future — limitless potential. But what I want to talk about is an 'application' that can run all the way through right now, can be monetized, can immediately serve my existing million high-net-worth female users — an emotional-counseling AI built on your engine's core capabilities."

The table went quiet in an instant. Even the two professors, who'd been talking in low voices, set down their chopsticks and looked over with keen interest. Jin Lan lifted her teacup, lashes lowered as if savoring the aroma, while in fact she was taking in every sound.

Zhao Yiming finally raised his eyes and looked Yao Yuan full in the face, the gaze behind his lenses sharp as a probe. "An emotional-counseling AI? There are plenty of chatbots on the market, plenty of algorithms dressed in psychology's clothing. Which kind does Miss Yao's 'Pretty Big One' want to be? A higher-grade script library, or…"

"Neither." Yao Yuan cut him off, her eyes blazing, with the near-fanatical light of someone speaking of her true field. "What I want isn't a chat companion, isn't a placebo of soothing scripts. What I want is a 'super consultant' that can genuinely 'understand' complex emotional relationships, that can run deep analysis based on a vast body of real cases and psychological models, and deliver executable strategies. It has to understand the subtleties of human nature, the games of self-interest, the power dynamics within intimate relationships — and it has to understand my users: those smart, wealthy, but equally fragile and bewildered modern women. The advice it gives can't be 'drink more hot water.' It has to be a deliverable, personalized 'solution.'"

She paused, leaned forward, dropped her voice lower still, but with more force. "Director Zhao, your engine's strengths are understanding, reasoning, and generation. My 'Pretty Big One' has accumulated five years' worth — over a hundred thousand hours of real counseling recordings (de-identified), several million pieces of user-interaction data, and an analytical framework we've iterated ourselves that actually works. What we lack is the 'brain' that fuses this framework deeply with your 'understanding and generation' power — an AI core that can keep learning and evolving, that truly possesses 'emotional intelligence.'"

Zhao Yiming fell silent. He leaned back against the chair, fingers tapping lightly on the table — his habitual gesture when sinking into deep thought. All that was left in the room was the low, lingering guqin of the background music and the faint clamor of the street far off.

"A very interesting direction." After a moment he spoke, his tone back to the calm of a technical discussion. "Affective computing is the deep water of AI ethics, and a technical hard problem. What you're asking for isn't pattern recognition — it's value judgment, predictive strategy generation. That involves high-dimensional modeling of unstructured emotional data, dynamic simulation of complex social relationships, and…" He looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze like fire. "How do you define a 'correct' or 'effective' strategy? Does your data framework itself carry biases you haven't noticed? And if the AI's 'advice' produces a bad outcome, how is responsibility defined?"

Every question struck straight at the core.

Yao Yuan didn't dodge. She met his gaze, the corner of her mouth even curving into an arc of appreciation. "That's exactly why I say this is a challenge only you and your team could possibly take on. On data bias and the ethics framework, my team has a preliminary plan, but it needs a top-tier technical mind like yours to refine and 'sterilize' it. On responsibility, we can design a layered-interaction and risk-warning system — the AI is a 'consultant,' not a 'decision-maker'; the final choice always stays in the user's hands. Most important of all…"

She leaned in, her voice carrying an almost bewitching certainty. "Director Zhao, this isn't merely a business project. It's a superb 'real-world testing ground.' Your 'Genesis Engine' needs to be forged, validated, and evolved on concrete, complex, uncertainty-filled real-world tasks. What could test an AI's 'intelligence' better than 'emotion' and 'relationships' — the oldest, most subtle, most elusive things humanity has? If your engine can run through here, that means it's taken a key step in understanding human nature and complex systems. Isn't that more valuable than simply burning money to pass generic benchmarks — and… sexier, too?"

The word "sexy," spoken from her lips and applied to a technical discussion, brought a strange jolt of impact. Zhao Yiming's fingertips stopped tapping.

At this Jin Lan set her teacup down lightly, the porcelain meeting the saucer with a crisp "ding" that broke the taut magnetic field between the two of them. She looked at Zhao Yiming, her voice calm as water yet carrying a quiet strength. "Director Zhao, I'm a layperson — I don't understand the technology. But I understand business, and I understand people. This idea of Yao Yuan's sounds like a flight of fancy, but its roots run deep. She has precise clientele in hand, accumulated data, a mature product logic — and more crucially, she has clear demand and a market willing to pay for high-quality solutions. You have the sharpest technical weapon. If the two can be combined, it might not just make a successful AI product — it might open a new window into understanding 'people.' That might… be closer to the essence of technology than many a seemingly grand project."

Zhao Yiming's gaze moved between Yao Yuan's burning expectation and Jin Lan's cool analysis. He picked up the tea that had gone stone-cold and took a sip; the chill of the liquid sharpened his thoughts further.

"I'll need to see all your data protocols, de-identification process, your existing analytical framework, and a preliminary ethics-review report." He finally spoke, his tone the rigor of strict business. "My team will do a technical-feasibility assessment and risk simulation. If the first-stage assessment passes, we can set up a joint project group. As for the form of investment — I won't use traditional financial investment. I want technical control of this joint venture and the corresponding share of intellectual property. If the project succeeds, Western World gets right of first refusal on the core technology modules of 'Pretty Big One Emotional AI,' plus licensing for subsequent development. At the same time, the technical insights and data dimensions this project generates — the ones that help the general model evolve — need to be fed back to the 'Genesis Engine' master model in a manner we agree on."

His terms were clear and hard, carrying the confidence and sense of boundary peculiar to one who commands a technology.

The smile on Yao Yuan's face widened — the thrill of a hunter watching coveted prey finally step to the edge of the trap, even if she herself might be prey at the same time. She didn't agree at once, but mused for a moment, a shrewd calculation flickering in her eyes.

"Technical control is negotiable, but product definition and user experience must be led by my team. Intellectual property shared — but the 'Pretty Big One' brand and customer data belong to our side. The mechanism and boundaries of the technical feedback need a detailed agreement. As for the investment amount, the equity structure, and the milestone bet-on-performance clauses…" She stood and held out her right hand, exaggeratedly long nails set off by vivid red polish, as if announcing to the world her singular personality and untamed soul. "Director Zhao, it seems we'll need a few long meetings on our own, to 'spar' over the details. Welcome to… the laboratory of human nature."

Zhao Yiming looked at that extended hand — well-tended, representing a vast female business empire — and slowly rose to his feet as well. He didn't take it at once; instead he picked up the decanter and refilled both his and Yao Yuan's cups with clear liquor.

"The laboratory welcomes all serious researchers," he said, handing her one cup and raising the other, its rim held slightly lower than hers. "But the first rule of my laboratory is: respect the data, respect the logic, respect the unknown. Cheers, Miss Yao."

"To verifiable solutions," Yao Yuan said, clinking her cup against his, her smile bright and sharp, "and to a feelings market about to be redefined."

The two cups met in the air with a crisp ring. The clear liquid swayed, reflecting the fine, scattered light of the crystal chandelier overhead — and reflecting, too, the ambition and anticipation in each pair of eyes: utterly different, and yet equally ablaze.

By the time the dinner broke up, the night had grown deep. Yao Yuan and Jin Lan walked side by side toward the club's entrance, talking in low voices. Zhao Yiming lagged behind with the two professors, still discussing some algorithmic detail. Wang Qian, Director Liu, and the others were each collected by their drivers.

Before they parted, Zhao Yiming said to Yao Yuan: "Tuesday next week, ten in the morning, Western World headquarters. Bring your core team and your data clearances. We begin, officially."

Yao Yuan nodded, her eyes startlingly bright in the dark. "It's a date, Director Zhao."

The car door closed, and the sedan carrying Yao Yuan and Jin Lan pulled into the neon-streaming streets. Zhao Yiming stood where he was, letting the night wind blow over him, and lifted his eyes toward the blurred sky above the city, its stars masked by light pollution — as if, within that chaos, he could see a corner of a clear, brand-new picture of the future, built of zeros and ones.

And in that picture, for the first time, two non-standardized variables had appeared so vividly: "emotion" and "relationships." The challenge was unprecedented — but this was exactly what he had always been searching for. Only the hardest problems are worth cracking.
