A Woman Should Spend with a Plan: Rescue Emergencies, Not Rot; a Soft Heart Is the Poorest Fate; Earn Money to Build Your Backbone
[Call connected.]
The caller (her voice like leather that has been beaten over and over until it lost all its spring — dry, exhausted, every word dragging a heavy chain): "Teacher Yuanyuan, my family of origin has dragged me down until I really can't hold on anymore."
She did not even have the strength to cry. Her account was calm in a way that was terrifying.
"My father has had a gambler's mindset his whole life — addicted to betting, to speculation, never grounded. Always dreaming of getting rich overnight, always running from reality. Our family went bankrupt long ago. He's in debt, he's on the credit blacklist, and time after time I've softened. The money I saved from working, the money I earned from my business — all of it went into filling his pit."
She paused, and inside that pause was a powerlessness as deep as an abyss.
"I used to believe blood was thicker than water. I thought if I helped him once, twice, he'd repent, he'd wake up, he'd settle down and live a steady life. It did nothing at all. Every time he earned a sum, he immediately squandered it, gambled it away, burned it up. Every time he ran up a debt, he turned straight around and came to me to cover it. The moment I cleared one debt for him and let myself breathe, he'd already run up another, bigger one out there. I came to a despairing realization: my 'help' had become the very thing that let him keep rotting. He knew that even if the sky fell, he had a daughter who'd sell her blood and cut off her own flesh to hold it up for a while."
"I'm so torn now. If I don't help him, I feel guilty, my conscience won't rest. If I help him, his life is like an endless fall, and I keep stupidly trying to be the soft human cushion at the bottom, hoping I can wake his guilt. But I forgot — a falling person only claws desperately for something to grab; he never looks down to see how many pieces the cushion has shattered into. Right now I feel dragged into a swamp I can never climb out of. Teacher Yuanyuan, what on earth am I supposed to do?"

[A soft heart is the poorest fate! Filling the pit forever, never a day to rise!]
Yao Yuan listened to the end in silence, no ripple crossing her face.
"What I heard isn't a family-ethics tragedy," she began, her voice as steady as if she were analyzing a failed business case. "What I heard is an economic system running on faulty assumptions, doomed to collapse. In this system, you are the only cash flow and the lender of last resort, and your father is a speculator who keeps adding leverage with zero sense of risk control. Your 'soft heart' is the unlimited credit line the system grants him. The ending was written the first time you injected capital without conditions: your liquidity is drained dry, his debt black hole expands without limit, and the system goes bankrupt — both of you."
[Countless cries of "Ah!" drift across the scrolling comments.]
[An economic system! Unlimited credit! Put that way, it all clicks!]
[So it's business logic, not an emotional problem!]
[Yao Yuan can always pull sentimental pain down into a rational model and take it apart!]
"Let's dissect your father, the 'underlying asset,'" Yao Yuan said, her tone like a cool risk assessor. "He didn't 'run into misfortune.' He's rotten. Rotten at the root. What is the core of a gambler's thinking? A fantasy of wealth divorced from real-world production. An extreme indifference to consequences. A parasitic foundation to the whole personality. Every risk he takes, every debt, every time he comes to you to cover it — he's repeatedly confirming that this mindset works. See, even when I blow it, there's a way (you) to bear the cost. Your money never 'helped' him at all. It kept feeding and reinforcing that parasitic, irresponsible 'gambler personality' inside him. You've been using the capital of your own life to supply endless ammunition for a bet he is bound to lose."
Her gaze cut sharply toward the camera, as if piercing the screen to look straight at that exhausted daughter:
"All your pain right now comes from one fundamental error in thinking: you've taken 'saving a rotten person' to be your responsibility, your duty. No. That is not your responsibility. An adult's first responsibility is to save themselves. Your responsibility is to defend the fragile little bit of survival capital and inner order you built with such effort — not to throw them into a bottomless pit that has no wish to repent and only wants to drag you down with it, just to hear the splash."
[Scrolling comments]: ["Feeding the gambler personality"! Right to the bone!]
["Saving yourself is your first responsibility"! That line just made my whole body shudder.]
[Sinking together… so true. He doesn't need help, he needs someone buried with him.]
"So you ask me what to do?" Yao Yuan asked and answered herself, her tone resolute, handing down a program of action with no room for compromise. "Step one: immediately launch a 'financial cut-off' and 'emotional quarantine.' Tell him clearly, and tell yourself: from this moment, on any matter concerning his debts, you will not provide one cent of financial support, nor will you carry any of his anxiety. Him on the blacklist, him hounded by creditors, him living in hardship — that is the bitter fruit he planted himself, and he must swallow it himself. Pain is the only possible generator of change. Only when you snuff out his last parasitic fantasy will he truly stand at the dead end of 'taking responsibility for himself' — and that is the one chance he has to begin becoming an adult."
"Step two: rebuild your 'economics of kindness.' Real kindness has a strong threshold and clear conditions. Rescue the emergency, not the poverty; rescue the repentant, not the rotten. An 'emergency' is a sudden, external storm. 'Poverty' and 'rot' are internal, ongoing states. For internal rot, any resource you pour in is just polluting your own ecosystem. The day he proves — not with words, but with years of steady honest work, with quitting gambling completely, and with taking real consequences for his past — that his personality system has finished its 'reformat and virus scan,' then the two of you can talk about a limited, conditional family bond built on respect rather than extortion. Remember: helping a person who has already stood up to walk steadier is a virtue; dragging a person who refuses to stand up is foolishness."
"Step three, and the one you should throw everything into: take back every bit of mental energy that's been drained by inner conflict, one hundred percent, and pour it into 'building your own capital.' Why are you in pain? Because your energy and resources are being siphoned off continuously, and your sense of self-worth is bound to the hopeless act of 'filling the pit.' Now take it all back and invest it in things that make you more valuable, more powerful: sharpen your professional skills, open new income channels, train your body, build up savings, construct your psychological and physical breakwater. A woman's backbone has never been her kindness — it's the thick layer of self-worth and wealth beneath that kindness, the layer no one can take from her. When you yourself are heavy enough, no one's storm can knock you over or drag you under so easily."
At the end, Yao Yuan's tone turned deep and full of force:
"Your father spent a lifetime acting out what a 'rotten fate' is — living life as a vicious cycle of endless taking from the outside to patch the hollow inside. And you are using your 'soft heart' to try to write the sequel to his script. It's time to tear it up."
"Your life is not his sequel. Your money is not the ransom for your fate. When you start guarding the boundary of your money and the energy of your inner world the way you'd guard your own life, you'll truly understand what 'wealth' is — not just the number in your account, but that steady force in your soul that says, 'no one gets to take anything from me so easily.'"
"Now, hang up the phone. Go do three things: One — block every middleman who relays his debt news to you. Two — open a savings account that belongs only to you, with no joint holder, and put a 'never to be touched' fund of backbone in it. Three — sign up for a course you've always wanted to take but felt was a waste of money. Tell the world, and tell yourself, with action: from now on, every cent of my money and every kind of my kindness flows only toward what adds value to my life."
[Call ended.]
Yao Yuan looked into the camera, no lead-up at all, opening straight with a conclusion quenched in ice:
"For so many women, the starting point of a ruined life is exactly this — kindness with money that follows no rules, and squandering the value of their own blood and sweat with no limits. Every woman, remember this: money must be spent with a plan. Money is your armor. Every cent in your hand is your backbone, your way out, your dignity — not someone else's bottomless pit. With anyone you spend money on — family, friend, lover, or your sweet little puppy of a boyfriend — there has to be a plan, a measure, a bottom line. When you've earned a certain amount, your awareness, your presence, your say, your power to choose your own life will all be different. At that point, guard your backbone first, and only then will you have a steady rest of your life.
So many women spend their whole lives passive, lowly, manipulated — not because they aren't kind enough, but because they spend with no plan, soften with no floor, and rescue people with no limits."
When she finished, she lifted her water glass and took a slow sip, her gaze drifting out the window to the endlessly rushing Yellow River. The muddy water never pauses or changes course for any person or thing that sinks into it. It obeys only its own gravity and its riverbed, flowing eastward all the way.
"All right, that's the end of today's 'surgery.'" She gave the camera a very faint, professional smile, that electric rose-violet still vivid on her lips in the softening light. "One life-saving line for all women: the greatest luxury in life is not how much you own — it's having the capital to say 'no' while the other party can do nothing about it. Half of that capital comes from the cold, hard number in your bank card; half comes from the searing, inviolable boundary in your heart. The former you have to fight to earn; the latter you have to be ruthless enough to draw. Don't indulge rotten people, don't burn yourself out, don't let others hijack your life. Let's hold each other to it. See you next livestream."
The equipment shut off, the lights going dark one by one.
The studio sank into a stillness made for reflection. Yao Yuan did not leave at once. She walked slowly to the great floor-to-ceiling window. Outside, the city's evening lamps were coming up, neon beginning to flow, and against it the Yellow River below — ancient, silent, churning its sand and silt forward — formed a strange and vast tableau: on one side the dazzling clamor of human civilization, on the other the chaotic indifference of natural time.
The "heart-guarding" old jade at her chest was warm and smooth as ever. The threads of the Mirror Pact were like cobwebs in the wind — faint, yet stubbornly pointing to this city beneath her feet. Yu Hao was running around Shanghai for his project. In Zhao Yiming's lab, the code of "Pretty Big One" was iterating in silence. And she herself had just, in a single livestream, handed down a calm verdict on a stranger's tangle of feelings.
All the threads, projects, relationships, and unsolved riddles were like the countless grains of sand rising and sinking in the Yellow River outside — unclear, uncountable, yet together they formed the entire foundation of her life at this moment. She no longer rushed to find an answer that would settle everything once and for all. Instead she was learning to live alongside this complexity and uncertainty, to build her own order within the turbulence, to keep a clear heading through the chaos.
Night fell completely, swallowing the last thread of daylight. Yao Yuan turned, took the cream-white silk blazer from the back of the chair, draped it on with elegant ease, then briskly switched off the lights and locked the door.
The sound of high heels striking the floor rang out in the empty corridor — clear, steady, step by step toward the elevator, toward the dazzling, nighttime depths of the city's splendor, and toward her next unknown tomorrow, the one she would have to face with a clear head.
