Chapter 80 — Facing the Controversy Head-On: Clear-Eyed Awareness Was Never the Commercialization of Human Nature

A clear note of admiration showed in Jiang Tiantian's eyes. She fell silent for a moment, as if digesting the density of what had just been said. Then she sat up straight, folded her hands in her lap, and her expression turned more serious as she put forward a more direct, more challenging question.

"Yao Yuan, this 'cognitive map' and 'methodology' of yours have given you enormous influence—but they've also put you at the center of public debate. I can't avoid a criticism people online have leveled at you very sharply, even abusively. Many accuse you of essentially objectifying women and objectifying feelings—of teaching girls not self-love and independence, but how to 'scheme' and 'extract' more cleverly. Others have dug up some of your early content and clients, attacking the fact that you started out serving so-called 'mistresses' and 'working girls,' arguing that your true colors are teaching people to take shortcuts and latch onto others. Faced with these voices—these very ugly, directly personal attacks—what goes through your mind? How do you face them, and how do you make peace with yourself?"

The question landed like a stone dropped into a still lake, instantly shattering the rational, exploratory mood of before and bringing real pressure, sharp with the smell of gunpowder. The air in the recording booth seemed to congeal.

There was no flash of offended anger on Yao Yuan's face, no flustered rush to defend herself. She even shifted her posture slightly, settling more comfortably against the back of her chair—a posture that was not defense, but acceptance. She was silent for a few seconds, seconds that would feel very long in the recording. Then she lifted her eyes and met Jiang Tiantian's gaze calmly, as if looking through the equipment at everyone who might hold such a view.

"Tiantian, thank you for asking this. It matters, and I never dodge it." Her voice was as steady as ever, but it carried a heavier weight now. "First, I understand where this criticism and anger come from. When we talk about feelings, marriage, self-interest, it's very easy to touch the parts of people they treasure most and feel most vulnerable about—their faith in pure emotion, their hope for fairness and justice, their ingrained sense of what a woman's identity and worth should be. The way I express things—direct, sharp, full of business and game-theory terms—looks to many people like a 'desecration' and 'objectification' of sacred emotion in itself. It's a fierce clash at the level of perception, and I respect that feeling."

Then her tone shifted, becoming clear and firm.

"But understanding is not the same as agreeing. There are a few things I need to clarify very plainly."

"First, on 'objectification.' I believe that pointing out the value exchange, risk assessment, and self-interested games that objectively exist within relationships is not the same as preaching 'objectification.' Quite the opposite—only by daring to face and discuss the real dimensions that romantic narratives deliberately conceal can women avoid being truly 'objectified' and exploited without ever realizing it. I'm teaching girls to see the board and the rules clearly, not teaching them to willingly become pawns to be pushed around on it—or worse, to become the stakes on someone else's board. 'Clarity' is the antidote to 'objectification,' not its accomplice."

"Second, on 'teaching people to extract.' The core premise and ultimate goal of all my advice is to raise a woman's own value, ability, and power to choose. It's teaching them how to build their own careers, protect their own assets, hold their own boundaries, and win equal respect and treatment within a relationship. I encourage 'earning' by your own ability, 'creating,' 'negotiating'—not 'scraping,' 'leeching,' 'begging.' There's a fundamental difference. If someone thinks that learning to protect the interests you're rightly owed, and demanding equal respect when you give, makes you a 'gold-digger,' then perhaps they need to re-examine their assumptions about the role of women."

"Third, on my early audience." Here Yao Yuan paused, and a flicker of complicated emotion crossed her eyes—understanding in it, but also an unwavering candor. "Yes. When I was just starting out, when my reach was still very limited, part of my audience really was women in non-mainstream romantic relationships, or women in especially difficult, even marginalized emotional situations. In mainstream discourse they were ignored, scorned, even cast out. But their pain was real, their predicaments were concrete, and they too longed to be seen, to be understood, to receive practical help. The analytical perspective and practical advice I offered may not fit traditional moral judgment, but it really did help some of them clarify their situations, make choices that served them better, even climb out of the mire. I have never used any group's 'moral standing' to pre-decide who is qualified to receive the help of 'clear-eyed awareness.' The cognitive tool itself is neutral; what matters is the person using it—to save themselves, or to hurt others. My content has always pointed toward the former."

Her tone gradually took on a deep, grounded strength.

"As for the abuse and the attacks—it would be a lie to say they don't affect me at all. I'm human too. But slowly I learned to distinguish. Some criticism comes from misunderstanding and differences in perception, and I'm willing to keep communicating, to keep explaining what I actually mean. But the larger share—the pure malice, the labeling and the insults—I choose not to listen to. Why? Because my time and energy are very limited, and I want to keep them for the people who genuinely need help, who are willing to grow alongside me—for my own work, my team, and the value I want to create. Being liked by everyone has never been my goal. My goal is that the people who trust me and follow me live a little better, a little clearer, a little stronger because of my content. As long as that core goal keeps being met—as long as I see more and more women lift their awareness and walk out of bad relationships, build their own careers, win more equal intimate relationships—then the noise outside is just noise."

She looked at Jiang Tiantian at last, her gaze clear and resolute.

"Tiantian, the 'instruction manual' this era gives women is too vague, even self-contradictory. I'm only trying—based on my extensive 'clinical' observation and my own practice—to write a supplementary guide that comes closer to the real complexity of things: about 'emotional and interpersonal risk control' and 'building your own value.' It isn't perfect; it may offend some people. But it serves others. That's what I do. As for labels—people are free to stick them on, but I know who I am, what I'm doing, and where the value lies. That's enough."

Jiang Tiantian was silent for a long time. She only looked at Yao Yuan deeply, scrutiny in that gaze, inquiry too, and finally it turned into a deep, moved admiration. She took off her glasses, rubbed the bridge of her nose, and put them back on—a tiny gesture that betrayed the stir inside her.

"I understand," she said at last, her voice a little low and rough.

After a pause she pressed forward with another pointed question.

"Yao Yuan, you've turned this extraordinarily profound 'cognitive map' of human nature and feelings into a phenomenon-level business—your livestreams, your courses, your community, and now the AI project you're running with a top technical team. Some marvel that this is wisdom turned into revenue; others question whether it 'vulgarizes' and 'commercializes' emotion. How do you yourself define what you're doing? Is it a business about 'growth,' or a business about 'human nature'? Or are the two simply one and the same?"

The question was sharp enough, and it touched the core of the controversy surrounding her. There was no trace of offense or defensiveness on Yao Yuan's face. She even smiled faintly, a smile with a candid clarity to it.

"Teacher Jiang asks very directly, so I'll answer directly too." Her posture was still elegant, her gaze as keen as ever. "I believe what I do is a business about 'clear-eyed choice.' And the premise of clear-eyed choice is to see the truth as far as possible—the truth about yourself, about others, about how the world actually runs. My content, my products, everything I put out, comes down to one thing: providing cognitive tools, reducing information asymmetry, helping my users—especially my women users—see a little more clearly amid complicated reality and emotional confusion, and thereby raise the odds that they'll make choices that serve them."

"Is emotional confusion a widespread, genuine need? Yes. Is understanding the rules of the games people play with each other an important awareness, one that can spare you a lot of pointless harm? Yes. Is it reasonable commerce to pass that awareness on through livestreams, courses, even smarter means in the future, and charge for it? I think it is. It's like a lawyer providing legal knowledge, a doctor providing medical knowledge. What I provide—based on extensive case study and my own practice—is an 'awareness risk-screening' and 'strategy optimization advice' for feelings and relationships. Of course it has value, and of course it can become a business."

She paused, and her tone grew deeper still.

"As for the charge of 'vulgarizing'—I think there may be a prejudice behind it, the belief that the emotional and spiritual realm should be 'pure,' untainted by commerce. But the reality is that knowledge, wisdom, and experience have value in themselves. Free things are often the most expensive, because they can cost you immeasurable hidden costs—time, emotion, even a lifetime of happiness. Paying for valuable awareness is respect for the person who produced that knowledge, and it's taking responsibility for your own growth. What matters is whether what I provide truly helps people, whether it truly brings positive change. From the feedback of countless users, I think the answer is yes."

"So it's both a business about growth and a business about human nature—because a person's growth is, in essence, the ongoing process of colliding with your own nature, with others' nature, and with the rules of the world, then understanding them and finding a way to coexist. I've simply distilled some of the shared, crucial 'awareness nodes' and 'game models' in that process and turned them into products that can be transmitted and learned." She concluded calmly and with conviction.

Quiet settled over the booth again, only the faint hum of the equipment's current. Jiang Tiantian looked at Yao Yuan for several seconds, then slowly, deeply nodded.

"What I'm hearing isn't just the logic of a successful entrepreneur—it's the life wisdom of someone who has completed a profound integration of self." She said it with feeling, then shifted, her tone taking on a more personal note. "One last question, Yao Yuan. For you personally, having walked all these roads and arrived at the 'clarity' you have now, what do you think matters most? If you could say one sentence to the young women still searching in the fog, what would you say?"

Yao Yuan fell silent. The question reached into a more private territory. She stroked the old jade at her wrist again, and its warmth seemed to flow into every limb. She thought of the unsolved fog of the Mirror Pact, of the starry sky over the Gobi and Zhao Yiming's rational eyes, of the joys and sorrows of different versions of "herself" she had witnessed across her travels through time, and of all the scars and medals she had earned along the way.

"For me personally," her voice grew very soft, yet every word carried the weight of a thousand pounds, "what matters most is perhaps this: that after all the disillusionment, the wounds, the scrutiny and the abandonment, I still didn't give up trusting and shaping myself—and that in the end I learned to make peace with the world's complexity, rather than fight it or flee it. Clarity isn't never being hurt again; it's knowing, after the wound, how to heal and grow tougher. It isn't never believing again; it's learning how to discern and protect yourself. It isn't becoming cold; it's being able, even after you understand the rules deeply, to keep the most precious warmth in your heart and the capacity to choose kindness."

She lifted her eyes, looking straight at Jiang Tiantian, and as if through the equipment, at countless possible listeners.

"If I had to say one sentence to young women, I'd say: cherish and invest in yourself with everything you have—your health, your awareness, your skills, your financial base. That is the lifeboat that will never sink under you, in any relationship, in any storm. Then go bravely—to experience, to love, to be hurt, to succeed, to fail. But whatever you go through, always remember: you are the only, and the most important, author and protagonist of your own life's script. Other people's judgments, society's clock, the comings and goings of love—they're only scenery or interludes in your story; they can't define you. The only thing that can define you is the choices you make for yourself, again and again, after clear-eyed awareness—and the effort you put into those choices, and the responsibility you bear for them."

When her voice fell, the booth stayed silent for a long while.

Jiang Tiantian didn't add any summary or assessment. She only nodded again, gravely and deeply, then made a closing gesture toward the control room.

The interview was over.

As she took off her headphones, the sounds of the world rushed back in. Yao Yuan felt a deep exhaustion, but also a strange ease, as if she'd finished a high-quality session of mental yoga. The thoughts buried deep in her heart—including her response to the sharpest doubts—had all been clearly sorted and expressed through this conversation.

"Thank you, Yao Yuan." Jiang Tiantian rose, came around the little round table, and this time, instead of shaking hands, gave Yao Yuan's shoulders a light embrace—a gesture full of the understanding and support that passes between women. "Today's conversation will mean a great deal to me, and to many of the people who hear it. Thank you for your work."

"Thank you too, Tiantian. It was a wonderful conversation." Yao Yuan returned a sincere smile; she could feel a maturity and generosity in Jiang Tiantian that ran beyond her years.

Leaving the booth, the daylight outside had already deepened to dusk. Her assistant handed over her coat and clutch. Yao Yuan walked to the great floor-to-ceiling window and looked out. The streetlamps in the campus came on one after another, the neon of the distant city beginning to flicker—another ordinary Jinshi evening.

The old jade at her chest quietly gave off its steady warmth. The podcast interview was over, but the clues about the Mirror Pact, the testing of the Pretty Big One, the brand-new relationship just beginning with Zhao Yiming, her own voyage through the currents of the age and her personal fate… all of it still went on.

She drew her coat closer, turned, and walked toward the elevator with a steady stride. Her silhouette, in the thickening dusk and the first-lit lights, looked clear and sure. Whether facing the probing of a deep interview or the unsolved riddles of life, she had already found a rhythm and a way of the heart that were her own—staying clear-eyed amid chaos, guarding her true self amid the games, creating value amid commerce, and, in growth, continually reshaping a stronger, ever more complete self. This was the game Yao Yuan had chosen: the game of the clear-eyed.
