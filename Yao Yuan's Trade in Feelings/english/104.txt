Chapter 104 — Lin Wan Learns the Truth Behind Shuai Hongqiang's "Good Fortune"

The corridor outside the ICU was steeped, as it always was, in the cold, sharp sting of disinfectant. The dead silence of the small hours was pierced by the faint, steady beep of the monitors, a stillness so heavy it was hard to breathe.

When Lin Wan heard what Shuai Hongqiang said, she only stared at him blankly, her eyes hollow, as if she couldn't understand how, at a moment when their son hung between life and death, he had the heart to talk about anything else. Every fiber of her attention was still fixed on that heavy door of the monitoring room, on those cold machines, on her son's swollen face.

Shuai Hongqiang shut his eyes, and when he opened them again there was a bottomless pain and remorse in them. He gripped her hand tightly. That hand had once been warm and soft; now it felt like holding a block of melting ice.

He knew there was no longer any room to hide it. This disaster, the one tearing through their whole family, had its roots in his own desperation two years ago and the greed that had driven him to break the deadlock at any cost.

Shuai Hongqiang's voice was hoarse and dry. He spoke slowly, every word scraped out of his throat with effort, trembling in a way he couldn't disguise. Slowly he traced back over everything, laying that absurd, eerie bargain out before Lin Wan word by word.

The story began two years ago, in the dead end of a near-total financial collapse. By then the company's cash flow had snapped, the bank loans were closing in step by step, and a mountain of debt left him unable to breathe. His business had hit a total impasse, and the whole household lay under the shadow of bankruptcy and debt. At the lowest point, with nowhere left to turn, he went to the bank to ask Wang Qian — who had by then been promoted to bank president — to help him maneuver. Over dinner after work, Wang Qian had murmured in his ear, half in jest and half in earnest, that he should give Yao Yuan the bronze mirror he'd bought at the "Yaji Studio" winter auction eight years earlier — the one said to be able to lend a man fortune — as a last desperate gamble. Every word struck at the soft spot of a man frantic to claw his way out.

Drunk and dizzy, cornered with no way out, treating any cure for a dying horse as worth a try, Shuai Hongqiang finally lost his judgment. Greed got the better of him for a moment, and the very next day he gave that ancient bronze mirror — treasured for eight years — to his ex-girlfriend, Yao Yuan.

He slowly laid out the strange truth of the mirror: this was no ordinary antique. A person with a special fate could trigger a hidden "Time-Reversal Talisman" within it. From the moment the mirror reached Yao Yuan's hands, and she — carrying the great fortune of the Nine-Purple Departing Fire — triggered the "Time-Reversal Talisman," the fates of the two of them were forcibly bound together, sealing the Mirror Pact with the bronze mirror. Each time Yao Yuan traveled back through time, the "good fortune" churned up from the altered course of fixed destiny fell, in full, onto him — the bearer of the Pact.

From then on, every dead end met a turnaround beyond all reason. He spoke of the morning after he'd given away the mirror: over breakfast, they received the five hundred thousand that Ji Wenshan owed — ten years of principal and interest at once. Then a municipal debt stalled for three years was suddenly settled; the looming eight-million bank loan crisis quietly dissolved; the business that had teetered on bankruptcy came back from the dead; and one prime project after another, things he'd once chased in vain, came falling into his lap.

Every one of these earth-shaking turns of fortune, Lin Wan remembered clearly. She had stayed up with him through the exhaustion of checking contracts deep into the night, sat through dinner after business dinner at his side, shared the joy of pulling back from the brink, and watched his career warm and recover. Back then the two of them had weathered the harshest winter hand in hand, and she had simply taken it as bitterness giving way to sweetness, the worst passing into better — a steady spring earned by their mutual loyalty, life's reward for their resilience. Never once had she imagined that behind this smooth run lay some force this absurd, this far beyond ordinary understanding.

Shuai Hongqiang went on. In those days of good luck pouring in, he had not been entirely at peace. He said that the moment the antiques appraiser, Old Chen, told him the Mirror Pact might carry a karmic backlash, a bone-deep dread had latched onto him.

To defuse the danger and dodge the disaster, he had formed a secret alliance with Yao Yuan. Two exes who had long since cut all ties were bound together again by an eerie Mirror Pact, and they searched everywhere in secret, behind everyone's backs, desperately seeking a descendant of the mirror-makers who could break the contract.

And that hidden bond with another woman was a secret his lawful wife had never known.

After many twists and turns, they finally found a mirror-maker's descendant, Wang Baoguo — only to learn the cruel truth: the Mirror Pact's karmic backlash did not necessarily fall on the bearer himself. It could just as easily strike his own flesh and blood.

In the instant that truth landed, every small doubt of the past linked up into a single line, turning into a cold blade that pierced Lin Wan's heart.

She had never known that her husband's smooth-sailing success these two years had hidden such a sinister bargain and such a heavy price.

Shuai Hongqiang spoke again of the moment Shuai Wenrui broke his leg, and the cold, bone-deep suspicion that had crept up his spine. He had lived for days on end in suspicion and panic, restless day and night, yet he had always clung to a sliver of false hope, never daring to face the fact that he was the chief culprit dragging his own children down.

Lin Wan thought of Shuai Wenrui's broken legs not long ago. At the time she'd taken it for an accident; now, looking back, that was the first warning of the Pact's backlash.

Shuai Hongqiang's account came at last to the present. Just that evening, before the three families gathered for dinner, he had finalized the only way out with Yao Yuan, Old Wang, and Old Chen — the four of them: tomorrow at dusk, in the hour of the Rooster, they would go to White Horse Surf and hold the un-binding ritual, severing the Mirror Pact for good and ending the karmic backlash.

But never had he dreamed that, before the ritual could even be held, the backlash had struck again. This time the disaster had fallen on their young son, Wenyao, sending the child straight into the ICU, his life hanging in the balance.

As he spoke out all of it — the buried secrets, the false hopes, the fear and the regret — Shuai Hongqiang's composure shattered completely.

His voice began to choke and break, yet he forced himself to lay out every crucial detail clearly. He hated his own foolish greed back then, his frantic grab at any cure; hated his false hope, the way he'd covered his ears and stolen the bell. When he spoke of his suspicion that his children's misfortunes were all the backlash, his whole body trembled uncontrollably; when he spoke of tomorrow's un-binding and how it might go either way, his eyes were full of a bottomless dread.

"I thought… I was only borrowing a little luck for a while, just to get through that crisis…" He gripped Lin Wan's hand so hard his knuckles went white, his voice shaking to pieces, every word weeping blood. "I really never thought — this wasn't borrowing luck at all. It was signing my life away in a deed of sale! Paying off a debt to the King of Hell with the lives and health of my own dearest children!"

A vast guilt and despair swept over him. He could hold on no longer; his breathing turned ragged, tears spilling freely, every shred of dignity and restraint crumbling away until nothing was left but the rawest, most absolute repentance.

Lin Wan listened in silence the whole time, never once cutting in.

At first her eyes were blank and hollow, as if she couldn't take in any of this absurd, impossible thing. But the moment the name "Yao Yuan" left his mouth, her dead, still pupils suddenly contracted, and the fog of her thoughts was pierced through.

Only after Shuai Hongqiang had finished recounting the whole affair — fantastical as a book from heaven, yet undeniably real — did she softly repeat the name "Yao Yuan," her voice faint and far-off, like talking to herself, or like seeking confirmation.

Hearing Lin Wan speak at last, Shuai Hongqiang reined in his breakdown for a moment, even as his heart clenched. The karmic truth of the Pact's backlash could not be told without Yao Yuan; he knew there was no way left to cover it, and he confessed in a rasping voice: "It's her. She's my ex-girlfriend. We cut off all contact completely before I married — I never imagined I'd be tangled up with her again."

Lin Wan slowly raised her eyes, their depths still and waveless, and asked softly, her voice drifting as if from somewhere far away: "The Yao Yuan who's so controversial online — the one people call the 'Godmother of Feelings'?"

Shuai Hongqiang lifted his gaze to her, unable to read the emotion beneath that calm face, and could only nod heavily. "Mm."

Another long, suffocating silence.

The corridor's lights were dim and cold, falling on Lin Wan's face, and in the play of light and shadow the gentleness of her former self had drained away. Her gaze fell first back on the ICU's sealed door, then moved slowly to the face of the wretched, regretful man before her. The chaos of her thoughts settled bit by bit, and after the extremity of grief came a piercing clarity.

She was pulling herself, inch by inch, out of the haze of her son's critical condition, parting the surging emotions to reveal the coldest, sharpest core at her center.

"So she's your ex-girlfriend." Lin Wan's tone was flat, as if recounting some past affair that had nothing to do with her. "She and I are both relationship streamers. I work deep in how men and women get along inside a marriage; she dabbles in every form of feeling between men and women. Once, like everyone else online, I stood on the moral high ground and judged her as I pleased, dismissed her style. Later, after I'd been through more, I understood that my naivety back then, my old aloofness, was just the privilege of someone who'd never tasted real suffering. Now, instead, I can understand a great many of her views."

She paused briefly, the image of an old household object flashing through her mind, then asked with precision: "So that ancient bronze mirror you used to keep in the wooden box in your study drawer — that's the one you said you gave to her?"

Shuai Hongqiang gave a slight start. He hadn't expected Lin Wan to still remember after all these years. From the day he'd bought it, that mirror had sat in his study for eight years, like a silent witness — to the rise and fall of his career, the shifts in his family, sealing away the deepest secret and cowardice in his heart. Then again, he understood at once: Lin Wan was always meticulous and sharp; every object in the house, every small change, she knew inside out.

He had nothing to argue, and only answered: "Yes."

Then Shuai Hongqiang explained to Lin Wan, in detail, why he'd given Yao Yuan that mirror in the first place — not only Wang Qian's egging-on, but the desperation deep inside him to break out of his financial bind. That urge to "turn his luck around fast," catalyzed by alcohol and a dead end, had twisted into this whole absurd, devilish story.

He asked Lin Wan whether she still remembered the five hundred thousand from Ji Wenshan — ten years of principal and interest — that had suddenly come in over breakfast two years ago. That was the first stroke of "good fortune" he'd received after Yao Yuan first traveled back through time.

Lin Wan did remember it. At the time she'd only assumed Ji Wenshan had finally become able to repay, or had suddenly found his conscience. She'd never dreamed it was because of some force this absurd, this far beyond ordinary understanding.

But now, looking back, every key "stroke of luck" she had counted herself so fortunate for — Shuai Hongqiang's career climbing higher and higher, sailing smoothly on — was now overlaid with an eerie, sinister shadow.

Shock, horror, disbelief — layer after layer of emotion swept through her, and at last settled into an ice-locked understanding.

She finally understood it completely.

Her husband's smooth success these two years had never been heaven rewarding the diligent. Behind all that easy sailing lay a price this eerie and terrible. The string of disasters that had struck Wenrui and then her son were not pure accidents, not fate's unfairness at all — they might well stem from a muddled, greedy bargain their father had made two years ago, mortgaging his soul and the lives and health of his own kin.

Lin Wan looked at this man half-kneeling before her, weeping bitterly, drowning in remorse — her lawful husband, the father of her son. She did not fly into a rage, did not scream her questions, did not even pull her hand back from his grip.

She only felt cold — a cold seeping out from between her bones. And a deep, all-consuming exhaustion.

So the "partnership" marriage she had worked so hard to run, the one she'd thought rational and solid enough, had a dark whirlpool churning beneath it that she'd known nothing about. So the accidents and storms she'd taken for ordinary life had, in part, been summoned by her own most intimate "partner's" own hands.

The long silence stretched on in that cold corridor thick with the smell of disinfectant. Only the faint beep of the monitors carried through the heavy glass door, weak and far-off.

She didn't know how long it had been when Lin Wan, ever so slowly, inch by inch, drew her own cold hand out of Shuai Hongqiang's sweaty, frantic grip, with a distance and finality that left no room for argument.

She straightened her stiff spine, rose, and walked to the observation window of the monitoring room, her gaze returning to that small, fragile figure inside. This time her body no longer trembled; the panic in her eyes had drained entirely away, leaving only the calm and resolve of someone who had settled.

A moment later she finally spoke, her voice so level there wasn't a single ripple in it — and yet it dropped Shuai Hongqiang, standing behind her, straight into an ice pit, cold all over.

"Shuai Hongqiang, every single word you just said, I heard clearly."

She slowly turned to face him, meeting his frantic, guilt-ridden eyes. The dim light of the corridor cut shifting shadows across her face, but her own eyes were unusually clear and sharp. It was the particular clarity — lucid to the point of being almost cruel — that belonged to Lin Wan the streamer, dissecting the truth of a marriage on camera.

"Now, tell me," she said slowly, every word weighed and measured to the extreme, heavy and forceful, "tomorrow, when you go to White Horse Surf — how good are your odds of breaking this evil… contract?"

Shuai Hongqiang opened his mouth, only to find his throat too dry for sound. He swallowed hard and rasped: "Old Wang said… it's the only way. But no one has ever truly un-bound a Mirror Pact like this, so… there's no guarantee."

"No guarantee." Lin Wan repeated it, as if stating an objective fact that had nothing to do with her, no joy or anger in it — which only made it more unnerving.

She pressed on, every word heavy, every line cutting to the heart: "And if, by luck, it is broken — will all this suffering my son is going through stop completely? Will he come back to me, whole and unharmed?"

The question went into Shuai Hongqiang's heart like a blunt knife, cutting slowly. He shook violently, unable to answer — because he truly did not know. The karmic backlash had already happened, the harm already done, the water already spilled. Even if the source were cut off and the backlash stopped, could the damage already done be taken back?

"I don't know…" he admitted in despair. "But if it isn't broken, the backlash might… might still go on. Wenrui's legs, Wenyao's injuries… I'm afraid… I'm afraid there'll be a next time…"

Lin Wan gazed at him quietly, silent for a very, very long while.

Then she nodded gently, as if she had finally traced out every thread and understood it all.

"I see."

Three light, weightless words — and yet they shook Shuai Hongqiang to the core. He would rather she wept bitterly, cursed and questioned him, broke down in hysterics — anything would be better than this dead, still calm.

She only stood there, dissecting this absurd, terrible truth that had dragged her family into the abyss with the cold steadiness of a scalpel.

Before he could collect himself, Lin Wan had already turned and walked back to the bench, picked up her bag, taken out her phone, and unlocked the screen. Her fingers slid swiftly across it, the motions smooth and steady, perfectly ordered — nothing like a mother whose young child lay dying, who had just learned of a catastrophe shattering her family.

In the face of the ultimate disaster, her emotions had already been forced to close their curtain, replaced by the instinct and resilience to confront the problem and resolve the crisis.

"Give me Yao Yuan's contact details." Lin Wan didn't even look up, her tone certain and calm.

Shuai Hongqiang was at a loss. "What are you going to do?"

"Contact her." Lin Wan raised her eyes, her gaze clear and resolute. "Since tomorrow's un-binding decides whether my son lives or dies, decides the fate of our whole family, I need to know every detail. The time, the place, the procedure, the taboos, every unknown risk — I want all of it clear."

Shuai Hongqiang didn't dare defy her in the slightest. Obediently he took out his phone and pulled up Yao Yuan's contact.

Her fingers tapped quickly across the screen, the motions fluid, her thinking clear — nothing like a mother whose son had just gone into the ICU, who had just learned a staggering secret. The rational, professional poise she'd once shown on camera now stood against the storm of disaster with an almost cruel composure.

"What are you going to tell her?" Shuai Hongqiang asked instinctively.

"Tell her Wenyao had an accident, that he's in critical condition in the ICU, and that I now know the whole truth of the Mirror Pact." She spoke evenly as she sent the message. "Ask her whether tomorrow's plan needs to change, and confirm every detail."

Once it was sent, she put the phone away and looked Shuai Hongqiang in the eye: "This disaster started with you and her, and it's dragged in my child and my family. There's no way I can stand outside of it. I have to have an answer — an answer for why my son is lying in that ICU."

Shuai Hongqiang stared at her blankly, realizing for the first time, truly, that he had never really understood the woman beside him.

He had seen her as a gentle, considerate wife, as a careful, patient mother, as a composed, clear-eyed streamer — but never like this. In the face of an apocalyptic dead end and a truth that brought the sky crashing down, she forced down the bone-eating grief, cast off all the inner churn of emotion, and switched at once into problem-solving mode.

This was never coldness. Quite the opposite — it was the deepest love and responsibility. On the brink of an emotional collapse, she had built a dam by force out of reason. She didn't dare break down, couldn't break down — the child in the ICU was still waiting for her, the tottering family still needed her to hold it up. She raised a high wall of reason to hold back the surging despair, all to guard the last shred of hope.

The phone buzzed faintly, breaking the dead silence of the corridor.

Lin Wan lowered her head to read Yao Yuan's reply, her gaze resting briefly on the screen, her face still as waveless as ever. She slowly put the phone away and repeated it calmly: "She says the plan stays the same. Tomorrow, the hour of the Rooster, at White Horse Surf, on time. Once the Mirror Pact is un-bound, the karmic backlash may stop completely — so tomorrow's ritual has to go ahead."

Shuai Hongqiang nodded numbly, drained to the soul.

Lin Wan slowly sat back down on the bench, her two hands clasped tightly on her knees, knuckles pressed white. Still she gazed at the ICU's sealed door, and after a long while she suddenly spoke again, softly — and this time her voice let slip a barely perceptible tremor, the emotion she'd just forced down quietly coming loose.

"Shuai Hongqiang, do you know what I hate most?"

Shuai Hongqiang's heart strings drew taut, his breath catching, and he looked at her in dread.

"What I hate most isn't that you got tangled up with your ex behind my back, nor that you hid the secret." Lin Wan's voice was very soft, almost talking to herself, yet every word cut to the bone. "Those are enough to turn my heart cold, but they aren't what's most fatal."

She turned her head to look at the man beside her, a pain suppressed to its limit and a cold disillusionment surging in her eyes.

"What I hate most is that you stripped away my right to choose — alone, without me."

"What is a marriage? It's the two of us swearing before the altar, contracting under the law, becoming each other's most intimate partners. We share our property, bear the risks together, raise our children, plan our future. And what is the heart of a partnership? It's being informed. It's consent. It's shared decision-making."

"But you?" Her voice trembled slightly, yet every word stayed clear. "You signed, all on your own, a contract that mortgaged our children's well-being — and you never once asked whether I was willing. You made me, with no knowledge of any of it, a co-liable party to that contract. You made me and the children bear the full risk of your decision, completely in the dark."

She drew a deep breath, the cool air filling her chest, the cold of it tightening around her heart.

"Day after day in my livestream room, I tell every woman trapped in a hard marriage: a good marriage is never held together by love and sacrifice. It's held together by clear rules, equal responsibility, transparent honesty. The bedrock of a marriage is being informed, living as one, having each other's back."

"I teach other people to sort out who's responsible for what in a marriage, where the alliance stands, where the emotional bottom line lies — but what about my own marriage?"

A single rhetorical question, holding all the absurdity and desolation within it. Her eyes finally welled, but she forced it down, refusing to let the tears fall.

"My own partnership agreement was built on lies and concealment from the very start. I thought we were running a home together — and it turned out you'd long since secretly mortgaged our future."

Shuai Hongqiang shut his eyes in pain. Every sentence from Lin Wan was like a sharp whip, lashing hard at his conscience. She was right about all of it. He wasn't only greedy and foolish — he was base and selfish. With his own hands he had destroyed the honesty of their marriage, trampled his wife's trust, stripped her of the most basic right, as a partner and as a mother, to know and to choose.

"I'm sorry…" was all he could do, repeating those three pale, powerless words.

"Is sorry any use?" Lin Wan quickly pressed down the ache in her heart, her voice returning to calm — but beneath that calm was a bottomless ice. "Can sorry restore Wenyao's face to what it was? Can sorry keep him out of the ICU, out of this life-and-death uncertainty?"

She shook her head, looked at him no longer, and turned her gaze back to the door of the monitoring room.

"Shuai Hongqiang, I don't want to hear sorry right now. Right now I only want to do two things."

Her tone was firm, every word ringing, carrying the finality of someone who had burned her boats.

"First, do everything possible to save my son. The best medicine, the best doctors — even if it costs every cent we have, I will keep him alive, and as well as he can possibly be."

"Second—" she paused slightly, a flash of icy resolve crossing her eyes, "tomorrow, White Horse Surf, I'm going with you."

Shuai Hongqiang's head jerked up. "What?"

"I said, tomorrow I'll go with you." Lin Wan said it word by word. "Since this absurd farce concerns my son, then as your wife and Wenyao's mother, I have to watch it end with my own eyes. I will watch that damned mirror be dealt with, watch you and Yao Yuan break that contract — with my own eyes."

"But it's dangerous there. Old Wang said—" Shuai Hongqiang hurried to dissuade her.

"Dangerous?" Lin Wan cut him off, her lips curving into an arc bitter to the extreme. "Is there any situation more dangerous than this? My son is lying in there, between life and death. My husband has just told me all of it may be because of one moment of his greed two years ago."

She turned her head and looked straight into Shuai Hongqiang's eyes. In those eyes there was despair, anger, pain — but not the slightest retreat.

Shuai Hongqiang gazed dumbly at the resolve in her brow and eyes, momentarily lost for words. He suddenly recalled how she'd looked when they first met — gentle and intelligent, yet resilient and bold at the core, never bowing to fate or hardship.

After a long while, he set aside every word of protest and nodded heavily, his voice hoarse and weak: "All right. Tomorrow, we'll go together."

Lin Wan nodded and said no more. Her hands stayed clasped tightly on her knees, her spine straight, keeping her quiet vigil outside the ICU door.

Beyond the windows the sky broke fully into dawn. The faint light of morning spilled the length of the corridor, driving off the darkness of the deep night — yet it could not drive off the heavy oppression and sorrow hanging in the air. The second day of the new year held no joy of new beginnings, only the torment of a long wait, and a ritual about to come, its outcome unknown.

Shuai Hongqiang looked at his wife's stiff, upright profile, at the sealed door of the monitoring room ahead, and felt a barren waste inside. It struck him with sudden clarity: tomorrow's un-binding ritual at White Horse Surf, win or lose, had already changed something for good — there was no going back to before.

The layer of marriage between them — that partnership agreement of reason, restraint, and mutual space — had long since been torn utterly to shreds by this bloody, brutal truth.

What they were about to face was no longer how to keep a comfortable distance in their marriage, but how to learn trust again on a field of ruins, how to redefine responsibility, how to rethink what it meant to share "good fortune and ill alike."

If, that is, there was still such a chance.

The beep of the monitors was especially clear in the bright morning light, echoing again and again down the corridor, like a countdown — or the tolling bell of a judgment.
