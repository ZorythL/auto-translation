Spring Ends, the Pear Blossoms Fall, and the Whirlpool of Fate Stirs Again

It was three in the afternoon. The chime that signaled the end of the livestream had sounded a while ago.

Yao Yuan shut down all her equipment, and the ring light dimmed. After such a stretch of intense, nonstop output, the exhaustion rolled in like a tide. She leaned back in her chair and closed her eyes to rest for a moment. The studio was quiet, with only the faint hum of the computer's cooling fan.

She stood and walked to the window, pushing it open a crack. At once the spring breeze poured in, carrying damp air and a faint fragrance. Far off, lining both sides of the main road, banks of pink-and-white mountain pear blossoms were in full bloom, looking under the afternoon sun like one grand, fleeting dream. Petals were swept up by the wind and scattered everywhere — beautiful, yes, but the bloom was too short. Just like certain human relationships: a dazzling opening, a hurried close.

In the stillness, her phone buzzed and the screen lit with a pale glow.

"I'm downstairs at your company."

It was Yu Hao.

Yao Yuan looked at the line of text, her gaze calm and unrippled. Ever since their last conversation about "a child and marriage" had ended badly, she hadn't answered a single one of his messages — not from then through the Spring Festival, and not since. She guessed he was probably waiting: waiting for her to waver, to reconsider, or to raise the offer herself. But she had simply vanished from his world entirely.

Now, he couldn't hold out any longer.

Fine. Some things have to be brought to a close.

She picked up her coat and her bag and walked out at a steady pace. As she passed down the corridor, her eyes drifted over that sea of mountain pear blossoms outside, so brilliant it almost seemed unreal.

The sun at three or four on a spring afternoon was just right, spilling warmly over her and chasing off the chill and fatigue she'd carried out of the studio. Yao Yuan stepped out of the elevator, crossed the empty, quiet lobby, and the glass doors slid open to either side.

Yu Hao leaned against his car in a well-cut black trench coat, no tie, the collar of his shirt left carelessly open. A cigarette was pinched between his fingers, unsmoked, the smoke curling away into the flower-scented spring breeze. When he saw her come out, he raised his eyes to her — deep, weighing, complicated. Behind him bloomed that blaze of mountain pear blossoms, and the pink-and-white cloud of flowers made him look somehow forlorn, somehow out of place.

"Finished for the day?" he said. His voice was a little low and rough, with less of its usual easy command.

"Mm." Yao Yuan stopped a few steps in front of him, with no intention of coming closer. Her calm gaze swept past him and settled on the brilliant flowering trees at his back. "Something the matter?"

Yu Hao looked at her calm, unrippled face. Those eyes that were always lit with a smile or with shrewd calculation now reflected her distance clearly — and a trace of something that might have been his imagination: a cool, detached appreciation as she gazed at the spring blossoms. The certainty he'd felt about all this suddenly began to waver. He'd assumed that if he let her stew for a while and then laid down heavyweight chips like "a child" and "marriage," she'd have to give some kind of response. Even if she didn't agree right away, there'd be discussion, bargaining. But there was nothing. It was as if she hadn't received those signals at all — as if she'd cut the line completely.

This feeling of being totally beyond his control made him deeply uncomfortable, even — there was a faint, indistinct unease, and anger.

"Find somewhere to sit?" He flicked away the cigarette butt and ground it out with the toe of his shoe, the motion a little too forceful.

"Let's just talk here." Yao Yuan glanced around. The little garden at the foot of the building had a few benches, and at this hour of the afternoon there was hardly anyone about — only the rustle of wind through the pear trees and a faint scent of flowers. "I have something at four."

Yu Hao stared at her for a few seconds, then suddenly laughed, the smile tinged with self-mockery and a probing edge, and a tension that wasn't quite hidden. "Yao Yuan, you don't even have time for a cup of coffee with me anymore? Have things between us really gotten this distant?"

"You're joking, Boss Yu." Yao Yuan curved her lips too, though the smile didn't reach her eyes, and brought her gaze back to his face. "We're each just busy in our own way. If there's something on your mind, just say it."

"Each busy in our own way..." Yu Hao chewed over the words, his gaze deepening. He took a step forward, closing the distance between them, and lowered his voice. "Is it down to 'Boss Yu' and polite formalities between us now? That thing I brought up last time — have you thought it over?"

"Which thing?" Yao Yuan asked, and the puzzlement in her tone was genuine, as if that unpleasant conversation had long since been forgotten in some corner of her mind.

Yu Hao choked, nearly losing his composure, the suppressed anger flaring up again. "The child. And... us."

"Oh." Realization dawned, and she gave a slight shake of her head, her tone as flat as if she were discussing when the mountain pear blossoms outside would fade. "Yu Hao, I thought I'd already made my position perfectly clear last time. I don't want a child born out of wedlock, one who might well spend years with his father absent — at least not now, and on that I'm as certain as you are that you don't want marriage. As for us..."

She paused, lifted her eyes, and looked straight into his — clear all the way down, no resentment, no lingering attachment, only a level, rational calm. "We've always been very good 'partners' — pleasant to work with, each taking what we need. Isn't that good enough? Why does it have to change?"

"Each taking what we need..." Yu Hao repeated, his gaze sharpening, his tone turning a few degrees colder. "You think what we have now is still just 'each taking what we need'? Yao Yuan, you know perfectly well how I've treated you. I thought what was between us was at least more than 'partners.'"

"Then what is it?" Yao Yuan countered, her tone still calm, even carrying a note of probing curiosity. "Love? Or have you finally decided that I'm 'suitable' to take home and make into a respectable Mrs. Yu, who'll conveniently bear you an heir?"

It was too blunt, edged even with a sharp, all-seeing scorn. Yu Hao's expression shifted, his jaw tightening. "Yao Yuan! Do you really have to put it so harshly?"

"Is it harsh?" Yao Yuan tilted her head slightly, her face carrying a look of genuine, earnest thought. "I'm only stating one possibility. You never used to mention marriage, never mentioned children. Now you suddenly do — is it because you've reached a certain age? Because your family's been pressing you? Or because you figure Yao Yuan's career is solid now, that I'm 'worthy' of being Yu Hao's wife, and that the child we'd have would be good 'value for money' in genes, in educational resources, in future connections?"

With every sentence, Yu Hao's face sank a little further, and the last bit of warmth drained from his eyes. He found, to his surprise, that he couldn't fully refute it. Those hidden calculations, the ones he hadn't wanted to examine even himself, had been laid out so nakedly in the sunlight, amid the scent of mountain pear blossoms — and it was suffocating.

"So this is how you've seen me all along?" His voice had gone completely cold, with the resentment of someone whose cover has been blown.

"No," Yao Yuan said, shaking her head, speaking very sincerely, and even smiling a little. "I used to think we were the same kind of person. Clear-eyed, rational, knowing the rules of the game and keeping to them. Happy together, dignified apart, binding no one and owing no one. That was the thing I admired about you. But now..."

Her smile faded. Her gaze swept over his tense face and went to that sea of flowers behind him, blooming at its peak as if it might wither in the very next instant, and her voice was soft. "It seems you've started wanting 'a little more.' There's nothing wrong with that — only... it isn't what I want. Like these mountain pear blossoms: gorgeous while they bloom, but I know the season is short, and I won't expect them to stay on the branch forever. To force them off and stick them in a vase only hastens their withering."

"What is it you want?" Yu Hao pressed, with a barely perceptible agitation he wasn't even aware of himself. "Yao Yuan, don't tell me a 'McKinsey of Feelings' is still waiting for some misty, formless love. Neither of us is twenty anymore. Be realistic!"

"That's right — neither of us is twenty anymore." Yao Yuan agreed softly, drawing her gaze back from the flowers to his face, clear and firm. "Which is exactly why we should be all the clearer about what works and what doesn't. I'll admit it: a woman at thirty-seven really is close to missing the prime window for having children, so I'll consider freezing my eggs. But right now, I don't want a child born out of wedlock, and I don't want to change the rhythm and center of gravity of my life for one. I don't have the energy to handle all those tangled family ties and clashing interests, and even less do I want my child born into a 'partnership' full of scheming and calculation. The way I am now is good."

She paused, and spoke each word with perfect clarity. "Yu Hao, let's part on good terms. Stay friends, or business partners — either is fine. But the 'partners' arrangement ends here. Thank you for this past year of... company."

The spring breeze gusted suddenly, lifting a rain of pink-and-white petals that drifted down between them, onto Yu Hao's shoulder, brushing past the ends of Yao Yuan's hair. Sunlight filtered through the swaying branches, casting a flickering play of light and shadow between them.

Yu Hao stood there, looking at the woman before him. She was still beautiful, still exquisite, still perfectly composed. But those eyes that had once held his reflection, that had carried admiration and even a faint, suggestive warmth, were now calm and unrippled, like the surface of a frozen lake, with nothing left beneath that he recognized as "tenderness" or "expectation." She had even used the mountain pear blossoms to describe what they had — beautiful, but brief, and she accepted that brevity with open eyes.

He suddenly understood, with absolute clarity, that he might truly have lost her. Not because she'd fallen in love with someone else, but because she had finally seen entirely through, and grown entirely weary of, the kind of relationship they'd had — one built on murky ground, full of scheming and calculation. Her wings had grown strong, her core had steadied; she no longer needed anyone to "shore her up" or "add to her luster," and she was no longer willing to pay any "partnership" emotion and future beyond its limits.

A mix of intense loss, of unwillingness, of the anger of being underestimated — and a trace of something he wouldn't admit even to himself, a wistfulness at losing this "comfortable arrangement" — surged up in him. But he was Yu Hao, after all, and he soon crushed those churning emotions firmly down, hanging that composed, faintly playful smile back on his face. Only the smile didn't reach his eyes.

"Looks like I flattered myself, read the whole thing wrong." He shrugged, his tone turning light, as if the tense standoff a moment ago had never happened, and lifted a hand to brush a petal from his shoulder. "All right. Since Boss Yao's mind is made up, I respect your choice. If we can't be 'partners,' we can still be friends, right? When a good project comes along, remember to call me."

"Of course." Yao Yuan smiled too — a proper, impeccable smile, the standard expression of social courtesy. "Boss Yu will always be a preferred partner."

The two of them traded a smile, and the mood seemed to recover its old ease. But only they knew that something had snapped completely and could never be put back — like those mountain pear blossoms blown from the branch, which, once parted from it, could never be rejoined.

"Then I won't keep you from what's next." Yu Hao turned first and pulled open the car door, his movements as smooth and crisp as ever, only the line of his back, amid the petals swirling everywhere, showing a deliberately straightened, lonely austerity. "See you around."

"See you around." Yao Yuan stayed where she was and watched him get into the car. The black sedan pulled steadily out of the lot, rolling over the fallen blossoms, and merged into the stream of traffic beneath that brilliant sea of flowers along the main road, soon vanishing from sight.

The spring breeze was still mild and warm, thick with the scent of flowers. Yao Yuan let out a soft breath, as though she'd finally set down a long-carried, invisible burden, and all at once her whole body felt lighter. She looked down and saw that, at some point, her palm had caught a single whole petal — pink-and-white, soft and tender, still holding the warmth of the sun.

She loosened her hand gently, and the petal rose on the wind, spinning, and dissolved into that boundless rain of falling blossoms.

Her phone buzzed once in her pocket. She took it out and looked: it was a message from Zhao Yiming, just a single image — what looked like the cover page of a document, titled "A Preliminary Risk Assessment and Benefit Analysis White Paper on the 'System Interaction Protocol' (Draft)."

Beneath it ran a line of text: "The first draft is complete. I respectfully request your review, Boss Yao. Might I have the honor of reporting in person this coming Saturday at six in the evening, in the Bamboo-Rhyme room at 'Yunting'?"

Yao Yuan looked at her phone screen, then raised her eyes again to that sea of mountain pear blossoms outside, blooming at its peak as if it were burning up its own life in beauty. Without realizing it, the corners of her mouth lifted, and a very faint but genuine smile crossed her eyes, like a slight ripple over spring water.

She replied: "All right. See you Saturday."

Tomorrow she would go to see Old Chen and unravel the mystery of the Mirror Pact.

After that... perhaps she could go and hear out that "white paper," and see what kind of "interaction protocol" another supremely clever, highly ordered "system" would put on the table.

Spring had come, and the mountain pear blossoms had bloomed and faded.

But there is always something new that quietly sprouts amid the breaking and the farewells.

Then she turned and walked, light and resolute, toward the bench in the little garden before the office building. The spring sun spilled down just so, carrying the lingering faint scent of the mountain pear blossoms, mingled with the smell of new grass and waking earth. She sat down on the bench, tilted her face up slightly, and closed her eyes, letting the warmth seep through her eyelids and bloom an orange-red haze across her retinas.

A moment later she opened her eyes, and her gaze fell naturally on the office building's glass curtain wall before her, smooth and mirror-bright. The wall was clear as a lake's surface, reflecting her figure whole — a wine-red silk shirt, a black leather vest, slightly waved long hair, and a face that, in the spring sun, betrayed not the slightest hurry of the passing years.

She looked quietly at "her." Still young, still pretty, the sharpness at the corners of her eyes and brows softened by the sun, leaving only a refinement that could withstand close scrutiny. The medical technology of this age really could, so long as you had enough money and were willing to keep pouring it in, buy a stay of execution against time, stretching the state of youth into a smooth curve that declined ever so slowly. Wrinkle removal, fillers, lifts, whitening... the cosmetic procedures came in endless varieties, extending from the face down to the neck — she'd even heard of people who, to keep a "girlish hand," had hyaluronic acid injected into their hands at regular intervals. As for the more drastic surgeries — double-eyelid cuts, raised nose bridges, chin fillers, jaw-bone shaving to reshape the face — they had turned "trending beauty" into a template you could download and swap out at will, so long as you could pay the price and endure the pain and risk of the recovery period.

But Yao Yuan didn't follow that assembly line. She had her own aesthetic line in the sand: beauty had to be high-grade, had to keep a "self" at its base, and must never sink into a forgettable factory product. She did only the most top-tier procedures, the kind that maintained the status quo — like the upkeep of the most precise instrument, meant to fight gravity and natural metabolism rather than to remake her face. As for her figure, a height of one hundred sixty-eight centimeters and a weight kept year-round at fifty-four kilograms were the joint result of a scientific diet and regular light exercise — an absolute management of her own body, woven into the everyday. That wasn't thinness; it was a taut, sculpted uprightness.

Just as she gazed at that still-"pretty" reflection in the glass curtain wall, her thoughts drifting slightly —

The anomaly struck without warning.

Across the smooth, mirror-bright surface of the glass wall, with no sign at all, a ring of ripples spread out.

It was not optical distortion, not a trick of the reflected light. The ripples spread out from the center of her reflection — one ring, then another, faster and faster, more and more violently, as if it were not a solid physical surface but a churning sheet of water into which a stone had been dropped.

Then a familiar, irresistible pull surged out from the center of the ripples! The force was formless and without substance, yet it seized her consciousness brutally, like a giant hand clamping around her soul and yanking — hard!

"Ugh—!"

The world spun.

The warm spring sun before her, the glass curtain wall, her own reflection, the brilliant sea of mountain pear blossoms in the distance... every image was wrenched in an instant by that "hand" into chaotic, garish blocks and lines of color, whirling, dragging, deforming wildly. A violent vertigo, with a strong wave of nausea, rose in her throat, and her vision was swiftly swallowed by a churning darkness.

Here it comes again...

In the instant before her consciousness was dragged completely down into the abyss of darkness, that thought streaked clearly across Yao Yuan's mind.

No omen, no pattern — like a die cast carelessly by fate, and she, once more caught by that formless force, was flung into the unknown turbulence of time and space.
