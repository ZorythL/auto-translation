Shuai Hongqiang's Stroke of Luck
Meanwhile, on the other side.
Shuai Hongqiang was jolted awake by the frantic ringing of his phone.
He had slept deeply the night before, so deeply it was like sinking into a pile of cotton, without a single dream. In the last three years of his life, that was a rarity. Ever since his project payments started getting held up — with material suppliers showing up at his door every few days and the bank calling nonstop to chase its debts — he hadn't slept a full night in a long time.
Then, two days ago, the ten million in city-government project funds that had been owed to him unexpectedly landed in his account. The first thing he did was pay off eight million in bank loans and settle the largest material debts. The thousand-pound boulder that had been pressing on his chest finally hit the ground.
And because that weight had lifted from his heart, last night he'd fallen asleep the moment his head touched the pillow — and slept unusually sweetly.
Now the name flashing on his phone screen made him bolt upright with a jolt — "Director Zhang, Municipal Housing and Construction Bureau."
His heart lurched. He knew this number all too well. Over the past three years he'd dialed it no fewer than a hundred times — mostly to be brushed off, occasionally to be scolded, never once with good news. He took a deep breath, cleared his throat, and answered, his tone carrying the habitual deference with a barely perceptible note of exhaustion. "Hello, Director Zhang. Good morning."
To his surprise, the voice on the other end was no longer coldly official. It even carried a rare warmth. "Boss Shuai. I didn't disturb your rest, did I?"
Shuai Hongqiang froze and instinctively glanced out the window — the sky was only just turning gray with dawn. "No, no. Go ahead, Director Zhang."
"It's like this. Do you have time this morning? Nine o'clock, come to the conference room on the third floor of city hall. The preparatory team for that public fitness center project has a few things they'd like to talk over with you."
The public fitness center? Shuai Hongqiang's mind raced. That was a flagship public-welfare project the city had put on its agenda last year. The investment was said to be considerable. But a small-time contractor like him — owed over ten million, his reputation in the trade nearly in tatters — couldn't even get near something like that. Why would they come to him?
"Director Zhang, do you mean to say…"
"It's hard to explain over the phone. You'll see when you get here. Oh, and," Director Zhang paused, lowering his voice a little, "that outstanding balance on your old Culture Plaza job — five million, right? I've already had a word with the finance department. The paperwork should go through today; the money should reach your company's account by this afternoon at the latest. It dragged on this long because our office didn't do its job properly. Don't take it to heart."
Shuai Hongqiang gripped the phone, his fingertips going ice-cold in an instant, then a scalding heat surged up after. He opened his mouth, but his throat felt stuffed with cotton, and he couldn't get out a single word.
"Hello? Boss Shuai? Did you hear me?"
"I — I heard you!" Shuai Hongqiang snapped out of it, his voice trembling slightly. "Thank you, Director Zhang! Thank you, sir! I — I'll be there right on time!"
After hanging up, he sat on the edge of the bed and didn't move for a long while. The faint light of early morning slipped through the gap in the not-quite-drawn curtains and fell on his face, reflecting the blankness in his eyes and an overwhelming sense of unreality.
Five million… that was settled too?
And the public fitness center project… they wanted to talk to him about it?
His heart pounded in his chest like a drum — not from pure joy, but from a violent mix of elation, suspicion, and a deep unease.
The third unexpected "stroke of luck."
She must have triggered the Time-Reversal Talisman again.
This was the third time.
And each time, the luck that came to him was more direct than the last.
Shuai Hongqiang raised a hand and scrubbed hard at his face. His palm came away damp — he couldn't tell if it was sweat or something else. He walked to the window and yanked the curtains open. The morning light stabbed at his eyes; the distant honking of cars on the road below drifted up. Everything was vivid and real.
But how had all of this come about?
Was it because of that mirror, because of the so-called Mirror Pact? Because he had unwittingly become the bearer — while Yao Yuan, that seemingly calm, powerful, all-capable Godmother of Feelings, was paying some price he couldn't comprehend, a price that crossed time and space? And those costs were converted by the Primordial Purple Energy sealed inside the bronze mirror into his good fortune?
The thought churned in his stomach. He couldn't say whether it was fear or shame.
He, Shuai Hongqiang, was no saint. After more than a decade scrabbling around in the construction trade, he'd seen plenty of gray areas, and he'd gotten his own hands muddy along the way too. He craved success. He craved a turnaround. He craved the chance to pay the workers the blood-and-sweat wages he owed them, to stand tall in front of his parents back home. He needed this luck — craved it, even.
But… what if this luck was built on another person's pain, even on some unimaginable overdraft against "fate" itself?
The phone buzzed again — a text from the bank confirming the deposit. He stared at that long string of numbers, the kind of figure he'd once only seen in his dreams, and now it felt like it was scalding his hand.
At nine-fifty that morning, Shuai Hongqiang was already standing in front of the imposing city hall building. He'd put on his best suit and shaved clean. He took a deep breath and walked inside.
The meeting went far more smoothly than he'd imagined — you could even call it "friendly."
Director Zhang, who chaired the meeting, and a few other officials were warm and gracious. They praised Shuai Hongqiang's diligence and sense of responsibility on the old Culture Plaza project (despite three years of withheld payments), the solid quality of his work, and the fact that he'd never once shortchanged his laborers' wages (the last line he'd held). Then the conversation pivoted to the public fitness center project, set to officially launch after the New Year. They said they needed a partner like him — "experienced, with a strong reputation" — to take part in building some of the venues.
"Consider it making up a little for the trouble the withheld payments caused you," Director Zhang said with a smile, his tone as light as casual small talk. "But Boss Shuai, the project has high standards. You'll need to put together your best crew — no slipping up."
Shuai Hongqiang nodded over and over, made his promises, voiced his resolve. He drew on years of practiced caution and deference toward clients and handled it all without a single misstep. But only he knew that the back of his shirt was already soaked through with a small patch of cold sweat.
It wasn't nerves. It was a deeper unease.
This was all too smooth — smooth like a carefully staged play. And he was like a lucky man suddenly shoved to center stage, the spotlight on him so warm it burned, yet he couldn't see where the light came from, much less know what the next scene in the script would be.
When the meeting ended, he politely declined the officials' invitation to "grab a quick bite together" and all but fled the city hall building. Once he was seated in his old-model BMW X6, hands on the wheel, they were still trembling faintly.
Sunlight poured in through the car window, dazzlingly bright. The phone in his pocket felt heavy, holding the electronic copy of the letter of intent they'd just settled on, and that text about the five million landing in his account.
He should have been happy. He could pay off the suppliers; his company could survive; and now he had an even more promising project. He should have laughed out loud, should have immediately called the brothers who'd toughed it out alongside him, called his wife at home to share the good news.
But he couldn't laugh.
What kept surfacing in his mind were Yao Yuan's eyes.
He pulled out his phone, his fingertip hovering over Yao Yuan's number for a long moment before he finally pressed it.
The phone rang several times before she picked up.
"Hello?" Yao Yuan's voice came through, calm, even carrying the brisk efficiency she usually had when handling business. Nothing seemed off. But Shuai Hongqiang still keenly caught the thread of exhaustion underneath it — heavier than before.
"Yuan — Yao Yuan," Shuai Hongqiang licked his slightly dry lips, trying to make his voice sound natural. "It's me, Shuai Hongqiang. Have you… been all right lately? Have you been time-trav — having any strange dreams again?" Though they both tacitly understood about the time-travel, when the words reached his lips, he still couldn't bring himself to ask outright.
The line went silent for two seconds. Those two seconds sent Shuai Hongqiang's heart up into his throat.
"I'm fine, just a little tired. Thanks for asking." Yao Yuan's tone didn't waver, and she cut straight to the point. "Actually, there's something I wanted to ask you. That old master you mentioned before — the one at the winter auction who specializes in appraising bronze mirrors — surname Chen, full name Chen Yanqing, right? When you brought him up before, did you mean he knows more about the Mirror Pact? I need to contact him immediately."
Shuai Hongqiang's heart gave a thud. Just as he'd feared — she'd asked, and gone straight for the heart of it.
"Old Chen… yes, Old Chen, that's his name." His pace slowed without his meaning it to, his mind spinning fast. "But Yao Yuan, you want to find him… is it about that bronze mirror? Or…"
"I have my reasons." Yao Yuan's voice stayed calm, but it carried an insistence that brooked no argument. "Do you have his contact information, or know how to find him?"
Shuai Hongqiang's throat tightened. Of course he knew. That old master Chen Yanqing was one of the few old scholars in the trade who might genuinely know about something as mysterious as the Mirror Pact — and he was the one who had once hinted to him, "The mirror opens a way to the hidden; the pact, once made, carries fortune." If Yao Yuan went looking for him, her purpose was obvious — she wanted to uncover the truth of the Mirror Pact, and maybe even unravel it.
She can't be allowed to find him. A voice shrieked in the depths of Shuai Hongqiang's heart. Find Old Chen, ask him everything, undo the Mirror Pact — and your luck is cut off! That five million, that new project, everything within reach — it could all vanish!
"Yao Yuan," he weighed his words, feeling his palms start to sweat again, "Old Chen, he… really is an authority on this sort of thing. But the old man's getting on in years, his temper's a bit odd, and he doesn't much like being disturbed. And I just heard a couple of days ago that he seems to have gone down south — Hainan, or Yunnan, somewhere like that. Said he wouldn't come back until winter was over and things warmed up after the New Year. So right now… you probably can't reach him."
Having said all that, Shuai Hongqiang held his breath. What he'd said was half true. Old Chen's odd temper was real; so was his dislike of being disturbed. But going south for the winter — that was something he'd made up on the spot. He just needed time. He needed to keep Yao Yuan steady until the new project was fully locked in and his company stood firmly on its feet again.
The line went silent once more. This silence stretched longer, so long that Shuai Hongqiang could almost hear his own heart slamming against his ribs.
Just as he was about to crack and say something to ease the mood, Yao Yuan's voice came again, betraying no emotion, only a touch more detached. "Is that so. Gone south… won't be back until after the New Year."
"Yes, yes. The south's warmer, better for an old man to winter in." Shuai Hongqiang chimed in stiffly.
"All right. I understand." After saying that, Yao Yuan paused, then suddenly asked something that seemed unrelated. "Boss Shuai, today… did something good happen to you?"
Shuai Hongqiang's scalp prickled, and the words all but blurted out of him. "No — no! What good thing could there be? Same as always — running errands, chasing down debts…" The moment the words were out, he regretted them. Too deliberate. Too guilty.
Sure enough, Yao Yuan let out a soft "Hmph." The sound was faint, but it was like a fine needle pricking Shuai Hongqiang's eardrum.
"Is that so." She repeated those words, then said, "Then I'll leave it there. I've got things to do — I'll hang up now."
"Yao Yuan…" Shuai Hongqiang still had more he wanted to say, but the dial tone was already buzzing in the receiver.
He sat frozen in the driver's seat, gripping the phone. Outside the window the sunlight was still brilliant, the city hall building grand and solemn, the phone in his pocket carrying the hope of a turning point in his life.
And yet he felt cold all over.
That soft laugh of Yao Yuan's at the end, that "is that so" — they were like a cold key, opening the door in his heart labeled "dread." Did she know? Had she guessed? Had she long since sensed the unsettling link between her "time-travel" and his "good luck"?
And his evasions, his lies just now — in front of those eyes of hers that seemed to see straight through people, didn't they come off as clumsy and laughable?
Shuai Hongqiang buried his face deep in his hands and rubbed hard.
The phone buzzed again — a message from his project manager. "Brother Qiang, the suppliers heard the money's coming and they're over the moon! Asking when they can settle up. Everyone's counting on this money to get through the New Year!"
He leaned heavily back against the seat and closed his eyes.
The sunlight came through his eyelids as a field of blood-red, throbbing spots of light.
After a long while, he picked up the phone and sent the project manager a voice message, his voice hoarse but struggling to stay steady. "Tell everyone: this afternoon, by this afternoon at the latest, the money will definitely be in. Have everyone… hold on a little longer. It's almost done."
After sending the message, he lifted his head and started the car.
The roar of the engine filled the cabin. The car pulled slowly away from city hall and merged into the flow of traffic.
In the rearview mirror, that building — the symbol of where today's "good luck" had begun — grew ever more distant. And the road ahead stretched, under the bright winter sun, toward some far-off place he couldn't yet see.
He knew that ever since he had given that Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror to Yao Yuan, something had already changed. The taste of this "luck" was sweet at first; but once swallowed, it gave off more and more bitterness and dread.
But an arrow loosed from the bow doesn't come back.
Did he — did he even have a choice anymore?
