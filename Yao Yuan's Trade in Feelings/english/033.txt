Chapter 33 — The Mirror's Fire, Not Yet Spent

It was in the very thick of that intoxicating, tender stretch of time that a fork in the road appeared. On the strength of her outstanding study and her solid foundation in dance, Yao Yuan was noticed by a top MCN agency in Shanghai. They extended an olive branch, laid out generous terms, and pressed her to come work for them. Looking at the contract, Yao Yuan hesitated—but not for long. As her pen touched the page, she knew exactly what she was doing: she and Zhuge Jinye were a magnificent garnish on each other's lives, a finishing flourish, but not a final home. Maybe one day in the future their flames would meet again and burn into something even fiercer. But not now.

And Zhuge Jinye had long since decided to stay in Hangzhou. He refused to give up his profession. He ran around making preparations, getting ready to open a professional dance theater of his own, dedicated to staging boy-group dance performances and building his own dance brand. His future was rooted in Hangzhou, while Yao Yuan's road ahead lay in Shanghai.

Two cities, not far apart on a map, yet the distance between them was one they could never cross.

At the same time, with Yao Yuan's patient counsel and careful strategizing, An Nanchan finally untangled the trap of her arranged marriage. She didn't openly defy her domineering father—that would only bring harsher restraints down on her. Instead she calmly went through the marriage candidates her father had screened and picked one: a young man from a good family, decent in character, whom she genuinely liked. That way she satisfied her father without shortchanging herself. Then she played her cards one step at a time. On the grounds of living independently and securing her own livelihood for the years ahead, she asked her father for a generous dowry and assets of her own, winning herself lasting security and a firm footing—so that even once she married, she would hold the high ground and never have to live at anyone's mercy.

You see, all those years ago, Yao Yuan had already shown that clear-eyed wisdom, that gift for reading people and laying her plans in silence.

With everything settled, the time came for Yao Yuan to leave.

The night before she left Hangzhou for Shanghai, she and Zhuge Jinye held each other through the whole night, in that dance studio so full of their memories. There were no extra words—only quickened breaths, scalding tears, and a tenderness drawn out to its very end. The summer wind outside the window couldn't carry off the ache in their hearts. They both understood: once tonight was over, it would be goodbye.

No quarrel, no plea to stay. In the world of adults, in the end you each have to set off down your own road.

The next day Yao Yuan dragged her suitcase, said farewell to An Nanchan, who had come to see her off, and boarded the high-speed train to Shanghai alone. Beyond the window, Hangzhou's scenery slowly slid away. The heat of that summer, the love, the tenderness, and the man named Zhuge Jinye all became a sweet, fleeting old dream in the depths of her heart.

And Zhuge Jinye, standing in the dance studio, gazed up at the scattered mirror panels on the ceiling. In them was only his own lonely figure. It was a long while before he slowly closed his eyes.

That high summer, that fierce, all-consuming love, in the end turned out exactly as they'd said it would—leaving behind nothing but a past that was destined to be forgotten.

Forced to stand and watch from within the pure-white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan felt her faded memories stir. The vivid sight of the young man and woman kissing before her was like a key, and in an instant it unlocked the sealed box of her memory, sending those forgotten flutters and that buried love surging hot through her again. The sweet days with Zhuge Jinye flickered through her mind like scene after scene of a film. And she realized what was about to happen next. They would lose themselves in each other in this professional dance studio, watching frame after living, scalding frame play out in the mirrors, the shame and the secret thrill churning all the more violently, her chest as though scorched by open flame—because that, too, was her. That was the woman who had once merged souls with Zhuge Jinye and held nothing back. Those poses caught frozen in the mirrors, that flawless attunement hidden in their eyes—all of it was her own past, faded long ago and now wrenched fiercely awake, a past carved to the bone.

The twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan had already let go of every tension. Her fingertips were the first to wind around Zhuge Jinye's wrist; the heat of his palm seared through her skin until her whole body trembled. Zhuge Jinye bent slightly, his kiss deepening, his arm sweeping around to pin her firmly against the mirrored wall of the studio, his warm breath falling all over the curve of her neck.

Mirror-light poured down from all four sides, weaving this pure-white studio into an airtight cage. The twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan was held fast against the mirrored wall by Zhuge Jinye, the cold glass pressing against her sweat-damp back, jolting a shiver through her—even as the burning heat of his body left every bone in her soft and yielding.

She was forced to look up, and her gaze ran straight into the mirror before her. In it, his head was bowed very low, his lips grazing the curve of her ear, every breath carrying a scorching heat that flushed the skin of her neck red. His hand was startlingly large, cradling her waist with perfect steadiness; a slight press of his fingers, and she surrendered entirely into his arms. The twenty-nine-year-old her tipped her head back to take it, strands of hair clinging in disarray to her cheeks, her eyes half-shut, infatuation and abandon roiling in their depths. Every faint expression was magnified, crisp and clear, in the mirror—even his eyes, reflecting the image of her, became the most unguarded brand.

Before she could pull her gaze from the picture in that mirror, the mirror on the ceiling overhead broke into view without warning. From that god's-eye view looking down, his broad back covered her small frame completely, their entwined bodies tracing lines of taut tension in the glass—her legs wound around his waist, the arch of her foot drawn into a beautiful curve, a dancer's suppleness and strength fusing without reserve in that moment; his arms locked across her back, his knuckles pale, every measure of force as precise as an attunement rehearsed a thousand times. The two mirrors, from the vertical and the horizontal, refracted and layered every tender motion over and over, leaving her nowhere to escape—she could only watch, wide-eyed, as she and he became one inseparable whole in the glass.

Every pose in the mirror carried temptation taken to its extreme: the flushed tips of her ears, her slightly parted lips, the trembling line of her shoulders; his taut jaw, his absorbed gaze, the controlling force of his hands—even the faint flush rising where their bodies touched glowed in the mirror-light. Shame shot through her limbs like an electric current, yet that secret thrill set her blood boiling. A dancer's acute awareness of the body was magnified without limit; in the mirror she could see exactly how much force he put behind each motion, and see every inch of her own surrender. That feeling of being watched twice over, possessed twice over, made her tremble more than the closeness alone ever could.

Zhuge Jinye had clearly noticed the mirrors too. Far from reining himself in, the obsession in his eyes only deepened. He tilted his head a little, his lips brushing the corner of hers, his voice low and rough, deliberately enticing: "Watch."

She was forced to look back from the mirror, and crashed into his ink-black eyes. The mirror-light flowed between them, making the reality before her and the reflection in the glass overlap perfectly, until she couldn't tell which was real and which was only image. His kiss landed harder, carrying a force that refused to be refused, his arm tightening abruptly to lift her up so that she hung entirely against him. The dancers' attunement reached its peak in that instant. Her body fit itself to his on instinct, every motion carrying the scalding resonance of two souls, every breath woven together with his. In the mirror-light on all four sides, they were each other's reflection, and each other's home.

In another corner of the pure-white space, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan, forced to watch this whole living tableau of her own past play out, looked at that pair of figures in the mirror—holding nothing back, fitted to each other completely. She watched her twenty-nine-year-old self bloom recklessly in Zhuge Jinye's arms, watched their every embrace, every caress, in the mirror-light, and the shame and the thrill rose into a tide that drowned her. That was her youth, the most scalding past in the depths of her soul—those wild, burning poses in the mirror, charged with a dancer's particular tension. So it had never been that the memory faded; it was that she had never truly let it go. The scene before her was the fusion of her and Zhuge Jinye, carved into blood and bone—the fierce passion and deep tenderness that belonged to them alone, blooming in the mirror-light.

The lingering warmth slowly dispersed, leaving only the two of them breathing themselves calm in the studio. They tidied their disordered clothes, and Zhuge Jinye lowered his eyes to ruffle the burning-hot crown of her head, murmuring that he'd go downstairs to buy some iced drinks. Then he turned and slipped out, easing the door shut behind him.

In an instant the vast white space held only the twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan. Her body still soft and aching, the feeling not yet faded, she had no strength to get up. She just lay sprawled on the cold floor, languid and dazed, her gaze fixed on the whole expanse of mirror above her head. In the ceiling glass, her reflection looked back—hair in disarray, cheeks faintly flushed, a misty haze of moisture still lingering in her eyes after that extreme tenderness, her face full of a blank, drifting daze.

Just then the once-clear, gleaming mirror suddenly clouded over with a faint white mist, like ripples spreading across water. And then a face, utterly unfamiliar yet utterly familiar, slowly surfaced from the depths of the glass.

It was the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan.

There she was, emerging in the mirror, looking down from on high at herself on the floor. Her features carried the steady calm settled by the passing years, and a trace of something hard to name—an indifference. Looking at the still-young, still-vivid twenty-nine-year-old self lying on the ground, for a moment she was choked into silence. There was helplessness, there was rue, there was the vexation of someone furious at another for not fighting harder—a thousand emotions jammed in her chest. In the end she forced out a hoarse voice and was the first to break this eerie, hushed standoff across time.

"When you were twenty-six, didn't I warn you, plain as day—leave Shuai Hongqiang at once, go straight to Shanghai? You clearly took it in. So why did you have to take the long way round to Hangzhou? Why, of all things, did you have to meet Zhuge Jinye there in Hangzhou?"

Word by word, weighted with the bitterness and reproach of the years gone by, it struck against the heart of the twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan.

Lying on the floor, she gave a violent start. The blankness in her eyes snapped into focus, fixing straight on the thirty-six-year-old self in the mirror. From below, the face in the glass carried a sharpness honed by the years, yet also a bitterness that wouldn't dissolve. That was her future self—the her who had been ground down by everything the world could throw at her.

The defenses in her heart collapsed all at once. The eyes of the twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan reddened faintly, her fingertips gripping the rubber dance mat beneath her. Her voice was light enough to shake, but still, word by word, she poured out the whole of the past she had never told another soul.

"After you vanished, I thought about it a long time. Shuai Hongqiang was so good to me back then—how could he not want to marry me? So I went straight at it and gave him two choices: marry me now, or break up now. He chose to marry me. We got our marriage license soon after and held a grand wedding. We spent six sweet months as newlyweds, and then I got pregnant."

She paused, a heavy bitterness rising in her throat. Remembering that agonizing stretch of the past, she couldn't help trembling all over.

"In March of our second year of marriage, the baby was just six months along, my belly already obvious. That day we were on a business trip together, and on the road we got into a fight over some trivial little thing. He lost his temper, lost his head completely, couldn't keep the wheel steady, and there was a car accident… He broke his shin. I hemorrhaged in a miscarriage and lost the baby. It was a boy who had already taken shape…"

The tears finally slid down from the corners of her eyes, dripping onto the floor and blooming into a small wet stain.

"The doctor said I was too badly hurt, that it would be very hard for me to conceive again. I hated him—hated that one moment of impulse that destroyed my child, that destroyed my right to be a mother. And he resented me—resented that I'd quarreled with him and brought the whole disaster down on us. There was no going back for us. We tormented each other, hated each other, couldn't go on living together at all. In the autumn of my twenty-seventh year, we filed for divorce, and Shuai Hongqiang split half his fortune with me."

"After the divorce I went to Shanghai, just as you said. I started in sales, worked myself to the bone, and in two years I made department head. This March the boss sent me to Hangzhou to study for half a year, with a promise that I'd be made assistant to the president when I went back."

"Zhuge and I… were classmates. There was a kind of fatal attraction between us. I couldn't resist it, and I didn't want to."

When she'd finished, she looked at herself in the mirror, her eyes full of confusion and pain. "Should I just have done exactly what you told me, every time? But I couldn't accept that… I wanted to walk my own way, to see whether the road was passable. What happened with Shuai Hongqiang taught me that fate can be changed at the small junctures—but there's a price to pay. The first warning you ever gave me played out the same way: after I went abroad to study and came back, it was because Mother fell ill and needed surgery that I couldn't stay on in Beijing, and ended up back in Jinshi, on the track fate had already set. Maybe, if fate hands you a gift, it quietly takes something away in the dark."

Hearing all of this, the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror went bloodless in an instant, ashen as though every spark of life had been drained out of her. Her lips trembled beyond her control, and even her fingertips were clenched white inside her sleeves. Looking at the young self in the mirror—tear-streaked, eyes full of bewilderment—every question lodged in her throat, all the speechlessness and unwillingness brimming in her heart turned into a heart-rending, dull ache that spread thick and dense through all her limbs. She opened her mouth, but for the longest time no sound would come. Time and again she had crossed through time to warn and remind herself, wanting only to let her young self escape those tangled, unbreakable knots, to avoid that bone-eating pain. Yet fate kept circling back, like an invisible net, and in the end dragged her viciously back onto the track it had set. The torment she was meant to endure was not lessened by even half; instead, knowing the ending, she bore double the despair and double the suffering.

So then—what, in the end, was the meaning of crossing through time again and again? Giving it everything she had, what could she actually change? In the heart of the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan a boundless confusion spread, like falling into a fog with no bottom, until she could no longer find even a direction.

"…" She opened her mouth in vain. A thousand words jammed in her chest—bitterness, pain, helplessness all tangled together—and in the end not a single word made it out.

The twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan saw through the complicated emotions churning in her eyes at a glance; that hesitation, that almost-saying, was far too plain. She took a step forward of her own accord and asked, her voice trembling faintly: "So—this time you've appeared, is there some advice you have for me?"

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan was silent a long while, her throat parched and aching, each word as though squeezed out from between her teeth, hoarse past all recognition: "This time, I have no warning. Cherish your days with Zhuge Jinye. They'll be the one thing you can hold up in the long years ahead, the most beautiful and the sweetest memory you have. Don't go out of your way to hurt him, and don't be a fool and let him hurt you. When you love, be happy with everything you've got; once you part, each of you keep well, and leave the other alone. If I have to add one reminder—within your means, put aside some funds and invest in him. The men's dance troupe he founds later will catch fire across the whole country and be adored by countless women. And one more thing—whoever your boss in Shanghai turns out to be, whatever you do, don't ever toward him…"

The words broke off without warning. With no sign at all, a familiar force, cold and powerful, wrenched the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan into a churning torrent of time. The familiar studio before her twisted, stretched, and blurred at speed. In the spinning of heaven and earth a vast dizziness swept over her, and she lost consciousness entirely, sinking into boundless darkness.

In the studio, only the twenty-nine-year-old Yao Yuan was left lying alone on the floor, gazing up at the empty mirror overhead.

In the glass, there was no one left.
