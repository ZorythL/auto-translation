Killing the Old Self Inside a Marriage
"Finally, let me share a little personal insight. Anyone who truly sees through the nature of marriage will understand this: a lasting marriage is never just the home that romantic love comes to rest in. It's more like a long collaboration, one that requires both sides to keep their dignity and to stand on equal footing. There's care in it, but also gamesmanship; there's tenderness, but also cool, rational calculation."
"But the woman I am now will never burn herself out again over some vague, intangible 'emotional value.' A good marriage is a journey two people take toward a new life together. At first I thought 'killing the old self' sounded too brutal a phrase, but later I understood. What gets 'killed' is never your true heart or your soul. It's the self that was too naive, too fragile, too full of unrealistic fantasies inside love; it's the obsessive, self-consuming, easily-lost old version of you from that relationship."
"It was the deep practice of marriage that let me break myself apart and rebuild myself with my own hands. I set down the infant's expectation of omnipotence, kicked the habit of endless emotional churn, shed my childish impulses of raw feeling, and learned to weigh things clearly and run things steadily. I threw away my fragile glass heart, gave up the dependent posture of pinning my whole life on another person, and through the polishing of the years and the friction of the relationship, I grew sturdier bones. I became a more independent, more clear-eyed self, with a steadier core."
"This was never compromise or surrender. It was a grand rebirth."
"And a genuinely healthy marriage is never a solo dance for one. It's a shared practice for two. Not draining each other, not dragging each other down, but you understanding how hard things are for me, and me understanding what you struggle with; you spurring me to become a better version of myself, and me walking with you through the fog that won't last. Inside this deepest of relationships, we sand down the edges that wound, correct the flaws in our characters, set aside stubbornness and self-interest, and toward the future we both long for, transform into more mature, more compatible, more complete partners."
"This so-called 'killing yourself' kills the old self that didn't know how to love itself, wasn't strong enough, lost itself too easily in a relationship. What it builds is a brand-new self that knows how to treasure itself, knows how to manage a relationship, and is full of inner strength. A good partner is a mirror that shows you your shortcomings; even more, a partner is a light that lights the road ahead, letting two independent lives reflect one another across the long years, growing together, both of them living out a freer, fuller shape."
"Know what the core need at the center of your heart truly is. Then let every word and every action revolve around that goal, clearly and firmly. Pursue it in the most efficient way, the way that spares the most of your energy and consumes the least of yourself. Once you can do that, you'll never fall into a passive position in any relationship again."
"It's like the 'plan' I later tailored for how Hongqiang and I get along. I take the lead in presenting myself as gentle, easy to talk to, even occasionally willing to be 'controlled' — a low posture that makes it look like he's the one in charge. But in truth, I'm the one quietly holding the overall rhythm of the relationship, the one who nudges the needle ever so lightly at the crucial moments. Only when I completely let go of greedily demanding 'emotional value,' and could instead supply it on demand — giving emotional support freely, dialing it up or down at will — did I truly become strong. The thoughtfulness, the tolerance, the empathy I offer are no longer lowly people-pleasing or helpless surrender. They're the clear-eyed choices of someone strong, an effective strategy and a controllable chip for protecting the relationship and reaching the goal."
"Human nature is often like this: the more desperately you crave something, the more easily that very thing has you pinned down, stuck in place."

"All right, sisters, that's where we'll end tonight's sharing." Lin Wan smiled into the camera, a warm and soothing smile. "If you have any confusion about marriage or about your feelings, leave a comment below and I'll try to read them. May you all keep your gentleness in a relationship, and also hold the strength of a clear head. Good night, see you next time."
She gave a light wave, the livestream image slowly dimmed, and the last frame froze on her calm, powerful smile.

Outside the study, the living room was lit by a single floor lamp. Shuai Hongqiang sat on the sofa at the border of light and shadow, the tablet screen in his hands long gone dark. He didn't get up right away. In the dim yellow light, he slowly finished the last sip of sobering tea at the bottom of his cup.
Everything about today felt like an unreal dream.
From the moment he got the invitation for the city's "National Fitness Center" project, that dazed, walking-on-clouds feeling hadn't stopped. Then there was the construction payment that had been owed for more than three full years, the one that had been eating him alive — ten million yuan, and it had actually landed in the account a few days ago. Just when he thought that was the whole of his luck, the remaining five million in the balance had, with no warning at all, hit the company account this afternoon too.
Fifteen million.
When that number flickered on the mobile banking page, Shuai Hongqiang stared at it for three solid minutes. Little Wang from finance was on the other end of the line, so excited he could barely string words together, while all he managed was a single "Mm." After hanging up, he realized his palms were soaked with sweat.
The money was in. The deadlock was broken. The boulder that had pressed on his back for three years crashed to the ground.
But what came with it was a deeper unease.
He thought of Yao Yuan, of those eyes of hers that seemed to see through everything, and of the warning from Old Chen, the antiques appraiser: "Everything has its cause and effect. The fruit you reap now is the seed you planted before; the seed you plant now is the fruit you'll have to repay in the future." This fifteen million that had finally been settled, this red-hot new project — they were sweet, fresh fruit, and yet his heart fluttered with dread. In the future, what kind of price would he have to pay for this "good luck"?
That evening, he did something he should have done long ago: he invited all the material suppliers he'd worked with, and owed money to, to the best restaurant in Jinshi. The private room was wreathed in smoke, wine cups clinking.
He stood up and raised his glass. The first toast was to their partnership, thanking everyone for their support over the years. For the second, he bent down and bowed deeply — for the goods he'd failed to pay for, and for those humiliating moments when they'd chased him all the way home, blocked his office door, and all he could do was say, red-faced, "Just a little longer." He apologized in earnest.
"I'm sorry, brothers. The material payments dragged on so long, and it put you all through hell. Thank you again for your loyalty and your patience these past stretches."
For the third toast he drained the cup in one go, then set it down, his voice clear and firm: "Every last material payment will be settled in full by tomorrow afternoon at the latest. Not a cent owed."
The room went quiet for an instant, then erupted into even louder commotion. Those faces that had once flushed with anger were now stacked with smiles, gripping his hand and calling him "Boss Shuai," saying "We understand, we understand," "It's hard for everyone," "We'll be counting on you to lead us to fortune from here on."
Amid the back-and-forth of toasts, he tossed out the olive branch of the "National Fitness Center" project too. The mood was pushed to its peak. It was as if all the squabbles, the complaints, even the curses behind his back during the debt-collecting days had never existed. He had turned back into that "Boss Shuai" everyone crowded around, riding high.
But only he himself knew that something was different. Beneath that "high" lay a layer of something hollow he couldn't name, and a faint worry over that "debt of gratitude" he owed Yao Yuan.
But whatever the case, the deadlock at the level of reality was finally untied. Coming out of the restaurant, with the night wind blowing, that long-lost confidence — the kind that belongs to a man — rose bit by bit from the soles of his feet, mixed with the buzz of the alcohol. He couldn't wait to get home, couldn't wait to see Lin Wan.
Most of all, he wanted to tell her: I've pulled through. This home can be carried by me again, the way it used to be.
He could finally put an end to the bind of the past half-year and more — an end to the humiliation of leaning on his wife's livestream income to supplement the household, pay their son's tuition, even cover his cash flow. That secret shame of being propped up, that unease and faint inferiority from feeling his role as "provider" teetering — it could finally be brought to a close. He was going to take back the place in this relationship that was most familiar to him, the place where he stood most firmly.
By the time he got home, Lin Wan's livestream was almost over. He didn't go into the study; he just sat down in the living room and watched silently on the tablet. On the screen her voice was calm, her analysis incisive, dissecting the daily gamesmanship between them like a sand-table war game. Listening, the corners of his mouth lifted on their own, and the restless heat of wanting to rush in and share his good news slowly settled.
The chime signaling the end of the broadcast sounded. He put down the tablet, got up, and went to the kitchen. From the temperature-controlled kettle he poured water at just the right warmth, scooped in a spoonful of the locust-flower honey Lin Wan liked, and stirred it slowly.
When had this habit started? It seemed to be right after Lin Wan's livestream income grew steady and truly began to carry part of the household's expenses. At first it had perhaps been an unconscious kind of compensation, a clumsy bit of thoughtfulness. Later it became a fixed, unspoken ritual at the end of every night's broadcast.
A faint sound came from the study door. Cup in hand, he turned and saw Lin Wan come out, a faint tiredness on her face after the high-intensity session, but her eyes still clear and bright under the warm yellow light.
"Master tactician of marriage," Shuai Hongqiang walked over and handed the honey water into her hands, asking with a smile, a lightness and anticipation in his tone that he himself hadn't noticed, "how was tonight's battle record?"
Lin Wan took the cup, her fingertips brushing lightly against his warm palm. She lowered her head and took a sip, the gentle sweetness sliding down her throat, then lifted her head, met his gaze, and gave a sly little smile: "A total victory. The enemy forces have been completely conquered by our side's wit and charm."
The same familiar lines, dressed up in pretend-joking. Shuai Hongqiang shook his head with a soft laugh and reached out to ruffle her neat, soft short hair — even this small affectionate gesture seemed long overdue. "Go wash up first," his voice dropped a little, carrying a flutter of excitement he couldn't hold back. "In a bit, I've got good news to tell you."
Lin Wan held the warm cup and took another sip, then raised her eyes and looked at him quietly. Their gazes met in the dim, hushed light of the living room, silently exchanging certain pieces of information only the two of them could understand.
On the wall, the clock's hands ticked quietly toward half past ten.
In the living room, their son Wen Yao's toys were tidied neatly into the woven basket; the warm aroma of the home-cooked dinner still lingered in the air; at the doorway of the children's room, a little night-light gave off a soft, fuzzy halo.
Another small "battle" with no smoke of gunpowder had drawn to a close. There was no declaration of who won or lost, but some new, subtle balance — or you could say the "terms of cooperation" for the next stage — seemed to have been silently confirmed and renewed in this cup of honey water at just the right temperature.
Tomorrow, life would turn a new page on its own, and a new game of chess, a new negotiation, might already be quietly taking shape. But at least in this moment, on this night, in this small patch of space wrapped in warmth and quiet, they shared this hard-won, unspoken "peace."
— It was the deepest understanding of an "opponent" worth taking seriously in the game, and even more, of a "partner" bound to her in fate; a complicated, hard-to-name respect.
Tonight, they walked side by side down the hallway toward the bedroom, their figures merging into one under the light.
The new tomorrow really did seem to be slowly unfolding. And the "good news" he was bringing might well be the very key to restarting some cycle. So Shuai Hongqiang thought, and for now the faint unease that Yao Yuan had stirred in him was pressed down beneath the solid warmth and anticipation right in front of his eyes.
