Chapter 70 — A Fork in Time, Witnessing a Proposal
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640317.html

Yao Yuan's consciousness slowly came back to itself inside a space of pure white.

Here we go again, she understood at once. The uncontrolled time-travel had pulled her back, after that brief peace and sweetness beneath the desert stars.

Not far off, in some empty, void-like patch of space, ripples began to spread out clearly, like a still pond struck by a stone. An image went from blurred to sharp, building and taking shape with startling speed—

It was an extravagant, meticulously arranged indoor proposal scene.

From the backdrop, it looked like the ballroom of some top-tier hotel, but completely transformed. From the high domed ceiling hung countless strings of glittering crystal chandeliers and fresh champagne roses; the floor was carpeted in a thick layer of white petals. All around were flickering candle flames in silver candlesticks, and the air drifted with fine perfume and the sweet scent of champagne. There weren't many guests—about twenty or thirty—but every one of them was sharply dressed and refined, clearly a carefully chosen circle of close family and friends.

At this moment, every face wore a smile of blessing, all eyes fixed on the center of the ballroom.

There, thousands upon thousands of Ecuadorian red roses had been arranged into a giant heart shape. At the center of the heart, a young man in a well-tailored navy-blue suit, tall and straight-backed, knelt on one knee.

It was Yu Hao. A few years younger than in Yao Yuan's memory, maybe twenty-seven or twenty-eight, his face still carrying a trace of un-faded boyishness—but the spirited brightness in his brow and his deep, focused devotion in this moment made him look especially radiant. In his hands he held an open navy-blue velvet ring box, and a pear-shaped diamond ring of at least five carats by the look of it refracted dazzling fire under the brilliant lights.

And standing before him, head slightly bowed to look down at him, was a younger Yao Yuan. By her appearance, she was a little past thirty—the age when the rawness of youth has worn away and a woman is at the height of her bloom. She wore an ivory-white satin strapless gown, cut with the utmost elegance, tracing her slender figure. Her long hair was carefully swept up, baring a long, graceful neck, and the diamond tassel earrings at her ears swayed with her faint trembling. One hand covered her mouth, her face full of disbelieving delight, of emotion, and of undisguised, overwhelming happiness, her eyes shimmering with tears that glittered like crushed diamonds in the light.

"Yuanyuan," said Yu Hao from where he knelt, his voice clear, carrying excitement and nervousness as it echoed through the silent ballroom. "From the moment I met you, I knew that my long wait and all my efforts were for the sake of reaching you. You're smart, independent, strong, and you have the softest heart in the world. Every single day with you makes me feel my life is more complete and full of hope than ever before. I won't claim I can give you the whole world, but I'm willing to spend the rest of my life guarding your smile, supporting your dreams, and standing beside you through every storm and every ray of sunshine to come. Yao Yuan, I love you. Will you... marry me?"

His words were sincere and ardent, his gaze blazing, full of a young man's reckless earnestness.

A low, good-natured chorus of cheers and applause rose around them. "Say yes!" "Marry him!" A few of the young women were already moved to cover their mouths.

The younger Yao Yuan's tears finally broke free, sliding down her cheeks. She looked at Yu Hao, then glanced around at all the friends and family smiling their blessings, and at last nodded hard, her voice choked with happiness yet utterly clear and firm: "I do!"

Pure joy burst across Yu Hao's face. Carefully he lifted that brilliant diamond ring from its box and, solemnly, with a slight tremble, slid it onto the ring finger of the younger Yao Yuan's left hand. A perfect fit.

The next second he rose, swept her into his arms, and amid the crowd's cheers and applause, kissed her deeply. A champagne tower was wheeled out by the waiters, bubbles rising merrily. Music came on at just the right moment, soft and romantic. The guests came forward one after another to embrace and congratulate this newly engaged pair.

(In the pure-white space, Yao Yuan hung suspended in midair like a cold bystander, watching this warm, romantic scene. Yet she felt nothing in her heart—only a barren, icy chill, and a sense of dislocation that bordered on the absurd.)

She spotted several familiar faces in the crowd—a few of her old girlfriends from back in Shanghai, still looking a little fresh-faced then; a couple of Yu Hao's buddies, grinning brightly; even... she saw Ding Shitong.

Ding Shitong stood a little toward the outer edge of the crowd, a champagne glass in hand, a proper, social smile on his face as he applauded lightly along with everyone else. But his eyes were deeper and more complicated than anyone else's there. In them was scrutiny, calculation, a faint trace of loss—perhaps one even he hadn't noticed—but more than that, a kind of calm certainty, as if this whole scene had long been within some prediction or observation of his. Now and then he murmured to those beside him, yet his gaze kept drifting, as if by accident, over that embracing pair, and especially over the glaring diamond on the younger Yao Yuan's ring finger; when it did, an unreadable shadow flickered in his eyes. Several young, pretty, fashionably dressed girls clustered near him, their eyes straying every so often to the happy Yao Yuan at the center—a look that held envy, jealousy, and a faint, hard-to-detect, subtle scrutiny of "what makes her so special."

The younger Yao Yuan was clearly fully immersed in the enormous joy of being proposed to and in everyone's blessings, her cheeks flushed, her eyes shining, fingers laced tight with Yu Hao's, accepting wave after wave of teasing and congratulations from her friends.

After a while, she seemed to feel her makeup had been smudged by tears. She murmured something to Yu Hao, then gathered up her skirt and headed toward the restroom off to the side of the ballroom.

The Yao Yuan in the pure-white space found herself drawn along by some invisible force, "floating" after her.

The restroom was decorated just as lavishly, silent and still, a contrast to the liveliness outside. The younger Yao Yuan walked up to the wide mirror, studied her makeup carefully, and took out a compact to touch it up lightly. Looking at her own flushed cheeks, her bright eyes, and that dazzling ring on her finger, she couldn't help breaking into a smile so happy it was almost dazed.

And just then—

The mirror's surface rippled and warped, like still water struck by a stone.

The younger Yao Yuan's hand froze mid-touch-up. She stared, eyes wide with shock, as another face gradually surfaced beside her own reflection—a face exactly like hers, only more mature, more coolly aloof. It was the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan.

In the mirror, the two Yao Yuans from different times faced each other once more across the dressing glass.

The younger Yao Yuan, too, took it almost as a matter of habit—just an instinctive startle at something appearing out of nowhere—but she quickly recognized the figure in the mirror and steadied herself. Sure enough, they were essentially the same person: faced with something they had no power to change, they accepted it fast and adapted fast. Different souls from different times though they were, the inner strength and the core of their thinking hadn't changed.

The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan looked at that young face in the mirror, brimming with unfeigned happiness and full of longing for the future, and her mind was like an overturned palette—tangled and hard to put into words. Only a few months ago, in the real world, she had just made a complete, final break with Yu Hao—the Yu Hao who claimed to love her and depend on her, yet flinched at the most crucial real-world interests and the promise of marriage, wanting only a "child" as a bond and a guarantee. The heartbreak and disappointment hadn't fully faded, and now here she was, traveled back, watching with her own eyes as a younger self in another timeline joyfully accepted a grand proposal from the very same man.

The absurdity of the contrast made her throat tighten.

She opened her mouth, wanting to say something. Wanting to demand answers sharply, to give a piercing warning. Last time, hadn't she already cautioned her younger self to beware of a man surnamed "Yu"? But the words reached her lips and then stuck fast.

That was it. Last time, she'd only managed to get out "If you meet someone surnamed Yu in the venture-capital world, don't get involved with him..." before the most crucial part went unsaid and she vanished abruptly into the mirror. The warning had been only half finished—and might even have backfired, making her younger self instead develop some improper, fate-like curiosity or fixation on the surname "Yu."

A vast helplessness and the mockery of fate swept over her.

In the end, she could only force down those fierce words, look at her young self in the mirror, and ask in a dry voice (she needed to make sense of this timeline's thread): "Last time I saw you... didn't you choose to be with Duan Yanqing of your own will? How... did you end up with Yu Hao instead?"

Hearing her bring this up, the younger Yao Yuan seemed to relax her guard a little, though her eyes dimmed for an instant. Facing the mirror, absently turning the diamond on her finger, she answered in a low voice, her tone carrying the complexity of memory:

"After I last saw you, I looked over and evaluated Zhao Yiming's 'Western World' carefully. The project really was excellent. I went back to Shanghai with the assessment report and some new ideas."

"Not long after, on Duan Yanqing's side... there were some problems with domestic policy and a few of his overseas assets. He decided to pull back all his investments and arrangements in the country, move abroad for good, and start a semi-retired life. Before he left, he formally invited me to go with him."

She raised her head, looking at her future self in the mirror, clear reluctance and ambition in her eyes: "I wasn't willing to settle. I was still young. I felt my life had only just begun—the stage I wanted, the things I wanted to prove, were all still here in the country. I hadn't reached the height I truly wanted. Go with him, to a completely unfamiliar country, and be a 'wife' kept under his protection? That wasn't the future I wanted. So, after weighing it for a long time, I chose to stay."

"About... half a year later," she went on, her tone turning lighter again, carrying the sweetness of someone fallen in love, "at a small closed-door symposium on cutting-edge technology, I met Yu Hao. He was one of the youngest speakers there. His views were razor-sharp, and his read on the future left a deep impression on me. We talked afterward and found... we actually saw eye to eye on so many things, from business trends to attitudes about life—even the books and films we liked lined up uncannily well. He's quick-minded and broadly knowledgeable, and even though he's six years younger than me, I felt no gap in age at all. If anything... he understood me better and respected my ideas and my career more than most of the men I'd met my own age or older."

A blush rose on her face, the natural glow of someone speaking of the one they love: "We started seeing each other very naturally. He courted me with real care, and with a sense of proportion. After half a year together, we... moved in together. Today is the official first anniversary of our relationship. He kept it from me, planned all of this with such care, and proposed." She looked at the ring on her hand, her smile happy and content.

The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan listened quietly. Every word was like a fine needle, pricking gently at her heart. The "self" of this timeline had made different choices, stayed in the country, put down roots in Shanghai by her own efforts, and then, at a more "right" moment, met a "better" version of Yu Hao—a young, passionate, sincere Yu Hao whose goal was marriage. No heavy calculations over real-world interests, no cold detachment of a "companion-of-convenience" arrangement, only pure attraction and a burning promise.

What a... "correct" path. What a "happy" version.

She looked at that face in the mirror, filled to the brim with love and hope, and all her warnings, her cautions, even the resentment carried over from her own wounds, turned pale and powerless—even... a little despicable. Was she really going to shatter with her own hands the happiness this "self" could already touch? On what grounds? Just because she herself, in another timeline, had met the wrong person and given her heart to the wrong man?

In the end, she said nothing. Only, ever so faintly, ever so softly, she breathed a few words at her young self in the mirror, her voice as drifting as a sigh:

"I wish you well."

The younger Yao Yuan clearly let out a breath, her smile blooming again. "Thank you!" She hesitated, then added with a little excitement at sharing the news: "Yu Hao doesn't hold it against me that I had a brief marriage before"—clearly referring to the one with Shuai Hongqiang—"he says it's just life experience. Next week he's taking me to America to meet his parents and family, to discuss the official wedding date." Her eyes shone with boundless longing for the future.

The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's mind stirred: "You're thirty-five this year?"

She got a firm yes from the younger Yao Yuan.

She was about to speak again, to ask the thirty-five-year-old Yao Yuan about the partnership and investment with Zhao Yiming—one of the most central and most hopeful career ties she had in the real world right now.

But then the familiar, icy grip seized her again, without warning—fiercer than before!

"Wait—" She had time only to cry out in her consciousness.

The scene before her—the younger Yao Yuan's happy face, the lavish restroom mirror, even that conspicuous diamond ring—all twisted and stretched in an instant, then were dragged brutally into a dark whirlpool by some immense force.

The mirror's surface returned to calm, reflecting only the thirty-five-year-old Yao Yuan's lovely face, freshly proposed to by the man she loved, still carrying traces of undimmed happiness. Startled again for a moment by the sudden disappearance of her future self, she turned back to the mirror and studied the ring on her hand—that symbol of a promise—the smile at the corner of her lips sweet and real.

...

Cold emptiness, a long sensation of falling.

Then came a heavy suffocation and return, like struggling up from the deep sea to break the surface.

Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open, her pupils contracting sharply, her vision a complete blur, unfocused for several seconds. In her ears was the steady noise of a car in motion; in her nose, the familiar smell of leather and air-conditioning inside the cabin.

She was still in the car. The passenger seat.

Outside the window, harsh sunlight blazed in. They had just emerged from the last long tunnel of Wushaoling, back into the daylight. The "Wushaoling Tunnel" signboard flashed past beyond the window.

She leaned against the seatback. To anyone else, she had perhaps only dozed against the window for a little while, woken by the sunlight as the car came out of the tunnel. But she alone knew that in this seemingly brief span, her soul had been through such an absurd, outrageous journey across time and space—witnessing a "happy" proposal utterly opposite to her own reality, and doing so right beside this man who studied the most advanced frontiers of human brain science and artificial intelligence: Zhao Yiming.

It seemed this time-travel had lasted a shorter time than any of the previous ones. Was it the protective effect of the "heart-guarding" jade that Old Chen had given her? Or because she was far enough from that locked-away bronze mirror that the pull of the "Mirror Pact" was weakened? She couldn't say. That hollowed-out exhaustion and icy aftershock deep in her soul still lingered, but it truly lacked the violent, drained-of-all-energy depletion she'd felt returning from the first five time-travels. The old jade gave off a steady, gentle warmth against her chest, like a warm current slowly mending her damaged spirit.

Just then Zhao Yiming, at the wheel, seemed to sense her slight movement. He turned his head, his gaze settling on her with concern, his voice gentle and low: "Awake? Had a little nap? Did you fall asleep?" His eyes held a natural care, and a faint, hard-to-detect tenderness that belonged to lovers.

Yao Yuan's heart slammed hard against her chest. The confession and intimacy beneath the desert stars, the dance by the bonfire, the scene she'd just witnessed of another timeline's "self" being proposed to, and the real-world break with Yu Hao... all these images collided and tangled wildly in her mind, leaving her dazed for a moment, unable to tell what time it was or where she was.

She quickly lowered her lashes, hiding the complicated emotions churning in her eyes, and when she looked up again she had put on her usual calm—only her face was still a little pale. She raised a hand to rub her temple, her tone carrying a perfectly judged, just-woken languor and slight huskiness: "Mm, I fell asleep. Had a... messy little dream." She tugged at the corner of her mouth, trying for a relaxed smile. "Maybe I relaxed too much these past few days—got drowsy the second I got in the car."

Of course she couldn't tell the top-flight brain scientist and AI pioneer beside her that she hadn't been asleep at all, but had, right next to him, gone through a bizarre "soul time-travel" that no existing science could explain, to watch another timeline's "self" be proposed to. It wouldn't just sound like a fairy tale—it would sound close to the delusions of a deranged mind.

Zhao Yiming studied her face carefully, his brow knitting almost imperceptibly. He seemed about to say something, but in the end didn't press. He only said gently: "Rest a bit more if you're tired. We've still got over two hours to go. If you get carsick or feel unwell, tell me anytime—there's a service area up ahead."

"Okay. I'm fine." Yao Yuan nodded, leaned back against the seat, and closed her eyes. This time she really did need a little while to settle her thoughts, to digest that enormous flood of information and the sense of absurdity.

The rest of the way back passed without incident. The convoy returned smoothly to Jinshi; by then it was evening. Zhao Yiming personally drove Yao Yuan to the foot of her apartment building.

The car came to a stop, and a few seconds of silence fell between them. The new relationship established by the desert trip and the bizarre time-travel she had just lived through formed a subtle, fractured tension in Yao Yuan's heart.

"Want to come up for a bit?" Yao Yuan asked out of politeness, though there wasn't much real invitation in her tone.

Zhao Yiming shook his head. His gaze lingered a moment on her still-tired face, his voice steady and understanding: "No. You've just come back from the desert—you need a proper rest. And besides," he smiled, that smile carrying a practicality they both tacitly understood, "we've been away a few days, and our companies each have a pile of things waiting to be dealt with. Rest up first. Tomorrow... we'll be in touch."

His thoughtfulness and sense of proportion eased Yao Yuan's heart a little, and stirred a flicker of genuine warmth. "Okay. You head back and rest early too—driving all that way was hard work." She unbuckled her seatbelt, ready to get out.

"Yao Yuan." Zhao Yiming suddenly called her back.

She turned.

He looked at her, his eyes especially focused and clear in the dim light of the car: "Whatever you dreamed, it's all in the past. Now, we're back." His words seemed to carry a hidden meaning, and yet seemed merely ordinary comfort.

Yao Yuan's heart stirred faintly. She smiled at him: "Mm, I know. Goodbye."

"Goodbye."
