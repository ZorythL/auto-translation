New Year's Night Fireworks, Disaster in an Instant
Shuai Hongqiang reached out at once to Wang Baoguo, descendant of the mirror-masters.
On the phone, the old man's voice was as low and unhurried as ever, betraying no emotion. Shuai Hongqiang fought down the anxiety churning in his chest, speaking fast, even with a faint, barely perceptible tremor, as he explained that Yao Yuan had just completed her ninth time-travel.
A long silence settled over the line, broken only by the faint sounds of pages turning and fingertips counting off calculations. A full five minutes later, Wang Baoguo's steady voice came back, every word landing with weight: "Tomorrow — the Yi-Si Year of the Snake, the third day of the twelfth lunar month, the hour of You."
"A yin year, a yin month, a yin day, a yin hour — four layers of yin at their peak. As the sun sets and the air sinks, when the Yellow River's vapors reach their most yin and heavy, that is the best time to dissolve the Mirror Pact and seal away the cursed thing."
He then fixed the final location: "The place will be the White Horse Rapids shoal below Jincheng Pass."
"The White Horse Rapids…" Shuai Hongqiang repeated under his breath, his heart sinking abruptly, an inexplicable chill creeping up his spine.
He knew well how treacherous that place was. The White Horse Rapids of the Yellow River — the perilous shoal below Jincheng Pass. The channel was narrow and choked with jagged rocks, the current roaring with towering waves; beneath the surface, hidden reefs tangled together and undertows and whirlpools ran thick. Mountains on both sides locked the valley shut, sunlight rarely reached it, and the gorge was cold and damp, the atmosphere thick and heavy with a sinking weight.
Wang Baoguo seemed to read his misgivings, and explained mildly: "From ancient times this place has been both a ferry crossing and a strategic pass fought over by armies. The killing energy is heavy, the current sinks deep, the field is sealed shut, yin and yang are clearly drawn. It can lock down evil, seal the malevolence, and steady the flow of time — it is the ideal place to dissolve the Mirror Pact and bring this karma to an end. There's no need to worry. Tomorrow at the hour of You, as the sun sinks behind the western mountains and the yin energy first rises, the timing will be exactly right. You and Yao Yuan need only bring the bronze mirror to the appointed place. Cinnabar, red cloth, the offering implements — I will have all of it ready. When the time comes, each of you will draw blood from a fingertip, and that will start the pact-dissolving rite."
"Understood. I'll tell her right away." Shuai Hongqiang answered, then paused and asked cautiously, "Master Wang, what about Old Chen… should we let him know? Old Chen was the first to see through the bronze mirror's secret; he gave Yao Yuan the soul-quieting old jade to protect her, and helped us track you down every step of the way. In all fairness, he deserves to know the outcome."
Wang Baoguo considered for a moment. "Old Chen and I go back a long way. He's a sensible man, and he can keep a secret. There's no harm in telling him. If he wants to come and witness the result, he may. Only bear in mind: this matter must not be made known to anyone unrelated. When dissolving the Mirror Pact, nothing is worse than a crowd of people and a tangle of energies leaking the workings of Heaven."
"You can rest easy on that." Shuai Hongqiang agreed.
Hanging up, Shuai Hongqiang immediately wrote out a message laying out everything Wang Baoguo had worked out and settled — the hour, the place, the reasons, and all the requirements for dissolving the pact — set down clearly point by point, and sent it to Yao Yuan so she could prepare in advance and gather her strength.
Afraid he might have left something out, he sent Yao Yuan another short, definitive message: "Tomorrow at the hour of You (5 p.m.), gather at the White Horse Rapids shoal. Master Wang will prepare the cinnabar, the red cloth, and the rest. We arrive on time."
With that done, he stood under the cold lights of the hospital corridor, drew a deep breath, and slowly let it out. Then he found Old Chen's number and dialed. The call connected, and Old Chen's voice was as gentle as ever. Shuai Hongqiang skipped the pleasantries and told him directly that the time and place for dissolving the pact were now set — tomorrow at dusk.
Old Chen was silent for a few seconds on the other end, then said slowly, "…So it's come to this, after all. Boss Shuai, I understand. I'll be there tomorrow. Some karma only feels complete when you see it ended with your own eyes. Take every care, all of you." There was a complicated emotion in his tone, like the sigh of dust finally settling, and also like a heavy gravity in the face of some unknown ending.
The words drifted down lightly, yet for no clear reason they pressed an aching weight onto Shuai Hongqiang's chest. The gloom that had clung to him for days hadn't lifted now that the date was set; if anything, it had grown thicker.
He stood in the cold hospital corridor, the smell of disinfectant seeping in everywhere. His daughter, Shuai Wenrui, had been bedridden with a leg injury for nearly a week, the limb fixed in a cast; she wouldn't be discharged to recover until next week. Qiu Ying had gone home briefly to wash up and change before returning to the room, her face freshly tidied, but with the exhaustion of days of bedside care in her eyes. She was sitting beside the bed now, looking at her phone. Shuai Hongqiang spoke with her quietly about their daughter's condition, looked at the girl's peacefully sleeping face, and let his heart ease a little, then turned and left the hospital.
Today was the first day of the new year, and he and Lin Wan had arranged earlier to have dinner that evening with two families of close friends.
These two families were deeply tied to him. One was Old Xu, a mid-level leader in a government department with real power, who oversaw approvals for one of his key projects; the other was Boss Li, who had worked under him for years and was now the contractor on his largest project. The three families were close, their interests entangled and their bonds warm, and the children were near in age, raised side by side, close to one another and frequently in and out of each other's homes.
On the drive to the mall where they were to meet, Shuai Hongqiang forced down the unease in his heart, sealing away for the moment all the gloom of the Mirror Pact, the dissolving of it, and the karmic backlash. He needed a brief stretch of ease, and he needed to maintain this solid foundation of connections.
He parked, walked into the Korean barbecue restaurant on the mall's top floor, and was met by a wave of noisy chatter and the smell of food. Old Xu's and Boss Li's families had already arrived, seated around a large table, the children chasing and roughhousing in the open space nearby. Lin Wan rose to meet him and asked quietly, "How's Wenrui?"
"Recovering well. She can be discharged next week. Qiu Ying's watching over her," Shuai Hongqiang answered simply, raising a hand to pat the back of hers. He shed the heaviness clinging to him, put on the mild smile of social occasions, and slipped into the gathering.
"Hongqiang's here! We were just waiting on you!" Old Xu called out with a laugh, while Boss Li was already on his feet pouring him a drink. The women smiled and exchanged pleasantries too. On the table the charcoal burned bright, pork belly sizzling on the grill, fat dripping and flaring into tiny flames that lit up the relaxed smiles on every face.
The three men were soon trading toasts. Old Xu lifted his cup and remarked with a meaning behind his words, "Hongqiang, that project of yours is really riding high lately, the prospects are excellent! Come, a toast to you — here's to a fresh start in the new year, and to climbing even higher!" Shuai Hongqiang thanked him over and over and drained his cup, knowing full well how much of that toast was friendship and how much was an expectation that the project would go smoothly. Boss Li raised his cup too, his words more down-to-earth: "Brother Qiang, next year on the construction side, don't you worry — I, Old Li, will do it up beautifully for you and never drop the ball! After all these years working together, it's the straight dealing that's carried us through!" Shuai Hongqiang clinked cups with him, a smile on his face, both their eyes holding the trust and unspoken understanding ground out over many years.
The adults traded toasts, talking business, current affairs, and their children's education, the mood lively and genuine — interests intertwined, but human warmth in it too. This was the social arena of grown-ups: complicated and solid.
On the other side, the three little boys — Old Xu's nine-year-old son Xu Hao, Boss Li's ten-year-old son Li Haoran, and Shuai Wenyao — had long since eaten their fill and, with the adults' tacit permission, run off to the lounge area by the window to play a card game they'd brought along.
"It's my turn to draw a card!" Shuai Wenyao reached out eagerly.
"Wait," Xu Hao said, pressing down on the deck, sounding like a little grown-up. "Wenyao, that card you just played broke the rules. It's supposed to be my turn." Though only a year older than Shuai Wenyao, he spoke with an unconscious certainty modeled on his father's.
Li Haoran looked at Xu Hao, then at Shuai Wenyao, and said nothing — he just quietly nudged a good card from his hand over toward Xu Hao. He was the oldest and the tallest, yet in their play he subtly deferred to Xu Hao as the leader. His father was the contractor, Xu Hao's father the representative of the "client side," and perhaps this delicate hierarchy, already picked up by the children from the way the adults spoke and behaved at the dinner table, had been internalized and was unconsciously playing out again in their own small world.
Shuai Wenyao blinked, looked at Xu Hao, then at the silent Li Haoran, pursed his lips — but pulled his hand back all the same. "Fine, you go first."
A card game among children, where winning or losing hardly mattered, and yet that brief moment of who got to "interpret the rules" and the small "concession" was like a single drop of water, reflecting a blurred, inverted image of the adult world's tangled web of relationships. Luckily, this little episode was soon swallowed up by the excitement of a cool new card someone drew, and the children were laughing and tumbling together again, that invisible barrier as if it had never existed. They really were good friends who got along, and that childhood friendship was just as real.
The dinner ended amid the noise and a pleasant buzz of wine. The three little boys had long been thinking about the main event still to come — setting off fireworks. The year before, the adults had promised to take them this New Year's night to the designated launch area by the Yellow River to enjoy themselves.
The three mothers drove out, the trunks stuffed full of every kind of firework they'd prepared, to the city-government-designated central launch zone by the Yellow River. It was a wide concrete embankment facing the rushing Yellow River, with an excellent view. By now it was packed with people: young couples leaning together with sparklers in hand, whole families of all ages looking up to watch, and most of all families like theirs, bringing half-grown children to experience the thrill of lighting fireworks themselves. Huge combination fireworks burst open with a roar in the night sky, scattering into dazzling rains of blossom; handheld sparklers traced lines of silver; the children's excited shrieks and the adults' laughter wove together with the distinctive smell of gunpowder smoke into a clamorous festival night scene.
The moment they arrived, Shuai Wenyao, Xu Hao, and Li Haoran were like colts let out of a pen. Amid their mothers' repeated calls of "Be careful!" "Don't point it at people!" "Hold it farther away!" they couldn't wait to start setting off their own, relatively safe fireworks. "Little Bees" buzzed and spun up into the sky, "electric flowers" hissed and burned in their hands, lighting up small faces flushed with excitement and joy. Lin Wan stood with Old Xu's and Boss Li's wives, chatting and smiling as they watched over the children.
Shuai Hongqiang, Old Xu, and Boss Li stood a little farther off by the embankment railing, a few "color-ball launchers" in their hands, firing them off casually at the black river surface, watching the colored balls of light shriek through the night and burst into brief flashes above the water before being swallowed by the darkness and the roar of the current. They chatted idly, savoring this rare, relaxed moment, their gazes drifting tenderly now and then to the children romping not far away.
No one noticed that in another knot of people not far off, a small boy about the same age as Shuai Wenyao and the others, in a blue down jacket, was staring longingly at the "handheld waterfall" Shuai Wenyao had just lit — it spewed a fountain of silver flame, quite a sight. Still less did anyone notice that this little boy had just been turned away by Shuai Wenyao and the others when he tried to cut in line to play with a large fountain firework at a public launch point, on the grounds that "you have to wait your turn." The boy had stomped off in a huff, muttering, "What's the big deal."
And right now, the blue-jacketed boy's father had his back to him, smoking and chatting with a few friends, a big plastic bag stuffed with fireworks at his feet. The boy's gaze flicked between his father's back and that plastic bag. Then he quietly crouched down and quickly fished out from the bag an adult-grade Gatling firework — a thick tube with multiple launch holes, shaped like a miniature rocket launcher. This kind of firework was powerful, with long range and a fierce, rapid discharge: a dangerous category expressly forbidden for children to set off alone.
The boy seemed to have no idea how dangerous the thing was — perhaps he'd only seen adults use them and thought it looked cool. Imitating the grown-ups, he glanced left and right, then pulled out a lighter and clumsily lit the fuse at the tail of the "Gatling."
"Hsss—"
The sharp hiss of the fuse burning fast was drowned out by the deafening firework blasts and the crowd's laughter all around.
The next second—
"Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang!"
A burst of dense, violent, machine-gun-sharp explosions cracked out all at once! Not toward the sky, but in a fan, firing level and flat!
Blinding white balls of light, searing to the eyes, wrapped in acrid gunpowder smoke and a visible wave of scorching heat, tore at astonishing speed straight into the backs of Shuai Wenyao, Xu Hao, and Li Haoran — who had their backs turned this way, crouched on the ground trying to light a new firework!
"AHHH—!!!"
The first to ring out was Boss Li's wife's short, piercing scream.
Time stretched and warped without limit in Shuai Hongqiang's eyes. He saw a ball of blinding white light explode where his son was crouched, accompanied by a dull thud of impact. Xu Hao and Li Haoran were yanked into a tackle by the quick-reacting Old Xu and Boss Li, rolling away to either side; the children's terrified cries and the adults' pained grunts rang out at once.
And the one at the very center of that fan of firepower — Shuai Wenyao—
That small figure in the red down jacket, like a little ball of fire, was shoved forward by some enormous unseen force, then crumpled limply, soundlessly, face-down to the ground. Across the back of the red down jacket, several charred black holes appeared in an instant, their edges still wisping faint blue smoke.
"Wenyao—!!!"
Lin Wan's scream cracked and broke. She rushed over like a madwoman, stumbling on the way, almost falling.
Shuai Hongqiang's mind went blank. Every sound in the world — the bursting fireworks, the crowd's cries, his wife's wails — all of it fell away in an instant, leaving only a piercing ringing in his ears and the wild pounding drum of his own heart. Almost on pure instinct, he barreled through every obstacle in his path and threw himself down beside that tiny red figure.
The overpowering, nauseating stench of burnt flesh, mixed with the smell of gunpowder, drove straight up his nose.
"Wenyao… Wenyao!" Lin Wan knelt beside him, both hands hovering in the air, shaking violently, wanting to touch her son but not knowing where she dared, tears bursting out like a breached dam, blurring her vision in an instant. "Baby, look at Mama… look at Mama, please!"
Shuai Hongqiang forced his scattered gaze to focus and looked at his son.
Shuai Wenyao lay face-down, his left cheek and ear pressed against the cold, rough cement. The right cheek that showed… Shuai Hongqiang's stomach convulsed violently.
It was no longer the smooth, rosy little face of his son that he knew. The right half of his face, from cheekbone to jaw, was a mess of blood and torn flesh. The skin was a horrifying mottle of charred black tangled with raw red, the flesh curled back in places to expose deeper tissue beneath, blood welling out from those ghastly wounds and rapidly staining the ground under his cheek. His right ear… the rim of it was scorched black and curled, the ear canal caked with dark red scabs and unidentifiable debris. Most shattering of all was his right eye, clamped shut, the lid swollen and black, a tear at the corner from which tear fluid laced with blood kept seeping, cutting a harrowing streak down the little face caked with black ash and gore.
And atop his head, through the thick black hair, there were clear marks of burning and bloodstains too.
The boy didn't move, gave no response to Lin Wan's heart-rending cries — only the faint, almost imperceptible rise and fall of his chest proved this little life hadn't been entirely snuffed out.
Shuai Hongqiang forced his crumbling composure steady and, with extreme care, used a trembling hand to first check his son's breath — faint as a thread. Then he felt for the carotid artery — a pulse, weak and fast.
Still alive! The realization split his fogged mind like a bolt of lightning.
"Hospital! Get to a hospital!!!" he screamed hoarsely, not knowing to whom. He tore off his own heavy down jacket, heedless of the cold, and with the utmost gentleness and care wrapped the inside of it as completely as he could around his son's bloodied head, neck, and upper body, avoiding the wounds and keeping him as warm as possible. Then, in a posture of near-reverence, summoning every ounce of his self-control, he steadily and firmly lifted that small body wrapped in the jacket — terrifyingly light and yet heavy as a mountain.
"The car! Our car's over there!" His eyes were bloodshot, veins bulging at his temples. He looked toward Lin Wan, who had all but collapsed and was holding herself up on sheer will alone, and his voice forced out through clenched teeth carried the taste of blood: "Lin Wan! Get up! Drive! To the Provincial People's Hospital! Hurry! Hurry, now!!!"
Lin Wan, on the brink of breaking down, was jolted awake by that scream into the most tenacious instinct of motherhood. Her scattered gaze snapped back into focus, landing on the small bundle her husband cradled so carefully — that little mound wrapped in the down jacket, still seeping its stark, vivid red. She forced down the surging despair, wiped away her tears, her eyes turning abruptly cold and sharp, staggered to her feet, and sprinted full-speed toward the parking area.
"Old Xu! Call the police! Hold that family! Don't let a single one of them get away!" Shuai Hongqiang shouted over his shoulder without turning, his son in his arms, as he sprinted toward the car.
"Don't worry! Leave it to me!" Old Xu already had his phone out, dialing 110, rapidly giving the location and the situation, while he and Boss Li planted themselves like iron towers in front of the blue-jacketed father and son — who were dumbstruck with terror and trying to slip away with the boy who'd caused the disaster. Boss Li barked at his own wife, white-faced with fright and clutching the crying Xu Hao and Li Haoran, "Get the kids in the car! Go home right now! Call me when you're there!" Then he glanced at Shuai Hongqiang and Lin Wan racing off and said urgently to Old Xu, "You handle things here! Keep an eye on them! I'm going with them to the hospital — they need the help right now!"
Two cars, against the backdrop of New Year's night's brilliant yet indifferent sea of lights and the fireworks still going off, shot out like two arrows of despair, ripping through the festive clamor, racing wildly and running several red lights, hurtling toward the Provincial People's Hospital as if for their very lives.
Shuai Hongqiang sat in the back seat holding his son while Lin Wan drove so fast the car all but flew, her knuckles on the wheel ghost-white from the force of her grip. With one hand Shuai Hongqiang pressed down hard on the jacket wrapped around his son; with the other, trembling, he fumbled out his phone and, in a hoarse, broken voice, called every leader and specialist at the Provincial People's Hospital he could think of who might be able to help.
"President Zhang! It's me, Shuai Hongqiang! Something's happened to my son! Blast injuries! Severe head trauma! We're on our way to your hospital! Please, get your best neurosurgeons, burn specialists, and eye doctors ready! Operate right away!"
"Director Liu! My son, Shuai Wenyao! Blast injuries! Open cranial trauma! His eye, his ear, both injured! Please, get to the hospital right now! I'll be there any minute!"
"President Wang…"
One call after another, his words incoherent, his voice choking, repeating "blast injuries," "severe head trauma," "he's slipping away." He set aside all his pride and dignity for the sake of buying back even one more second of life for his son. He could feel the small body in his arms — so light, so soft, the breath of life so faint, as if it might slip through his fingers at any moment. A terror unlike any he had ever known flooded him from head to toe like an icy tide, all but suffocating him.
