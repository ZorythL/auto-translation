A circle isn't something you wait for, it's something you fight your way into; tenderness isn't weakness, it's a weapon.

Eleven o'clock sharp.

Yao Yuan started the livestream right on time.

【Livestream Status】

Bright, full early-summer morning light poured in through the entire wall of floor-to-ceiling windows, mingling and colliding with the cold white beams of the ring lights to form a clear, intensely modern field of brightness. Outside the windows lay Jinshi's lush summer greenery and a clean, open sky; the glass curtain walls of the surrounding buildings sliced the blue sky into geometric blocks that gave back a crystalline shine.

Yao Yuan sat composed within this play of light and shadow. Her dress of deep peach-pink silk and the crisp, eye-catching "Tiffany blue" polish on the fingers resting on her black mouse formed a clever, bold visual clash under the sunlight and the lamps. It lit up the whole frame in an instant — striking and commanding, yet carrying a lively, playful chic.

The scrolling comments rolled past quickly with the morning's topic tags: #HowToGetOutOfTheSingleZoneWhenYourCircleIsTooNarrow# #CantProvideEmotionalValue# #WednesdayWakeUpCall#.

Yao Yuan glanced at the steadily climbing viewer count in the top right and the comments flying past, her expression calm, carrying the particular professionalism and focus of someone who has slipped into work mode. She adjusted her headset, her sky-blue fingertips tapping unconsciously on the smooth desktop like fingers striking the keys of a soundless piano, and spoke, her voice clear and steady:

"Good morning, everyone. Welcome to my livestream. Let's get right to it and take our first call."

【Call Connected】

Caller (female voice, 25, clear but plainly lost and dejected): "Hello, Teacher Yuanyuan. I've followed you for a long time, and today I wanted to ask about something that's probably very common, but that I genuinely can't solve on my own… I'm 25 this year, I have a stable job, and my looks and figure are above average, I'd say. But I really don't know how to date, how to get married."

She sighed, the breath carrying real helplessness through the microphone: "Right out of school I went into admin at a state-owned enterprise. My coworkers are basically all women, or older uncles and aunties. Day to day it's just home and office, and on weekends, apart from shopping and movies with the same set of close girlfriends, I almost never meet anyone new and suitable of the opposite sex. My social circle is incredibly narrow. It feels like being trapped under a transparent dome."

"A few months ago, a relative kindly introduced me to a guy — pretty outstanding among people our age, runs his own business, good circumstances. We tried dating for less than three months. He was very generous with me — he'd give expensive gifts on holidays, and never let me pay when we ate out. But… our personalities are too alike, both stubborn. Once we argued over something small, where to go on the weekend, and neither of us would back down first. We gave each other the cold shoulder for days. In the end he messaged first, but it wasn't an apology — he said he felt he wasn't getting the 'emotional value' he wanted out of the relationship, that it was too exhausting, and then… he broke it off, cold and flat."

Her voice dropped, carrying both reluctance and a deeper confusion: "Teacher Yuanyuan, I really do want a serious relationship, but right now I don't know where to meet new, reliable men, and I don't know how to manage a relationship either. With a personality like mine, am I just not cut out for dating? I can't even find a chance to start."

【The scrolling comments came alive, flooding with people who related:

[Sis, you're literally me! I'm at a state enterprise too, my circle is cleaner than my face!]

[Two stubborn mules together really is hopeless… they just torture each other.]

[Are guys all this practical? Generous with gifts so they demand emotional value?]

["Emotional value" is almost a worn-out phrase by now, but it really does seem to be a sore spot.]

[Where do you even meet people? Same question! Feels like the moment you leave campus you become an isolated island.]】

Yao Yuan (after listening, she didn't comment right away. She leaned back gracefully, the delicate collarbones beneath the peach-pink off-shoulder neckline half-hidden, a faint, knowing half-smile curving her lips):

"Twenty-five, stable job, above-average looks, a short-term partner who was generous in material terms but clearly wanted emotional value — and then you broke up because 'you're both stubborn.'" She recapped, her pace even, as if sorting through a case file. "Your problem, broken open, is two separate layers. First, a channel problem — in your fish pond, either there are no fish, or the fish that swim in aren't the ones you want. Second, a problem of angling and rearing technique — a fish finally swam over, and you didn't land it, or you used the wrong methods and either drove the fish off in a huff or let it starve away."

【Comments: [The fish-pond theory is vulgar but the logic's solid!][Hits the nail on the head!]】

"Let's deal with the second one first — the technique problem." Yao Yuan leaned slightly forward, her gaze turning sharp, like a scalpel. "You said you're both stubborn, that when conflict comes up neither of you backs down, each waiting for the other to come smooth things over. Then the guy says he 'isn't getting emotional value.' Girl, sit with that — really sit with that sentence. A man who, early in the relationship, was already willing to be generous in material terms, who seemingly held a degree of the upper hand — why would he blurt out a relatively introspective complaint like 'I'm not getting emotional value'?"

She didn't wait for an answer, asking and answering herself, her tone calm and certain: "Because he was using the most direct, most 'weighty' method within his frame of understanding — material giving — to express that he valued you and was sincere, and he didn't feel any 'glad acceptance' or 'emotional reciprocation' from you toward that giving. Your 'stubbornness,' which to you might be standing firm in who you are, in his eyes, in that situation, very likely read as resistance, ingratitude, even cold confrontation. When he gave you a gift, what he subconsciously hoped to see was probably not your taking it as your due, but the smile it brought to your face, a warmer atmosphere between you, and the more intimate connection that grew out of that — not, at the next argument, the same stone-cold face that wouldn't yield an inch. His demand for 'emotional value,' translated into plain words, is: I hope my giving makes us more comfortable together, sweeter, not more exhausted and more rigid. He was looking for positive emotional feedback and a return on his investment, and he didn't find it."

【Some in the comments had a dawning realization:

[So that's what emotional value means! It's not just being good at talking.]

[Sounds like it makes sense — taking an expensive gift and then sulking through a cold war all day really is a turn-off.]

[But can't a girl have her own temper and principles? Why should she have to coax him?]】

"You can have a temper. You must have principles." Yao Yuan's voice was clear and forceful, carrying an affirmation that brooked no argument. "But it depends on timing, on the person, and even more on the means and wisdom you use to reach your goal. In relationships between men and women, especially when the other side has already shown sincerity about the long term and concrete material giving, a woman's smartest, most effortless strategy is usually not to butt heads in a contest of who's more stubborn, but to use softness to overcome hardness, skill to break force. This isn't pandering, it isn't losing yourself — it's using the smallest cost, based on a deep understanding of human nature, to resolve the biggest conflict and to bring harmony and progress to the relationship."

She paused, her eyes briefly lost in memory, then launched into a story full of that signature "Yao Yuan" flavor:

"Many years ago, when I'd just started my business — running a high-end private club — there was a time I brought along a young assistant fresh out of school to meet a client. Over the meal, the little assistant, wanting to talk me up in front of the guests, and maybe wanting to show off too, said in a deliberately exaggerated tone: 'Our Boss Yao is really something. Her ex tried to give her a place worth tens of millions by the Bund as a breakup-win-back fee, and she sent it back without a second glance. She just wanted the breakup, not the apartment.'"

"One of the guests at the table — a man of considerable status and weight — heard this and said to me, half-joking, half-serious: 'Boss Yao, this isn't right of you. Such a good man, such a heavy gift, and you turn it down just like that — that's not appreciating what you have. A woman like you is bound to meet a man one day who doesn't know how to cherish you, and you'll get hurt.'"

"That remark," Yao Yuan looked into the camera, her eyes clear and bright, "actually had a barb in it — that typical, condescending male gaze passing judgment, even a faint 'you'll get your comeuppance sooner or later' lecturing tone." She seemed to ask the girl on the call, and all her listeners too: "If it were you — fresh out of a not-so-pleasant relationship, hearing a fairly important business contact or elder appraise you that way — how would you react? Would you feel wronged and snap back on the spot? Or sit there in awkward silence, refusing to accept it inside but with no idea how to respond?"

【The comments guessed: [Probably annoyed but wouldn't dare say anything?][Feels like it just hit a sore spot?]】

"In that moment, I didn't push back at all." Yao Yuan smiled, and in that smile was a cunning, unhurried insight into the ways of the world, and absolute control over her own emotions. "I just raised my head, looked at the man with the most innocent, the clearest, even slightly bewildered eyes, and said, soft and gentle, with just the right touch of confusion and coquettish pout: 'How could that be? I think… no one could bear to hurt me…' I paused deliberately for half a second, and in the instant he was caught off guard, only then added the last half: '…unless that person were you.'"

【The comments exploded at once, boiling over:

[Holy crap! Brilliant! Absolutely brilliant!]

[Who could withstand that?! Anger turned to a racing heart in one second!]

[Retreat as advance, turn guest into host, with a touch of flirtation thrown in!]

[Learned something!! The point isn't the words, it's the eyes and the tone! Yao Yuan's a real actress!]】

"And then?" Yao Yuan asked and answered herself, her tone easy. "That man clearly froze for a moment, then caught on, and not only did that faint trace of lecturing vanish into thin air — he burst out laughing instead, saying over and over, 'Boss Yao's something else.' Afterward, far from falling out, we became good friends, and he introduced me to plenty of valuable resources. With one line that looked like showing weakness, with a hint of playful charm, I easily dissolved a possible confrontation, even turned the situation around, and won respect and future opportunities."

She put away the playful look and grew serious: "That is the power of using softness to overcome hardness. It's not that you're actually weak — it's that you know how, at the right moment, to use a woman's innate warmth and air of softness to dissolve conflict, to guide the other person's emotions, and to reach your core goal. This is especially useful in intimate relationships. When the two of you are at a standstill, swords drawn, a single coquettish 'come on, give in to me a little,' a wronged, pitiable look that melts the heart, can be a hundred times more effective than three days of cold war or a red-faced screaming match. Men need a sense of accomplishment, need the feeling of being needed and admired. It's something written into a lot of people's genes. Your occasional, well-judged 'softness' and 'showing weakness' is precisely what gives him that emotional value of being needed and relied on, which makes him more willing to give to you, more proactive in working for the harmony of the relationship."

【The comments split between doubt and agreement, the debate fierce:

[Sounds exhausting — you have to act just to date? You have to scheme?]

[Isn't this just calculation? Shouldn't feelings be sincere?]

[But honestly, most straight guys really do fall for this… it's human nature.]

[Managing a relationship wisely so both people are comfortable — that's not acting, is it? Do you have to hurt each other for it to count as sincere?]】

"Think this is 'acting'? 'Calculation'?" Yao Yuan raised an eyebrow slightly, the arch carrying a trace of cold wisdom. "Then let me give a 'grander' example. When Wu Zetian had just come to power, across the whole court, how many noble-house ministers fought to the death, fiercely opposing the idea of a woman 'crowing like a hen at dawn,' standing above the realm. So after court let out, what did Wu Zetian do? She changed into the plainest clothes, brought no retinue or ceremonial guard, and walked on foot herself to the homes of those few chancellors and ministers who opposed her most fiercely — and wept before them. She wept over the trembling, walking-on-thin-ice fear of a lone woman, over how crushingly heavy was the realm the late emperor had entrusted to her, over the difficulty and loneliness of governing a country. She used the most traditional tears and shows of weakness, those belonging to a 'frail woman,' and successfully stirred, in the depths of those iron-blooded statesmen and great Confucian scholars, a natural pity and protective instinct toward women. And the result? Quite a few of them became, from then on, her most steadfast, most capable supporters, steadying the court for her in those early days."

"A truth that top statesmen and emperors well versed in the arts of rule all understood," Yao Yuan concluded, her voice carrying a calm that pierced through history. "When the balance of power is hugely lopsided, or when a stalemate where both sides lose has already become inevitable, butting heads blindly is often the stupidest choice. Read the situation, show weakness at the right time, reveal the 'soft' side, and thereby rouse a stronger sense of responsibility and protectiveness in the other person, turning resistance into help, achieving your strategic goal. In the micro-world of feelings, this shares the same underlying logic as Wu Zetian facing her whole court — using the cleverest leverage to resolve the hardest conflict, steering the relationship in a direction favorable to you. This isn't hypocrisy; it's the wisdom of survival and growth."

【The comments fell into deep thought, the rhythm slowing:

[I actually can't argue with this…]

[That Wu Zetian example really broadened my perspective!]

[So it's not that you can't be tough — you need toughness and softness together, and proportion and timing are the keys.]

[Feels like a door to a new world just opened… dating is a game of interpersonal strategy too.]】

"All right, that's the inner method on the technical level for now. Let's come back to the first one — the channel problem. How does your fish pond get fish, and good fish?" Yao Yuan pulled the topic crisply back to reality.

"You said your social circle is narrow, that apart from work it's just those few close girlfriends. This isn't solely a problem of your environment — to a large degree it's a problem with your social initiative and the depth of your reach." She cut straight to the point, sparing no one. "This is the mobile internet age. Geography and profession stopped being the iron walls that trap you long ago. You have ten thousand ways to actively break through them."

"First, break the ice online, take the initiative to show yourself. All sorts of relatively reliable social apps, professional forums in vertical fields, interest-based Douban groups, even the quality communities on knowledge-payment platforms — these are all places where you can 'cast your net.' The key is, don't just be a submarine. Speak up. Share your real interests and hobbies, your insightful opinions, even the things you're confused about. Start with high-quality text or voice exchanges online, and gradually filter out the people whose values align with yours, who you click with, who make you feel comfortable. This is the lowest-cost first round of screening."

"Second, convert it offline, bravely break out of your circle. Many of the forums and communities you follow regularly organize offline themed gatherings — book-sharing sessions, film discussions, citywide hikes, board games and murder-mystery games, wine tastings, craft workshops… Even if you don't know a single person there, go anyway — bravely. Put yourself together, look presentable and bright, and bring an open, friendly attitude. Go once, and you'll very likely meet five to ten new friends from all different backgrounds and industries. Their perspectives and experiences may open a whole new world for you."

"Third — and this is the fastest, most effective method — find and 'lock onto' that natural 'social hub' type of friend around you." When Yao Yuan reached this point, her tone took on a faint amusement, as if sharing a secret that never failed. "Almost everyone's social network has one or two of those people who are naturally warm, love to organize things, have friends all over, and seem to know everyone. They're the key nodes in the interpersonal web, the junction where information and resources meet. As it happens, I myself, in most of the circles I belong to, often play exactly this role."

Her eyes were bright, carrying a frank confidence and a generous gladness to share: "I enjoy meeting interesting new people, and I'm genuinely happy to introduce my friends to one another and connect them. To me, sharing my social resources — letting the right people meet, spark off each other, even create greater value — is in itself something full of fun, accomplishment, and an energy return. If you have a friend like this around you, take part sincerely in the gatherings she organizes, and put your heart into talking with her and her friends. Very soon, your social network will expand like a rolling snowball, exponentially — a friend introduces you to their friend, and the friend's friend brings their own circle… After one round of it, the industry information, the ways of thinking, the life possibilities you come into contact with will be richer than you can imagine."

【The comments were full of envy and agreement:

[Where do I get a divine friend like this!]

[Yao Yuan herself has to be a top-tier social hub! So envious!]

[Honestly — the turning point of my life was meeting a close friend like this, who brought me into the circle I'm in now.]

[At the end of the day you still have to take the initiative yourself, and you have to provide value, not just be a taker.]】

"So, to sum up." Yao Yuan sat up straight and gave her clear, actionable final advice. "Inwardly, cultivate the wisdom of 'softness.' Learn to provide 'emotional value' in intimate relationships, use clever leverage to dissolve conflict, and bring the relationship into a virtuous cycle of mutual nourishment. Outwardly, take the initiative to widen your social boundaries, combine online and offline, and be good at borrowing the strength of 'social hub' friends to open up the situation quickly and build your network."

"Don't let yourself stay trapped long-term in the self-pity and complaints of 'my circle is so narrow,' 'my personality is so stubborn,' 'no one understands me.' Channels are found and fought for by yourself; relationships are managed and maintained with wisdom. When you first become a more open, wiser woman who better understands connection and value, that 'right person' will naturally cross paths with you, or be drawn to you, in a far wider world."

【Call time up; the feed cut off cleanly and decisively.】

The calm, unruffled professional look returned to Yao Yuan's face. She looked into the camera, her tone steady: "A circle isn't something you wait for, it's something you fight your way into. Tenderness isn't weakness, it's a weapon. All right, let's take the next caller."

After a moment of digesting and being stunned, the livestream's comments were once again flooded madly with 【So clear!】【Got the practical guide!】【Going to make plans with my super-social bestie right now!】. And that 25-year-old caller's predicament might not suddenly turn bright from this one speech, but at the very least, with her sharp, profound insight and vivid, living examples, Yao Yuan had cut open a visible path for her and pointed out a set of inner methods she could practice again and again.
