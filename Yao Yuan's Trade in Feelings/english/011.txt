Chapter 11 — Zhang Fengxia's Love Is a Tenderness With Barbs
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640258.html

Yao Yuan watched, half-amused, as her mother's face turned suddenly indignant at the mention of the past. "All right, all right, stop being angry. It's all behind us now. Don't let unimportant people get to you."
Zhang Fengxia nodded and said no more. She didn't really understand the specifics of how her daughter's company ran—only that it made money, and that was enough. The way she saw it, a woman who could stand on her own two feet beat anything else.
"Your Uncle Zhou said," Zhang Fengxia began suddenly, her tone offhand, though Yao Yuan could hear the probe in it, "that there was someone else at lunch today?"
Here it comes. Yao Yuan's chest tightened, though her face stayed calm. "Mm. A friend."
"What kind of friend?" Zhang Fengxia fixed her with a stare, those eyes still sharp.
Yao Yuan knew there was no hiding it, and she had no intention of hiding it. "Just a close male friend. We have a few business ventures together."
The air in the living room seemed to freeze for a moment. Zhang Fengxia set down the orange in her hand and leaned slightly forward. "What's his name? How old? What does he do?"
"Yu Hao. He's in investments. A few years younger than me." Yao Yuan answered each question, her voice steady.
"What's his family like?"
"Both parents are abroad. He has a sister who's abroad too."
"How long have you known each other?"
"A little over a year."
The questions came out one after another like bullets, each one taking precise aim at the most essential parts of reality. This was Zhang Fengxia—straight to the point, always. She didn't care for roundabout things, and she couldn't do them anyway.
Zhang Fengxia settled back against the sofa and studied her daughter—from the calm of her face, to the straightness of her spine, to those hands resting on her knees, drawn slightly tight.
"Yao Yuan," Zhang Fengxia's voice dropped, "you're thirty-six this year."
"I know." Yao Yuan met her mother's gaze.
"Then you should also remember—the year you turned twenty-two, fresh out of school, you told me you'd fallen in love, that in a couple of years, once work settled down, you'd get married." Zhang Fengxia's tone was very calm, calm in a way that made the heart clench. "You said that man understood you, loved you, that the two of you were soulmates. And then? Two years later you threw away your job in Jingshi without a second thought and came home crying. Such a bright future in Jingshi—given up for one man. If back then you'd chosen to stay in Jingshi and seized that chance to study abroad, you might have done just as well as you have now, and you wouldn't have had to put up with all that gossip."
And then? And then, when it came down to interests, I was the one they threw away. Yao Yuan finished the thought silently, her nails digging into her palm.
"Mom," she said, her voice steadier than she'd expected, "I'm thirty-six, not twenty-four. Yu Hao and I aren't in a relationship headed toward marriage."
"Then what kind of relationship is it?" Zhang Fengxia pressed. "Tell me. What kind of relationship leaves a change of clothes at my daughter's place?"
Yao Yuan fell silent. How was she supposed to describe Yu Hao? Say he was steady and reliable? That he could see the real her? That they'd met on that chaotic night and yet fit together so strangely well? That between them was an understanding and a rapport beyond love? That they were an unconventional but solid enough pair of "strategic partners" and "bed buddies"?
In the end, all she said was: "He makes me feel safe."
Zhang Fengxia stared at her daughter for a long, long while—long enough for the clock on the wall to tick through a full five minutes. Then she stood up. "I'll boil some water, make some tea."
That was the signal that the conversation had been put on pause. Yao Yuan let out a breath. Not knowing how to explain things to her mother, this unresolved, hanging feeling left her even more on edge.
From the tea table came the sound of water heating, along with the clink of a tea canister opening and closing. Yao Yuan walked out onto the balcony and looked at the few pots of pothos. The leaves were a glossy deep green, thriving, the vines trailing down and swaying gently in the afternoon breeze. She remembered her mother had brought these pothos from the old apartment; she'd been growing them for over a decade.
"Your father, back then," Zhang Fengxia's voice came suddenly from behind her. Yao Yuan turned and saw her mother standing in the balcony doorway with a tea tray. "Said the very same thing to me."
Yao Yuan froze.
Zhang Fengxia came over and set the tray on the little round table on the balcony. It was excellent jasmine tea, steam curling up from it, carrying a familiar fragrance.
"He said, Fengxia, you make me feel safe." Zhang Fengxia sat down in the rattan chair and motioned for her daughter to sit too. "I was only twenty then, working at the textile mill, and he drove for the transport crew. Other people set me up with matches, all of them better off than him, but I just saw that he was a solid man, someone you could count on."
Yao Yuan slowly sat down. This was the first time her mother had ever brought up her father unprompted. In her memory, her father was a forbidden subject—her mother never spoke of him on her own, and she had never dared to ask.
"Later he passed," Zhang Fengxia lifted her teacup and blew on the steam, "and only then did I learn that the least reliable thing in this world is those two words: 'feel safe.' Gone just like that, leaving you alone with a seven-year-old child. What were you supposed to do?"
Her voice was very flat, as if she were talking about someone else's affairs, but Yao Yuan saw that the hand holding the cup was trembling slightly.
"Mom…" Yao Yuan wanted to say something, but didn't know where to begin.
"The year you turned eleven," Zhang Fengxia cut her off, raising her eyes to look out the window, "I married your Uncle Zhou—half of it was for you. Dance lessons burned through money. My little salary couldn't cover it. Lufei was your father's best friend in life. A solid man. He brought it up himself. He said, Old Yao's child is my child."
Yao Yuan's throat tightened. She had always known that part of the reason her mother married Uncle Zhou was so she could keep dancing. But hearing her mother say it out loud for the first time felt completely different.
"I know you resent me," Zhang Fengxia took a sip of tea, her gaze still fixed on the distance, "resent that I remarried so quickly, resent that I later had Xiaofan. You think I forgot your father, that I wronged him."
"I don't…" Yao Yuan wanted to argue, but her mother raised a hand to stop her.
"You do. I know." Zhang Fengxia turned her head to look at her daughter, the rims of her eyes faintly red. "The way you looked at me back then—I remember all of it. But Yao Yuan, your mom is a person too. Your mom gets tired too, gets scared too. The year your father passed, I worked at the factory by day and took in sewing at night, sleeping less than four hours a day. Once I nearly toppled off the sewing machine, and right then I thought—if I went too, what would happen to you?"
Tears welled up without warning. Yao Yuan fought desperately to hold them back, her fingertips driving deep into her palm.
"Marrying Lufei was the most selfish decision I made in my life, and also the one I regret the least." Zhang Fengxia's voice was very soft, yet every word landed hard on Yao Yuan's heart. "Selfish, because I really did want someone to lean on. No regrets, because he let you keep dancing, let the two of us go on living—living like proper human beings."
She set down her cup, the ceramic base clinking crisply against the glass tabletop.
"Later, when you got into Beijing Dance Academy, I was so happy I couldn't sleep all night. But then you went off to Jingshi, lived on campus, came home twice a year, and every time you came back, I felt like you were drifting further and further from me." Zhang Fengxia gave a bitter little laugh. "I know—you found me crude, found me uneducated, found me too blunt, no good at all those fancy words. But Yao Yuan, your mom is just this kind of person. I grew up rolling in the mud, then scraped by in a hotpot shop. I can't learn gentleness, can't learn how to be considerate. I can only be good to you my own way."
"Mom, I never found you crude…" Yao Yuan's voice broke. "I remember it so clearly," she went on, her voice a little unmoored. "The year I got into Beijing Dance Academy, the tuition was steep, and you sold the gold bracelet Grandma left you. The day I left for Jingshi, you and Uncle Zhou saw me off onto the train, and when the train started moving, I saw you wiping your eyes on the platform—that was the first time, and the only time, I ever saw you cry."
"You did. It's all right, I don't blame you." Zhang Fengxia waved a hand. "The winter you were twenty-four, you came back from Jingshi looking like you'd lost your soul. It broke my heart, but it made me even angrier—angry that you wouldn't pull yourself together, angry that you'd let a man wreck you like that. So I scolded you, said you'd let down everything I'd given, said I'd raised you for nothing. I said those things because I was afraid you'd never get back on your feet, afraid you'd just throw yourself away."
She paused and drew a deep breath. "But do you know—every night when you cried in your room, I sat right outside your door and cried too. I was afraid you'd hear me, didn't dare make a sound, so I bit down on my sleeve and cried. I, Zhang Fengxia, have never been afraid of anything in my life, but in that stretch of time I really was afraid—afraid my daughter would never be able to smile again."
"Mom…" Yao Yuan's voice caught, but she held it back. "I know you love me—in your own way, loving me with everything you have. But sometimes your love is too heavy, so heavy I can't breathe. When I was twenty-four, heartbroken, and transferred my job back to Jinshi, you scolded me for being useless, said I'd let down everything you'd given. Yes, I disappointed you. But back then I also needed someone to tell me it was okay to have my heart broken, okay to be dumped, that I'd still meet someone better, that the road was still long… and that there were other roads too." Yao Yuan could hold back no longer, and the tears spilled over.
"Later you got better, went into business, started a company, did better and better." Zhang Fengxia wiped the corners of her eyes with her sleeve, the motion coarse and clumsy. "I was glad, but I was worried too. Afraid you'd run into another bad person, afraid you'd get hurt again. Yao Yuan, your mom's getting old. I can't protect you for the rest of your life."
She reached out and took her daughter's hand. That hand was rough, dry, covered in calluses—yet so warm it made the heart tremble.
"Just now you said that this Yu Hao makes you feel safe." Zhang Fengxia looked at her daughter, her eyes softer than they'd ever been. "Then bring him home, let your mom take a look at him. I might not be a good judge of people in this life, but I've got to vet him for you—got to know whether he can really make you feel safe."
Yao Yuan turned her hand over to clasp her mother's, and said gently, "Mom, Yu Hao and I… we're just keeping company like this. We don't plan on registering a marriage." She paused, then decided to be completely open with Zhang Fengxia. "I'm telling you the truth up front, so you and Uncle Zhou don't put too many hopes on him."
"What do you mean, 'just keeping company like this'?" Zhang Fengxia's eyes went wide, then narrowed again. "Neither of you is young anymore, and you're a few years older than him on top of that. Not getting married—what kind of thing is that? It's all well and good now, but in a few more years you'll be forty, and if you split up… A man in his forties or fifties can still find someone younger, can still have kids. It's different for a woman. Older, and you're hard to match, and having children gets dangerous too…"
"It's just… having each other for company, but without being tied together."
Zhang Fengxia was silent for a while, then suddenly let go of her hand and stood up. She had returned to that usual stiff expression of hers, but her eyes weren't quite so tense anymore.
"Fine, I get it." She pursed her lips. "It's just that thing you young people call being 'buddies' these days, isn't it… Your mom goes online too, you know. I'm not some old fossil." She shot Yao Yuan an exasperated look. "Watching all those men and women in your livestream, all those marriages of every shape, all those strange and wondrous relationships, I've come around too. What does it matter whether you marry or not? Money in hand, a life lived in peace—that's what's real."
Yao Yuan was taken aback. She'd braced herself for a long explanation, even an argument, and never imagined her mother would accept it just like that, so plainly and quietly. The string that had been pulled tight inside her suddenly went slack, and her eyes welled up again.
"What's there to cry about—thirty-six years old, how embarrassing." Zhang Fengxia turned her head and headed for the kitchen, her voice carrying back from over there. "I'm going to make dinner. What do you want to eat?"
"Anything's fine." Yao Yuan wiped the corners of her eyes, but she was smiling.
"Braised pork, then—your favorite when you were little." As Zhang Fengxia said it, her footsteps paused at the kitchen doorway. "Don't just sit out here. Go to the study and keep your Uncle Zhou company, have a chat with him."
Soon the sounds of washing and chopping drifted from the kitchen, a clatter of pots and knives, full of the warm, lively bustle of everyday life.
Yao Yuan sat on the balcony, watching the sun slowly sinking in the west, and somewhere inside her that had been pulled taut for so long suddenly let go.
Yao Yuan leaned back in the rattan chair and closed her eyes. The last of the sunset shone through the glass onto her face, warm. She suddenly thought of many years ago—an evening just like this one, when she'd been practicing dance at home, her mother cooking in the kitchen, her father not yet back from work. The girl she'd been then didn't yet know that some stability is so brief, that some partings are so sudden.
But now, on this winter afternoon, in the rhythm of her mother chopping vegetables, she seemed to touch once more a long-missed warmth that belonged to the word "home."
This home wasn't perfect, and there was still so much left unsaid between her and her mother, so many knots untied—but at least in this moment, they were trying to draw closer.
That was enough, Yao Yuan thought. For a stubborn pair of mother and daughter like them, just being able to sit together like this, drink a cup of tea, and say a few honest words was already great progress.
From the kitchen drifted the fragrance of scallion and ginger sizzling in the pan—the smell of home.
Dinner was a feast. The braised pork was glossy and meltingly tender, the rich dark-soy-and-oil dish of memory, simmered to just the right point; the steamed fish was laid with fine threads of scallion and ginger, its soy dressing balanced fresh and sweet; a plate of stir-fried greens was jade-bright and crisp, and there was also Uncle Zhou's specialty yam-and-pork-rib soup, stewed to a milky white, its steam rising thick. At the table, Zhang Fengxia didn't bring up the earlier subject at all, only kept piling pork into Yao Yuan's bowl, muttering, "Dieting should be done in moderation—eat more." Uncle Zhou smoothed things over with a chuckle, asking Yao Yuan harmless little questions about her company. The atmosphere had a deliberately maintained yet reassuring ordinariness, as if that earlier baring of hearts had been nothing more than a wisp of steam rising from the soup pot and dissolving away.
Yao Yuan ate a lot, her stomach full and warm. It was a long-missed feeling, of being steadily caught by the most ordinary home-cooked food.
After dinner, she kept Zhang Fengxia company washing dishes in the kitchen, helping to slowly wipe down the stove with a cloth, glancing at her now and then. Under the kitchen's amber light, the line of her mother's profile seemed a little softer than usual.
With everything tidied up, Yao Yuan picked up her bag. "Mom, Uncle Zhou, I'm heading back."
"Mm. Has the car you called arrived?" Zhang Fengxia knew she hadn't driven today. "Send a message when you get there."
"Okay."
Uncle Zhou saw her to the door. "Come home for dinner often."
Night had grown thick, the evening wind carrying a chill. Yao Yuan got into the car. The city's neon lights came on one after another, the traffic weaving like threads. The cabin was quiet, with only the low hum of the engine. The warmth in her stomach was still there, but the taut, battle-ready strength in her heart had quietly dispersed, turning into a trace of weariness, and more than that, into a calm and settled peace. Her mother's last words, and that solid, hearty meal, were like a weighty ballast stone, finally steadying a heart that had drifted for so long.
Back at her apartment in the city center, she switched on the lights to a cold, empty room. But that chill, for now, didn't seem so hard to face. She poured herself a glass of water and stood by the window, looking down at the dazzling, soundless river of cars below.
This day had been like one long emotional trek. Arriving at its end now, weary as she was, the scenery had already changed.
