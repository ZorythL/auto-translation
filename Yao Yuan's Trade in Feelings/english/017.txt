Weekend rest is always short. When Monday's dawn cracks the night sky, countless "beasts of burden" are swept up against their will by the surging crowd, carried along, and thrown back into the grind of every trade and profession—like cogs in a vast, precise social machine, turning round and round, a millstone that never stops.

In the gaps between the busyness, some people might pause for a moment, a flicker of confusion rising in their hearts: why work so hard? What, exactly, is all this toil for? Is it only so you can scrape together a little money and, in some brief vacation, rush off to a scenic spot that capital carefully built for you, leave snowy-mountain and beach photos on your social feed, splurge freely, and chase a fleeting taste of pleasure? Trading long stretches of labor for a flash of relaxation and joy, then crawling back to your post to grind again—just to save up enough capital for the next splurge?

For others, work means things like the satisfaction of personal achievement, rising above mediocrity, the pursuit of excellence—or earning social recognition and realizing social value, though the measuring stick for that value is held by an invisible hand. Aristotle held that man is a political animal, born to take part in social life; so people, willing or not, labor away in the city's cubicles or out in the fields, never stopping.

More people are simply breathing on repeat, drifting through life. They have no time at all to ponder the meaning of work, because from the moment they're born, their parents say, "Study hard and find a good job," and their teachers say, "Be a useful member of society"—all passing down the same idea: when you grow up you should work hard, and only by working hard can you live a better life. But what does this so-called "better life" actually look like? The parents never spelled it out, and school never cleared it up. The "better life" is like the carrot dangled in front of the donkey: always one step ahead, always fresh and tempting, never defined. We learned how to run, but no one taught us to question the direction we were running in.

Standing on the thirty-eighth floor, she watched the crowds below, dense and teeming, rushing along the gullies of the streets, pouring into subway gates, vanishing into the glass grid. In the distance, the Yellow River rolled on as it had for thousands of years, surging without pause, gleaming dull gold in the sunlight—tireless, indifferent to meaning, simply flowing.

Yao Yuan couldn't help reflecting on what, after all these years of hard struggle, she had really been chasing.

There was no doubt that for her, the hunger for more and better—for the most lavish material life—had been planted deep in her heart since childhood. After all, growing up in an art world saturated with fame and vanity, the pursuit of material things had become a kind of instinct. She had outrun the childhood version of herself who once stood at the edge of the art world's fame-and-fortune circus, peering in. Mansions, luxury cars, the freedom of top-tier luxury goods—she had chased down by her own effort the wealth she'd craved since she was small, and that achievement made her proud.

But where would her road go from here? Apart from more money, what else did she want? What should she want? She had no answer.

Her livestream business looked to be at its peak now, blazing hot. But the company's blueprints, the team's expectations, the tide of the industry—all of them, like invisible yet powerful forces, were shoving her, no argument allowed, toward the "next milestone": building her own AI emotional model. This journey that looked so much like initiative, like forward drive—wasn't it just another, more hidden, harder-to-escape form of being swept along?

Heidegger said, "Man is thrown into the world." Yao Yuan felt that she had been thrown, from childhood, into the hunger and scramble for fame and fortune. After a long, hard run, she seemed to have reached the summit. But standing at this seemingly glorious height, she couldn't help asking herself: what, in the end, lies at the finish line of this chase?

"Yuan-jie, the studio's all set up. About twenty more minutes till eleven—we go live right on time, okay?" The crisp reminder from her young assistant cut through Yao Yuan's drifting thoughts.

Right. Living in the present is the truest reality there is. The matters waiting to be handled were piled up like a mountain; she couldn't afford any more time to brood over an unknown future. They say pessimists are right and optimists move forward—she gathered her wits and gently pressed those scattered thoughts back down into her heart. The lights were set, the camera ready. A livestream was about to begin.

Monday, eleven in the morning. The Beauty's Worry-Cure Shop livestream opened right on time.

Behind the host's chair, the backdrop was still that vast, silent Yellow River. It flowed on, unchanging since the dawn of time, its water murky and heavy, weighed down by countless grains of sand in shapes too blurred to make out. The corner of wall the camera could catch still held that same gold-framed "Energy Pyramid" diagram.

"This view—I picked it on purpose."

Yao Yuan slowly extended her hand, showing the fans behind the camera the window and the river behind her. That hand seemed to carry its own spotlight, instantly seizing everyone's attention. Her fingers were long and slender, each fingertip like a finely carved work of art—the dramatic long nails like ornate miniature sculptures. The nails extended elegantly past her fingertips, far longer than most people would think possible, lending her fingers a distinct edge and allure.

That vivid red polish was like a blazing flame, flamboyant and fierce. Pure red, dazzling red, like the deep luster given off by congealed blood, carrying an inborn seduction and mystery. When the light spilled over it, the surface of the polish threw back a brilliant glow, like a flowing lake of red gemstones—each tilt refracting a bewitching halo, calling to mind burning desire and endless temptation.

This hand looked utterly different from anyone else's, as if announcing to the world her singular character and untamed soul. The dramatic long nails paired with the bright red polish had become her own unmistakable mark, impossible to forget once seen.

"My darling fans, do you know why I chose this backdrop?

Let me tell you. Every livestream, I sit right here, my back to it. I let my face show every detail under the ring light—every inch of my expression broadcasting 'control'—while behind me lies the most uncontrollable force of nature, thousands of kilometers long, mud and sand pouring along together. It's telling you this: all your tangled feelings, all those messes you can't untie—in my eyes, they're just like that river outside the window. Vast and chaotic on the surface, but there's a riverbed underneath. And my job is to dig out that riverbed for you, and scoop up the little bit of real gold inside."

When the afternoon light burned strongest, the river reflected a heavy, metallic glow, like molten copper congealed and splashed across the window. That light fell on Yao Yuan's profile, glazing her in a cold, hard, otherworldly enamel. One darling fan said, Teacher Yuanyuan, your backdrop has such presence.

She smiled. "Presence? No. That's the price tag. It's a reminder to everyone who comes in: every gift was long ago marked with a murky price in the ledger of fate—just like this river, which can carry boats and also swallow silt."

"Sometimes, between call-ins, I turn my head to glance at it. I watch that muddy yellow water flow on, never looking back; I watch the scenery on the far bank blur and warp in the rising mist. That's good. Chaos means possibility; blur means it can be defined—by me. What I want is exactly this: to sit before this ultimate, primal chaos, and with every word that leaves my mouth, build for you a new order—cold, but clear.

At dusk, the river darkens to a deeper shade, an ochre-brown close to clotted blood. That's when my studio lights look brighter, warmer, more like a refuge. Countless girls dial in, projecting their emotional troubles onto this ancient river that stays utterly unmoved by all their love and hate. They ask, 'Does he love me,' while outside the window the Yellow River silently demonstrates what 'existence' itself means—nothing to do with love or hate, just moving forward, carrying off what it will carry off, leaving behind what it will leave behind."

"See it, my darlings? Sometimes I tap the screen lightly with my pen, the way you'd knock on the glass that separates me from the Yellow River. Feelings are like the water in this river. You see it surging mighty and grand, but ninety percent of it is useless silt. My studio doesn't teach you to admire the scenery—it teaches you how to sift the gold out of the silt. Don't talk to me about the volume of feeling. Let's talk about how many grams of gold you've actually got in your hand."

"Remember: your eyes can't tolerate a grain of sand, and only then can your hands hold on to gold."

"Now—call-ins begin."

Yao Yuan (suddenly setting down her lipstick, snapping the cap shut with a click, cutting in): "Stop. This question of yours is worth a thousand yuan—the call-in fee—so I'll say it only once. Listen up."
