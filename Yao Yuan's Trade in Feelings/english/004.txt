Chapter 4 — Once Within Reach, Now Beyond His Grasp

Once within reach, now beyond his grasp.
The car slid into Jinshi's new CBD on one of those rare northern winter days—clear skies, brilliant sun spilling across the whole city. The glass curtain wall of that skyscraping office tower was cold and sharp as a blade, mirroring back an entire sky's worth of blue, and farther off, that ancient, silent ribbon of the Yellow River. He parked, but didn't get out right away. He rolled the window up, and in the cramped space there was nothing left but the sound of his own breathing.
Three years. Only three years.
He had once been one of the players in this golden district too. Sixteenth floor. Three hundred and sixty square meters, facing southeast, floor-to-ceiling windows the whole way around. In the morning he could watch the sun rise from the edge of the city, gold light flooding half the district; in the evening, that silent Yellow River in the distance would turn into a band of molten metal. Back then he'd thought life was like this skyline that kept setting new records for height—no ceiling. Money came too easily. Real estate, guesthouses, a farm outside the city—he bought up land and projects the way other men collect medals, certain he could hold on to something permanent.
Then the tide went out, and you find out who's been swimming naked.
Heavy assets became the chains that bound his hands and feet. Liquidating them was less a business operation than a slow self-dismemberment, a sentence served on his own body. He sold off house after house at fire-sale prices, each one breaking through the floor of the last, like carving off flesh that was still healthy—agony at first, then nearly numb, until all he wanted was something "clean," some "closure." The guesthouses closed; the farm changed hands; those investments that had once stood for vision and feeling were now nothing but rows of staggering loss figures on a financial statement. He poured most of the flesh and blood he'd accumulated over the past decade and more into a pit that had suddenly caved in.
In the end he was like a rich man who'd bankrupted himself paying for treatment: he kept his life and the clothes on his back, but inside he'd been hollowed out, his vital force badly drained.
And Yao Yuan—the woman who had always been calmer and sharper than he was—had placed her bet against the grain just as the whole world was singing of recession. Paid in full. Seven hundred and thirty square meters, thirty-eighth floor. A number and a height he now had to look up to reach.
Life is always this dramatic. He thought, with a bitter twist, of that internet line: "The me you can't be bothered with now—the me of the future is more than you can ever afford." Fitting. And true.
He pushed open the car door, and the city wind swept into his face, carrying all the restlessness of the lunchtime rush. He stood still and looked up. In the reflection on the glass curtain wall, white clouds drifted slowly past, and for one instant he had the illusion that the moving play of light and shadow had settled on a window pane somewhere up on the thirty-eighth floor. She who had once been within reach up there was now beyond his grasp. "We" had long since become "he" and "she." He stood below; she was up in the clouds. Between them lay a past of bankruptcy and liquidation, and a glass cliff—invisible, yet utterly solid—called fortune.
He straightened the cuff of a jacket that had no wrinkle in it at all, and walked toward that vast revolving door that seemed capable of swallowing the whole of his past. Every step landed on the wreckage of his old ambitions, soundless.
The mirrored surface of the elevator reflected Shuai Hongqiang's smooth collar. As the numbers climbed from 1 to 38, he reached up to fix his cuff again without thinking. The elevator doors slid open with a "ding," and when he stepped onto the pale gray carpet of the thirty-eighth-floor corridor, his footsteps were swallowed nearly to silence. The receptionist met him with a professional smile: "Hello. Do you have an appointment?"
"I'm here to see Yao Yuan." When Shuai Hongqiang gave the name, his face stayed mild and unbothered, but his fingertips drew in despite himself.
He looked at the enormous abstract art installation hanging down from the ceiling behind the front desk, at the white-collar staff in fitted skirt suits and immaculate makeup hurrying past, the smell of coffee mixed with perfume. He'd once been one of the masters of this place. Now he was a visitor.
The receptionist's smile paused for half a second. She picked up the internal line, murmured a few words, hung up, and made a "please" gesture: "Boss Yao is in a meeting. She asks that you wait in the reception area first."
Crossing the open-plan office, the clatter of keyboards and low discussion rolled over him like a tide. The staff's eyes brushed over him, here and gone, touched with curiosity.
The glass wall of the reception area faced the CEO's office, the blinds drawn; he could only make out figures moving faintly inside. Shuai Hongqiang sat on the sofa, his gaze falling on the corporate brochure on the coffee table. The cover bore a photo of Yao Yuan in a stylish business outfit, plunging neckline, a black leather waist cincher accentuating a graceful line. Her big, loose waves were full and shapely, her brows and eyes sharp, her look aimed straight at the camera, radiating confidence and an ambition she made no effort to hide—the magnetism and presence of a mature woman. It gradually overlapped with the girl in his memory who used to curl up in his arms, smiling like a flower.
He'd lost track of how long it had been when the office door opened. A cluster of people came out around Yao Yuan; she had her head turned, listening intently to a deputy's report. Catching sight of Shuai Hongqiang in the reception area, her step faltered slightly, and then she said to the group, "Work the rest out among yourselves and bring me the result."
The crowd dispersed. Yao Yuan came over with an unhurried grace, still in the same red dress she'd worn an hour earlier on her livestream. At 168 centimeters, paired with a pair of five-centimeter pointed heels glinting with fine gold, she set off her figure all the more—curves front and back, like a peach ripened to perfection, tempting a man to lean closer and breathe in that intoxicating, heady scent of woman. The crisp, forceful click of her heels on the floor recalled Shuai Hongqiang from his moment of dazed admiration. She really did suit red—or maybe her beauty was just that flagrant, or even more than that.
Yao Yuan sat down in the single armchair across from him. She didn't call him by his name the way she used to, only said lightly: "Did Boss Shuai need something from me?"
"Mm." Shuai Hongqiang took a document from his briefcase and pushed it across. "I've got a project in hand. I was hoping we could work together."
Yao Yuan didn't look at the document. Her fingertips tapped lightly on the armrest, her gaze resting on his face. Shuai Hongqiang's 178-centimeter frame was wrapped in a well-cut dark gray cashmere suit, the shoulder line still firm—only, with his body sunk into the sofa, the fabric had left a few creases at his waist that wouldn't smooth out. His left hand rested on the armrest; the wrist that used to wear a Rolex Submariner Green was bare, while his right hand held a phone with a faint awkwardness. Most of his hair was still jet-black, but right near the crown on the right side, a few strands of silver had pushed through, stark and gleaming. His brows grew thick and black, almost without a curve from start to finish, the tails sloping gently down to either side like two relaxed arcs, carrying a touch of careless ease. There was no high rise and fall at the brow's peak the way ordinary eyebrows have—they simply lay steady above his eyes, with a hard, upright spirit. Beneath the brows was a pair of clearly defined eyes, broad double lids, the creases deep and clean, running smoothly from inner corner to outer. The eyes themselves were round and large, the whites and the dark sharply distinct, the whites carrying a clarity rare in a middle-aged man; the deep amber irises turned a warm, gentle luster under the light. His long, dense lashes had real presence; when he lowered his eyelids, they cast a small, soft fan of shadow over his lower lid. Just now, too stiff to know what to say, he pretended to look out the window, and as those long lashes lifted slightly to reveal the full, bright eyes, what they held was the shrewd, situation-reading wit deposited by years of rising and sinking in the sea of business—and a barely perceptible trace of the merchant's calculating self-interest. His nose bridge wasn't very high; the tip was full without being blunt, with a settled, certain roundness. His lips were on the thin side, his jaw line clear but not gaunt, a fine match for his upright, square-round face, giving an impression of trustworthy steadiness and approachability. His skin was on the fair side, with fine lines at the corners of his eyes and across his brow worn in by time—a mature man whom the years and the world had blended into something richer and more complex.
Now, at forty-six, Shuai Hongqiang sat before her. This man, nearing fifty, his bearing settled now, was the man who, in the year she was twenty-eight, had once made her offer up a true heart, ready to hand over everything and spend her whole life with him.
Yao Yuan picked up the document and flipped through it quickly. The pages rustled, especially clear in the silence of the office. Suddenly she lifted her eyes and fixed her gaze on him: "Shuai Hongqiang, we haven't seen each other in eight years."
Shuai Hongqiang's throat bobbed. The weight of time sometimes presses down on a single form of address, a single meeting of eyes. "You're a big internet celebrity now." His voice was a little tight, as if afraid of breaking something. "I keep coming across you in videos. I left messages for your assistant a few times before I got this contact."
Yao Yuan closed the document and set it back on the mirror-bright glass coffee table. Her tone was flat, unruffled, as though appraising the most ordinary of projects: "Still the same old line of business. Traditional manufacturing—the profit margins were squeezed dry ages ago."
The fingers gripping his phone tightened a fraction without his noticing. In the past she had often said such things at his side too—those forward-looking, incisive business insights, like flickering stars. But back then he'd only taken her for a pretty girl with a little cleverness who liked to look good. When she spoke, he'd listen with a smile, thinking it was just her cute, impractical fantasy; he hadn't taken it seriously, let alone acted on it. Later, reality bore out her predictions again and again, accurate to the point of cruelty. Only by then, she was no longer at his side.
"The profit is thin," Shuai Hongqiang's voice dropped, carrying a resigned sort of weariness, "but this project is the steadiest. It's a field I know inside out—I know every dip and rut in it. It's hard work, hard work, but at least… it's guaranteed not to lose money."
"Leave the document with me. I'll give you an answer once I've read it." Yao Yuan stood. The advantage of her height naturally lent her a scrutinizing air. The diamond pendant at her collarbone swayed with the motion, refracting a cold, sharp light.
"All right. I'll wait to hear from you." Shuai Hongqiang stood as well, took an elegant paper bag from beside the armrest, and held it out. "Eight years apart—a small token."
"What is it?" Yao Yuan took it and drew out a dark brown rectangular wooden box. She opened it, and on the velvet lining lay a bronze mirror the size of a palm.
It was cool to the touch, heavy—a weight that seemed able to sink straight to the bottom of one's heart. The mirror itself was no more than seven or eight centimeters wide, its edges worn jade-smooth by long ages, glowing with a dark blue-brown patina. At the center of the back was a small bridge-shaped knob, and around it coiled intricate cloud-and-thunder patterns and a relief of a Kui-dragon; though the lines had been blurred somewhat by time's erosion, they still breathed an ancient, vigorous air.
Strangest of all was the mirror's face. An ordinary old bronze mirror should long since have gone dull and clouded, yet this little mirror dimly reflected her face. Only the image was hazy, blurred, as if behind a layer of misty Jiangnan rain that would never lift. She ran a fingertip across the surface—icy and slick, with not the slightest roughness or rust.
It was a beautiful, peculiar object. Yao Yuan turned it over in her hands, but a question stirred in her: why give her a mirror that couldn't show a face clearly?
Shuai Hongqiang read the look in her eyes. "I remembered you liked these fine little things," he explained. "This mirror was bought at that auction on the winter solstice in '17. They called it an antique—not all that old. The moment I saw it, I felt it should be yours. I wanted to give it to you, called you… but you were already in Hangzhou by then." He paused, his voice sinking further. "And so it sat for eight years. Now it's gone back to its rightful owner."
The year seventeen. The winter solstice.
The needle of memory jabbed hard at Yao Yuan. It was that very winter she had severed every last hesitation and flown south to Hangzhou alone.
"I'm surprised you still remember." For an instant Yao Yuan's expression softened almost imperceptibly, her tone turning gentle. "Thank you. I'll keep the gift."
Without warning, a sour warmth welled up in her chest, then was covered over by a deeper bewilderment. She was no longer the girl who was so easily moved. As today's cold-hearted "Godmother of Feelings," known for her decisive ruthlessness in both feeling and business, how could she be so easily stirred by an old mirror and a few old words?
"Bzzz, bzzz, bzzz—"
The phone on the coffee table buzzed right on cue, the hum short and dense, instantly breaking that subtle stillness in the air.
It was a call confirming the departure time for a tech-world dinner Wang Qian had invited her to.
"Yao Yuan…" The word "Yuan'er" turned over a few times on Shuai Hongqiang's tongue but in the end wouldn't come out. "You go ahead and get to your work. I won't keep you. When there's time… let's have a meal together."
Shuai Hongqiang took his leave and turned toward the office door.
Just as he was about to step out, Yao Yuan's voice came from behind him—not loud, but clear:
"Shuai Hongqiang."
He turned back.
Yao Yuan stood at the office doorway, the vast floor-to-ceiling windows and the city's daylight at her back. Against the light, her fine silhouette blurred; he couldn't make out her exact expression.
"I'll get back to you as soon as I can," she said.
Then she turned, and the office door closed without a sound, sealing the two of them back into their separate worlds.
The elevator descended at a steady pace, the numbers ticking down floor by floor. Shuai Hongqiang stared at the flickering red digits, as if silently counting all the old days that had slipped away along the way, never to be retrieved.
The elevator mirror was cold, reflecting a middle-aged man's face that couldn't hide its weariness. He drew a deep breath and straightened his spine.
On the other side of the door, Yao Yuan didn't go straight back behind her desk. She stood where she was, her fingertips brushing absently along the edge of the wooden box. The bronze mirror lay quietly inside, its misty, rain-blurred face seeming to have quietly absorbed that brief softness of a moment ago as well.
She returned to her desk and tossed the project document onto the top of a stack of materials waiting to be read. But her gaze drifted, against her will, to the bronze mirror.
The office held nothing but the low hum of the air conditioning. Yao Yuan picked up the bronze mirror again, the pad of her finger rubbing the cold back. The intricate cloud-and-thunder patterns pressed against her skin like some ancient code.
"The winter solstice of '17…" she repeated the year under her breath.
That had been the most strained moment of their relationship. Yao Yuan remembered fine snow drifting outside the window that day; she'd stood in a hospital corridor, asking Shuai Hongqiang over the phone for the last time when they were going to register their marriage, and he'd only laughed it off, saying he'd take her out that evening to a newly opened French restaurant. It was on that snowy day, too, that she had gone through an abortion alone in the hospital, recovered for three days, and booked a one-way ticket to Hangzhou.
The bronze mirror lay quietly in her palm, its face still hazy, like a thin fog that could never be wiped clean. On impulse she raised the mirror up, angling it toward the ceiling light. In the instant the light struck the surface, something seemed to flash, very fast, through the depths of that dim, blurred face—not the reflected light of the lamp, but more like a knot of chaotic shadow, twisting, contracting, then vanishing. An instant later, a chill more piercing than the metal itself shot up her fingertips into her arm, jolting her into letting go.
"Clang—"
The bronze mirror fell onto the thick wool carpet with a muffled thud, face down.
Yao Yuan's heart jumped. She bent to pick it up. The moment her fingertips touched the mirror's back, a sudden sting—she picked the mirror up, and in its face her own outline, blurred, was still reflected. The mirror's edge was smudged with red. Looking closely, she found she'd somehow cut her finger, and the blood had touched the surface. Just then, again, a flash of light seemed to come and go in an instant.
Yao Yuan froze for a moment, her hand still pressed to the bronze mirror, her breathing a little ragged. A hallucination? The fatigue of running flat out at high intensity these past stretches?
"Knock, knock, knock."
A knock came, and her assistant poked her head in: "Boss Yao, the car's ready. President Wang's side just asked when you'll be setting off."
"…Got it." Yao Yuan gathered up every trace of emotion and recovered her habitual cool composure. She pulled open a drawer, put the bronze mirror back in its wooden box, and tossed it in; the sound of metal striking wood was crisp and clean.
"File the document, mark it 'pending assessment.'" She rose and grabbed her coat. Passing the coffee table, she glanced at the now-empty gift bag without breaking stride. "Also—look into 'Shuai Hongqiang's' financial situation and litigation records over the past five years. I want a detailed report."
"Yes, Boss Yao."
The sound of her heels faded away. The office sank back into silence, with only the closed drawer seeming to hold a faint chill that didn't belong to this era.
(Meanwhile)
Shuai Hongqiang walked out of that mirror-bright glass curtain-wall tower standing at the city's heart. The early-winter wind, carrying a biting edge, swept the city's dust and clamor against his face. He didn't get into his car right away, only walked along the sidewalk, aimless.
The phone in his pocket buzzed once. He took it out—an unsigned message:
"The thing—did she take it?"
He stared at that line for a long while, then pressed his thumb hard against the screen and deleted the message. He looked up at the towering glass curtain-wall building rising into the clouds; sunlight reflected off a window on the top floor stung his eyes.
Shuai Hongqiang had, in fact, concealed a few things.
This mirror—bidding for it back then hadn't been only because he'd thought she would like it. The white-haired old appraiser, at the handover, had hesitated as if about to speak, then in the end only murmured a vague warning: "This mirror… the period's hard to say, the aura is a little 'heavy.' There's something 'uncanny' about it. They say it's a piece from the late Ming or early Qing, but no exact year can be tested. If you mean to keep it yourself, sir, no harm done; but if you mean to give it away—especially to someone close to you—you'd best… think it over."
At the time he'd taken it for mystification. Until, in recent years, his cash flow broke several times over. In the thick of his troubles, as if led by some unseen hand, he'd thought of this mirror—thought of the "Great Fortune of the Nine-Purple Fire" that the feng shui master who once read her fate had spoken of, and of the appraiser's tight-lipped, evasive expression.
Perhaps this was not merely a gift eight years late, a clumsy bit of nostalgia—it was even, in a way, a… cry for help. But in the end he hadn't managed to say it out loud. Facing the Yao Yuan of today, blazing with light, her eyes sharp, that line—"help me"—had stuck in his throat and turned into a pale, feeble "guaranteed profit, no loss."
Shuai Hongqiang drew a deep breath of the cold air, pressing his sigh down into his heart, got into the car, and pulled slowly into the surging current of traffic, soon lost beyond recognition. He didn't know that the ancient mirror—spanning eight years' time, wound about with tangled feelings and an unexplained strangeness—lay at this moment in a dark drawer high above the city, like a riddle lightly tapped, waiting for the next chance to be opened, quietly rewriting the ruts that fate would run in.
