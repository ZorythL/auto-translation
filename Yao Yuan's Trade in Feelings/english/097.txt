"For those three months, I just couldn't work. By day I'd stare at the computer screen, my head full of the last thing he said to me; at night I couldn't sleep, scrolling through every one of his social-media posts. "I quit. I wasn't laid off—I just couldn't face staying. I thought quitting would let me focus on 'processing' my feelings, but it got worse. I maxed out two credit cards buying dresses he'd once said looked good on me, going to places he might go to 'run into' him, even… believing the nonsense those relationship outfits sell, paying over thirty thousand yuan to learn some 'how to win him back' technique. Now I have no job, no savings, I'm in debt, and every day I lie in bed scrolling through his posts from the small hours until dawn. I watch my old coworkers get promoted, travel, show off their lives. I don't dare answer my parents' calls, don't dare see my friends. I feel like I'm rotten through and through, stinking from the inside out. Teacher, am I beyond saving? I know I should pull myself together, but the moment I think of him, think of how I've lost him forever, the grief crushes me all over again…"

"All your pain, your collapse, your self-loathing right now—the root of it isn't 'losing him.' It's that the moment you lost him, you severed the main lifeline of 'making a living.' You made love the entire structure of your life, and 'making a living'—your job, your savings, your social ties, your personal order—you tore down the load-bearing walls of that building yourself, brick by brick, until it was rubble. Now the main structure—the love you imagined—has collapsed, and you're lying in the wreckage, feeling cold, feeling hopeless. This isn't the aftermath of heartbreak. This is the most direct physical reaction to having your survival foundation hollowed out. You're not heartbroken. Your whole life has stalled out, lost power, and is about to be forcibly evicted for unpaid bills."

"Let's do a clear attribution," Yao Yuan said, her pace even but every word a hammer striking against the walls of false belief. "A relationship ended. That's normal. The way a healthy adult handles a breakup is this: allow yourself to grieve, then repair while you keep running along a stable life track. Like a car with a flat tire—you pull over, put on the spare, and keep driving toward your destination."
"And you—what did you do? You decided the flat tire meant the car wasn't good enough. So you ripped out the engine, smashed the steering wheel, drained the fuel tank, then sat down beside a heap of scrap metal and wept about how hard the road is."
"You set making a living—your job, your income, your social value—completely against making love—your emotional needs—and without a moment's hesitation you sacrificed the former on the altar of the latter. That isn't devotion. That's stupidity, and it's an extreme failure of responsibility to yourself."
She let the cruel verdict settle, then turned to clear, cold marching orders:
"Now, stop every form of self-pity and rumination. You don't need comfort. What you need is an emergency plan for rebuilding after the disaster."
"Step one: execute 'financial and information isolation' immediately. 1. List all your debts, savings, and remaining assets on a single sheet of paper. See exactly how far you are from your survival line giving way. 2. Permanently delete every channel that lets you track any news of him. That isn't a love memory—it's a malicious program blocking your brain's recovery. 3. Cut off all spending that isn't essential to survival. You've lost the right to splurge."
"Step two: launch the 'minimum survival guarantee' program. Your one and only top priority right now is to restore cash flow. Forget your former title, your face, your career plan. Your goal is to find any legal work that brings in income before your next rent is due. Delivering food, working as a shop assistant, picking up odd jobs—there's no shame in that. Going hungry, getting thrown out, going bankrupt on your credit—that's the shame, that's the real dead end. Keep yourself alive first. That's the baseline duty you owe yourself as the sole person responsible for your own life."
"Step three: on top of survival, launch 'value-system repair.' Once you're no longer in danger of starving or freezing, set aside a fixed time each day to answer one question: What is the core skill I make my living with? How much has it depreciated? How do I make it appreciate again at the lowest possible cost? Go learn—even the cheapest online course. Go practice—even if it means starting over from the most basic cases. This process will be long and slow, but every bit of progress lays new foundation on the rubble of your life."

"I know you'll ask: why is this so hard? Why can other people get over a breakup so fast, while I destroyed everything?" Yao Yuan's gaze deepened, moving into a more fundamental explanation. "Because your underlying operating system has the wrong survival priorities preset into it. And tonight, I want to use three mirrors to help you see that error clearly—and reinstall your system."
"The first mirror: Qiong Yao—the ultimate risk sample of the 'love above all' system." Her tone held no praise or blame, only the calm of a scalpel.
"Her whole life she wrote, lived by, and deeply believed that 'love transcends everything.' Her story with Mr. Ping Xintao was the real-life version of the romance-defies-convention tales she penned. But you should go read the last few pages of that story. When the ledger of his life was about to close, at the final reckoning, the partner she had poured all her feeling into, fought the whole world to 'win,' made the choice most aligned with traditional social rules and real-world interest in dividing his estate: he left the substantive wealth to his own blood children."
She paused, letting the fact echo in the silence.
"What does that mean? It means the 'world-shaking love' she spent a lifetime burning for, singing of, and building her career upon may never have truly shaken his core hierarchy of values or his ultimate framework of interests—a framework whose underlying code was always blood, duty, and real-world security. She got the title of love, the copyright to the story, even the prosperity of the publishing empire they built together. But in the end, in the most symbolically meaningful transfer of substantive wealth, she was drawn outside the category of 'one's own people.' This cruelly reveals: in the face of cold, real-world property rights and blood-based inheritance law, the protective power of even the most extreme romantic narrative can be as fragile as paper. Her case is a warning bell: when you stake your entire life on the narrative of 'being loved' and treat that as your only proof of value, you may well find, at the final real-world settlement, that all you're left holding is a stack of deeply moving but unredeemable old scripts—while the real hard currency completed its preordained course long ago, in a dimension you couldn't see."
"Qiong Yao's dream tells you love is great. But when the dream ends, cold reality will tell you this: whoever holds the means of production and the property rights holds the final say over the story—and the final security."
"The second mirror: Yi Shu—the demonstration of the rational architecture of 'make a living first, then make love.'" Yao Yuan's tone took on a cold, almost admiring rationality.
"People say she's cold-hearted, but her coldness is of a higher order. Her core algorithm is crystal clear: make a living first, then make love. Making a living is building the hardware of your life—your financial independence, your professional skills, your coordinates in society. Making love is the lovely application running on top of that stable hardware. Only when the hardware is powerful and stable can the application run smoothly—stable enough, even, that you can run several at once and choose at your leisure."
"That line from her character Xi Bao—'I want lots and lots of love. If there's no love, then lots and lots of money. If there's neither, well, health is good too.'—is treated by many as a classic quote."
Yao Yuan picked up the book at her elbow. "What's the bedrock of a Yi Shu heroine? That 'the most important thing in life is to carry yourself well.' And what does carrying yourself well rely on? Not a man's pity. It relies on having money in your own pocket, a plan in your own head, a road under your own feet. A woman is like a flower; and a flower that wants to bloom—vivid, dignified, free—has to be nourished by money. Tenderness, devotion, companionship with no material foundation beneath them are all castles in the air. Broke up? You can shut your door and cry for one night. But the next day the sun rises as usual, and you have to put on your makeup, go to work, answer your emails, and keep the number in your account climbing steadily. Yi Shu tells you: love is only the icing on the cake."
"Let me correct a fatal misconception every woman should hear: many people think making money is vulgar, that being money-minded is wrong. Dead wrong. Stop letting the poisonous fluff of 'talking about money is crass' brainwash you. Making money and having ideals are never in conflict—they reinforce each other. The more you can earn, the more choices you have. The more assets you hold, the less you depend on anyone. The wealthier you are, the less you'll wrong yourself for a scrap of emotional value."
"Dou Wentao said it on Round Table: 'When we were young we all fancied ourselves the type to see money as dirt. But after going through some things, we understood—money was never dirt; we are. Earning money legally isn't crass in the slightest, and it isn't the least bit shameful. Being able to make money on your own ability is, in fact, an adult's dignity.' So understand this: money is the security firewall of your personal system, the passport to your freedom of choice, the line of calm, effective 'access denied' code you can run when you face any injustice."
"Yi Shu's own road in love was hardly smooth, but through a single pen—clear-headed, diligent, fiercely disciplined—she earned herself 'lots and lots of money' and absolute autonomy over her life. Her 'cold-heartedness' is a strategic protection of her own life energy and attention. The result: she may never have tasted the searing, peak love of a Qiong Yao romance, but she was thereby exempt from the risk of being swallowed by an emotional quagmire, spared the humiliation of becoming a sacrifice when interests came to a head, and granted a lifelong, stable dignity, high output, and autonomy of the soul. She proved that when the lamp of 'love' may go out, gripping tight the bundle of firewood that is 'money'—your own capacity to create value—is the best and most reliable strategy for keeping the fire of your life burning."

"The third mirror: Eileen Chang—the lonely, ultimate form of the 'insight into human nature' system, and a specimen of love's disillusionment." Yao Yuan's voice dropped low, carrying the cold precision of a museum docent.
"She saw the calculation, the desolation, the illusion in human nature and love down to the marrow. In her writing there was never any wishful thinking: love is the fleas crawling beneath a magnificent qipao, the eternal ruin left after a city falls for one love affair. But her tragedy is this: she saw through the barrenness beneath all tenderness, yet failed—or found it nearly impossible, in that era—to build for herself a strong system of 'making a living' that depended on no one and could carry that clarity. Her own relationship with Hu Lancheng is the most painful and most truthful footnote to her cold philosophy—even one as brilliant and clear-eyed as she could sink low into the dust in love; and his promiscuity and betrayal merely confirmed, in the cruelest way, her pessimistic expectations of human nature."
"In her later years she chose to live apart from the world, nearly in total isolation, carrying that detachment to the very end of her life. Her strategy was this: since she had long seen through the calculations of interest and the perishability of feeling beneath every veil of tenderness, she would no longer hold unrealistic illusions about any person or relationship. She actively shut off the emotional interfaces of deep dependence and expectation in exchange for absolute independence of the inner world and purity of perception. Toward love she was, her whole life, a thorough deconstructionist and a pessimistic prophet. She gained a depth and clarity no one could match, and avoided the possibility of being deceived by illusion. But this stance of extreme defense—even self-exile—came at the price of eternal detachment and an inerasable desolation in the base color of her life. She is a proudly lonely lighthouse: logically self-consistent, never falling. But beneath the lighthouse there is only the vast sea and the endless echo of solitude."
"Eileen Chang is an awe-inspiring cautionary mirror: merely 'seeing through' the illusions of love is not enough. Without the solid footing that 'making a living' provides, seeing through only deepens the pain instead of leading to release."
"Eileen Chang's famous line: 'Life is a magnificent qipao, and love is the fleas crawling beneath it.' But I want to tell you, girl: you have to be able to earn that magnificent robe yourself first. As for the fleas—the troubles of love, the setbacks of life—once you have a solid robe, you'll naturally have the mood and the means to clean them off, little by little. When you're standing there half-dressed and shivering, the thing you should be thinking about isn't whether the embroidery on the robe is pretty. It's how to find the cloth first, so you can stay alive and stay warm."
Yao Yuan drew back her gaze and faced the camera head-on, as if to see through the screen straight into that soul curled up in the rubble:
"Three women, three survival algorithms. Qiong Yao is a romantic single process—high energy cost, fragile permissions. Yi Shu is steady multithreading—heavy defense, sovereignty intact. Eileen Chang is an absolute inner loop—zero dependencies, at the cost of utter solitude."
"The road you took before was Qiong Yao's. Now you lie at the end of it, having tasted the bitter fruit in full."
"Eileen Chang's road is a possible destination once you see everything clearly—but it's too cold there, too desolate."
"The only road you should choose, and the only one you can choose now, is Yi Shu's—first become a fanatical builder of your own infrastructure, then think about a poetic dwelling for the spirit."
Her voice gathered, at the last, into a calm and boundless force:
"So, to answer your first question: are you still beyond saving?"
"You're not. But what saves you won't be any love, any feel-good fluff, any outside force. What saves you can only be your own two hands—hands that can work, can earn, can clear away the rubble bit by bit—and that brain of yours that has finally agreed to set 'survive, and live well' as the highest priority of your life."
"From tonight, from this moment, treat yourself as the sole CEO, chief engineer, and fully responsible party of the project that is your life. Your company—your life—is currently insolvent, on the brink of bankruptcy. Your job is not to sit in the failed office and weep about how heartless your competitor—your ex—is. It's to walk out the door right now, land the first piece of business that brings money in, pay next month's rent, and keep the company—you—alive first."
"Love is a 'strategic partnership' or 'joint venture' between two healthy, thriving 'life limited companies,' based on appreciation and joy. It is never—and should never be—a 'bankruptcy-restructuring plan' or 'rescue financing' that one near-bankrupt 'company' begs from another."
"Get your own company running well first—a healthy balance sheet, stable cash flow. Only then will you have the right, and the confidence, to consider whether to enter a new 'partnership,' and with whom."
The sobbing on the other end of the line had long since stopped. There was only heavy breathing, and one quiet line, soft but weighted with force: "…I understand. Thank you, Teacher."
[Call ended]
Yao Yuan gave a slight nod, and spoke tonight's closing words to the camera:
"Remember: on the balance sheet of your life, your capacity to make a living is the permanent core asset—it's the '1.' Love, respect, happiness, freedom—those are all the '0's that follow. Without that '1' up front, no matter how many '0's come after, your life still adds up to zero."
"May we both forge that solid '1' with our own hands. Everything after it only has meaning then."
"My dears, that's where we'll end tonight's broadcast. I wish you all a smooth and happy year in the new year to come."
With that, Yao Yuan reached out a finger and pressed the red "End Broadcast" button, clean and decisive.
The screen went black in an instant, swallowing all the scrolling text and the virtual clamor.
The ring of fill lights went dark one by one, giving off a faint electrical hum like a sigh. In the end, only the antique-style desk lamp in the corner remained, spreading a small, warm, quiet pool of light.
Only now did the immense exhaustion seem to break free of every restraint of reason and well up, gentle and heavy. The expression she'd worn for hours—sharp as a drawn blade—slowly melted and faded. In its place came a deep calm, and beneath that calm, a vast emptiness no one would ever see.
The room was very quiet. Quiet enough to hear the gurgle of the humidifier's circulating water, the bitter wind of the northern winter night blurred behind the glass. And beneath the wind, the never-ceasing flow of the Yellow River—thick, all-embracing, free of any joy or anger, simply existing, simply moving forward, sweeping along endless time and silt, as if silently digesting and carrying all the despair, the sudden understanding, and the resolve to be reborn that had poured out of this small broadcast room tonight.
She sat quietly in the dim glow, watching her own rich, still silhouette in the black glass of the window across from her.
A long while.
Ever so faintly she tugged at the corner of her lips—like the almost cold clarity of confirming, after a precise surgery that drained the last of one's strength, that the diseased tissue had been removed and the system could reboot.
At last she rose slowly and walked to the window.
Outside was the all-devouring winter night, thick as ink, and that great river—unseen yet forever present, flowing on through the ages.
She knew there were still countless souls swept along by the silt of feeling, longing to receive from her a "filter"—perhaps sharp, perhaps painful.
And she would still sit here. Behind her, that silent Yellow River, carrying down silt and all, yet surging magnificently forward.
With the most clear-eyed and most cruel language, she would forge such "filters."
And then hand them to every person who, in the dark, finally decided to reach out a hand.
As for whether each of them could use that filter to sift out their own "true gold," and in this ceaseless river of life build a boat that would not sink, would not drift—a boat of their own—
that answer lay in every act of restraint that said "deal with work first," in every income earned "for myself," in every late night that said "I can stand firm on my own."
