The Secret History of Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang's Entangled Fates
The starting point of all this entanglement traces back to a dinner held even earlier — a banquet thrown to win the furniture-bidding project for Jinshi's provincial theater.
That year, Yao Yuan was twenty-six.
It was 2015, the sixteenth day of the first lunar month, a Friday — the day of "Awakening of Insects."
The flavor of the New Year still lingered. Red lanterns still hung along the streets of Jinshi, and the air carried the sulfur tang of leftover firecrackers along with the lazy ease that only holidays bring. But on the top floor of "Sky-River Tower" — the most lavishly gilded club along the banks of the Yellow River — a grand banquet had just begun, the kind held after the New Year's first day back to work, meant to grease the right palms and open up new ground.
The private room was named "Guanlan," and it lived up to its imposing name. One wall was a single seamless sheet of floor-to-ceiling glass. Beyond it, the muddy, surging waters of the Yellow River churned, carrying chunks of unmelted ice, looking — under the night sky and the city's brilliant lights — like a roaring dragon of dark gold rushing past below. Sitting inside, raising and exchanging cups, one felt a fleeting, illusory grandeur, as if perched above the eternal river itself. The opposite wall held an enormous Suzhou-embroidery screen, stitched with a panorama of mountains and rivers across ten thousand miles, its gold thread faintly shimmering in the light. The room was large enough to easily seat twenty at a revolving table, yet right now held only nine — which made it feel all the more spacious and extravagant.
Shuai Hongqiang was, beyond question, tonight's host. He wore a finely tailored navy cashmere suit with a subtle weave, no tie, the top button of his shirt left open. A green-dialed Rolex flashed at his wrist whenever he carelessly lifted his hand. His smile was hearty, his voice booming, his command of the room assured — he embodied the word "magnanimous" to the fullest.
"Brother Wang, Director Li, Section Chief Zhang... gentlemen, leaders, Happy New Year! New year, new beginnings — our first drink back at work has to be here, looking out over our Mother River!" He raised his glass to open the evening, his tone warm yet respectful. "Tonight there's nothing else on the agenda — just good cheer! Drink as you please, talk as you please!"
The waiters filed in one after another, and the dishes were so extravagant they left people speechless. None of the usual hotel fare — every plate was pushed to the limit of regional specialty and seasonal rarity. The Yellow River carp had to be a "Golden-Scale General" over three feet long, with only the two liveliest crescent-shaped cuts beneath the gills taken, blanched in clear chicken broth, the flesh tender as tofu. There was wild Alashan lamb, the most tender cut of the ribs, slow-roasted over fruitwood until the skin was crisp and the inside ran with juice. From the south came butter crabs, their roe rich and golden, steamed in Huadiao wine, the fragrance filling the whole room. And there was ganba mushroom — hailed as "the king of mushrooms" — stir-fried with Xuanwei ham, its aroma overpowering. With every dish that arrived, Shuai Hongqiang could smile and explain its story: the difficulty of sourcing this ingredient, the pedigree of that chef. It showed his care while, without a trace of effort, broadcasting his means and his taste.
The wine was the real headline act. "I know you leaders have tasted every fine liquor there is. Tonight we won't play those empty games — we'll drink the real thing!" Shuai Hongqiang waved a hand, and the waiters carried in two cases of eighth-generation Wuliangye, then at the very peak of its fame and its price. "This stuff is rich and smooth, it won't go to your head — perfect for the bold spirit of our Yellow River!" He opened the bottles himself, poured every glass himself, and went a full round without his expression changing. His toasts were artful too — never putting anyone on the spot, yet always making you lift your cup willingly. "Director Li, I'll drain mine, you drink as you like — this one's to your steady rise in the new year!" "Brother Wang, this one I have to offer to you — last year's project owed everything to your guidance!"
The mood at the table soon warmed under his coaxing. Just as the wine had gone three rounds and the dishes had been savored, with everyone deep in lively talk, there came a soft knock at the door, which then swung open.
Director Ren of the Culture and Tourism Bureau walked in smiling, apologizing all the while: "Sorry, sorry I'm late — just got out of a meeting. Hongqiang, everyone, my apologies!" But the figure who followed in behind him made the noisy, animated room go still for an almost imperceptible instant.
It was Yao Yuan.
She was dressed all in red — not a gaudy, vulgar bright red, but a darker shade, a deep crimson like aged wine or fine velvet. It was a crimson off-the-shoulder midi dress in subtly lustrous satin, cut with extreme simplicity and a close fit that perfectly traced her curves. The hem fell to the knee, leaving a stretch of fair, straight calf, and on her feet were a pair of black velvet pointed-toe stilettos. She wore little jewelry — just two faint sparks of diamond at her earlobes, and at her throat a very fine platinum chain whose pendant was a small ruby that echoed the color of her lips. Her long hair flowed smoothly over her shoulders, rippling gently with each step.
Against the room's lavish but somewhat sober palette, that red was like a walking flame — restrained yet scorching — and it seized everyone's gaze at once. The smile on her face was pitch-perfect, neither meek nor proud. It blended into the festive remnants of the New Year, yet strangely she commanded the room without seeming the least bit flippant. Her beauty was aggressive, almost weaponized, yet steadied through and through by that calm temperament.
Director Ren, taking the seat of honor, introduced her to Shuai Hongqiang: "Hongqiang, this is the leading star of our provincial song-and-dance troupe, Yao Yuan. Not only does she dance beautifully, she's one of the young backbone talents in our bureau — capable, and sharp as a tack. I brought her along to add a little artistic flavor to our gathering, and to let her see the world a bit." His words, in and out of the lines, made clear her "status" and her value, while leaving ample room for imagination.
The moment Yao Yuan stepped through the door, something undisguised flashed in Shuai Hongqiang's eyes — pure dazzlement. He rose at once, came around half the table, and stretched out his hand, his smile even broader than before — a smile mixing admiration, warmth, and the bearing of a host: "Teacher Yao! I've long heard your name — today I finally meet you in person! Truly... radiant! Happy New Year, Happy New Year!"
As they shook hands, he felt her fingertips slightly cool, her grip just right. Her voice was clear and bright, edged with a smile: "Boss Shuai, Happy New Year. Sorry to intrude." Her manner was easy and gracious.
Shuai Hongqiang personally led her to her seat, placing her right beside Director Ren. Returning to his own chair, he raised his glass and announced to the whole table in his booming voice: "Come, let's welcome Teacher Yao! This cup — to beauty, to art, and to a new year that blazes red-hot for us all!" He laid deliberate stress on "blazes red-hot," his gaze sweeping over Yao Yuan's deep crimson dress, the implication clear. It drew a knowing chuckle from everyone, and they all chimed in.
Yao Yuan lifted her glass too — the Wuliangye a waiter had just poured for her. She stood, her posture tall and graceful, her smile bright: "Thank you, Boss Shuai, thank you, leaders. I can't really drink, but I'm happy today, and it's the new year, so let me offer everyone a toast — may you all have everything you wish for this year, and thank you, Director Ren, for bringing me along." With that, under the slightly surprised and admiring gazes around her, she drained the clear liquor in one go, the motion crisp and clean. A faint flush rose to her cheeks, lending her even more color.
"Well done! Teacher Yao doesn't hold back!" Shuai Hongqiang was the first to cheer, leading the applause. The look he gave her now held, beyond the initial dazzlement, a good deal more probing and intense interest. This woman wasn't just stunning to look at — in a setting like this she was poised and proper, even a little bold.
Through the dinner Yao Yuan didn't volunteer much, but whenever the talk turned to artistic performances or marketing, or whenever she was asked about the troupe, she always had something substantial and clear to say. Now and then she could even field a harmless joke, smoothing the mood at just the right moment. When someone, emboldened by drink, half-jokingly asked whether "she had connections up in the capital," she only looked over with those clear, bright eyes, gave a slight smile, and swirled her empty glass: "Leader, now you're teasing me. If I really had such a powerful backer, would I still be sitting here learning from all of you? I'd have ascended to heaven long ago. Me — I'm just someone raised by the soil and water of Jinshi. Right now all I want is to dance well, to polish our province's cultural calling card a little brighter, and for that I'll still need all you leaders to lend your support." A few words, and she'd turned aside a thousand pounds with four ounces. She neither denied the vague halo that the rumor lent her, nor handed over any handle that could be used against her. The mystery surrounding her supposed transfer tied to the Jiang and Hai families was one of her best suits of armor.
When she finished, she again drew laughter from everyone present, and the leader who'd asked just pointed at her with a grin and let the matter drop.
Shuai Hongqiang held his glass and watched her through the mingled fragrance of food and wine. When she leaned in to listen to a bureau chief beside her, her lashes lowered, casting a small shadow on her eyelids, her bearing intent; when she turned to murmur for the waiter to refill her tea, she seemed utterly serene. She was beautiful — but beautiful in a way that was very "useful," very "safe," even very "professional." It made him set aside his initial casualness and study her with more seriousness.
While Shuai Hongqiang worked the room among the various leaders, he took in everything at the table.
As the dinner wound down, he waved his hand grandly: "Let's go — next stop! 'Jinding' Club. I've booked the biggest private room. Tonight we have to enjoy ourselves to the fullest!" "Jinding" was then Jinshi's most exclusive private club — members-only, with excellent privacy.
After dinner, as they moved on to the club, Director Ren patted Shuai Hongqiang on the shoulder and said with a low laugh: "Little Yao's something, isn't she? She makes her living on real ability, even has a little business of her own — very sharp. You young people should talk more." The words served to clear up a few things, hold out a little possibility, and dispel Shuai Hongqiang's suspicion that Director Ren and Yao Yuan might have some hidden relationship.
The light in the club's private room was hazy and ambiguous. There was a huge ring-shaped leather sofa, and mellow blues music drifted through a top-grade sound system as background. Fruit platters, snacks, foreign spirits, and red wine had long been laid out. Some of the leaders began to sing, some gathered in twos and threes to chat, some started up a game of dice.
Yao Yuan sat in a corner of the sofa, quietly sipping the warm lemon water a waiter had brought. In the shifting colored lights, her deep crimson gown took on different sheens, and she was like a silently burning flower, keeping a subtle distance from the faint din around her.
After making his rounds, Shuai Hongqiang finally came over with a square-cut glass holding amber liquor (very likely Hennessy XO) and sat down on the single armchair beside her — not too close, not too far, a distance that allowed conversation without being overly intimate.
"That outfit suits the occasion today, Teacher Yao — and it looks especially good on you." His tone now carried a touch more ease and a lower register than at the table, his gaze settling on her sincerely, full of appreciation.
Yao Yuan turned her head and gave him a smile, one that in the hazy light shed some of its official polish and gained a bit more truth: "Thank you, Boss Shuai. It's the new year, after all — red is auspicious. But it's you who went all out today, arranging everything so thoughtfully."
"As it should be — everyone having a good time is what matters most." Shuai Hongqiang swirled his glass and asked, as if in passing, "From the way you spoke at dinner, Teacher Yao, you know your way around marketing? And you have a company of your own?"
"Just a small company I run for fun with a friend — mainly taking on some performance-related promotional design, earning a bit of pocket money, and it makes my work with the troupe more convenient too." Yao Yuan answered modestly, but there was a barely perceptible thread of confidence in her tone.
"Impressive." Shuai Hongqiang praised her sincerely and leaned slightly forward — a posture signaling focus and a closing of psychological distance. "I'll be honest with you, I've been mulling over a few cultural-investment projects lately. I think there's a lot to be done in that space. Down the road, there may well be a place where Teacher Yao and I could work together."
"You're joking, Boss Shuai — you're a man who does big things. But if there's ever anything I can be useful for, I'll certainly do my best." Yao Yuan lifted her water glass and lightly touched it to the glass in his hand, drawing a soft "clink," her smile perfectly judged.
Just then, someone on the screen queued up Faye Wong's "Legend," the melody gentle. Shuai Hongqiang suddenly asked, "Would you like to sing, Teacher Yao? Pick one? Whatever you like, I'll cue it up for you."
Yao Yuan shook her head and declined with a smile: "I'm all right at dancing, but I'll pass on singing."
"What a shame." Shuai Hongqiang laughed, not pressing it, and instead began talking about a concert he'd been to recently — the conversation flowing on naturally.
After a while, Director Ren came over to call Shuai Hongqiang away to drink with another bureau chief. Shuai Hongqiang rose and gave Yao Yuan an apologetic smile: "Sit tight a moment, Teacher Yao, I'll just be over there."
Yao Yuan nodded. As he turned to leave, her gaze seemed to drift, as if by chance, over his retreating back — the well-cut suit, the upright bearing, and that air of control and bold confidence that belongs to a successful man, evident in his every word and move. Then she lowered her lashes, the long fringe casting a small shadow beneath her eyes, her feelings unreadable.
Halfway across the room, Shuai Hongqiang glanced back. He saw Yao Yuan still sitting there quietly, her profile tracing a graceful curve in the hazy light, the cup of lemon water cradled in her hands, the rising steam seeming to blur her face. In that moment, the calm — even slightly aloof — beauty about her formed a strange contrast with the lively poise she'd shown at the table. She was like a vivid flame he couldn't quite touch, and some desire to conquer began to quietly take root in his heart.
He knew this "meeting" would by no means stop here. This "koi carp" — he'd already silently given her a code name in his mind — he was set on reeling in.
That night, they exchanged contact information. Shuai Hongqiang's pretext was: "In case the troupe needs anything on the promotion side down the line, or there's a chance for your own company to collaborate — let's keep in touch." Lofty, unassailable.
After Yao Yuan left, Shuai Hongqiang stood at the club's entrance and lit a cigarette. The chill of the spring night carried a lingering bite that scattered some of the wine's haze. He recalled the feel of her red velvet hem brushing his knee as she came around to sit beside him, remembered the faint light in her eyes when she spoke of her work, and remembered the composure beyond her years with which she'd parried every probe.
This woman was interesting. There was a contradiction about her: beneath the stunning beauty lay a clear head and a definite drive for her career; she seemed attached to the system, yet kept her own independent boundaries and livelihood; rumor wrapped her in a fog, while she herself displayed solid, genuine ability. To conquer her seemed not merely to conquer a beautiful woman, but more like conquering a stretch of uncharted, challenging territory not easily mapped. It stirred a powerful interest in him — an interest mixing male instinct, commercial appraisal, and the urge to conquer.
So it turned out everything had been foreshadowed. When Shuai Hongqiang first looked at Yao Yuan, there may have been dazzlement, but more of it was scrutiny and assessment. Her "usefulness," her resistance to easy control — these were precisely the bait that first drew him in.
A month later, Shuai Hongqiang won, with no suspense at all, the provincial theater's entire fixed-furniture-and-fittings project for every zone, totaling 15.8 million — from the seats in the auditorium to the furnishings of the conference rooms and VIP lounges, all of it swept into his pocket. With the project secured, the dealings grew frequent. His contact with the theater, the Culture and Tourism Bureau, and related units became ever closer — and that naturally included Yao Yuan.
In the course of all this back-and-forth, his gaze gradually shifted from "business partner" to the woman herself.
Over half a year, Shuai Hongqiang's commercial map quietly expanded. His company image badly needed an upgrade, and almost without a second thought, he handed the entire commission for the corporate visuals and promotional materials over to Yao Yuan's still-growing advertising company. From promotional brochures and custom gifts to a full set of business cards — everything her company could take on, he gave her. That steady, substantial order earned Yao Yuan's company a very real first bucket of gold, and let it gain a firm foothold in the industry fast.
Everything seemed to fall into place of its own accord.
He kept displaying the deep resources, bold style, and mature charm of a successful man, precisely delivering care and resources. Nor did she shrink from the stage — whether in professional insight or in the deft maneuvering of social settings, she handled it all with ease, showing the radiance and value of a woman who could hold her own ground.
Mutual appreciation, mutual designs — plus those subtle flutters that passed between their brows and eyes outside the bounds of professional exchange — a tacit, unspoken attraction had long since quietly grown.
And so, the date arrived: February 14, 2016.
That evening, Shuai Hongqiang sat on the observation deck of Jinshi's highest revolving restaurant at the cloud-top. Beyond the glass curtain wall, the city's lights merged with the sunset glow on the horizon into one glittering river of stars. Tender live jazz floated through the air, and in the center of the long table sat a large bouquet of deep-red roses, every petal beaded with water — a delicate contrast to the still-chilly spring night outside.
He himself wore a well-fitted bespoke suit, looking more formal than usual. Yao Yuan arrived as promised, led in by an attendant, wrapped in a calf-length black cashmere coat of superb texture. The coat's cut was crisp and flowing, cinched only by a narrow belt at the waist, tracing her slender waist. She raised her hands and slipped the coat off, handing it to the attendant at her side — the gesture itself like a small, elegant ritual.
Beneath the coat was the true showpiece of the evening: a dark-red satin slip gown. That red was like aged port — deep, rich — and under the restaurant's carefully designed lighting it flowed with a fine sheen, as if velvet and silk were woven together. The thin straps perfectly displayed the lines of her graceful shoulders, neck, and collarbones. The dress clung as it fell, drew in slightly at the waist, then spread out in a slow fishtail curve, so that with each step the light rippled across it, setting off skin white as snow. She was like a velvet rose suddenly breaking free of the night's embrace, burning hot — a beauty of extreme tension and aggression that seized all the air and every gaze in the room at once.
Undisguised admiration and a sense of conquest flashed in Shuai Hongqiang's eyes. He strode forward, personally pulling out her chair, his fingertips brushing — barely there — against her bare, faintly cool upper arm. "Cold out there?" he asked in a low voice, his tone an intimate concern.
"It was fine." Yao Yuan sat down and revealed a smile to him, a smile that, set off by the dazzling light and her red dress, was bright beyond compare. The attendant carefully stored away her coat — that quiet black cashmere, like the last shred of cool armor she'd carried in from the real world, now laid aside.
Dinner was a carefully composed Chef's Menu, paired with vintage champagne. The atmosphere gradually warmed amid the fine food, the fine wine, and the ever-deepening, ever-more-brilliant night view beyond the window. At just the right moment, he took out a deep-blue velvet jewelry box. Opening it revealed not an ordinary ring but a diamond necklace. The center stone was a fancy-color yellow diamond of about three carats, surrounded by sparkling white diamonds like stars attending the moon, refracting blazing fire in the light — precious and dazzling, yet distinctive, never falling into cliché.
"Yuan'er," he called her name, his voice lower than usual, his gaze focused and deep. "This necklace is called 'Toward the Sun.'" He paused, then went on, "From the first time I saw you, I felt you were like a flame — bright, radiant, full of life. I don't want to bind that flame. I only hope I can stand beside you — and perhaps, give you something to lean on."
His words deftly avoided any frivolous promise, yet expressed his admiration and his wish to protect. There was no dramatic scene of going down on one knee, yet he set his posture sincere enough. He picked up the necklace, stepped behind her, and gently fastened it on. The cold diamonds met her warm skin, and the hanging yellow diamond settled exactly at the center of her collarbone, glittering, reflecting the light in his eyes.
Yao Yuan touched the diamonds at her throat — hard and brilliant against her fingers. She lifted her eyes to their reflection in the glass: he stood behind her, his hand still resting lightly at her shoulder, his posture intimate and full of possession. The restaurant's lilting music, the boundless night view beyond the window, the heavy, dark-piercing brilliance at her throat, and before her this man laying his resources, his thoughts, and his desire bare — all of it composed an irresistible, grand, and exquisite temptation.
In that moment, the heart of twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan was flooded with a mix of vanity, emotion, a vague longing for the future, and a genuine attraction. She turned her head and gave him a smile beyond bright, even touched with a little sweet shyness, a smile that against the diamond's glow was beautiful beyond compare.
She didn't say "yes," but that smile — and the fingertips she quietly laid over the back of his hand — were already her silent consent.
On this Valentine's evening, at the highest point of the city, between diamonds and lights, the relationship between them crossed past the push and pull of ambiguity and formally began.
The restaurant's brilliant lights, the cold glint of diamonds, and the bubbles of champagne all finally settled into a private stillness high above the city. In the presidential suite on the top floor of the cloud-top hotel, the heavy door closed soundlessly behind them, sealing out the whole city's clamor. The lights in the room were dimmed to their lowest, leaving only a few wall lamps in the corners casting warm yellow halos, the air filled with the scent of high-end perfume. Beyond the floor-to-ceiling windows lay the boundless, silent sea of city lights, interwoven with the moonlight of the sixteenth night — dreamlike and ambiguous.
The elegant waltz that had begun at the restaurant, the moment it reached the room, turned fierce and exhilarating in rhythm and step — exactly like a tango between the two of them.
Words became superfluous ornament, replaced by the language of bodies, the cadence of breath, the meeting of gazes. The expensive suit and that dark-red satin gown lay heaped on the thick carpet — black and red, a perfectly matched pairing of colors.
Shuai Hongqiang's breath was scorching, carrying an unquestionable desire to dominate, sweeping over frozen ground like a warm current, set on surveying every inch of territory and leaving his own mark and warmth upon it. Yao Yuan's response was like waterweed beneath a hidden tide — seeming to sway with the current, to lean and yield, yet possessed of its own supple roots and a will of its own in how it twined, quietly drawing the life-force and a certain confirmation the warm current brought, through every winding closeness.
Shuai Hongqiang was conquering a rich and mysterious land he had long desired, while Yao Yuan was experiencing the thrill of being wholly enveloped by a powerful force, briefly surrendering to dizziness and loss of control — and at the same time, with all her sensitivity and responsiveness, appraising this "conqueror's" sincerity and strength.
Beyond the window, the city's river of light flowed soundlessly. Within, the temperature rose, and the most primal force and beauty played out their ageless drama against a backdrop of extreme luxury and privacy.
In the end, all the exploration, negotiation, tides, and storms came to rest in a deep silence carried with exhaustion and a strange kind of satisfaction. Only the not-yet-settled heartbeats of the two of them tapped out the lingering echo between their pressed-together chests.
Shuai Hongqiang's arm circled her into his embrace — a posture full of possession. His chin rested against the crown of her hair, and his voice came hoarse and satisfied in the aftermath: "You're mine now, Yuan'er." The words were like a stamp sealing the verdict on everything tonight.
Yao Yuan didn't answer right away. She only pressed her cheek to his sweat-damp chest, listening to that steady, powerful heartbeat. In her eyes were reflected the distant lights beyond the window — bright, yet somewhat unfocused. The pleasure of the body was real; a certain emotional dependence and the satisfaction of vanity were real too. But beneath this real, lingering warmth, a very faint clarity, thin as a thread of ice, was quietly stirring. She had gained the attention she craved, the proof of material things, and a seemingly reliable support to lean on — but she had also handed over a part of herself. And not only her body.
