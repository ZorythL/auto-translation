The Gobi Data Center

Time slipped away like sand through her fingers, and before she knew it, another month had passed.

The search for the descendants of the mirror-masters was like hunting for one particular grain of sand in the whole Gobi—the progress was painfully, maddeningly slow. Old Chen quietly worked his old connections to dig for clues; Shuai Hongqiang put out feelers through certain back channels; and Yao Yuan herself reached in every direction, using the web of information she had built up through the Pretty Big One. In the end, all of it added up to a single lead, so faint it was almost a wisp of nothing—it seemed to involve a family with the surname "Wang," whose ancestors may have included practitioners of the old occult arts, active long ago in the Shaanxi-Gansu region. The dates were ancient, no one knew whether the line had survived unbroken, and where its descendants might be today was anyone's guess. The lead was thin as a cicada's wing, yet it weighed on her like a thousand pounds: the one faint glimmer in the heavy dark, impossible to abandon, yet with no clear place to begin chasing. On the phone Shuai Hongqiang's voice was full of guilt and urgency, but Yao Yuan, by contrast, stayed calm, answering only: "Having a direction is good. Keep looking. There's no rush."

Maybe it really was the protection of the heart-guarding jade Old Chen had given her, or maybe it was that the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror, locked away in the deep, had lost whatever it leaned on. Either way, this past month Yao Yuan's mind had been unusually steady. Those sudden waves of vertigo, that weightless feeling of being dragged somewhere else, never came back. She slept solid and deep at night, and went through her days bright and full of energy. Even the sharp edge she carried on her livestreams now seemed plated with a warmer, more settled light. The warmth of the old jade against her chest had become a firm barrier between her and that eerie curse, and a quiet, secret reassurance deep in her heart.

In the very hottest stretch of high summer, the third deep model-fusion between the Pretty Big One's emotional core and Zhao Yiming's Genesis Engine reached a critical milestone.

In the fusion lab, on the enormous screen, the intricate branches of the Pretty Big One's emotional decision trees were being docked, infiltrated, and rebuilt against the vast, nebula-like node network at the base of the Genesis Engine—all through some delicate algorithmic protocol. Data streams poured down like a river of stars; indicator lights flashed like mad. Yao Yuan and her core algorithm team, separated by a glass curtain wall from Zhao Yiming's engineers, watched every parameter's flicker without blinking.

After a full forty-eight hours of sleepless joint testing, the last interface protocol passed validation. The two formerly separate systems of light and shadow on the screen finally merged completely, forming a single seamless picture—one that kept evolving in real time, flickering with sparks of intelligence. The lab erupted in a cheer that had been held back far too long.

"Third fusion, core channels all open, baseline efficiency up one hundred seventy percent, emotional-intent recognition accuracy breaking ninety-one percent, complex-relationship inference logic self-consistency hitting eighty-nine point seven percent!" Zhao Yiming's chief engineer—a stern middle-aged man who rarely cracked a smile—announced the preliminary numbers, his voice trembling with excitement.

It meant that the envisioned "Emotional Super-Advisor" AI core—the one that could deeply understand the games people play with one another and offer real, workable strategy—had finally crossed the chasm from zero to one in its hardest, deepest, most foundational construction. What came next was to take this powerful "heart" and refine its "blood vessels" and "nervous system," to fill it with ever more vast and varied "blood" (data), and only then to give it a concrete "body" and "voice," to finally present it to users.

Zhao Yiming walked over to Yao Yuan. His eyes were bloodshot, stubble had pushed up along his jaw, but his gaze was startlingly bright. "It worked," he said, looking at her, his voice rough with exhaustion yet carrying enormous satisfaction. "The hardest bone—we've gnawed it clean."

Yao Yuan was just as exhausted, but her mind sat in a strange calm that comes after a high. She looked at the fused map on the screen, glowing with data, the crystallized labor and wits of countless people, let out a soft breath, and gave Zhao Yiming a smile that was genuine and radiant. "You've worked hard, Boss Zhao. Our 'child' finally has a healthy, strong 'heart.'"

At the celebration banquet the mood ran warm. Zhao Yiming raised his glass to everyone who had taken part, and at last his gaze settled on Yao Yuan. "Next comes optimization and turning this into a product. We'll need to work even more closely together. Boss Yao," he paused, then issued the invitation, "the Genesis Engine's home turf is out in the Gobi. Phase two of the data center just finished its final tuning, with the newest quantum-computing prototype unit hooked in as an auxiliary. Any interest in bringing your people out to see it in person—the 'body' this 'heart' will live in someday, and the 'universe' it runs in? Think of it as a team outing, too. The summer night sky over the Gobi is worth seeing."

The invitation was reasonable on every count: a deepening of the technical partnership, and a chance to draw the teams together. Yao Yuan barely hesitated before agreeing. "All right. I've been wanting to see with my own eyes what kind of 'beast' can swallow that much computing power."

Three days later, at dawn, a convoy of three off-road vehicles pulled out of Jinshi and headed northwest, into the depths of the Gobi. Yao Yuan brought two of her core executives and her head of technology; Zhao Yiming's side came similarly staffed. The journey was four hundred kilometers. The first stretch ran along expressway, oases and farmland still visible on either side, but the farther west they went, the more the green receded like a retreating tide, and the land gradually bared its most primal, coarse, immense face.

The sky was that extremely high, extremely distant blue, scoured clean year after year by wind and sand, without a single cloud. As far as the eye could see lay the endless Gobi flats, paved with black gravel, dotted here and there with stubborn camel-thorn and red-willow thickets, like blood vessels bulging up on the land's parched skin. The far mountains were a dull ochre-red, their lines hard and steep, standing silent on the horizon like guards out of the depths of time. The wind was dry, carrying the scorch of sunlight and the grit of sand; on the face it stung in a way that cleared the head.

By afternoon the convoy reached its destination.

What met the eye first was not the sprawling complex of buildings she had imagined, but a long, low wall of some special material, almost the same color as the Gobi itself. They passed through a checkpoint set with layer upon layer of verification, and the view suddenly opened wide.

It was a... strange presence, hard to describe in ordinary architectural terms.

It looked nothing like any traditional data center—more like a silver metallic ecosystem set into the earth, brimming with a sense of the future. The main buildings were several huge, flattened-oval, silver-gray pods, half-embedded beneath the solid ground at angles that satisfied both mechanics and aesthetics. Their surfaces were sheathed in optical material that adapted to the environment, throwing back a cold, pure metallic sheen under the blazing sun—a starkly jarring contrast against the barren surroundings. Between the pods ran transparent giant pipes, pale blue coolant flowing through them, like the arteries of a living thing.

Farther out stood a matrix of wind turbines on a staggering scale, their huge blades turning slow and steady in the never-ceasing Gobi wind, and great fields of deep-blue solar panels, like a layer of scales laid over the Gobi to drink in light and heat. Every power line was neatly buried underground; almost nothing cluttered the surface.

"All the electricity here is over eighty-five percent self-supplied," Zhao Yiming's voice rose in the wind as he pointed at the power arrays. "Wind and sun are the Gobi's most generous gifts. The geology under our feet is unusually stable, far from any fault line. The average annual temperature is low and dry—a natural, ideal environment for heat dissipation. To make the most of that natural cold source, the main buildings sink below ground, and inside we use our own composite system that combines indirect evaporative cooling with liquid cooling. PUE—power usage effectiveness—can be pushed to a stunning 1.05 or lower."

He led Yao Yuan's group into the main pod. Inside it was utterly clean, ordered, and cold. In the soaring space stood row upon row of neatly aligned racks, like black metal monoliths, their dense indicator lights blinking like breath, giving off a low, far-reaching hum that seemed to rise from deep in the earth. Not a single stray cable was in sight; everything was sealed into uniform modular units. The temperature held steady at around twenty degrees, the humidity extremely low; even in the special anti-static jackets, you could still feel a faint chill. Technicians in matching uniforms quietly inspected and worked on the transparent gangways and platforms, like priests of a future world tending these silent "gods" of vast computing power.

"This—Zone A—is the main compute array already carrying the Genesis Engine 1.0 and part of the 2.0 modules," Zhao Yiming reeled it off like family treasures, his tone carrying a builder's pride and an almost reverent focus. "Next to it, Zone B, is the just-tuned phase two. We've reserved enough space and power capacity specifically to carry our fused 'Emotional Super-Brain' and its future iterations. Every rack group has independent, redundant power, network, and cooling; a single module failing won't affect overall operation. Data travels over a multiply-encrypted, physically isolated 'air-gap' network. Core data is generated, trained, and iterated right here, and never leaves. The backup data center sits inside a mountain thirty kilometers away, synced in real time over dedicated fiber."

Yao Yuan listened and watched in silence. She was no tech fanatic, but standing now inside this "temple of the future" poured out of barrenness with cutting-edge technology, enormous capital, and extraordinary willpower—even though she had already glimpsed this scene back when she'd traveled through time into the days of the thirty-three-year-old Yao Yuan—seeing it again with her own eyes still struck her with a deep awe. This was no laboratory toy. This was hard, foundational bedrock already up and running, ready to support the next wave of artificial intelligence. Zhao Yiming's ambition, his scope, his ability to execute—all of it found its most direct expression here. The executives and engineers she had brought were long since stunned by the sight before them, murmuring to one another, their eyes alight.

The tour lasted several hours. Toward evening, the convoy Zhao Yiming had arranged carried everyone to a "desert town" tourist center more than twenty kilometers away, on the edge of the county seat. The town was clearly a carefully planned creation of recent years—tidy streets, buildings blending modern minimalism with the character of northwestern dwellings, inns, restaurants, specialty shops, even a small desert museum, all of it there. Yet the overall atmosphere still kept the rugged, open feel of the Gobi frontier.

The welcome dinner was not set at a restaurant in town, but deliberately chosen on a flat patch of sand at a campsite outside it, opened up specifically for visitors to experience—its whole selling point being "nostalgia" and "wild fun." A huge bonfire had already been lit, dead Euphrates poplar wood and red-willow roots crackling in the flames, the leaping tongues of fire dyeing the surrounding dunes a warm orange-red. A plump roast lamb turned on a special spit, worked by a local old master; fat dripped into the fire, sparking up brighter flames and a tempting smell of char. The long tables were laid out with great platters of mutton, naan, assorted cold dishes, and locally brewed, none-too-weak liquor served in rough clay bowls—both a local specialty and a quick medium for closing the distance between strangers.

There were no delicate utensils, no fussy etiquette. The Gobi night wind, still carrying the day's warmth, brushed across everyone's face. Both teams quickly broke past their initial reserve, and under the spell of the bonfire, the food, and the wine, they merged into one lively crowd. They laughed and talked loudly, toasted one another, traded stories of what they'd seen that day, and dreamed aloud about the future of the partnership project. The strong liquor went down like a line of fire, burning warmth into their chests, flushing their faces red.

Yao Yuan, too, had changed out of her daytime formal wear into a loose, cream-white linen dress, a thin knit cardigan over it, her long hair braided into a loose plait falling to one side. Holding a rough clay bowl, she sipped the wine the locals pressed on her in small mouthfuls, feeling that coarse heat burn all the way down her throat, drawing up a strange relaxation and high spirits. The firelight played across her face, light and shadow leaping, stripping away her usual shrewdness and distance, revealing a rare beauty—languid and vivid.

Zhao Yiming sat not far off on a wooden stump, also holding a bowl of wine, shirtsleeves rolled to his elbows, the profile of his face clean-cut in the firelight. He didn't say much, mostly listening, answering now and then, his gaze drifting unintentionally again and again over Yao Yuan. Seeing her eyes lit bright by the fire and the relaxed smile at the corner of her mouth, a faint ripple stirred in his own eyes too.

After a few rounds of wine the mood grew freer still. No one knew who started it, but someone put on music—a bright melody thick with the flavor of the western regions. A few local staff and tourists were the first to stand and dance a simple dance around the bonfire. Soon the younger, more uninhibited members of both teams joined in too, laughing and giggling. Their movements were clumsy but full of joy, and the laughter, the music, and the crackle of the flames mixed together, carrying far across the open Gobi night.

At some point Zhao Yiming came over. Seeing the slight haze the wine had brought to Yao Yuan's eyes, he sat down on the empty stump beside her. Half an arm's length apart, the two of them watched the lively, joyful scene together. Above their heads the starry sky unrolled in silence, fading from deep blue into ink-black, the Milky Way as clear as a glowing road of milk, stretching across the vault of heaven. Billions of stars glittered, cold and brilliant, looking quietly down on this small, warm bonfire on the Gobi, and on these briefly joyful lives gathered around it.
