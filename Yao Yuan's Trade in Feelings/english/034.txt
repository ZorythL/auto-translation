Yao Yuan's Reflections on Fate: Who Steers Our Destiny?
Yao Yuan struggled back up to the surface of consciousness through a distant chorus of voices.
"Yao Yuan… Yao Yuan! Wake up, can you hear me?"
The voice drifted near and far, as if filtered through a thick layer of water. Her head throbbed as though it might split open, her whole body limp and weak, without even the strength to lift an eyelid. Scenes from this life and the last whipped through her mind in a frenzy of overlapping images—the glint of the dance-studio mirrors, Zhuge Jinye's scalding breath, the bewildered face of her twenty-nine-year-old self—all the memories churned into a single tangled mass, until she could no longer tell what day or year it was.
It took an enormous effort just to pry her heavy eyelids open.
Her vision blurred, then sharpened. Jin Lan's face was the first thing she saw. Jin Lan was crouched by the side of the sofa, both hands gripping Yao Yuan's wrist so tightly her nails nearly dug into the flesh. Her eyes were red-rimmed, with unfallen tears still clinging to her lashes.
"Yuanyuan…" Jin Lan's voice cracked with the threat of tears, and also with relief.
Yao Yuan rolled her eyes around blankly, staring dazed at the scene before her. Yu Hao, Wang Qian, and Gao Jieyuan were all gathered at the side of the sofa, every face etched with lingering fear and worry.
"I…" She opened her mouth, throat dry and tight, her voice hoarse and disoriented as if just startled from sleep. "Where am I?"
"You scared us half to death!" Jin Lan swiped at her eyes, equal parts angry and frantic. "You were lying here all alone, out cold, and no matter how we shouted you wouldn't respond! We almost called an ambulance!"
Yao Yuan braced herself against the sofa and slowly sat up. Her temples pounded, and the heart-wrenching pain from before still lingered in her chest, leaving even her breaths slightly labored. She forced herself to stay calm, swiftly weaving together a cover story in her mind.
"It's nothing…" She pressed a hand to her temple, her voice still weak. "I just came out of the restroom, the alcohol hit me, and I walked into the wrong room… I thought I'd rest for a bit, and didn't expect to fall asleep."
She said it lightly, brushing it off, but only she knew that a film of cold sweat had broken out across her palms.
Time-travel—say a thing like that out loud and people would only take you for a lunatic.
"You'd been gone more than half an hour, and you weren't picking up your phone!" Yu Hao stood behind Jin Lan, brow tightly furrowed, his voice low. "We searched the whole corridor."
Yao Yuan lifted her eyes to him. Yu Hao's gaze was very deep, something roiling within it—it looked like worry, and like something else.
She looked away. Seeing the concern on everyone's faces, a warmth welled up in her heart, yet that warmth couldn't smother the bitterness and confusion underneath. She quickly murmured an apology, soothing them with a few words, forcing herself not to let anything strange show. Seeing that aside from her pale face and weary expression she seemed fine, everyone finally set their dangling hearts at ease. Jin Lan fussed and nagged for a good while longer, telling her never again to wander off alone after drinking, before they all gathered their things and filed out of the room one by one.
By the time they stepped out of the KTV, the night had grown deep. The north wind carried a chill, and it bit at her face, sobering her in an instant—though it couldn't blow away the chaotic images in her mind.
Yu Hao insisted on driving her home. Yao Yuan didn't refuse—she genuinely had no strength left.
The car was eerily quiet. Yao Yuan leaned back against the seat, eyes closed, pretending to be asleep. But beneath her lids her eyeballs trembled faintly. All the fragments churned in her mind—the despair of thirty-six, the bewilderment of twenty-nine, Zhuge Jinye's future, a fate that could not be changed…
The car came to a steady stop beneath her building. Yao Yuan slowly opened her eyes and unbuckled her seatbelt, but didn't get out right away.
Everything that had happened that night was too chaotic—the exhaustion of time-travel, the helplessness of witnessing the past, the lost and overwhelmed feeling about the future, plus the alcohol—her thoughts were jumbled to the breaking point, body and mind both pushed to their limits. Right now all she wanted was to hide away alone, to quietly digest every emotion, to face no one and say not one more word.
She raised her eyes to Yu Hao, the exhaustion and the weight on her mind no longer hideable, her voice as light as a feather. "I'm all right now. You head back. I won't ask you up."
Yu Hao turned to look at her. His profile was half-lit, half-shadowed in the glow of the streetlamp outside the window, his gaze deep, as if it could see straight through all of her feigned calm. He was silent for a few minutes, but he didn't press, didn't insist—he only reached out and very gently tousled her hair, an almost doting gesture, so tender it stung the bridge of her nose.
"All right." That was the only word he said.
With that, he withdrew his hand without the slightest hesitation, lingered not a moment longer, and turned to walk toward his own car. His back was straight and upright, yet it carried a quiet, understanding kind of retreat, gradually vanishing into the night.
Yao Yuan sat in the car, gazing in the direction he'd gone, unmoving for a long time. The north wind slipped in through the gap in the window, carrying its chill. She leaned back against the seat and finally let every disguise fall away, her eyes filled with bewilderment and pain.
She only felt tired. An exhaustion that seeped out from the marrow of her bones, as though someone had drained her of all her strength.
She didn't know how long she sat in the car, until the cold had soaked all the way through her, before she finally pushed the door open and dragged her heavy steps upstairs.
She opened the door to her home, and a wave of cold emptiness met her. Without turning on the lights, she groped her way to the side of the living-room sofa and sank into it completely, as if sinking into the deep sea.
Moonlight leaked in through the gap in the curtains, casting a pale streak of light across the floor.
She just curled up there in the darkness, letting those chaotic thoughts careen wildly through her mind. The little moments with Zhuge Jinye when she was twenty-nine, the weariness and pain in her twenty-nine-year-old eyes…
So the past she'd fought with everything to change had, in the end, been in vain. So the pain she'd thought she could sidestep, the endings she'd thought she could reverse, had been written in stone by fate long ago. Each time she traveled through time, she had merely glimpsed the ending in advance, yet still had to walk through every hardship with her own two feet—worse, she had to bear the double torment of foreseeing everything and being powerless to change it.
What was the point?
She asked herself over and over, but there was no answer—only the ceaseless dull ache in her chest, reminding her how real that conversation across time had been, and reminding her that she could never, in the end, escape a life already laid out.
The alcohol still worked faintly in her veins, and exhaustion surged over her whole body like a tide, yet she felt not the slightest urge to sleep. She raised a hand to cover her face, and from between her fingers a suppressed sob slowly seeped out. She didn't cry aloud, but she was more grief-stricken than at any other moment.
She thought of her younger self asking, "Do you have any advice for me?"—thought of how she'd never managed to give all the warnings she meant to—thought of that line, "Together we were ecstatically happy; apart, we each found our own peace"—and her heart ached all the more.
That was the sweetest memory of all the rest of her life, and yet, knowing the ending full well, she could only watch helplessly as her younger self rushed headlong in, watch that love begin, burn white-hot, and then move toward its parting.
The moonlight outside shone in through the gap in the curtains, casting a cold, clear stripe of light across the floor. Yao Yuan stayed curled on the sofa like that, letting the confusion, the regret, the pain in her heart spread unchecked. She didn't know when the next time-travel would come, didn't know whether she'd ever see her younger self again, and least of all knew with what frame of mind she should face the days ahead.
Only in this moment did she finally understand: some fates simply cannot be fought; some meetings are destined only to be remembered.
Not until the sky outside began to pale did she sink, in utter exhaustion, into a heavy sleep. In her dreams there was still the abyss into which she endlessly fell, and her own face in countless mirrors.
When Yao Yuan woke, the daylight was already bright.
She was lying on the bed in her own bedroom, the morning light filtering through the gauze curtains and scattering soft patches across the teak floor. Everything from last night—the faces of her friends in the [Crimson Night Palace] room, Yu Hao's silent gaze, and the eyes of her twenty-nine-year-old self in the mirror, brimming with tears—all of it was now frighteningly clear.
Thirty-six years old.
Yao Yuan sat up and walked barefoot to the full-length mirror. The woman in the mirror wore a silk robe, her long hair slightly tousled, a faint line at the corner of her eye that you wouldn't notice unless you looked closely—a mark left by the years. But her eyes were calm. She had been through betrayal, loss, the hardship of building a company; she had also known white-hot love and resolute parting. She was the "Godmother of Feelings" her fans spoke of, a company partner, the Teacher Yuanyuan who could make a breaking-down woman stop crying with just three sentences in the livestream room.
Yet in this moment, looking at herself in the mirror, she suddenly felt like a stranger.
"Godmother of Feelings." She murmured the title under her breath, the corner of her mouth tugging into a very faint, almost self-mocking curve.
What an irony. She could dissect feelings for countless others, point them out of their confusion, yet she couldn't untangle this snarled mess of her own life. Time and again she had traveled through time, trying to change the past, only to discover in the end that not an ounce of the pain she was meant to suffer had been spared, and not a single step of the road she was meant to walk had been avoided.
Fate was like a meticulously woven net. She'd thought she'd leapt clear of one knot, only to land on another, more tightly bound, intersection of its threads.
Yao Yuan walked into the bathroom and turned on the tap. The cold water splashed against her face, stinging her skin and bringing her fully awake.
She had no consultations scheduled today, and no livestream. The morning was time set aside for herself.
She brewed a pot of hand-drip coffee and carried the cup to the floor-to-ceiling window of her study. From the height of the twenty-seventh floor, she could see half the city's skyline waking in the morning light. Traffic streamed like a woven cloth, pedestrians moved like ants, each person rushing along their own track.
But how many of them had ever wondered: who laid down these tracks?
Yao Yuan took a sip of coffee, the bitter liquid sliding down her throat.
She thought of last night, in that pure-white space, when her twenty-nine-year-old self had said, "I want to walk it myself, and see whether the road goes through."
The stubbornness of youth, with the naivety of a moth flinging itself at the flame. Yet it was precisely that naivety that had led her through everything that came after—that fierce, fruitless love with Zhuge Jinye, the ups and downs of her career in Shanghai, being betrayed by people she trusted, and then gritting her teeth and climbing back up, founding her own brand, becoming the Yao Yuan of today.
If there were also a "future self" from beyond thirty-six who came to tell the present me not to make the "Pretty Big One AI Emotional Model," would I obey outright?
And if the twenty-six-year-old Yao Yuan back then had truly listened to the "thirty-six-year-old me" who came back from the future—her own future self's warning—and left Shuai Hongqiang immediately, gone straight to Shanghai, would her life have been better?
Maybe she wouldn't have gone through that heartbreak. But maybe, too, she wouldn't have ended up at that MCN agency in Shanghai, pushed into an even deeper abyss by the boss she had once trusted with her whole heart.
That unfinished warning—"Whatever you do, do not, with him…"
Yao Yuan's fingertips tightened, the warmth of the cup pressing against her palm.
It was Ding Shitong. Her Shanghai boss—the man who had once taught her, hands-on, how to invest, how to operate a business, who had brought her into the industry, and who, in the end, just as her wings were growing strong, had used the filthiest means to kick her out of the game.
If her twenty-nine-year-old self had known in advance, would she have steered clear? Maybe. But if she'd steered clear of Ding Shitong, would she still have founded her own brand? Would she still have become today's "Godmother of Feelings"?
Fate was never a simple game of "dodging the pitfalls." Every choice, right or wrong, was tied to flesh and blood, and had grown into the bones she carried now. Pull out any one piece, and she might no longer be who she was today.
Yao Yuan set down her coffee cup and walked to the bookshelf. An entire wall of books—psychology, sociology, relationships, personal growth, and quite a few volumes of philosophy and religion. After becoming a feelings blogger, her reading had soared; she was always trying to dredge up, from the wisdom of others, a few lines that could soothe the human heart.
But this time, what she pulled out was a very thin booklet—a modern annotated edition of "Zhuangzi: On the Equality of Things." The page that fell open read: "In being born there is dying; in dying there is being born. In the possible there is the impossible; in the impossible there is the possible."
All things are in flux; right and wrong have no absolute boundary.
She closed the book, her gaze settling on a sandalwood box in the corner of the shelf. She walked over and opened it. Inside were the mirrors she had collected, bit by bit, over the years. There was a handmade Venetian mirror she'd picked up while traveling, a cloisonné enamel vanity mirror a friend had given her, and a very small vintage hand mirror set with dark-red agate.
But not one of them could send her back into the past the way the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror did.
Why mirrors?
Yao Yuan sat back down at the desk and opened her laptop. She didn't search keywords aimlessly the way she had when she was young. First she sent her assistant a message: "That background research I had you look into last time, on the 'Winter Auction' held in Jinshi eight years ago—did you find it? Especially the roster of resident antique appraisers, the master surnamed Chen who specializes in authenticating antique bronze mirrors—did you locate him? I want it detailed: age, credentials, lineage, contact information. Also, look into whether there's any literature or folk record about the term 'Mirror Pact.' Compile every lead you find and send it to me."
After sending the message, she leaned back in her chair and closed her eyes.
She had heard plenty of bizarre, otherworldly stories: people who claimed to have dreamed of a past life, people convinced they were "cursed," people who always felt their lives were controlled by some invisible hand. In the past, she had mostly explained these with psychology—trauma flashbacks, the workings of the subconscious, compulsive repetition.
But when this kind of thing happened to her, every theory seemed pale and useless.
Three time-travels, all connected to mirrors. The first was the makeup mirror in her bathroom at home, the second the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror, the third the wall mirror in the KTV room.
According to Shuai Hongqiang, ever since she had sealed the Mirror Pact with the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror, any mirror could serve as the medium for all three of her time-travels—but the trigger was a violent surge of emotion in her, stirring up the buried feelings she had never properly dealt with.
The first time-travel may have happened because of that day's livestream call-in: that girl, freshly graduated from college and "dumped," whose situation was so much like hers and Jiang Hai's, triggering the past she'd buried and the hidden ache that had never fully vanished from her heart.
The second time-travel may have happened because she'd gone to eat at a mall and seen Shuai Hongqiang with his family—that adorable little boy stirring up the regret she pretended not to care about but had buried deep inside.
And the third time-travel? Yao Yuan sorted through everything from the day before and reached a conclusion: yesterday's livestream call-in had several women who, in seeking a partner or in their married lives, had overlooked the importance of ██—failing to grasp the importance of "physical attraction" between men and women. Later, catalyzed by the alcohol, it had triggered her own "sweet frosting."
And there was one more thing Shuai Hongqiang hadn't said: how to undo the Mirror Pact.
What she needed to do now was start from a professional appraisal channel and trace the origins of the "Mirror Pact" and similar objects.
After sending the message, Yao Yuan turned back to the computer screen. She opened an encrypted folder containing a "case library" she had built as a psychology-and-feelings blogger—all personal information had, of course, been anonymized. But it recorded many "sensations," "dreams," or moments of déjà vu described by those involved, things hard to explain by common sense.
She typed in the keywords "mirror," "recurring dreams," "temporal illusion."
A dozen or so records popped up. She read through them one by one.
One woman kept dreaming of a shattered mirror, and each time she woke, she could find some unlucky real-life event that corresponded to the dream. Later this blogger had guided her back through her memories, and discovered that as a child she had once broken her mother's most treasured vanity mirror and been severely punished for it. That mirror had become a symbol of fear in her subconscious.
There was also a boy who always said he could see another "self" in the mirror doing different things. A psychological evaluation ruled out mental illness, and in the end it was found to be a severe dissociative tendency, rooted in childhood trauma.
These could all be explained by psychology.
But her situation was different. That was no dream, and no hallucination. It was a real, conversable "time-travel" in which she could perceive emotion and the body. And what's more, what she saw was her "past self," and she could speak with her.
This touched on a more fundamental question: self-identity.
Who is "I"? Is it this present conscious subject, here and now, or an existence formed by countless "past-moment selves" strung together in continuity? If the "past me" can hold an independent conversation, is she still "me"? Or is every "momentary me" an independent, parallel existence?
Yao Yuan thought of the "many-worlds interpretation" in physics—every choice splitting off a parallel universe. So in countless parallel universes, were there not countless Yao Yuans, having made different choices, living utterly different lives?
In one universe, she might never have left Beijing, tangled with Jiang Hai for a lifetime.
In another universe, she might have steered clear of Shuai Hongqiang and gone straight to Shanghai, only to meet some other setback.
In yet another universe, she might have ended up happily ever after with Zhuge Jinye…
Then how had this universe of hers—this universe in which she became "Godmother of Feelings" Yao Yuan—been the one chosen? Would there be another "future Yao Yuan," based on the present her, who would come to help her correct her present?
And what role did mirrors play in all of it? A passage? A trigger? Or… the proof of some kind of pact?
"Mirror Pact."
Yao Yuan softly read the two words aloud. She opened a new document and began to record:
Hypothesis One: The mirror is a special medium that, when a person's emotions swing violently or their consciousness is weakened, can connect the "self-consciousness" across different times and spaces.
Hypothesis Two: This connection is not unlimited; it seems to follow "rules." So far, it appears she can only meet her self at specific ages (22, 26, 29), and the time for communication is limited, forcibly cut off.
Hypothesis Three: The content of the communication may influence the future, but to a limited degree, and may incur an unforeseeable "cost" (as in the Shuai Hongqiang incident—the process changed, but the ending was similar, or even worse).
Hypothesis Four: The mirror itself may have a "binding" or "contract" attribute. The term "Mirror Pact" hints at some kind of agreement or constraint. The exact meaning needs to be uncovered.
Having written all this, Yao Yuan leaned back in her chair and let out a long breath.
All these years, she had used reason to take apart emotions, used logic to analyze relationships. But when the problem landed on herself—especially a problem that defied common sense—she found that she still panicked, still feared, still didn't know what to do.
But she had also learned one other thing: rather than passively endure, better to actively seek answers.
If this was her fate, then she would know clearly, too—just who had decided this fate, and how it could be broken.
The phone on the desk buzzed. Her assistant had sent over the preliminary materials.
"Boss Yao, on the Winter Auction side, there are three veteran masters skilled at authenticating antique bronze mirrors. One of them, a master named Chen Yanqing, is seventy-three this year. He studied under the late grandmaster of antiques, Old Master Zhou, and is said to have a particular expertise in obscure, out-of-the-way old objects. He commands great respect in the circle, but his temperament is rather reclusive and he rarely appears in public. His contact information has been sent to your email. As for the 'Mirror Pact,' public material is extremely scarce. I only found someone mentioning the term on a very small folklore-research forum, saying it has to do with some ancient folk notion of an object 'recognizing its master' or a 'spatial medium,' but the account was vague. I've tried to contact the person who posted it, but they haven't replied yet."
Yao Yuan replied: "Set up an appointment for me with Master Chen Yanqing, as soon as possible. Tell him I have an old family-heirloom mirror I'd like him to take a look at. Be courteous about it."
"Will do, Boss Yao."
Next, then, was to go see that Master Chen Yanqing.
Yao Yuan shut off the computer and walked to the window. The sun was just right, the city's skyline clear and resolute in the light.
She suddenly recalled something her dance teacher had said many years ago: "When you dance, don't always be thinking about whether the next move is right. You have to trust your body—it remembers all the rhythms. Just dance. Wrong is right, and right is wrong; only when you've finished is the dance complete."
Perhaps life was the same.
Always thinking about correcting the past, always worrying about choosing the wrong future, you only ended up trapped in the present, unable to move.
Since fate had given her this mirror, given her this power to travel through time, then she would use it—to see clearly, to understand, and then, to choose.
Even if, after the choosing, it was still the track laid out for her.
But this time she would walk it with her own eyes open, step by step.
Yao Yuan turned and walked into her dressing room.
She picked out a fitted leather jumpsuit-dress, tailored close to the body but never tight, the waistline cinched to just the right degree—simple and striking, with a cold, alluring air all its own. On her feet, a pair of black high-shafted boots, crisp and icy. Paired with a luxurious fur coat, it was at once regal and seductive.
She did her makeup carefully before the mirror, drawing her brows and setting her face, finishing with a stroke of rich, vintage true-red lipstick, bright and dazzling.
The woman in the mirror—calm of gaze, composed of bearing.
She was Yao Yuan, the Godmother of Feelings whom everyone looked up to. She was the Yao Yuan who had been through three time-travels and seen her past selves. She was the Yao Yuan who now carried a riddle in her heart, about to set out and probe the hidden truth behind the mirror.
And she was, too, the Yao Yuan who would keep walking forward, no matter what the truth turned out to be.
Picking up her handbag and car keys, she took one last look at herself in the mirror, then turned and pushed the door open and out.
Beyond the door was the world she had to face, and the answers waiting for her.
