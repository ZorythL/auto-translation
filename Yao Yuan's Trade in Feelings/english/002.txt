No one saw that before every stream she needed at least two hours of absolute silence and solitude, emptying all the tangled thoughts of daily life, like a warrior about to take the field wiping down armor and blade — honing and tuning her chain of logic, her stock of scripts, her emotional valves and mental state to the sharpest, steadiest, 'drawn-from-the-sheath' condition. Through the four hours of the stream, her brain had to run like a top-tier processor in overclock, multithreaded and parallel: instantly catching the real demands and deep fears behind a caller's words; monitoring in real time the latent drift of opinion on the public screen, ready at any moment to steer or suppress it; precisely controlling the rhythm of her every line, its emotional density, and the various readings and legal-moral risks it might trigger; and at the same time weaving every shard of information seamlessly into her self-consistent, forceful, and highly shareable value framework of 'quantifying emotional interest.' She had to keep absolute reason and control at all times, yet at the crucial moment release, with precision, a thread of almost cruel tenderness that was 'for your own good'; she had to give advice that was resolute and seemingly irrefutable, while leaving herself, in every single line, enough room to maneuver and reinterpret on the legal and moral plane. Every minute was a high-intensity, high-density burning of mental force.
For four hours she had to hold that 'breath' the entire time — the 'professional breath' that made the eyes blaze, made the voice cohere, gave every line an undeniable weight and incitement. That breath could not scatter. If it scattered, the whole thing collapsed, and that invincible, forever-clear-eyed virtual image called 'Yao Yuan' would show its cracks.
The little assistant came over softly and set a cup of honey water at just the right temperature on the low table beside the sofa. "Sister, you worked hard. That last bit on 'cashing out' — the comments are going wild, calling you the 'ceiling of clear-headedness.' There are some curses too… but the heat is exploding."
Yao Yuan didn't open her eyes, only let out a barely audible "Mm" from her nose by way of reply. The ceiling? That was nothing but onlookers, standing at a safe distance, voicing their bloodthirsty, excited admiration at a brilliant and brutal 'emotional surgery.' She rubbed at her still-tight brow, fingertips icy. Success? Glamour? Yes — these days she had a luxury car with a driver waiting when she went out, lived in a penthouse overlooking half the city's river view, and could send tens of thousands of people into thought or argument with a single line. But beneath this seemingly 'effortless' success was her day-after-day chewing over and dissecting the most subtle, most unbearable side of human nature; her sustained tolerance and digestion of a flood of negative emotion and extreme cases; and, once the stream was off — like now — a soul-deep exhaustion that made lifting even a single finger feel like spending the last of her strength, along with an unseen, sediment-laden emptiness she had to swallow down alone.
The little assistant thoughtfully shut off all the harsh photography lights in the stream room, leaving only a soft-lit floor lamp in the corner. The afternoon sun, filtered through the large glass window, turned tame and languid, slanting into the room and warmly enveloping the body on the sofa — that body that had, for the moment, laid down all its armor and its halo, looking unusually soft and worn.
Glamour was her armor — and her cocoon.
And that Yellow River outside the window, forever rushing on, muddy and silent; that sediment that clung to her soles with every upward step she climbed, and had perhaps, by now, quietly seeped into her very blood and bones — that was the real, heavy ground tone beneath this dazzling stage.
