Chapter 3 — A Tiger Brought Low Is Bullied by Dogs

In the living room, a woman's voice drifted out of a phone—thin and cold, like silk soaked in ice water. It was wistful, resentful, faintly mocking, and carried a weary, deathly chill:
"This upside-down world, how absurd it is
A kind soul never comes to a good end
This profit-chasing world is hard to fathom
You can pluck a sky full of stars and not trade them for a single candy
Different mouths telling the same lies
Different beasts trapped in the same skins
One hand doing every evil deed, smug and proud
The other pressed in prayer, begging the gods for pardon
□□ dress up in gold, all righteousness as they sit in high halls
The pheasant calls itself a phoenix, appearing only after a thousand summons
The fox turns merchant for a day, and the ants lose home and life
The lynx trusts the jackal—one heart traded for a body of wounds
The camel carries its pack, wandering lifetime after lifetime
The old dog dares not bark, sleepless and tireless for a mouthful of broth
The migrant bird through cold and heat can find no wall to block the wind
The bee, neither grasping nor fighting, stores up grain for others in the end
Different mouths telling the same lies
Different beasts trapped in the same skins
One hand doing every evil deed, smug and proud
The other pressed in prayer, begging the gods for pardon"
It was inside this song that Shuai Hongqiang jolted awake.
The afternoon sun, filtered through double-paned glass, had gone lukewarm and feeble, sprawling lazily across the living-room floor. He lay on the sofa, a thin cashmere throw over him. Shuai Hongqiang hadn't gone back to the bedroom; the master bed was too big, too quiet—quiet enough to make a man's heart race. The sofa was better. The space was cramped, but it gave him the illusion of being wrapped up, held.
He'd thought he couldn't fall asleep. His eyelids were heavy, but his mind was strangely clear, his nerves drawn taut every second, like a frightened stray dog with its ears always pricked, watching for the slightest stir outside. Inside, the sunlight carried visible motes of dust, turning slowly before his eyes, rising and falling. He stared until his eyes went dry, and at last his consciousness was dragged into a gray-white murk of fog.
He dreamed he was running across a vast, unfinished construction site. It was the sports-and-culture center he had taken on a few years back, its blueprints grand and sweeping. But there were no workers on the site, no machinery—only countless sheets of white A4 paper, sent whirling by the wind across the sky, like a silent blizzard. He fought desperately to grab a few, to make out the words on them, but the moment his fingertips touched them, they crumbled to ash. In the distance, where the building should have risen, there was only an enormous, bottomless foundation pit, like a jeering mouth.
He cried out and snapped his eyes open. A dream. Once awake, his temple throbbed with a stabbing, pulsing pain.
The song was still going in the living room, line after line, each one pricking into his eardrums like a needle:
"……□□ dress up in gold, all righteousness as they sit in high halls……The old dog dares not bark, sleepless and tireless for a mouthful of broth……"
"Damn, that's vicious." Shuai Hongqiang stared at the minimalist ceiling lamp overhead and gave a soundless, twisted little grin, his throat dry and aching. These lyrics—not a single line mentioned the human heart, yet every line was the human heart. Vicious as a boning knife, scraping away every last scrap of what lay beneath a person's skin. Back in his glory days, when he listened to music he listened only to the fun of it, never really caring what the words said. Now the lyrics had sunk into him, and every word felt like a little hammer striking his heart, dull and echoing.
Shuai Hongqiang steadied himself, got up, and poured a cup of cold tea, draining it in one go. Only then did the heavy, muddled exhaustion in his body start to ebb away.
"Ring-a-ling—ring-a-ling—"
The phone rang right then. Not his usual ringtone, but the most basic, most piercing default alert, jarring in the silent living room, like an alarm.
Shuai Hongqiang's hand jerked, and he nearly dropped the purple-clay teacup. He stared at the string of numbers on the screen—no contact name, but the location read "Langfang, Hebei." The one before had been "Foshan, Guangdong"; the one before that, "Chengdu, Sichuan." The collection agency's trick: virtual numbers, calling from every corner of the country, running you ragged, keeping your nerves on edge every second.
He drew in a deep breath. The breath went in and settled, heavy, in his lungs. He answered.
"Hello." His voice was a little hoarse, but steady enough.
"Good afternoon. Am I speaking with Mr. Shuai Hongqiang?" It was a young man's voice, standard Mandarin, the enunciation so crisp it sounded trained—almost too clear. In the background were the faint clatter of keyboards and other voices speaking just as fast, just as flat, like some cold-blooded chorus.
"Speaking. Who's this?" Shuai Hongqiang walked to the window and looked down at the street below.
"Good afternoon, Mr. Shuai. I'm a collections specialist with Zhongxin Asset Management Co., Ltd., employee number K307, surname Wu." The man sounded like he was reading from a notice. "Acting under commission from Bank of Communications Co., Ltd., I'm contacting you regarding the eight million yuan in principal owed by your affiliated enterprise 'Hongchang Real Estate Co., Ltd.,' along with the overdue penalty interest and breach penalties accrued to date. May I ask whether you have a concrete repayment plan today?"
Here we go again. Shuai Hongqiang shut his eyes, his fingers unconsciously tightening on the window frame. "Specialist Wu, I've said it many times—it's not that I won't pay. It's that the city government still owes me fifteen million yuan in project payments, and they keep dragging it out! If that money comes back, I can settle your debt immediately!"
"Mr. Shuai, your dispute with a third party—the city government—has nothing to do with the debt my company has been commissioned to collect." The man's tone was utterly flat, even carrying a touch of programmed impatience. "According to our system records, you have used this excuse to put us off many times. The bank's patience, and ours, is limited. You are currently in serious default."
"Put you off?" A surge of anger shot up, and Shuai Hongqiang raised his voice. "That's a fact! There's a contract in black and white! They won't give me my money—what am I supposed to pay with? If you're so capable, go get the government's debt back for me! Get it back and I'll settle the whole thing, principal and interest, in one shot!"
The other end fell silent for two seconds. When the young man spoke again, his voice had dropped a little, the background noise seemingly deliberately muted, every word landing like a bead of ice, crisp and clear: "Mr. Shuai, please stay calm. My company operates lawfully and within the rules. You have two choices right now. First: within three business days, come to our office and sign an installment repayment commitment, with a first payment of no less than five percent of the total debt—that is, four hundred thousand yuan. Second: if you refuse or are unable to comply, tomorrow we will formally submit materials to the people's court with jurisdiction, applying for measures to restrict your high consumption, and applying to freeze all bank accounts in the names of you, your spouse, and your children, along with WeChat, Alipay, and other third-party payment channels."
Shuai Hongqiang felt a rush of hot blood slam into the top of his skull. "So what do you want? Hound me to death—will that make money appear?"
The other end was silent again for two seconds, and then the young man's voice dropped a little, the keyboard clatter in the background seeming to recede as well, and he went on in that tone, steady to the point of cruelty: "Mr. Shuai, we're only following procedure. Also, a friendly reminder—" he paused, as if weighing his words, but the threat in them spilled out, naked. "We've noticed you have a son and a daughter. Your daughter, Shuai Wenrui, will sit the college entrance exam next year, and her grades are excellent. Certain special institutions—military, public security academies, some classified majors at key universities—are extremely strict in the political-review stage about the credit records and enforcement status of immediate family members. For your child's future, you'd do well to consider this carefully."
"Don't you dare!" Shuai Hongqiang growled, the veins at his temple jumping.
The blood in his whole body seemed to rush to his head in an instant, then freeze the next second. Wenrui… his daughter with his ex-wife, Qiu Ying. Shuai Wenrui's quiet, stubborn eyes flashed through his mind; even as a child she'd never given him much to worry about. The college exam… the political review…
"You… you're threatening me!" He forced a few more words out through clenched teeth.
"Mr. Shuai, this is a risk advisory, not a threat." The man's voice returned to that professional calm.
"Fine, fine… you're really something," he heard his own voice shaking, whether from rage or fear he couldn't tell. "Go get the fifteen million in project payments the city government owes me! Once that's back, I'll pay off everything in one shot, principal and interest! And I'll personally throw in fifty thousand for your trouble! How about that? Huh?!"
From the other end came a very faint, almost inaudible snort of a laugh—or maybe he only imagined it. "I'm sorry, Mr. Shuai. A debt is a debt; a dispute is a dispute. We all go by the rules, and we handle only the lawful claims we're commissioned to collect. Please give me a clear answer today."
"I'll answer your mother!" Months of pent-up rage, humiliation, and despair detonated in that instant. Shuai Hongqiang roared into the receiver, then jabbed the call dead.
The phone screen went dark, reflecting his own face, slightly distorted with anger. His chest heaved violently, like a fish out of water.
A few seconds later, the ringtone exploded again, another unfamiliar number flashing on the screen, location "Liuzhou, Guangxi."
Shuai Hongqiang didn't even look. His finger swiped across the screen, blocking it, the motion so practiced it was heartbreaking. In the past six months, he'd blocked more numbers than the contacts he'd saved up over the past few decades.
The living room sank back into dead silence. Only that long-finished song lingered, leaving behind an empty stretch of background sound. "……This upside-down world, how absurd it is……A kind soul never comes to a good end……This profit-chasing world is hard to fathom……"
Outside, the sunlight blazed on the glass curtain wall of a distant high-rise, glittering gold, sharp enough to make his eyes sting.
A tiger brought low is bullied by dogs.
The phrase leaped into his head without warning. What welled up after it was something more tangled, more viscous—rage, humiliation, and a thread of icy panic.
Shuai Hongqiang thought, with a self-mocking edge: A tiger? Was he even worthy of that? A real tiger, even brought low onto the plain, kept some of its old menace, claws that could still tear off a few hunks of flesh. And him? What was he? A creature with its teeth pulled out and its legs broken, able only to hide in a corner licking its wounds, hounded and bitten by the very stray dogs that once fawned and wagged their tails at him—a homeless cur.
His ruin was plain to the eye. Compared with the old "Boss Shuai" who drove a Mercedes S-Class, frequented the clubs, and was waited on by crowds, it was heaven and earth. The fifteen million bad debt from the city was like a yoke around his neck; the eight million overdue at the bank was a sword hanging over his head. His other businesses and assets—everything that could be liquidated, everything that could pay down debt—had mostly been poured in over the past two years, just barely plugging the other holes, holding on to this apartment and a basic standard of living. Lin Wan's income from livestreaming was steady, even higher than many white-collar workers', and it carried the household's daily expenses and the children's tuition, letting him still maintain a bare minimum of dignity. Only he knew how fragile this "dignity" was—a thin sheet of ice, with bone-chilling cold water and man-eating debt right beneath it. The people who used to fawn on him now either avoided him or, like this collections specialist, jabbed at his sorest spots in the most "compliant" way possible.
He thought back to those years. That account manager surnamed Wang from the Bank of Communications, who'd dropped by his "Hongchang Real Estate" office every other day, pouring tea and offering cigarettes, smiling so wide his eyes vanished. "Boss Shuai, try this, the Jinjunmei tea just came in!" "Boss Shuai, our bank's got a new credit offer for quality enterprises—high limits, low rates. Why not consider it? Think of it as helping a little brother hit his targets!" Back then he was a fairly well-known private entrepreneur in the city, with two well-regarded residential developments under his belt, smooth relations between business and government, and banks chasing after him to hand over money. He really hadn't been short of cash, but he couldn't withstand little Manager Wang's pestering, and figured a bit more working capital never hurt—and that was how the eight million came to be.
How many years was that?
The pandemic had swept in like a sudden tsunami, carrying everything away. Construction halted, sales stalled, and the cash flow snapped with a single crack. The bank that had once begged him to take its loans turned on him faster than flipping a page. "Little Manager Wang," now promoted to branch president, was nowhere to be seen, replaced by these cold "specialists" scattered across the country. The "friends" who'd called him brother dodged him wherever they could—not answering calls, not replying on WeChat. The fifteen million in project payments from the city government he'd been chasing for three years, running his legs off and talking himself hoarse, only ever got him the same reply: "Fiscal difficulties—we're coordinating."
"Thirty years on this side of the river, thirty years on the other…" he murmured to himself, his voice dry. Who needed thirty years? Three or four was enough to drop a man from the clouds into the mud, smashing him to pieces.
He was alone in the house. Wenyao had long since left for school. Lin Wan… Lin Wan wasn't home either. He didn't know whether she'd gone for a run or off to some library or café again. She had more and more of her own things to do, more and more of her own plans. She'd never again be like she was just after they married, giving up her doctoral spot because of a pregnancy, never again revolving around him and the children in everything.
The phone rang again—a local, unfamiliar number. He hesitated two seconds, then answered.
"Hey, Boss Shuai!" The other voice was loud, the background noisy. "It's me, Old Liu! Old Liu from Tongda Building Materials! Remember?"
Shuai Hongqiang frowned and kept his tone as level as he could. "Boss Liu, hello."
"Aiya, Boss Shuai, sorry to bother you. Just wanted to ask… that payment the city government owes you—any word on it lately?" Old Liu's voice carried a shrewd warmth. "See, that batch of exterior-wall stone you took from me back then—the balance has been dragging quite a while now… I run a small operation, I really…"
Shuai Hongqiang shut his eyes and pinched the bridge of his nose. "Boss Liu, the moment the money comes back, you'll be the first one I settle with. Things really are hard right now—you know the situation."
"I know, I know. Aiya, it's not easy for anyone." Old Liu laughed it off, but his tone took a turn. "Still, Boss Shuai, I hear your wife's doing livestreaming now—they say livestreaming makes good money these days… so I was wondering whether maybe…"
"Boss Liu," Shuai Hongqiang cut in, his voice going colder, "I'll pay the money back. The moment there's news, I'll let you know. I've got something to deal with here, so I'll hang up now."
Without waiting for a reply, he ended the call. There was a tightness in his chest. Old Liu used to bow lower than anyone when he saw him.
He walked back to the tea table, poured another cup of the cold-through tea, and gulped it down. The water slid down his throat, leaving him a little clearer-headed, pressing down that floating, unsteady feeling a bit.
He couldn't just sit here. He had to move—even if it was only aimless wandering.
He went to the bathroom and freshened up. The man in the mirror had faint blue-black shadows under his eyes, the mark of long anxiety and lack of sleep. Shuai Hongqiang splashed his face with cold water, droplets rolling along his jawline into his collar—his clothes were always pressed neat by Lin Wan, kept tidy and impeccable. He changed, choosing a fine-quality crew-neck cashmere sweater, a style from several years back, but the cut still crisp.
In the underground garage, the Mercedes S-Class and the sports car he used to park there were long gone—sold to fill the holes. Only in the corner stood his old BMW X6. Deep black, a thin layer of dust on the body, but the silhouette still commanding. It was the first good car he'd bought after he'd made his name, and it had run with him to construction sites, met clients, and carried the whole family on trips. He couldn't bear to sell it, and it wouldn't fetch a good price anyway, so he'd kept it—like a stubborn medal from days gone by.
He pulled open the door and got in. The interior was well kept, the leather seats still soft, though the mileage was high by now. He turned the key, and the engine roared low to life, the sound no longer as full-bodied as in its prime, carrying a wheezing, raspy edge that echoed in the silent garage. Like an old beast caged too long behind iron bars, scarred and weary yet still refusing to lie all the way down, letting out a whimper of defiance. The car rolled slowly out of the garage and out of the compound. The gatekeeper recognized the car and saluted. Shuai Hongqiang gave a slight nod. The sun fell just right across the windshield, a little dazzling.
He gripped the wheel and merged into the gathering early rush-hour traffic. The string inside him was still drawn taut, but life had to go on like this. A tiger brought low onto the plain was still, in the end, a tiger. As long as he could still stand, this breath in him couldn't be allowed to scatter completely. It was just that this barely-held-together "ordinary middle-class" life felt, at every step, like walking on thin ice, listening to the faint, ceaseless "crack" of the layer beneath his feet.
