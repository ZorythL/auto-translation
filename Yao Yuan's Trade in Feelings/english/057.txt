Chapter 57 — Seeking a Way to Break the Mirror Pact — Old Chen

When Shuai Hongqiang woke, daylight had already slipped through the gap in the heavy curtains, cutting a sharp blade of light across the floor.

Out of habit, he checked his phone first. Two emails sat on the screen. One was from the tender office of the city construction commission: "Invitation to Tender for the Construction and Operation of the Jinshi Public Fitness Center Project (Phase One)" — an official notice from the commission. Shuai Hongqiang stared at that line of subject text for a good ten-odd seconds. He sat down and read through the whole thing slowly, carefully. This wasn't a feeler, wasn't an informal sounding-out. It was in black and white, stamped with an official seal — a formal invitation to bid. His company, Hongchang Properties, was named among the seven units invited. Ranked toward the bottom, sure, but in the door all the same.

The other email was a weekly construction report from his project manager, with a few photos of the site attached. That old-town redevelopment support project — the one he'd discussed with Yao Yuan before the New Year and then taken on independently once the funding came through — now bristled with tower cranes, pile foundations driven deep, everything advancing on schedule, even a little smoother than expected. A few approval processes that had been stuck for ages had, inexplicably, gone through recently. He leaned back against the headboard and stared at those two photos for a long while. The sun glared off the phone screen, a little harsh on the eyes.

The phone buzzed again. A message from Lin Wan — she was at the supermarket, asking what he wanted for dinner, mentioning that Wenyao's math had improved on this month's exam and the teacher had singled him out for praise. A cute little smiley face followed.

His career was back on track, even faintly trending upward; home was steady, wife and son both doing well. After those years of running himself ragged, hitting wall after wall, lying awake until dawn — all of this should be called "sweetness after long bitterness," or "a small fullness in life."

He should be content, and he did feel a steady warmth.

But beneath that warmth there was always one spot left hanging — cold, hollow, sinking a little now and then.

He thought of Yao Yuan again.

This afternoon at four, they'd arranged to see Old Chen at the Guanguta Hall. About that mirror. About all that mystifying business of "time-travel" and "borrowed fortune." About the exhaustion that flickered through her eyes now and then, the kind she covered so perfectly.

Was there any way at all to undo this uncanny "Mirror Pact"?

Two-fifty in the afternoon, outside the Guanguta Hall.

Shuai Hongqiang had arrived early. He didn't go in. He leaned against a corridor pillar by the door and lit a cigarette. The antiques street was quiet in the afternoon; the sun lit the old wooden plaque reading "Guanguta Hall" until it gleamed, the years legible in its grain. Halfway through the cigarette, he remembered that winter eight years ago.

That auction. He and Yao Yuan had not long parted, both of them carrying wounds, carrying a hardness. When the auction catalogue arrived in his hands, he'd only meant to leaf through it idly — until he saw that "Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror." The print was blurry, but the distinctive shape, the intricate cloud-and-thunder pattern, and the line in the description — "date uncertain, unusual ornamentation, possibly with an occult background" — somehow lodged in his eye, and on instinct he felt Yao Yuan would love it.

As if driven by something, he went. Raised his paddle, bid, and in the end took it for a price that was neither high nor low — but at the time still pocket money to him. When he had the thing in hand, he sat alone in his car for a long time. The mirror was packed in a brocade box; to the touch it was strangely, bone-deep cold — not like metal, more like gripping a piece of ice drawn from a deep pool. In the dim light of the car, the Kui-dragon pattern on its back seemed to circulate a faint, dark sheen. Those beast-eyes, inlaid with silver dots, looked coldly back at you from any angle — indifferent, hollow, as if taking the measure of your soul.

A strange tremor went through his heart then, and he nearly turned it over to someone else on the spot. But in the end he carried it home and locked it deep in a cabinet.

Later, when his business hit rock bottom, when he was at the end of his rope, a single thought rose up at that moment — soundless, like an undercurrent beneath the water. That "maybe" became a phantom straw to clutch at.

"Give it a try... what if?"

That thought, faint to the point of being negligible, was like a pebble dropped into stagnant water. He hadn't thought hard at the time about what "give it a try" meant — only had the vague sense that the mirror might bring a "different" kind of aura. He wrapped it carefully and decided to give it to the person who, as he saw it then, had the "most energy" of anyone around him, the one "most likely to change her circumstances": Yao Yuan.

Now that he thought back, that unnatural coldness at his fingertips, that eerie answering gaze of the beast-eyes — perhaps they had all been warnings from some unseen quarter. And he, his mind clouded by his troubles, had ignored them.

When he gave her the mirror, that secret, barely-admitted delusion he carried — "maybe she can turn things around" — that was the starting point of the "pact."

Now he understood it all.

"Shuai Hongqiang."

A clear, cool voice cut into his recollection. He looked up.

Yao Yuan was stepping out of that sleek-lined red Bentley of hers. The afternoon sun was fierce; she raised a hand to shield her eyes, and a platinum square watch set with diamonds flashed a sharp glint at her wrist. Today she wore a true-red silk shirt, the collar carelessly open at two buttons, baring slender collarbones and a very fine diamond chain whose pendant vanished beneath the placket. Below, sharply cut black wide-leg trousers set off her tall, commanding figure. Her long hair was permed into loose big curls, worn down without fuss; her makeup was exquisite, her red lips striking, and a large pair of sunglasses hid most of her face — the kind of aggressive beauty that turns heads on the street and dominates a boardroom.

But Shuai Hongqiang saw it plainly. When she pushed the sunglasses up and tucked them carelessly into her shirt pocket, the faint bluish shadow beneath her eyes — even the best concealer hadn't fully hidden it. Her spirits looked passable, even "good," you might say, but that bone-deep exhaustion, forced down by will, was like the hairline crackle on porcelain: it couldn't fool anyone who knew.

"Yao Yuan." He stubbed out the cigarette he hadn't even lit and nodded. "Let's go in. Master Chen is waiting."

Inside the Guanguta Hall, tea-scent still curled in the air, the light dim, carrying the particular smell of aged timber and old books — an atmosphere that settled the mind.

Old Chen seemed to have anticipated their coming. Tea sat clear in three purple-clay cups. He gestured for them to sit, his gaze lingering a moment on Yao Yuan's bright but visibly tired face, sweeping over the costly diamond collarbone chain at her throat, before meeting Shuai Hongqiang's faintly anxious eyes. He let out a barely perceptible sigh.

"Old Chen, sorry to trouble you." Yao Yuan settled into the seat across from him, composed, a measured faint smile at her lips. First she pushed a brocade box gently toward the center of the tea table. "When I reached out earlier, you said we'd talk in detail come spring, so we've been waiting for you to return to Jinshi. The Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror — I've brought it today." Her tone was gentle, a junior's respect for an elder, but with no excess pleasantries; she went straight to the heart of it.

She paused, her gaze passing over Shuai Hongqiang beside her, then settling back on Old Chen's face. Her smile dimmed a little, revealing the candor of a pragmatist after answers. "I'll be honest with you. Just yesterday afternoon, I traveled back through time again, against my will."

Her account was steady and clear, with no deliberate dramatizing — as if she were reporting a project bottleneck that urgently needed solving. "Counting this one, it's the fifth time. Every time it ends, it feels much the same — utterly drained, mind hollowed out, like I've pulled several all-nighters in a row. The good news, for now, is that rest brings me back, and physical exams have found nothing organically wrong."

She leaned forward slightly, eyes fixed intently on Old Chen, a barely detectable urgency entering her voice — the urgency of someone seeking a solution — though she kept her cool framework. "But this can't go on. A completely passive, unpredictable experience like this has too much uncertainty; it's no long-term plan. That feeling of having no control, of your soul suddenly being yanked away — it's eerie and deeply unpleasant. We came today hoping you could help us get to the bottom of this whole thing, see if there's any thread to follow."

She showed no fragility, but didn't force toughness either — just a pragmatic stance, seeking professional help out of a real predicament. Shuai Hongqiang noticed that, from the moment they walked in, she had addressed him as "Shuai Hongqiang" — full name, polite and distant, locating him precisely on the scale of "someone with a shared past, now a business partner, forced by a strange affair into the same boat." Not a degree closer.

Old Chen nodded slightly. His gaze didn't go at once to the brocade box; instead it turned first to Shuai Hongqiang, those eyes that had seen all the world's affairs carrying a question. "Mr. Shuai, the matters that should have been explained — have you made them all clear to Miss Yao?"

Shuai Hongqiang's throat tightened. Under the double scrutiny of Yao Yuan's gaze — which had turned questioning, and naturally wary, at his words — and Old Chen's knowing eyes, his face went stiff, and with difficulty he shook his head, his voice dry. "There are... still some key parts I haven't told her."

The measured smile on Yao Yuan's face didn't change, but those beautiful eyes narrowed for an instant, and the scrutiny in her gaze at Shuai Hongqiang sharpened all at once — like a precise scanner, trying to parse from his murky expression and vague words just what kind of problem these hidden "key parts" really were. She didn't press immediately, but that instant of change in her eyes was enough to freeze the air by half a degree.

Old Chen took a slow sip of tea and set the cup down, his aged gaze carrying a clarity that saw through everything. "Since Miss Yao traveled only yesterday, has Mr. Shuai had any good news happen today?"

Shuai Hongqiang nodded with difficulty. So that formal, sealed-and-stamped invitation to tender for the Jinshi Public Fitness Center Project (Phase One) construction and operation, received first thing this morning in black and white — that was the "good fortune" this trip of Yao Yuan's had brought him.

"Miss Yao." Only now did Old Chen turn to her, his voice slow and clear. "Lately, have you often felt run-down, easily tired, sleeping lightly and dreaming a great deal — bizarre, lurid dreams, sometimes hard to tell from reality? At certain moments without any warning — gazing at a mirror's surface, say, or in the flicker of lamplight, or just when your mind suddenly relaxes — do you feel a brief dizziness, a blanking-out, even... flashes before your eyes of images or scenes that aren't where you are now, abnormally real?"

Yao Yuan's spine straightened, almost imperceptibly. She was silent for two seconds before she spoke, her voice still steady, but the deliberately maintained ease was gone. "Yes. Especially the latter. These past few months... it's gotten much more frequent. I thought it was just overwork, frayed nerves." She didn't mention what specifically she'd seen — that was the deepest secret in her heart.

"That isn't frayed nerves." Old Chen shook his head, his expression grave. "That is your 'spirit-mind,' drawn by that mirror, frequently and passively crossing the barrier of time and space."

He paused, as if weighing how to make these mystifying words more credible. "Mr. Shuai has surely told you this mirror can make a person 'travel back through time.' But the principle behind it — I'm afraid neither of you understands. At this mirror's core is no ordinary bronze, but a sealed strand of an exceedingly rare thing, refined by ancient occultists: the 'Primordial Purple Energy.' This energy is the wellspring from before heaven and earth were parted; it holds the seed of creation itself, and is the purest form of 'fortune.' Using this energy as its source, two ancient talismanic arts were inscribed on the mirror, each completing the other."

"The first: the 'Time-Tracing Draw.'" Old Chen's gaze locked onto Yao Yuan. "This art does not work on everyone. It resonates only with one special kind of fate — what the old texts call the 'Nine-Purple Departing-Fire' destiny. Those of this destiny are strong-willed, unwilling to be mediocre; their life paths tend to swing wide and dramatic, sharply up and down, and deep within they often carry obsession and powerful regret. When such a person, triggered by real circumstances, gives rise to an intense thought — especially extreme remorse, unwillingness, or the 'if only I could do it over' impulse toward some major past choice, some deep regret — their violently fluctuating spirit-mind resonates with the mirror's 'Time-Tracing Draw,' rousing the Primordial Purple Energy, and a strand of their consciousness is briefly dragged back to that particular 'past moment.'"

The hand Yao Yuan had resting on her knee curled slightly, fingertips folding into her palm. Nine-Purple Departing-Fire destiny... powerful regret... unwillingness... these words pricked at her heart like needles. All those things in Shanghai, Ding Shitong, Duan Yanqing, those "what ifs" that gnawed at her heart on the deep of the night... so this was the key.

"So I can 'trigger' it because of my... destiny? And my 'regret'?" she asked, her voice betraying no emotion.

"Yes, and not entirely." Old Chen said. "The destiny is the fuse; powerful regret is the catalyst. But the deeper cause may lie in the 'intensity' and 'uncertainty' of your own life energy. Your character, your experiences, your choices have shaped a fate full of tension and variables. This 'high-energy,' 'many-possibility' state is precisely the best 'fuel' for the 'Time-Tracing Draw' to operate. Each time you go back, prompt your past self, make a different choice, you are in fact disturbing your original fate-line, producing a kind of 'variable energy.'"

He shifted, looking at the ever-paler Shuai Hongqiang. "And this leads to the second talismanic art — the 'Fortune-Shifting Pivot.' This art transfers and grafts. The Primordial Purple Energy has the power to refine: it can refine the 'variable energy' produced by your disturbed fate into a purer, guidable 'fortune.' And the direction of that guidance is decided by the 'pact.'"

"Mr. Shuai." Old Chen's gaze was like fire. "Eight years ago, at the winter auction, when you took this mirror — did you think of Miss Yao? And later, when you gave her the mirror, your business floundering, was there a single moment — however faint, however fleeting — when you thought 'this mirror is no ordinary thing,' or 'Yao Yuan's circumstances are special,' that perhaps it might... bring some kind of different turnaround?"

Cold sweat beaded at Shuai Hongqiang's temple. Under Yao Yuan's suddenly knife-sharp gaze, there was no avoiding it; he could only force the sound out of his throat. "...Yes. At the time... I did have that delusion."

"The moment that thought was born, the 'pact' was formed." Old Chen spoke each word distinctly. "Mr. Shuai gave the mirror harboring the thought of 'borrowing her fortune to mend my own straits,' and so he made a 'fortune-borrowing' pact with the Primordial Energy in the mirror — became the target locked on by the 'Fortune-Shifting Pivot.' From then on, each time Miss Yao's regret triggered the 'Time-Tracing Draw,' each time she traced back and changed something, the 'fortune' refined from her disturbed fate flowed steadily along this 'fortune-borrowing pact' — straight to him."

"Miss Yao, you traveled yesterday afternoon and came back exhausted; Mr. Shuai had good news first thing this morning." Old Chen looked at Yao Yuan. "The timing, the cause and effect — they all line up."

The inner hall fell dead silent.

Even the tea-scent seemed to congeal. Only the faint sounds of the street outside threw the air inside into heavier relief.

Slowly, very slowly, Yao Yuan turned her head and looked at Shuai Hongqiang. There was no expression on her face — no shock, no anger, only a fathomless, ice-cold calm. But beneath that calm, magma seemed to surge. Her red lips parted, her voice low but every word clear, edged with ice:

"So when you told me at 'South Lake Lodge' that you'd 'nearly had an accident' at the ski slope, the relief and guilt in your eyes weren't just worry for me — it was because you knew perfectly well that your 'good fortune' was bought with my 'discomfort'?"

"Yao Yuan, I..." Shuai Hongqiang's face was paper-white, his lips trembling, a vast shame and helplessness flooding over him like a tide. He wanted to say "I didn't know it would be like this," wanted to say he'd found out later but hadn't dared speak, wanted to say how many times he'd regretted it... but under her cold, all-seeing gaze, every word turned as pale as paper ash.

"Miss Yao." Old Chen spoke up at the right moment, his voice carrying a heavy sigh. "This affair is uncanny, beyond ordinary reason. When Mr. Shuai first gave the mirror, he could hardly have foreseen such consequences. And this fact of 'borrowed fortune' — I myself only confirmed it later, deducing it again and again from the phenomena, piecing it together with what the surviving fragments record. Later, when he knew part of the truth and kept it from you, yes, there was self-interest — but there was also my warning. I feared that if you learned the truth of the borrowed fortune, the violent shock to your mind would only intensify the mirror-art's pull on your soul and its drain on you, making things worse."

He looked at Shuai Hongqiang, his gaze complicated. "Still, concealment is in the end the lesser course. Cause and effect are linked; laying it open today, though it hurts, is a necessary pain."

Then he looked at Yao Yuan again, his eyes earnest and worried. "More crucially — the harm this art does to you may go far beyond exhaustion. The fragments record: 'When the spirit-mind crosses too often, the root is easily harmed; when fortune shifts without constancy, bear its fruit with care.' Meaning: a person's consciousness crossing time and space frequently is a fundamental drain on the soul. Right now you're only mentally tired, and rest can ease it, because you're young, your vitality is strong, and the intervals are not yet at the limit. But over the long run — especially if the triggers grow more frequent... at best, scrambled memory, a mind not your own; at worst, soul-damage, consciousness scattered — the consequences are unthinkable!"

Yao Yuan's spine tensed for an imperceptible instant. The hand on her knee gripped tighter, the dark-purple lacquered nails nearly digging into her palm. She wasn't afraid of challenges, wasn't afraid of being schemed against, wasn't even afraid of getting hurt — but she could never tolerate becoming "unclear-headed," "irrational." That was more terrifying to her than being killed. Still, she did not lose her composure.

"Then... then what do we do?" Shuai Hongqiang's voice was hoarse, his eyes bloodshot. "Old Chen, I don't want this 'fortune'! How do I give it back to her? Or — I'll take the mirror back! Destroy it! Won't that cut it off?"

"Absolutely not!" Old Chen stopped him sharply. "The Mirror Pact is formed; cause and effect are deeply rooted. To force the mirror back or destroy it — if it happened just as Miss Yao's spirit-mind was in mid-trace, it would be like severing the rope of one crossing a river: her consciousness would surely be lost in the chaotic currents of time and space, never able to return! That would be the calamity of a soul scattered to oblivion!"

Shuai Hongqiang flinched as if doused with ice water, staggering back a step, slamming into the chair-back, his face ashen.

"For now, the two of you must understand two things." Old Chen's tone was heavy as a mountain. "First — Mr. Shuai, every bit of good fortune you've received is not rightfully yours. Like a tree without roots, it comes by an improper road. You cannot enjoy it in peace; you must use it with a sense of shame. From now on, keep this in mind at all times: act so as to keep a clear conscience, accumulate good deeds and store up virtue — perhaps you can slightly ease the backlash that will one day surely come. Heaven and earth keep their own measure. To harm others for one's own gain is, in the end, not the proper way; everything you've gained may be repaid, doubled, in the way you'd least wish to see."

"Second." He looked at Yao Yuan, his expression grave. "We must find a way to fully end this pact as soon as possible. Otherwise, with your two fates so uncannily bound, her soul daily worn down, your fortune and misfortune impossible to foresee — there can be no resolution."

"Is there a way?" Yao Yuan spoke up at last, her voice colder than before, and steadier — like steel tempered in fire.

Old Chen rose and went to the deepest part of the bookshelf, crowded with mottled old volumes. After a moment's searching he drew out a very thin, very old thread-bound booklet, carefully wrapped in oilcloth. He undid it with slow, gentle movements; the pages were yellowed and brittle, the edges damaged, the binding-thread already rotted away.

"This is a surviving fragment my school acquired in early years; it happens to record this kind of 'Mirror Pact' art." He opened it carefully to one page.

Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang fixed their eyes on it. On the yellowed page, drawn in fine, meticulous brushwork, was a bronze mirror — its ornamentation and form identical in every detail to the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror on the table! Beside the figure ran several lines of small vertical script, the ink faded but still faintly legible:

"The mirror pact: begun in sincerity, swollen by greed, ended in regret. Should regret arise, seek the descendant of the mirror-master; with blood as the medium, break the art and return to the true."

"The descendant of the mirror-master?" Yao Yuan seized on the key point sharply.

"That is, the descendant of the occultist who made this mirror and set down the 'Time-Tracing' and 'Fortune-Shifting' arts back then, or the heir to his teachings." Old Chen closed the booklet, shaking his head with a sigh. "But this book is damaged; several key pages are already lost. How to find one, how to confirm a 'descendant of the mirror-master,' whether 'with blood as the medium' refers figuratively to the price of sincerity or to an actual blood-pact rite, the specific steps to 'break the art' — none of it is recorded. A thousand years have passed, seas turned to mulberry fields; whether such a lineage still has an heir alive in the world is truly... a remote hope."

Hope seemed both near at hand and far beyond reach.

The inner hall sank into silence again, with only despair spreading soundlessly.

After a long while, Old Chen seemed to make up his mind. He turned and took something from a locked, old boxwood case beneath the curio shelf.

It was a jade pendant.

Not large — a little wider than a thumb, carved to follow the stone's natural shape, not regular. The jade was the most lustrous mutton-fat white, but through that congealed-fat whiteness ran several natural strands of pale purple, like drifting mist and rosy cloud, like the purple energy coming from the east. The pendant's surface had a thick, rich patina, its luster restrained, warm to the touch — a soothing warmth seeping slowly into the skin.

"This jade is named 'Heart-Guarding.'" Old Chen pushed it gently toward Yao Yuan, his gaze earnest. "It was given to my founding master long ago by a hidden Daoist master while he sought the Way at Mount Zhongnan. It's said that, sustained by the recitation of true Daoist words, it can settle the soul and steady the spirit, guard the clarity of the mind-altar, and ward off the intrusion of outside evils. My teacher passed it to me with his own hands, charging me not to use it lightly except at the most critical hour."

He gazed at Yao Yuan, his tone graver than ever. "Miss Yao, your soul is unstable from frequent tracing. Worn against your body, this jade may guard a strand of your original clarity when your consciousness is drawn away, easing the soul's shock and drain after you return. It can't cure the root — it can't stop the traveling — but it may hold off the harm for a time, buying you more time to find the way to break this."

Yao Yuan looked at the soul-settling old jade, its warm, lustrous glow flowing in the dim light, and did not reach for it at once. She raised her eyes and looked straight at Old Chen. "Old Chen, this jade is a treasure of your school, a thing of its lineage. My disaster with this mirror has its own cause and effect; it shouldn't drag in your school's greatest treasure."

"A jade has its own spirit; it chooses its own master." Old Chen shook his head, his tone brooking no refusal. "Your situation right now is exactly what calls for this thing's protection. If you came to harm through the Mirror Pact, how could I rest easy? If this jade by your side can help you steady your mind, that is its merit fulfilled. Take it, and don't refuse. Breaking the Mirror Pact is likely no work of a single day; you must first keep yourself whole."

Shuai Hongqiang too looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes full of pleading and guilt.

Yao Yuan held Old Chen's gaze a moment, then finally reached out her long fingers, lacquered dark purple, and gently picked up the soul-settling old jade. The pendant was warm in her hand, and that warmth seemed almost alive, rising thread by thread along her meridians, gently wrapping and soothing the spirit she'd kept strung tight and burning these past days with traveling, livestreaming, and scheming. She felt clearly that her temple, which had been faintly aching all along, seemed to ease a little.

"Thank you." She closed the pendant in her palm and gave Old Chen a slight nod — brief in words, but in earnest.

"See that you keep it well, wear it against your body, never let it leave you." Old Chen counseled, his expression still grave. "Once you two are back, Mr. Shuai should keep my words in mind and act only so as to keep a clear conscience. Miss Yao must watch for bodily signs; if anything is amiss, come tell me at once. I will leaf through other old texts as well and ask after a few reclusive old friends, to see if I can find any trace of the 'mirror-master' lineage or any clue to breaking the pact. This matter... can't be rushed, but it can't be put off either."

When they left the Guanguta Hall, the sun had slanted west, drawing their shadows out long and thin.

Shuai Hongqiang and Yao Yuan walked side by side down the gradually noisier antiques street, silence lying between them — colder, heavier than when they'd parted eight years ago. The soul-settling old jade was clenched in Yao Yuan's palm, its warm, lustrous heat the only real thing in that moment.

"Yao Yuan, I..." Shuai Hongqiang spoke up with difficulty, his voice rough.

"Shuai Hongqiang." Yao Yuan cut him off, stopped, and turned around. The setting sun scattered gold across her dense lashes, but couldn't reach into her deep eyes. She looked at him, her gaze calm, even composed — but beneath that composure was a frost that kept all comers a thousand miles off. "It's come to this. Remorse is useless, blame is useless. Old Chen is right — the urgent thing now is to find a way to break it."

She paused, her tone pure business-negotiation. "I'm keeping the mirror, but I'll be careful. The jade — I've accepted it. Your career: do it well, but don't forget Old Chen's warning — what you've gained may not be a blessing. Any clue about the 'mirror-master' or anything else, however remote — notify me the moment you have it."

With that, she no longer looked at him. She turned, and on her fine high heels walked at a steady pace toward where she'd parked — as if all the earth-shaking secrets she'd just heard hadn't truly shaken her at all. The retreating figure in the red shirt was like a searing flame in the sunset, and like a resolute boundary marker.

Shuai Hongqiang stood frozen where he was, watching her briskly pull open the car door, slide into the driver's seat, start the engine. The red Bentley merged smoothly into traffic and vanished around the corner ahead.

Dusk closed in; the antiques street's lanterns came on. He stood alone on the street as it grew lively, his palm cold. That faint "small fullness in life" born of his career's smooth turn had long since been ground to nothing by the heavy, near-suffocating guilt, dread, and deep fear of an unknown future.

The Mirror Pact was like a lock, locking two people together — one end tied to the peace of her soul, the other to his uncertain fortune. And the key, hidden somewhere in the folds of time — how was it to be found?

The road ahead was thick with fog, and every step felt like treading at the edge of an abyss.
