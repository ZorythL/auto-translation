Chapter 106 — A Whole Life of Striving, and It Turns Out It Was All Someone Else's Script

A whole life of striving, and it turns out it was all someone else's script.

Yao Yuan's consciousness clawed its way upward, inch by inch, as if dragging itself out of the bottom of a deep, viscous mire of chaos.

No sound, no light — only a boundless weightlessness wrapped around her. She felt herself sinking endlessly in the deep sea, then floating in a vacuum — until, at some critical point, gravity caught hold of her again.

In absolute silence, she slowly opened her eyes.

Beneath her was a cool, hard, smooth floor; before her stretched an endless pure white. White ceiling, white walls, white floor — clean, without a single impurity. It was the pure-white space she had come to know across nine journeys through time, familiar and strange all at once.

"Back here again." Yao Yuan let out a soundless sigh in her heart. She braced a hand against the floor and rose slowly, her mind full of doubt. "The pact-breaking ritual was clearly complete. Why have I fallen into this way-station between times once more? Did the ritual fail?"

This white void was a way-station she had privately named — the "window" through which, across nine journeys, she had looked back on her past, watched it, and spoken to it.

This time the feeling was strange. Her body was lighter than after any of the previous nine crossings, as if some invisible burden had been lifted from her. But the crossing itself had been more painful than any before — that searing agony of a soul torn forcibly from its body still echoed faintly through her nerve endings.

She looked around. She had been to this pure-white space nine times. Every time, it had been utter emptiness — no objects, no sound — and every time she had stood as a silent observer, watching another version of herself in another timeline struggle, choose, get hurt, and grow. This place was the way-station of her fate, the window through which she spoke to her past.

But this time, something seemed different.

Yao Yuan's gaze drifted slowly across the empty white, then suddenly fixed.

On the wall some ten meters ahead, a door was set silently into the surface.

Its color blended almost seamlessly into the pure-white wall, perfectly hidden; were it not for the faint, cold glint off the metal handle, it would have been impossible to know the door was there at all.

"This door… was it here before?" she murmured to herself, memory rewinding fast. In all nine previous stays, she had never once seen any solid object in this space. It had always been an empty white nothing — until the images of her past surfaced like a projection.

It was no illusion. This door was new.

Yao Yuan was not the type to sit and wait for death. Thirty-eight years of life, nine journeys through time, countless instances of forcing herself back onto her feet in hopeless straits — all of it had long since worn away any passivity or timidity. What was carved into her bones was the nerve to take the initiative and break the deadlock, and she would not flinch from the unknown.

She drew a deep breath and stepped toward the hidden door.

Her footsteps were jarringly loud in the absolute silence, yet eerily without echo — every sound, the instant it was born, was swallowed whole by the white space, not a trace left behind.

She reached out and gripped the handle. The cold, hard metal was utterly real, nothing like a dream. A light twist of her fingertips —

Click.

The soft sound landed, and the door slid soundlessly inward.

The scene beyond made Yao Yuan freeze for a moment.

It was still pure white, but of a different texture. The floor underfoot had become silver metal that gleamed with cold light, smooth as a mirror, reflecting her blurred form. In the air hung an extremely faint, high-frequency hum, like the background noise of some precision instrument at work.

This was a laboratory.

A vast, empty, futuristic laboratory.

Yao Yuan stood in the doorway, her gaze running forward. About thirty meters off, six men in white lab coats stood with their backs to her, gathered in a ring, intent on a huge display screen before them. The screen nearly filled the whole wall, complex streams of data and rapidly switching images flowing across it, the shifting light casting changing blocks of color over their white-clad backs.

For no reason, her heartbeat skipped.

An inexplicable pull drew her forward, step by step, toward the six men. The silver floor was bright as a mirror, and her reflection warped, stretched, shattered, and reformed with each stride. The high-frequency hum in the air seemed to intensify slightly as she drew near.

When she was still ten paces from them —

The six men turned as one.

The motion was almost mechanically precise, yet carried a strange coordination.

In that instant, time froze.

Yao Yuan's breath stopped completely. The blood drained from her limbs back to her heart, then froze to ice the next second. Her eyes went wide, pupils contracting sharply, her whole body rigid where she stood, unable to so much as tremble a fingertip.

Six faces.

Six faces carved into the course of her life, fused into the marrow of her memory, faces that had made her taste every shade of love and hate, joy and grief.

From left to right they stood, in order — as if every important node of her thirty-eight years had been rewound and laid out before her eyes.

Jiang Hai. The earnest first love of her youth. At twenty-four they had spoken of marriage, but in the end the gulf between their families was too wide, and under his clan's crushing pressure he had resolutely given her up. Now he stood as the youngest, most handsome version her memory held, the white coat setting off his clean, upright frame — but those eyes, always brimming with gentle laughter, now held only a researcher's cool scrutiny. He looked at her as if observing an interesting colony of bacteria in a petri dish, worth studying.

Shuai Hongqiang. Once her lover of three passionate years, who had poured out tenderness and indulgence and given her extreme favor — yet who, when she fell unexpectedly pregnant at twenty-eight and most needed the security of a marriage certificate, had pulled back on the excuse that she was "too dazzling, too hard to handle." Now he was stripped of the smooth, weary glaze of a middle-aged businessman; his brow and eyes held the sharp, calculating keenness of their first meeting, and deep in them lay a cold appraisal she had never glimpsed before, his gaze sweeping over her like the verification of a precise data point.

Zhuge Jinye. Their brief half-year together had been the most ardent, purest time in her barren life. No debts owed, no scheming — only the white heat of two matched souls and a love that ran both ways. The sweat of the dance studio, the hormones, the passionate tenderness — all of it was vivid still. Now he was as dazzling and sharp as ever, the control bred into a dancer's very bones holding him still as a drawn bow — yet those eyes, which had once burned with sincere love for her, now held only obsessive focus, as if fixed on an experimental work nearing completion, waiting to be accepted.

Duan Yanqing. Their acquaintance had begun in calculation and deals, yet he had genuinely built her a staircase to grow on, gifting her rare connections, resources, and scope. That line beside the window of his private jet — "You are my most successful investment" — she remembered to this day. Now he carried the steady, weighty bearing of middle age, that familiar knowing smile still at the corner of his lips, but stripped of all desire and the urge to conquer, leaving only a detached, connoisseur's interest, looking down on her, head tilted slightly, quietly studying her.

Yu Hao. The companion who had begun in compatibility and ended in interests. Matched in thought, aligned in outlook, drawn by hormones, the feeling had deepened over time — yet in the end it could not outweigh his calculus of profit and loss. He had wanted an heir to his family who would carry her excellent genes, but had refused her a shred of legal protection. Now he was as handsome as ever, the look behind his gold-rimmed glasses bright and sharp, holding a faint curiosity and appraisal — but underneath lay a cool, outsider's detachment she had never once read before.

Last, her gaze fell on the man at the far right.

Zhao Yiming.

She breathed the name softly, a surge of warm memory roiling in her eyes. He was the one lover in her life who had never brought her pain, who had given her nothing but tenderness and security. The cold wind off Baima Beach, the warmth lingering in his arms, the anxious calls at her ear, the cold touch of the ring on her fingertip — all of it came rushing back, overlapping perfectly with the man before her.

But this Zhao Yiming wore the same white coat as the others, his frame tall and upright. Behind the gold-rimmed glasses, his eyes held the calm reason she knew so well — yet without a trace of love, without worry, without the thrill of having lost and found her again, and least of all the dread of nearly losing her at the beach just now. There was only pure, professional, absolute calm, with a touch of the relief and certainty of one who has just completed a major experiment.

In the instant their eyes met, his softened for the briefest moment, as if some deep-rooted program had triggered a conditioned reflex — so fast one might mistake it for an illusion. The next instant, he returned at once to a detached clarity and ice.

Yao Yuan's throat tightened, so dry it could make no sound. Countless thoughts smashed, exploded, and collapsed in her mind.

She had been through nine journeys through time, had tampered again and again with the trajectory of fate — could it be that the six people tied to those altered turning points had all been swept into this timeline together?

Just as her mind reeled and her thoughts scattered, the corner of her eye caught, without intent, a decorative mirror beside the giant screen.

The silver mirror was clear and bright, reflecting her present self in perfect detail, sharp down to the finest hair.

In the mirror was a face young enough to be a stranger.

Not the thirty-eight-year-old Yao Yuan, weathered by storms, the vicissitudes of years settled in her eyes.

It was her at twenty-three — still naive and sharp, not yet ground down by the world.

Her skin was full and clear, her eyes bright and limpid, holding the newborn edge and stubbornness of youth. Her long hair fell loose over her shoulders like a waterfall; a simple white dress made her clean and pure. She could even see the small, pale-brown "tear mole" below her left eye — the one that had been removed the year she turned twenty-eight.

In disbelief she raised a hand and touched her own cheek. The skin was smooth and fine beneath her fingertips, without the faint roughness of long-worn makeup, without the fine lines the years had left. She lowered her head to look at her hands — ten slender fingers, nails trimmed clean and neat, none of the exaggerated manicures she had later worn for the livestream's sake. These hands, this face, belonged completely to the twenty-three-year-old Yao Yuan.

Could it be she had truly shaken the foundations of fate, reset all cause and effect, and returned with her memory intact to twenty-three, when nothing had yet begun?

The thought had barely risen when a dizzying wild joy swept through her — but in the next instant a deeper unease welled up.

No, the logic was wrong.

Everything was wrong.

Their eyes were wrong, their bearing was wrong, the air about them was wrong; the cold, withdrawn, observing atmosphere that filled this space — all of it was wrong.

Yao Yuan stepped forward, her gaze sweeping urgently across the six familiar faces, trying to find on them a single trace of shock, bewilderment, the alarm of being caught in this place together, or the tremor of reunion.

But she found nothing.

Not one of the six showed any flicker of emotion.

The six stood quietly in the silver laboratory — uniform white coats, uniform detached posture, uniform scrutinizing gaze — looking down on her from above, as if through her young twenty-three-year-old body they were examining the entire core of her thirty-eight years of life. More like six researchers in a sterile lab, observing a sample in a petri dish that had just finished a long life-cycle experiment.

No thrill of long-parted reunion, no bewilderment at being in another world, no surprise at her sudden youth.

Only an extreme cold, professional, detached deathly stillness.

Yao Yuan fought to steady herself and looked at them, her voice hoarse, dry, and shot through with dread. "You… you don't know me?"

The question was absurd in the extreme. These six faces, carved with half a lifetime of her love and hate, were familiar to the bone. Yet the strangeness and detachment in their eyes was cold enough to break her heart.

After a long, dead silence, Zhao Yiming, at the far right, finally spoke.

His tone was steady, rational, restrained — the voice she knew so intimately — yet every word was tempered with ice, precise, sharp, cruel, driving one by one into her eardrums, shattering all her understanding and belief.

"Of course we know you, Sample 001."

Sample 001.

The five words burst like a thunderclap. Yao Yuan's mind went instantly blank, buzzing. She opened her mouth but could make no sound at all, every drop of blood in her body frozen solid.

Zhao Yiming kept the flat tone of one stating an experimental conclusion and went on, slowly, every word a blow to the heart:

"The small sandbox world you have just left is an immersive observation field we built exclusively for you."

"The thirty-eight years of life you have lived — from the moment of your birth to the end of the pact-breaking at Baima Beach on the Yellow River — is a complete life-observation sample we tailored and set for you."

"The birth, family, schooling, career, the tangles of love and hate, the rises and falls you believed were real — even your power of journeying through time nine times and the mystery of the Mirror Pact —"

He paused slightly, the eyes behind his lenses reflecting her suddenly pale face with perfect clarity. "All of it was the simulated environment and the stimuli we laid down for you, to observe how certain variables would respond."

Boom — !

In Yao Yuan's ears it was as if hundreds of millions of stars collapsed and exploded at once, then abruptly fell into dead silence.

Every sound, every ray of light, every sensation receded in an instant. She felt herself standing in an absolute void, a bottomless chasm splitting open beneath her feet.

An illusion?

An observation field?

Her whole life — only an experimental sample?

Was the sweat of practicing dance before dawn fake? Were the wrinkles at the corners of her mother Zhang Fengxia's eyes, as she watched her off to school at the mouth of the alley, fake? Was the drum-loud thrill of first love fake? Was the agony of losing her child, her body hollowed out, fake? Was the dryness in her throat as she dissected herself before tens of thousands of strangers' IDs in the livestream room fake? Was the tremor of Zhao Yiming's fingertips as he slid the ring onto her in the mirror room on New Year's Day fake? Was the pact-breaking ritual on the bank of the Yellow River just now, which had spent every last drop of her hope, also fake?

Her whole body went stiff, her limbs cold and numb, even her thoughts frozen solid to ice. Only Zhao Yiming's voice flowed on — steady, clear, cruel — each word grinding over every inch of the thirty-eight years of land she had believed was real:

"You must always have wondered why you alone possessed the power to journey back through time."

"Why, returning to the past nine times, persuading, mending, struggling, seeming to change the details of the process again and again, you could never shake the final direction of the key fated nodes."

Yao Yuan's eyes snapped up, already shot through with red. This was the fixation and confusion rooted in the deepest part of her soul, the source of her despair countless times over. She had thought it was destiny, the will of heaven, the karma and lesson she was bound to carry, a heavenly decree no mortal could resist.

Zhao Yiming watched the pain and accusation roiling in her eyes, tilted his head slightly, as if reading off a set of fixed data, then said lightly the ultimate, absurd, despairing truth:

"Because every 'past Yao Yuan' you met on each journey back was never truly you."

"Those were nine sets of secondary clones we cultivated from your original genetic sample, numbered 001-1 through 001-9, corresponding precisely to the nine key fate-correction points of your life."

Yao Yuan's body lurched violently, as if struck full in the chest by an unseen sledgehammer, swaying on the verge of collapse.

"Every 'young Yao Yuan' you spoke with, tried to warn, to save, to pour your hope into — all of them were scripted roles we arranged, carrying preset response programs."

"As for every key fated node, its direction was set from the very start of the observation experiment as an unalterable observational constant."

"You thought you were journeying through time."

"You thought you were making peace with your past."

"You thought you were redeeming yourself."

"You thought you were defying heaven and rewriting your fate."

"You thought that through one trauma after another you were forging a tougher, more resilient soul, and would at last break free of destiny to win clarity, independence, and love."

Zhao Yiming's gaze settled on her face. In it there was no mockery, no pity — only the absolute calm of a scientist stating an experimental conclusion. And that calm, more cruelly than any blade, brought an end to all the faith and meaning she had built over thirty-eight years of blood and tears:

"You were only performing, within the closed experimental environment we set up, following the observation script — day after day, flawlessly enacting the behavioral model and emotional curve of 'an intelligent, resilient woman gradually awakening through repeated trauma.'"

"Every pain, every breakdown, every struggle, every so-called breakthrough of yours fell within our expected range of data fluctuation."

"The more earnestly you lived, the harder you fought, the more genuinely you threw yourself into those 'lives,' the more precious the observation data you produced — and the better it verified our string of hypotheses about 'post-traumatic growth mechanisms,' 'the entrenchment and rupture of emotional patterns,' 'the internalization of and resistance to social gender roles,' and the rest."

Dead silence.

In the laboratory only that high-frequency hum remained. Now, in her ears, it was like hundreds of millions of bees beating their wings at once, laughing at her earnest, obsessive life.

Yao Yuan stood blankly where she was, looking at the six faces before her — familiar to the bone, yet strange to the point of horror — and everything in her heart collapsed, shattered, ground to dust.

Thirty-eight years of life's rise and fall, nine journeys through time, countless climbs out of the abyss, battles against fate, vigils kept over a flickering light in despair. Those tears no one knew of in the dead of night, those hopeless straits gritted out through clenched teeth, that relief at surviving disaster, that certainty of having at last grasped tenderness… it turned out that from beginning to end, it had all been a meticulously choreographed play.

Her suffering was a precisely designed experimental variable.

Her time-travel was a program-preset observation mechanism.

Her hope was bait to keep her producing data.

All her heart-rending struggles, all her cries she had thought came from the depths of her soul, her phoenix-like rebirth — all of it had only ever been code, charts, data, and raw material under the pens of six observers.

A long time passed.

So long it felt as though another lifetime had gone by.

Yao Yuan raised her head with excruciating slowness. The blood-red had drained entirely from her eyes, leaving only the desolation of land after a storm of wind and snow — empty, cold, barren of a single blade of grass. Her voice was light as something drifting outside the world, every word carrying the dust of a soul broken apart:

"Then who… am I?"

The six exchanged a glance. There was no communication in it — more like a set of coordinated programs completing some confirmation step.

In the end it was again Zhao Yiming who answered, his voice clear and ringing, echoing through the empty silver laboratory, pronouncing sentence on the entire meaning of her existence.

"You are a sandbox-observation clone we cultivated privately, numbered 001."

"You have no birth family, no real past. The mother who raised you, Zhang Fengxia, was a high-precision personality simulation program; your stepfather, Uncle Zhou, was a directed environmental-interaction model; Jin Lan, Liu Qingzhuo, and all your relatives and social ties were group-interaction variables implanted for you."

"Your childhood, your studies, your career, your social life, your loves and hates — your entire life history — were all projections of an immersive narrative backdrop."

"You had no self from the moment you came into being. The sole meaning of your existence was, within the holographic sandbox we built — one that approached real rules as closely as possible — to live out, with a complete system of biological perception and emotion, a full life on our behalf: one no one could live for you, that could not be escaped, struggled through with your own hands, experienced through from start to finish."

"The entire meaning of your existence was, within this holographic sandbox we built — approaching real physical and social rules as closely as possible — with a complete body's perception and emotional-feedback system, to live out a full life on our behalf: one no one could take over, that could not be escaped, that from beginning to end you had to grope through, struggle through, and fight your way up entirely on your own, until you finally broke free of your fetters."

In Yao Yuan's chest, a violent, real, physical wringing pain suddenly came.

The pain was so sharp, so concrete, that it bent her double. Yet the pain itself had now become an absurd joke — she could no longer tell whether it was a real reaction of her body, or a preset neural feedback of the clone vessel.

So that was it.

She was not, at all, one of the living.

She was only an observation tool, cultivated from who-knew-where.

And the whirlpool of destiny she had spent her whole life bleeding and weeping to escape — that she had thought she was steering a lone boat through towering waves to break free of — was nothing but a water-circulation track drawn by an experimenter on the outside of a glass tank.

She had swum with all her might for a lifetime.

She had gritted her teeth and fought for a lifetime.

She had thought she was fighting the whole world, when in fact she had only been hurling herself, again and again, against a transparent wall, at boundaries set in advance.

What she had taken for "breaking the deadlock" — the clarity, the independence, the resilience she had been so proud of — had only ever been part of the experiment's design.

It was the performance she, as "Sample 001," was required to complete in the small sandbox world.

Her whole earnest, striving, love-and-hate-tangled, hard-seeking life had, from beginning to end, been nothing but a play under someone else's pen.

Yao Yuan stood in the center of the silver laboratory, before the six "creators" who had wrought all the tragedy and joy of her life, and suddenly she wanted to laugh.

But the corner of her mouth had barely moved when scalding liquid, without any warning, came surging out of her eyes.

These tears — were they real?

She no longer knew.
