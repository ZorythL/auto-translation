Chapter 24 — Yao Yuan and Yu Hao: Algorithms and a Sincere Heart
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640271.html

The meeting wrapped up just as the sky outside took on that gray-blue tint unique to a winter evening. Yao Yuan rubbed the space between her brows, putting an end to a day of intense mental work. That strange, mystical conversation with Shuai Hongqiang about the bronze mirror still hung over her like a thin haze. She gathered up her files and was getting ready to leave when her phone rang. It was Yu Hao.
"I just got back from Shanghai. I'm right downstairs from your company — wondering if Boss Yao would do me the honor of dinner." The voice on the other end carried a smile and a road-weary, magnetic warmth that chased away a little of her fatigue.
Hearing his voice, she found the corners of her mouth curving up before she realized it. "Sure. I was just thinking I wanted to talk to you. Where to?"
"The usual place — Yellow River Garden. I've already booked a table."
"Great. I'll be right down."
Yu Hao's car was parked at the curb not far from the company exit, and he was standing beside it. When he saw Yao Yuan come out, he took a few quick steps forward to meet her. "Hungry?" he asked with a grin.
At thirty, Yu Hao was a man in his prime. Six feet tall, he carried himself straight as a pine, each step steady and self-assured, with a presence that was impossible to ignore.
His single-lidded eyes were long, narrow, and deep, and beneath that depth ran a keen sharpness. A single glance from him could take in details most people would miss, the light in his gaze seeming to cut through fog straight to the heart of a thing. A high, straight nose lent his face a strong, sculpted line, adding a touch of resolve and decisiveness. His clean, crisp jawline carried an unmistakable firmness that matched the polished air he gave off overall — a handsomeness that was thoroughly modern and intelligent.
Today he wore a dark gray wool overcoat that fell just above the knee, its simple straight cut making him look even taller and leaner. The collar was a folded-lapel design; underneath he had on a white turtleneck cashmere sweater, with black straight-leg jeans below, the lines perfectly straight and the cuffs tucked into black Martin boots. A light gray wool scarf was looped casually twice around his neck, one end left to hang naturally. On his wrist sat a silver-gray mechanical watch with a black crocodile band. He looked every bit the stylish elite — sharp and put-together.
Yao Yuan was caught off guard by how handsome he looked for a moment. She walked toward him with a smile and teased, "I was pretty hungry, actually, but the second I see our dashing Boss Yu my appetite just disappears. Truly a feast for the eyes." Then she burst out laughing, mischievous and loud.
The sudden flirting flustered Yu Hao into a blush, and the tips of his ears stayed red all the way through dinner.
Yellow River Garden sat along the city's riverside scenic belt, famous for its refined fusion cuisine and its up-close view of the Yellow River at night. They walked to their reserved table, and Yu Hao naturally pulled out Yao Yuan's chair, took her coat, and handed it to the waiter — smooth, thoughtful motions. He shrugged off his own coat and draped it over the back of his chair, the turtleneck underneath making the flat line of his shoulders stand out all the more.
Yu Hao sat down, his gaze lingering on her face for a moment, sharp enough to catch a deeper thread of preoccupation in Yao Yuan, something beyond her usual work fatigue. "What's wrong? From the look on your face, did something thorny come up on the project today? Is it the 'Pretty Big One Emotional AI model' progress?"
He could always read the center of gravity of her state at a glance. Yao Yuan picked up the warm barley tea and took a sip, the warmth sliding down her throat. "Yes — and no. A lot happened today, actually. I've been wanting to talk it all through with you."
Yu Hao didn't push. He simply topped off her tea and ordered the dishes to both their tastes. Only once the waiter had gone did he lean forward slightly, elbows on the white tablecloth, hands clasped, settling into the posture of a listener — his gaze gentle and focused.
Outside the window, the winter Yellow River lacked the surging force of summer. Under the dusk and the lights along both banks, it looked like a quiet ribbon of ink-dark silk, flowing slowly eastward. The lights on the far shore scattered like stars, blending into the deep-purple line of the horizon.
The dishes arrived one after another. Yu Hao naturally served her some food and chatted about what he'd seen on his Shanghai trip and the latest industry trends. After a few bites of hot food, the mood loosened, and Yao Yuan finally got to the point.
"This morning, my team and I went to Zhao Yiming's Western World. We imported one of the cases from the 'Pretty Big One AI' emotional core database into the Genesis Engine." Yao Yuan set down her chopsticks, her tone turning serious.
Yu Hao dropped the small talk too, slipping into focused listening mode. "Mm — as I recall, the core is the dynamic emotional map and the multimodal feedback engine. Hit a bottleneck?"
"The 'Pretty Big One' core framework and the 'Genesis Engine' tried to be compatible and deeply activate each other for the first time — and it failed. The biggest challenge right now isn't actually the model itself." Yao Yuan frowned slightly. "It's the boundaries and the ethical presets."
"Oh?" Yu Hao leaned in even further, showing real interest. "Tell me more."
"The higher the model's learning ability and realism, the harder its 'emotional responses' are to tell apart from a real person's. In internal testing, a tiny handful of senior testers have already reported that in certain deep, situational conversations they get an intense feeling of being 'understood,' even 'empathized with.' That's the successful side of it, of course." She paused, lowering her voice. "But the problem that comes with it is this: are the ethical guardrails we've set up actually solid and smart enough? When the model 'understands' a person's loneliness, longing, even pain, and uses that as a basis to 'soothe' or 'guide' them — where's the boundary on that 'guiding'? Its 'empathy' is, at bottom, an algorithm optimizing for patterns, but the user may be investing genuine emotion. There's an asymmetry there, and a potential risk of emotional dependence."
Yu Hao listened in silence, his fingers tapping unconsciously on the tabletop — a habit of his when he was thinking deeply. When Yao Yuan finished, he was quiet for a moment, as if rapidly digesting and integrating the information.
"That's a crucial question, and one that was bound to come up," Yu Hao said, his voice steady and clear, carrying his trademark analytical penetration. "It means 'Pretty Big One AI' has already taken half a step into the blurry zone between a tool and a 'quasi-subject.' Traditional ethical guardrails based on rule-driven prohibition lists definitely won't cut it anymore."
He thought for a moment, then went on. "My suggestion is that before the official release, you have to add an independent 'dynamic ethics assessment module.' This module wouldn't interfere with how the model runs; instead it would monitor metrics in real time — the emotional depth of an interaction, topic sensitivity, user investment level — and build a risk early-warning mechanism. At the same time, the user agreement has to be overhauled. It needs to tell users in the clearest possible way that they're interacting with a highly realistic emotional simulation model, that all of its responses are based on algorithms and data patterns, and that it has no genuine emotion or consciousness. That's not just legal protection — it's a necessary reminder for the user."
Yao Yuan nodded. "Zhao Yiming feels the same way, but the technical implementation and the performance overhead still need to be assessed. There's also the commercial side: over-stressing the 'non-human' attribute might hurt how some users — the ones chasing the ultimate experience — accept the product."
"It's a balance," Yu Hao said, his gaze sharp. "But the bottom line can't be compromised. The commercial value of emotional simulation is enormous — and precisely because of that, its ethical risks and responsibilities are equally enormous. We can make 'safety' and 'transparency' one of the core selling points, drawing in the users who genuinely need 'Pretty Big One AI' and who value their own mental health and sustainable interaction. Short term, it might filter out some extreme demand. Long term, it's the cornerstone of building brand trust and avoiding systemic risk."
He paused and looked at her. "Also — how's the team holding up? Zhao Yiming is a technical genius, but a project like this is a huge test of a core leader's mindset and values. He needs support, and he needs regular ethical review too."
"You could consider bringing in an external advisory committee," Yu Hao suggested, "made up of experts in psychology, ethics, sociology, even philosophy, to periodically review the project's direction. That gives you authoritative backing and forms an institutionalized check and balance."
Their conversation went deep and ran efficiently, from technical details to business strategy, then stretching out to team management and long-term planning. Yu Hao's insights always struck right at the heart of things — strategic in altitude, yet practical in execution. Yao Yuan felt some of the knots she'd been wrestling with alone during the day gradually loosening in an exchange like this, and new lines of thinking beginning to surface.
With the business settled, the mood relaxed. Yu Hao naturally served Yao Yuan another piece of a dish she liked, his tone softening too. "Sounds like you were under a lot of pressure today. Besides the project, is there something else?" He remembered her saying earlier that "a lot happened."
Yao Yuan hesitated. The bronze mirror and all of Shuai Hongqiang's bizarre talk about "time-travel" and "the Mirror Pact" turned over once on the tip of her tongue — and in the end she swallowed it back down. It was too personal, and too… hard to put into the rational language of technology and ethics they'd just been discussing. She didn't want to ruin the clear train of thought that had formed after their professional discussion, and for now she didn't want to bring that vague, unsettling daze into her time with Yu Hao.
"It's nothing — just all of it piling up at once, it's a bit draining." She smiled and changed the subject. "It's good the Shanghai project went smoothly. You must have gotten a lot out of this trip?"
Yu Hao noticed her brief hesitation and the way she'd steered the conversation away, but he didn't press. He simply picked up her thread and talked about what he'd seen in Shanghai, his gaze resting gently on her face, full of understanding and tolerance.
Dinner ended on a warm, easy note. As they paid and left, the wind by the river was bitterly cold, carrying the dry chill unique to the Yellow River in winter. Yu Hao carefully tugged the collar of her coat up snug and slipped an arm naturally around her shoulders to shield her from the wind. The thoughtful gesture sent a wave of warmth through her.
Settling into the warm car, Yao Yuan leaned back against the seat and watched the city neon flash backward outside the window, the Yellow River dark and dim in the distance. The conversation with Yu Hao had helped her sort out several key threads on the "Pretty Big One AI" project, but the riddle that Shuai Hongqiang and that bronze mirror had brought still lingered, like the deep night beyond the glass, hovering in some corner of her heart.
She turned her head to look at Yu Hao, focused on driving, the lines of his profile shifting between light and shadow under the streetlamps — steady and dependable. At least on the level of work and reality, she had a partner she could trust, a strategic teammate like this. As for that more private, more bizarre puzzle… she needed time, alone, to think it through.
The car merged smoothly into the night traffic and headed for her apartment. Outside the windows the city blazed with light, and in Yao Yuan's heart the reasoning about tech ethics and the doubts about the ancient bronze mirror wove together — like the waters of the Yellow River and the lights along its banks — into an inner landscape of uneven light and dark.
