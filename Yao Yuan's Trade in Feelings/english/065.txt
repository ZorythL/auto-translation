Chapter 65 — Only When You Have the Capacity for Happiness Does Life Have Meaning
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640312.html

Only when you have the capacity for happiness does life have meaning.
The livestream was still running, the viewer count holding steady at a high. In the center of the frame, Yao Yuan shifted slightly in her seat, pressed a fingertip lightly to the spot between her brows, then settled back into that flawless, razor-sharp expression. Behind her, beyond the vast floor-to-ceiling window, the Yellow River flowed on forever—silent, heavy, as if it carried every unspoken word and all the silt and grit that come with growing up. She looked into the camera, her gaze steady and clear as a scalpel.
"Alright. Let's connect with the last caller of the day."
[Call connected]
Caller (Su Jin, female, 32, her voice like a flower wrung dry of all its moisture—soft, withered, hollow with the exhaustion of a long, hard journey): "Teacher Yuanyuan, I... I can't find any meaning in life. I don't know what I'm living for."
Her very first sentence struck straight at the deepest black hole.
"My husband and I... I suppose, in other people's eyes, we're 'winners at life.' Both our families come from similar backgrounds, plenty of assets—house, cars, savings, we want for nothing. We'll probably never have to worry about money for the rest of our lives. I don't have to work. I live in an apartment with the best river view there is, and the children have a nanny and the best private school. To everyone looking in, my marriage is respectable, my life is comfortable, I lack nothing—I ought to be content."
She paused. The sound of her trembling breath came through the microphone, heavy with emptiness.
"But I've been depressed for a long time. I've seen doctors—I have depressive tendencies. I fall apart over the smallest things and feel like nothing matters. I clearly have everything, so why... why can I just not feel any happiness? My heart is always hollow, like I'm living in a lavishly decorated palace that never echoes. Every morning I wake to an endless, exquisite emptiness. I have no goal worth getting up early to fight for, no anticipation that makes my heart race, not even... not one single thing that makes me feel I'm 'useful.' I've tried shopping, travel, flower arranging, yoga—all the things people call 'enjoying life.' They only bring a moment's numbness, like giving a painkiller to a dying person. Once the drug wears off, that vast, all-devouring sense of nothingness comes back even stronger."
"I look at myself in the mirror—my makeup is perfect, my clothes are expensive, but I feel empty inside. I've never created a single thing of value that belongs only to 'me.' My life feels like nothing more than a respectable footnote to my husband's, a backdrop mother in my children's. I have so much—so why do I feel instead that my life is as weightless as a wisp of fog that could scatter at any moment, with no substance and no meaning at all?"
[After a brief stunned silence, the scrolling comments surged.]
[I get this so much. Material needs met, but the spirit left barren.]
[So money doesn't guarantee happiness after all. Feeling more balanced now.]
[Feels like she's been "kept" too well and lost her vitality.]
[She's describing a state of "weightlessness in life."]
[A lack of meaning—is this the terminal illness that comes after material saturation?]
Yao Yuan listened quietly, not responding right away until Su Jin's last trembling note faded. She leaned back into her chair, brought her ten fingertips together against her chin to form a steady triangle—her signature posture when thinking deeply.
"I hear your confusion," she said at last, her voice like a river flowing in the dead of night, calm yet carrying the power to cut through the surface of things. "But first, I need you to recognize one premise: the fact that you can sit here and calmly examine 'I can't find any meaning in living,' instead of frantically scrambling over tomorrow's grocery money or next month's mortgage—that, in itself, is a luxury granted to you by your material foundation."
Her eyes were bright, fixed on the camera as if to drive the point into every listener's mind.
"Because when a person is still struggling on the survival line, all their energy goes to weathering the storms of reality. They have nothing left to spare to tend the garden inside—to ask whether it's flourishing or barren. Material wealth is the ordinary person's ticket into the world of the spirit. You've long since secured a VIP seat. That's your good fortune—and your starting point."
[Scrolling comments]: [Hits the nail on the head! Searching for life's meaning is a luxury you get after you're fed and warm.]
[So it's not that money is useless—it's that money's function has changed.]
[Yuanyuan is always this clear-eyed. Affirm the material first, then talk about transcending it.]
"So, Su Jin, what I'm hearing is not a question about 'the meaning of life.'" She spoke, her voice like the first crack splitting through a sheet of ice—clear and cold. "What I'm hearing is the wail of a fully functional person's life instinct, after being placed for too long in a 'de-functionalized' environment. Your pain isn't because you have too much. It's that the few most core 'capacities' at the foundation of your life have, even as you came to possess all of this, been quietly uninstalled."
She leaned forward slightly, her gaze like a searchlight aimed straight at the heart of the problem.
"You think meaning is a treasure hidden in some corner of the world, and that once you accumulate enough chips—wealth, marriage, status—you can trade them for the key. Wrong. Meaning has never been an object to be 'found.' It is the 'sense of happiness' that arises naturally when you, as a subject, act, feel, create, and connect in the world. When you stop acting, when your feelings dull, when you can no longer create, when you lose deep connection—you lose the capacity to create happiness. That is the true source of your emptiness: it's because you've lost the capacity to create happiness that you can't find any meaning in living."
[The scrolling comments seemed to hit pause—then exploded.]
[Lost the capacity to create happiness! My skull just got blown clean off!]
["De-functionalized" environment... so we've been kept until we lost our function?]
[So it's a problem with the state of one's life, not a philosophical problem!]
[Yao Yuan can always pull the most mysterious, abstract questions down to a workable level!]
"Which 'capacities,' specifically, have been uninstalled?" Yao Yuan asked and answered herself, raising three fingers, each one like a verdict. "First, the capacity to experience and process emotional tension. You live in absolute safety and ease, so even a tiny emotional ripple—the nanny taking a day off, a child throwing a tantrum—becomes an unbearable catastrophe for you. It's not that you're fragile. It's that your emotional muscles have atrophied from a long lack of 'weight training.' Real life-force exists in the complete process of facing pressure, digesting frustration, and adjusting your stance within it. You skipped that process and went straight to the result of 'calm'—and that calm is stagnant water."
"Second, the capacity to create value and receive feedback. The human brain needs the positive feedback loop of 'effort—overcoming—reward' to release dopamine and confirm 'I exist, I'm useful.' Your life is perfectly 'delivered'—no effort required, nothing to overcome, and the rewards have become things you simply take for granted. It's like being placed in a climate-controlled terrarium where the food arrives automatically: safe, but your instincts to hunt, to plant, to build all fall into disuse. You've lost the joy of the 'producer' and are left only with the emptiness of the 'consumer.'"
"Third—and this is the root—the capacity for self-definition and the construction of your own narrative." Yao Yuan's tone grew heavier, every word like a hammer driving a nail into your cognition. "The script of your life was written by others: rich man's daughter, good wife, good mother. You've played the parts well, but you've never asked yourself: if I tore up all these scripts and roles, who am I? What kind of story about 'me' do I want to tell? The meaning of your life is an external plug-in, running on someone else's server. The moment the main server—your family, society's judgment—fluctuates in any way, or the moment you yourself grow weary of the role, your whole world loses its commands and crashes to a black screen, and up pops a giant error dialog box reading 'no meaning.'"
On the other end of the line, Su Jin let out a very faint, sharp intake of breath, as if a wound that had been tightly bound in invisible bandages had suddenly been sliced open with precision.
"So, you're not happy because you've lost the capacity for 'happiness.' You feel life has no meaning because you've surrendered the right to 'create meaning.'" Yao Yuan's summary was cold as the glint of a scalpel. "These two are two faces of the same thing—different symptoms of the same paralyzed system."
[On the scrolling comments, countless people seemed to be looking into a mirror.]
["External plug-in system"... that's exactly me. Living inside my parents' expectations, and I crashed.]
[The emptiness of the "consumer"—from today on, I'm going to be a "producer"!]
[So we have to take back the right to narrate our own lives!]
"Then... what should I do?" Su Jin's voice carried the exhaustion of being thoroughly dissected, but also a trace of desperate longing to grab hold of something. "Can I still... reboot these functions?"
"Not reboot—rebuild. And you'll have to build with a will tougher than an ordinary person's, because your 'terrarium' is too comfortable. Breaking out of it takes far more strength." Yao Yuan's tone turned clear and practical, like an engineer laying out a repair plan.
"Step one: actively seek out 'beneficial discomfort' and retrain your emotional and value muscle groups. Go right now and do something with a clear threshold—something that requires sustained effort before you see any progress. Not 'elegant pastimes' like flower arranging or yoga, but something genuinely challenging. For example, study for a difficult professional certification related to your interests; spend a year quietly mastering a cuisine to the point where you could host friends with it; go anonymously into a real charity project and take on concrete responsibilities that will meet with setbacks. The goal isn't the certificate, the dinner, or the project's outcome. It's to experience again, in the process of going 'from can't to can,' 'from unable to able,' every ounce of struggle, anxiety, breakthrough, and joy. That is the most primal fuel for a sense of value."
"Step two: undertake a detailed 'rehabilitation of the senses and the mind.' Each day, deprive yourself of one hour of being 'fed'—binge-watching shows, scrolling through other people's glamorous lives. Use that hour for two things. One: deeply experience the most ordinary thing. For example, truly chew a single grain of rice through its whole process from hard to soft, releasing its sweetness; touch the textures of different materials in your home—wood, metal, wool—and feel their temperature and their character. Two: record undirected thought. Write down whatever drifts through your mind, without judging it, without chasing meaning, just observing the flow of your own thoughts. This reconnects you to the real world you've ignored and to your inner self. They are the soil in which a sense of meaning is nurtured."
"Step three—and this is the most crucial step—begin drafting your own 'life narrative.' Answer these three questions, without lying to yourself. One: when I'm completely alone, with no one watching, what makes me lose track of time? Two: setting aside all responsibilities and roles, what is the deepest, never-faced longing or fear inside me? Three: if my life were a novel, what kind of person do I want 'me,' the protagonist, to ultimately become? (Not what to 'have,' but what to 'become.') You don't need to have the answers—the questioning itself is construction. Start from the faintest clues in your answers and make the smallest of attempts. Even if it's just beginning to write a novel no one will read, researching some obscure historical mystery, learning to make a piece of leather goods. When you begin to act according to the draft inside you—however small—you stop being an actor in a script and become the author of your own life. An author never asks what meaning the story under their pen has, because the meaning is right there at the tip of the creating pen."
Yao Yuan paused, letting these instructions sink into the caller—and into the heart of every listener. Then she delivered her final, unifying closing words.
"Su Jin, your life until now has been like a well-equipped ship moored in a calm harbor—safe, but it has never set sail. You thought meaning was on some distant far shore. Now I'm telling you: meaning is not on the far shore. Meaning is in every motion of you raising the sails, trimming the sails, battling the wind and waves, and finally deciding your own heading. The capacity for happiness you've lost—that is this ship's sails, its rudder, its engine. What you need to do now is not gaze toward the empty distance, but repair these instruments on your ship with your own hands."
"There is no perfect template for life in this world. Agonizing over 'the meaning of life' will only trap you where you stand. Ten thousand idle thoughts are worth less than one action. In action, your feelings will revive, your sense of value will be rebuilt, and you will gradually see the outline of the story you long to tell. When you are the creator of your own life, 'happiness' and 'meaning' will no longer be two answers you must search for in pain. They will become the scenery that grows naturally on either side of your path of creation. Do it wrong, and you can adjust. Do it slow, and you can speed up. But standing still will only keep feeding your confusion and helplessness."
"You have an enviable material starting point—that is already enormous good fortune. Don't waste it. Let it be your safety net, then bravely go and become the person who gives her own life meaning and creates the experience of happiness with her own hands. When you begin to do this, you'll find that lavish palace gradually takes on your breath, your traces, your story. It will no longer be just a 'palace.' Slowly, it becomes a 'home.' Now—hang up the phone. Start with the first, smallest action you can think of, the one that takes 'effort' to complete. To move is the only spell that makes 'meaning,' that abstract ghost, take concrete shape as living experience."
[Call time up. The frame cut out amid an atmosphere of utter clarity and a charge to act.]
Yao Yuan leaned slowly back into her chair, her gaze drifting out the window to the Yellow River flowing on as it had since time began. The muddy water said nothing, yet it seemed to say everything—meaning is not in the still scenery on the far bank, but in the eternal, ceaseless flow itself, the flow that shapes mountains and rivers.
She turned her gaze back to the camera and left the day's livestream with its last and calmest words.
"Remember: we respect wealth, because it can buy the ship for exploration. But the oars on that ship, the fire in its hold, the map for the voyage, and the heart that ultimately decides what we sail for—these things, which make up happiness and meaning, can never be bought. They can only be forged by your own hands and honed, again and again, across the rest of your life. May you and I both become worthy helmsmen of our own vessels. Alright—that's where our livestream ends for today."
A few seconds later, she reached out and, with the index finger painted in a cool sky-blue polish, tapped crisply on the [End Stream] button on the control panel.
The screen went dark in an instant, switching to pure black, with only a small line of white text in the bottom right corner: "Stream ended."
The ring light went out with a snap, in sync.
The studio plunged abruptly into a relative dimness and silence, leaving only the bright—almost dazzling—natural daylight of an early-summer Jinshi afternoon flooding in through the window, washing the room in a warm, pale-gold glow. The central air conditioning hummed low and steady, the only background sound at that moment.
Yao Yuan held her upright posture, not moving right away. She leaned back a little, sinking fully into the soft, contoured ergonomic chair, closed her eyes, and let out a barely audible sigh of deep weariness from the depths of her chest. Four straight hours of high-intensity, high-density mental and emotional output—her brain felt as if it had weathered a precise and violent storm. Now that the storm had passed, what it left behind was a spiritual exhaustion sweeping through her whole body, along with a real burning dryness at the back of her throat.
She held that emptied-out posture for about a minute, just breathing evenly, letting her nerve fibers—strung to their absolute limit—slowly slacken and settle back into place. The heart-guarding jade against her chest pressed close to her skin, radiating its constant, unchanging warmth, like a gentle yet resilient current of energy quietly soaking into and soothing her overspent spirit.
Then she opened her eyes. The physical fatigue was still there, but that clear, calm core and sense of control that belonged to "Yao Yuan" had swiftly gathered itself back together. She stood and walked to the window, arms folded, looking down at the roadside trees and the flow of traffic glossy and green under the summer sun. A faintly warm breeze drifted in through the half-open window, stirring a loose strand of hair at her cheek and carrying with it the blurred clamor of the distant city.
The stories, tears, anxieties, despair, and newly risen sliver of hope from the callers during the stream now receded like a tide from the shore of her consciousness. What remained was a deeper observation of and compassion for the collective plight of women in this era, an almost instinctive, powerful sense of responsibility to "do something," and a faint trace of the helplessness of one who acts even knowing it cannot be fully done. She could split the fog, point to the root of the problem, and offer the method and the path—but to actually wade across that reef-strewn river and complete the hard transformation, each individual had, in the end, to walk it step by step themselves, to struggle, to see it through. She could light a stretch of the road, but she could not walk it for anyone.
