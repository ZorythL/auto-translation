The Bathroom Mirror Devours Her Soul: Traveling Back to Twenty-Two and Her First Love, Jiang Hai
It was nearly midnight by the time Yao Yuan got home.
The huge apartment was silent. Only the smart system, sensing its owner's return, had quietly lit a few warm-yellow floor lamps, tracing the cold, empty outline of the vast living room. She kicked off the high heels that had tormented her all evening. Her stocking-clad feet met the icy marble floor, and that faint sting actually cleared away some of the tipsy haze left over from the dinner, a haze tangled with everyone's hidden agendas.
The bathtub was already full of hot water, with a few drops of nerve-soothing aromatherapy oil added. She lowered herself into the warmth, closed her eyes, and tried to let her tight muscles and her tangled thoughts unwind together. Fine bubbles floated on the surface; the rising steam blurred her vision. Zhao Yiming's eyes, calm to the point of cruelty; Jin Lan's face, still as water; Wang Qian's smooth, oily smile; and all those probing thrusts and counter-thrusts moving like undercurrents beneath the clinking of glasses... they spun through her mind like images on a revolving lantern. Western World, the Genesis Engine, emotional AI, vast ambition, intricate calculation... The hot water rose past her collarbone, yet somewhere in her chest she still felt a chill she couldn't shake.
A long while later, when the water had cooled, she finally rose. She wrapped herself in a soft silk robe and walked to the wide vanity mirror. The glass was spotless, reflecting her face with perfect clarity. With her exquisite makeup removed and the polish washed away, that face had lost some of the flawless sharpness it wore by day. Under the light her skin looked too pale, almost translucent, betraying a faint weariness that long years of tension couldn't quite hide. At thirty-six, the marks of age had been carefully held back by expensive skincare and strict self-discipline. But at the corners of her eyes and brows there had finally settled something only time and experience could give, something hard to put into words. It wasn't wrinkles. It was a kind of presence, a clarity that had sunk into her bones after watching the rise and fall of things and tasting both warmth and cold, and behind that clarity, a certain emptiness.
She picked up her serum and, out of habit, began her nightly skincare routine. Her fingertips slid across her cheeks, cool and slick. The lingering effect of the alcohol left her mind sluggish and blank. The sharp-edged conversations of the day, the precise calculations of profit and loss, had receded for now, leaving behind a hollow exhaustion.
Just then, the tip of her right index finger, where that afternoon the edge of the antique bronze mirror Shuai Hongqiang had given her had carelessly cut a tiny wound, one that had long since stopped bleeding, suddenly and without any warning flared with a clear, burning, stabbing pain!
The pain was so vivid it could not be imagined. Yao Yuan froze and frowned down at her fingertip. The skin around the cut was slightly swollen from soaking in the water, and the searing sensation crept upward along the nerves of her finger, thread by thread, carrying a strange, heart-pounding throb.
Instinctively she lifted her head and looked at herself in the mirror.
In the glass, her face was the same, her eyes dazed with pain and surprise. But in the very next instant, her pupils contracted sharply!
The surface of the mirror... was rippling.
Not a physical crack or warp, but like the surface of water struck by a pebble. Centered on her reflection, soundless, transparent ripples spread outward in rings. The "her" in the mirror blurred, stretched, and distorted within the ripples, and those eyes, wide with shock, seemed to become two bottomless whirlpools.
An irresistible, immense pull, not from physical space but acting directly on the depths of her consciousness! As if an invisible hand had suddenly seized her soul and yanked it hard!
"Ngh..."
A short, stifled groan caught in her throat. The world spun. Everything before her eyes, the warm bathroom light, the gleaming tiles, the neatly arranged bottles of skincare, was instantly stretched, twisted, shattered into countless dazzling, blinding blocks of color, then swept by some brutal force into a fast-spinning, formless vortex! In her ears was a piercing, eardrum-shredding drone, mixed with countless broken, tuneless murmurs and meaningless noise drifting in from distant times and places. The feeling of her body vanished, leaving only her consciousness tumbling and plummeting through an endless chaos...
She had no idea how long it lasted. Perhaps an instant, perhaps an eternity.
Her consciousness, like something struggling up from the bottom of the deepest, thickest mire of chaos, fought its way to the surface bit by painful bit. The first thing to return was her hearing.
A... an absolute silence.
No, not entirely soundless. It was a "white noise," a foundational kind, stripped of every other sound, pure to the point of panic. There was no faint hum of an air conditioner, no distant traffic from the city outside the window, no pulse of blood flowing past her eardrums, not even the sound of her own breathing and heartbeat. Everything had been filtered out, swallowed up, leaving only this vast, even, boundless "stillness," pressing down on every nerve.
Then came touch.
Beneath her was a hard, faintly cold, flat surface, smooth, without texture, without warmth. She lay on top of it like a specimen laid out on an impossibly huge, pure-white laboratory table.
Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open.
White. Endless, all-consuming white that devoured every sense of direction and distance.
The ceiling was white, the floor beneath her was white. Front, back, left, right, everywhere she looked, all of it the same even, soft, yet lifelessly cold white. There was no light source, but the light filled the space evenly. She tried to sit up. Her movements were a little slow, but her body was fine, and the searing pain in her fingertip was gone, as if everything just now had been a hallucination. She touched her own face. It was warm, the sensation real. And her body... she was still wearing the silk nightgown she had changed into after coming home, the soft fabric clinging to her skin.
This was not a dream. At least, not any kind of dream she knew. Dreams did not possess this kind of clear, unsettling, internally consistent absurdity of sensation.
"Hello? Hello, is anyone there?" she tried calling out. Her voice carried into this pure white with no echo and no response of any kind, as if instantly absorbed by this strange space.
She stood up and looked around. Apart from white, there was only more white.
She forced down the urge in her throat to scream and made her nearly frozen brain start working again.
The burning in her fingertip, the strange change in the mirror... the bronze mirror Shuai Hongqiang had given her? That ancient, exquisite, beautiful mirror she had tucked into her office drawer, the one with her blood on it? Could it be that I've somehow gotten blood-bonded to that bronze mirror and traveled through time? Impossible, I've probably just been watching too many time-travel mini-dramas.
The absurd thoughts surged up beyond her control. But this surreal scene before her left no room for ordinary logic to explain it. Not long ago she had been at a dinner table discussing the cutting edge of artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and virtual reality with top scientists and investors, and in the blink of an eye she was trapped in this pure-white cage that looked like the set of a cheap sci-fi film. The enormous contrast brought on a dizziness that bordered on the absurd.
"Calm down, Yao Yuan, calm down," she said quietly to herself, her voice pitifully faint in this eerie silence. "Analyze the environment, find the patterns, find the exit..." She began to walk through the white, barefoot on the smooth, cold floor. With no point of reference, she couldn't even tell whether she was moving in a straight line or simply turning in place. But no matter how far she walked, the scene around her didn't change in the slightest. It was as if she were trapped in a white prison without borders.
Just as that icy terror was about to drown her, the white "space" not far ahead suddenly began to ripple.
Like a drop of thick ink falling into clear water, except what spread was not black. First came an extremely faint gray, almost imperceptible, and then colors and lines appeared out of nowhere, swiftly sketching, filling, growing solid and vivid... as if an invisible, masterful brush were painting in the void before her at an astonishing speed.
In a few seconds, perhaps less, a complete, vivid, richly detailed scene hung suspended in the boundless white.
It was a... slightly worn-out balcony, full of the texture of everyday life. By its style, it looked like the kind common in old residential buildings many years ago. Rusted green iron railings; a few plastic storage bins printed with supermarket logos piled in the corner, stuffed with out-of-season shoes and odds and ends. A few potted plants, probably aloe or spider plants, sat thriving on the concrete ledge. The afternoon sun was lovely, golden, spilling across most of the balcony, so clear she could even see fine dust motes drifting and swimming in the light. On the drying pole hung a few pieces of clothing, a white cotton T-shirt and a pale purple dress, swaying gently in the breeze. The background was a blurred apartment-building exterior wall, equally dated, with peeling paint and rows upon rows of windows.
Everything was so real. The light and shadow, the texture, the detail of the breeze stirring the hems of the clothes, even the warm, dry smell of sun-baked cotton that seemed to drift through the air, mixed with the faint dustiness of an old house. And yet this lifelike scene, like a three-dimensional scroll carefully cut out, hung suspended in this boundless white void, its edges drawn sharply apart from the white space, strange and inexplicable.
Yao Yuan's heart began to pound violently. Cautiously, step by step, she approached that "picture." About two or three meters from it, she stopped. She could see a corner of the living room inside the balcony: simple furniture, pale yellow wood flooring, a television set in the center. And then she saw a "person."
A young man, his back to her, stood by the sink on the balcony, head lowered, seeming to be washing something. He wore an ordinary white T-shirt and dark sweatpants, his frame tall and straight, his hair a little tousled. A few strands of black hair stuck up unruly, their edges glowing a faint gold in the afternoon sun. Just the sight of his back carried an indescribable... familiarity.
As if he'd heard the movement, or sensed he was being watched, the man stopped what he was doing and slowly turned around.
The sunlight fell unobstructed onto his face, lighting up its clear contours.
It was a very young face, the eyes clear, carrying the cleanness particular to the young and a trace of not-yet-faded boyish rawness. The bridge of his nose was high, his lips well-defined and pressed together. His gaze was very still, a quiet that ran deeper than his years, settled too early, and in the depths of that quiet there was a barely perceptible scrutiny of his surroundings.
This face... Yao Yuan's breath seemed to stop on the spot.
Time, at this strange boundary between pure white and vivid life, seemed stretched and frozen without end.
Yao Yuan stood rooted in place. In that instant her blood seemed to stop flowing, then surged madly again, pounding against her eardrums with a thunderous roar. She stared fixedly at that face. Every detail slammed against her brain, fitting seamlessly over an image buried deep in her memory, one she had deliberately sealed away yet that had never truly faded.
Jiang Hai.
It was Jiang Hai. Jiang Hai at twenty-five.
This place... was that little room, the small, hope-filled "love nest" she had rented near his workplace right after graduating from the Beijing Dance Academy. That year, she had been twenty-two.
Countless images, sounds, smells, sensations... were brutally pried out from behind their seal by this single simple recognition, surging up all at once, almost drowning her.
Yao Yuan had studied dance since childhood. The practice rooms were forever thick with the mingled smell of sweat, rosin, and old wooden floors. The girls in the mirrors were each slender of waist, long of limb, lovely of face. They were the seedlings of art and the focus of every gaze. From a clueless child to a graceful young woman, Yao Yuan had grown used to the eager attention that swirled around the beautiful girls beside her. There were callow classmates, there were high-spirited senior students, and there were the hot-eyed "successful men" from outside the school who came with all sorts of intentions. She had understood early on that beauty was a gift, and also a kind of leverage. Love, in her eyes, had never been merely the flutter of romance and moonlight. It was a transaction that could be carefully calculated, something to hold out for the best price. She meant to use her beauty, her talent, and this carefully cultivated "love" to trade for a richer, more secure, more admired future. In four years at the academy she had watched too many similar stories with cold detachment. The suitors around her classmates changed like images on a revolving lantern: young masters of the Beijing circle, sons of southern entrepreneurs, advertising directors, art-world investors... Lively, yes, but in the end the ones who actually saw it through were vanishingly few. Most played the role of passing strangers, longer or shorter, in that gorgeous drama called "love," and when the curtain fell and the crowd dispersed, each rushed off toward the next bit of excitement. She, Yao Yuan, refused to be such a passerby.
Her professional grades were excellent, her flexibility in waist and legs outstanding, her sense of rhythm and stage presence praised by teacher after teacher. In her sophomore year, a teacher recommended her for a fairly famous televised dance competition. She danced for three full months, refining every movement to the extreme, and in the end took only fourth place, just 0.5 points shy of the top three. It seemed to always go that way: her technique was clearly outstanding, but she always lacked a bit of luck or a bit of the audience's favor. On the awards stage, the spotlight fell on the gold, silver, and bronze winners. She stood in the dimmer corner, keeping a fitting smile on her face while her nails dug deep into her palms. People only remembered the top three. Fourth place? Who cared. And yet, even so, she remained one of the most striking girls in her year at the academy, never without suitors. There were brilliant but ordinary-born senior students who wrote beautiful love poems, and there were rich heirs who drove sports cars to wait at the school gate, generous with their spending, tossing off sweet words without effort. She turned them all down with a smile, polite but distant. Her love could not be only romance and moonlight; it had to carry more solid value.
Meeting Jiang Hai happened in the winter of her senior year, during an important off-campus collaboration with a symphony orchestra. The school took it seriously and put her in charge of liaison work and part of the choreography. Jiang Hai was the contact person from the other organization, come to coordinate the details.
When he pushed open the door, he was wearing a well-made dark gray overcoat over a crisp white shirt, no tie, his posture upright. At a height of one hundred eighty centimeters, he held his own even among a crowd of artsy young men. What struck people most was his face. Not the delicate, fashionable kind of handsome, but a very "upright" handsomeness. His brow and eyes were open and broad, his nose high and straight, his jawline clear. When he wasn't speaking he looked somewhat severe, but his gaze was very clean, focused and earnest when he looked at people, never drifting or shifting. He reached out to shake hands, his fingers long and strong, his palm dry and warm. "Hello, I'm Jiang Hai. I'm handling the coordination for this performance."
His voice wasn't loud, but it was steady and clear.
The whole coordination process was efficient and smooth. He didn't say much, but every sentence hit the mark; he considered problems thoroughly and carefully, and for the requirements raised by the dance side he could always offer practical yet flexible solutions, with none of the buck-passing or bureaucratic air common among people in the system. Professional, steady, reliable. That was Yao Yuan's first impression of him.
The performance was a great success. At the celebration banquet, the crowd was noisy. Jiang Hai came over with a glass of wine and toasted her. "Classmate Yao, you worked hard. You danced beautifully." His praise was simple, but his eyes were bright and sincere.
Later, from classmates who had taken part in that performance, Yao Yuan "happened" to hear some news. Jiang Hai's family was no ordinary one. Three generations had been in government, with deep roots in the Beijing circle, a true "old establishment family." The classmates were vague about the details, speaking with a touch of mystery and envy.
Hearing it, the precise scale in Yao Yuan's heart tilted ever so slightly.
And Jiang Hai's pursuit of her was ardent and direct, yet not off-putting. He had none of those overblown romantic gestures, but he would wait at her door with a thermos of late-night supper when she rehearsed deep into the night; he would go to great lengths through other people to track down some out-of-print professional book she had casually mentioned wanting; before she went onstage he would send a brief "Good luck, you're the best," and then quietly watch her entire performance from the most inconspicuous corner below the stage.
His goodness was solid and real, lodged in the details, carrying a near-clumsy sincerity that didn't quite match his family background.
Yao Yuan was not made of stone. On the contrary, beneath all those days of disciplined, goal-driven training and cold-eyed observation, didn't she too, deep down, hide an ordinary girl's faint yearning for pure love? It was only that this yearning had been tightly wrapped and pressed down by thick layers of reason and reality.
Jiang Hai's appearance was like a beam of sunlight, shining without permission into the defenses she had so carefully built, brick by brick, out of calculations of profit. His family was the ladder she wanted, and the man himself... seemed, as it happened, to be exactly the type that could make her heart move. Clear-eyed calculation and unconscious attraction, subtly intertwined.
They got together as a matter of course, on Jiang Hai's explicit promise that marriage was the goal. After graduation, on the strength of her excellent grades and the fame she'd built up through several performances, Yao Yuan smoothly joined a fairly prestigious dance troupe in Beijing. Jiang Hai's job was stable but often required night shifts. They rented a one-bedroom apartment near his workplace, not large, but Yao Yuan made it warm and tidy. It was the first time she had a little home that was completely "her own," full of the warmth of everyday living.
The early days were as sweet as if soaked in honey. When she went out of town with the troupe and they were apart for ten days or half a month, Jiang Hai would always have the little place spotless by the time she came back, with her favorite flowers on the table. A short parting was sweeter than a wedding night; the brief separations only made their reunions more tender and clinging. She sank into a steady happiness she had never known before, and even began to think that maybe love won through calculation could be real too.
In the second year, because of his outstanding ability, Jiang Hai was transferred to the Ministry of Commerce. His prospects were bright, but his business trips suddenly became frequent and longer. Sometimes he'd be gone for two or three months at a stretch. In the days of guarding an empty room alone, Yao Yuan would sometimes drift off while practicing in front of the mirror. The longing was real; the comfort his voice brought over the phone was real; and when she received the small gifts he had gone to such trouble to have someone carry back from far away, gifts perhaps not very valuable but full of feeling, the leap of joy was real too.
She began to feel a faint flicker of panic. She found herself caring more and more about his comfort, aching over the toil of his travels. Once, while out shopping, she saw a wristwatch she liked very much, expensive. Sun Feifei, who performed alongside her, had an identical one, said to be a gift from her rich-heir boyfriend, worth several months of his salary. In the past, she might have hinted demurely that she liked it and waited for him to give it to her. But that time she hesitated for a long while and in the end said nothing. She even started learning to make soup, and on the rare days he was home, she fumbled together a table of not-quite-tasty dishes.
"A woman who aches for a man is unlucky for life, miserable to the end." In the depths of a midnight dream, that cold, almost cruel voice from the bottom of her heart would leap out and issue its sharp warning. She would jolt awake in alarm, look at the sleeping profile of the man beside her, and feel a chill run through her. She knew she was straying from her original purpose, sinking into a dangerous descent called "love." Yet the next time, when he came home travel-worn, dropping with exhaustion but still pulling her tightly into his arms, that flicker of clear-eyed warning was easily shattered by the warmth of his body and the familiar scent of him. She analyzed herself clearly even as she sank helplessly into it, like someone dancing at the edge of a cliff, knowing the danger yet hypnotized by the scenery beneath their feet.
And yet every story that seems beautiful seems doomed to a sudden, graceless ending. And that ending is often unworthy of the sincere, passionate opening it began with.
After two years of living together, Yao Yuan felt the time was about right. Once, after a tender moment, she nestled in Jiang Hai's arms, her fingertip tracing absent circles on his chest, and mentioned, as if in passing, that a colleague in the troupe had just registered her marriage and taken very beautiful wedding photos. Jiang Hai was silent for a while, then kissed her forehead and said, "It's about time I took my wife home to meet my mom and dad."
The meeting took place at a Jiang family residence that wasn't lived in often, yet still managed to look understated and refined. Jiang Hai's parents had fine bearing, dressed impeccably, smiled politely, and yet carried a natural, casual distance. Throughout the meal, Yao Yuan's back stayed stiff. They asked her in detail about her work, how often she performed, what sort of people she came into contact with. Learning that she had lost her father young, that her mother had remarried, and that she had a ten-year-old half-brother by the same mother, they only nodded slightly, betraying no emotion. Then, as if chatting idly, they mentioned that dancers lived on their youth, that being out in the public eye all the time was never quite steady, and that a family like theirs needed a daughter-in-law who could sit firmly in the background, not draw so much attention. At last, as if suddenly remembering, Jiang Hai's mother said to him with a smile, "Oh, right, Uncle Zhou's daughter is coming back from England next week, isn't she? That girl used to love running after you when she was little. Uncle Zhou called just the other day, said the two families haven't gotten together in ages, and the children are all grown now..."
As her words fell, an eerie silence settled over the dining room for a moment. Jiang Hai frowned and was about to speak, but a faint glance from his father stopped him.
After that meal, a thorn lodged itself in Yao Yuan's heart. A cold, sharp thorn marked with an unmistakable sign of class. She knew Jiang Hai's parents were not satisfied, not with her family background, not with her profession, and perhaps not even with her as a person. Her old calculation now revealed its naive and cruel side: she had only seen the ladder behind Jiang Hai, and forgotten to weigh whether she was even qualified to set foot on it.
On the way home, Jiang Hai held her hand tightly and said over and over, "Don't overthink it, Yuanyuan. That's just how my parents are, old-fashioned. Who I love, who I marry, is my own business. I only love you. I'll only marry you."
His palm was hot, his tone firm. Yao Yuan leaned against his shoulder and said nothing. But the thorn in her heart only sank deeper. Did she believe him? Perhaps she believed the sincerity of that moment. But reality?
In the days that followed, as if to prove something, Jiang Hai refused the meeting his family had arranged with that "Uncle Zhou's daughter." The consequences showed up quickly. He began to run into inexplicable obstacles at work; a project that had been a sure thing fell through; the promotion he'd been expecting never came up again. The calls from home grew colder with each one. Jiang Hai came home later and later, his face darker and darker. The steady, reliable young man he had once been now carried a stubborn gloom and irritation in his brow.
Their first fierce quarrel happened on a rainy night. The cause was small, nothing more than Yao Yuan asking a few extra questions because he had once again broken his promise and failed to come to an important performance of hers. The accumulated pressure, frustration, grievance, and that deeply buried thorn were all set off in that instant. She accused him of being dismissive, of not caring about her feelings; he roared that she was being unreasonable, that she had no idea how much pressure he was under. Words flew toward each other's most fragile places like poison-dipped knives, beyond all control. He said she "knew nothing but dancing, only knew how to take," and she sneered back, "Right, I'm beneath you, so go find your well-matched Miss Zhou!"
By the end of the fight, both of them were utterly spent, staring at each other with red eyes, like strangers, like enemies. In that moment, Yao Yuan saw clearly that something had shattered between them.
Later, Jiang Hai apologized, reeking of alcohol and heavy exhaustion. He held her, buried his head in the crook of her neck, and said hoarsely, "Yuanyuan, I'm sorry... I'm just so tired... Let's not fight anymore, all right?"
Life seemed to return to a surface calm. He still brought her gifts; she still left a night light on for him. But something, in the end, was different. That unreserved closeness and sweetness was gone. When they were together, there was now a careful, probing wariness and an unspeakable distance. In bed, the fierce motions were more like a release than a communion.
Two more months passed. On an ordinary weekend afternoon, Yao Yuan met a close friend for afternoon tea at a newly opened mall in Wangfujing. Just as they were chatting happily, her friend suddenly nudged her with an elbow and gestured with her eyes toward something off to the front.
Yao Yuan followed her gaze, and in an instant her whole body went rigid, her blood running cold.
Not far away, at the counter of a brand-name jewelry store, Jiang Hai stood slightly turned to one side, and beside him was a young girl in a Chanel skirt-suit. The girl was holding a necklace up to her neck, tilting her head back and laughing as she asked him something. Jiang Hai looked down at the necklace in her hand, his profile the one she knew so well, but his expression one she hadn't seen in a long time: relaxed, even gentle. The girl was lovely, the kind of beauty that came obviously from a well-off family and careful pampering, with a refined air and a bright smile.
An hour earlier, Jiang Hai had been on the phone, telling her in that familiar voice, faintly tired but gentle, "I have to work overtime tonight, there's an urgent job, I can't come back to have dinner with you. Remember to eat well on your own."
The voice from the receiver still lingered in her ears, yet the scene before her stabbed into her eyes like the sharpest icicle, piercing straight through her heart. A sense of absurdity, cold and sharp, swept over her in an instant. So this was the answer. So clear, so blunt, so... unbearable.
She didn't move, and she didn't rush over right away. A faint curve even slowly rose at the corners of her mouth, one she herself wasn't aware of. That smile was cold, hollow, carrying a near-masochistic clarity. She watched them, watched the girl naturally loop her arm through Jiang Hai's, watched Jiang Hai seem to freeze for a moment, yet not push her away at once.
Then she drew her gaze back and said to her friend, "Let's go."
She rose, picked up her bag, and the route toward the mall exit happened to pass right by the door of that jewelry store. She didn't slow her steps. Only in the instant she passed parallel to Jiang Hai, she turned her head slightly, let her calm gaze fall on his face, frozen in shock and then drained white, and in a voice neither loud nor soft, just clear enough for the few people nearby to hear, she greeted him lightly:
"Hi, Director Jiang, what a coincidence. Out shopping with your girlfriend?"
Her tone was as flat as if she'd asked, "Have you eaten?" With that, without even waiting for his reaction, without sparing the girl a single glance, she linked arms with her equally dumbstruck friend and walked steadily, straight out, into the clamoring crowds and the blinding sunlight outside the mall.
