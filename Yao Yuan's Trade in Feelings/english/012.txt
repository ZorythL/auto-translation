Borrowed Fortune
Ever since he'd seen Yao Yuan the day before and handed over that mysterious bronze mirror, Shuai Hongqiang had gone home in a daze, his mind restless and uneasy.

Winter mornings came late. A fuzzy bloom of frost had crystallized on the glass. It was only half past seven when Shuai Hongqiang, asleep in the guest room, was woken by the thump-thump of running footsteps in the living room. He listened with his eyes shut for a couple of seconds — it was his six-year-old son, Shuai Wenyao. The boy always had more energy on weekends than on school days. It baffled Shuai Hongqiang: on mornings he had to get up for school, the kid would burrow under the covers and refuse to move, but on weekends, when he was supposed to rest, he'd wake up bright and early to play. He was at that age, brimming with energy, never still for a moment.

Shuai Hongqiang pushed open the bedroom door and was met with a wave of warmth and the rice-sweet smell of congee. In the living room, Shuai Wenyao was racing around with a toy airplane held high, his little face flushed red. "Daddy! My plane can fly over that mountain!" The boy pointed out the window at the distant, mist-shrouded cluster of buildings. Shuai Hongqiang looked out. The winter sky was a pale gray, and the old scholar tree in the yard, its branches bare, swayed gently in the cold wind.

From the kitchen came the sizzle of frying. Lin Wan, in a checkered apron, was lifting steaming buns out of a bamboo steamer. In the frying pan beside her, a few eggs had crisped golden-brown at the edges, their yolks still trembling at the center. On the stove, white congee bubbled in a small clay pot, fine bubbles rising. "Awake?" She turned and smiled, fine beads of sweat on the tip of her nose. "Wenyao crawled out of bed at seven, insisting he had to help beat the eggs."

By the time breakfast was on the table, the sky outside had brightened a little more. Shuai Wenyao knelt on his chair, his small hand fumbling with the chopsticks as he tried to pick up a piece of pickled cucumber. "Mommy, it's so slippery!"

"Slow down — you can use a spoon," Lin Wan said with a laugh as she ladled out his congee. In the little blue-and-white porcelain bowl in front of him, the white congee steamed; on a small dish beside it sat half a cube of fermented bean curd and a few strips of pickled cucumber. In front of Shuai Hongqiang was a bowl of golden cornmeal congee — the way they ate it back in his hometown, which Lin Wan had remembered.

The family of three sat down around the table. A smear of sauce clung to the corner of Shuai Wenyao's mouth as he concentrated on "building" an imaginary dam in his bowl of congee with his chopsticks. Lin Wan placed a perfectly fried egg into Shuai Hongqiang's bowl. "It's really cold today. Put a scarf on Wenyao before you head out."

Shuai Hongqiang gave a small nod. His chopsticks had just lifted that golden egg when the phone in his pocket buzzed softly. He pulled it out on instinct — a bank text. "Your account ending 8810 received a transfer of RMB 500,000.00 at 08:02…" The string of figures stood out starkly on the screen, and his chopsticks froze in midair.

"What is it?" Lin Wan asked with concern, still holding the wooden ladle, her eyes full of question.

Before Shuai Hongqiang could answer, the phone rang sharply — and it was Priscilla Chan's "Thousand Songs," its familiar, faintly weathered melody oddly out of place in the calm of the morning. He glanced down at the screen: an unfamiliar overseas number flashing there. He was instantly on guard — almost certainly another scam call. He'd meant to hang up, but somehow his finger slipped and hit Answer instead.

"Brother Qiang! It's me, Ji Wenshan." The voice on the other end was hoarse but smiling, and behind it he could faintly hear the drone of a chainsaw and men shouting in a foreign language.

Shuai Hongqiang couldn't help freezing. He really hadn't expected it to be him — counting it out, they hadn't seen each other in eleven years. He hurried to his feet and walked toward the balcony. As he slid the glass door open, a blast of icy air rushed in, swallowing him whole.

"Brother — I really didn't think it'd be you. All these years, how have you been?" There was surprise and warmth in Shuai Hongqiang's voice.

"I'm doing all right now. You saw the text, didn't you? Five hundred thousand! Three hundred thousand principal, and the rest is interest — don't say anything yet!" Ji Wenshan spoke fast, as if words he'd stored up for years were spilling out all at once.

Shuai Hongqiang gripped the phone tightly, his fingers stiffening of their own accord — and not from the cold.

"Eleven years, Brother Qiang," Ji Wenshan went on, his voice thick with feeling. "The winter of 2014, my factory went under completely. I was buried in debt. I was in despair, ready to give up on everything. I crouched alone at the edge of the Yellow River, honestly thinking about ending it all, jumping in. And right then, you showed up with three hundred thousand in cash. You found me and said, 'Go lie low for a while. Being alive beats everything else.'"

Shuai Hongqiang remembered it clearly. That winter had been bitterly cold, the howling river wind cutting like a knife. Ji Wenshan had been huddled in a tattered cotton coat, clutching a half-empty bottle of baijiu, his eyes full of despair and helplessness.

"I went to Laos — Luang Prabang. The first three years…" Ji Wenshan's voice suddenly caught, and after a moment he said quietly, "I slept in the workers' shed, learned to cut timber from the locals. Got cheated twice along the way. At my lowest, I got lost in the forest for two whole days and only pulled through by drinking from a stream. Later I met an old overseas Chinese man, a master craftsman in rosewood. He took pity on me and made me his apprentice."

Beyond the balcony, the bare branches of the scholar tree shivered in the cold wind. On the street in the distance, early risers hurried along, coats pulled tight, faces tense.

"Bit by bit, I finally figured out the trade — from grading timber for other people at first, to buying it myself later, and eventually opening a small workshop," Ji Wenshan said, a smile creeping into his voice. "A few years back I bought a patch of forest. Not big, but the timber quality is excellent. I caught those good years when the rosewood furniture market was booming, and I finally made a little money… I put some aside every year, always thinking I had to pay you back — principal and interest both."

Something seemed to lodge in Shuai Hongqiang's throat. He forced the words out: "The interest is too much."

"Too much, nothing!" Ji Wenshan raised his voice, firm. "If it hadn't been for your three hundred thousand back then, I'd have been gone long ago — the grass on my grave would've gone through who knows how many seasons by now! I heard the economy hasn't been great at home these years, and it might've hit your business. Think of this little bit of money as a brother's small show of support. Oh — and I've shipped a tea table back to you. Lao red sandalwood. It should arrive when spring comes. Same address, your old place, right? My workers are calling me — Brother Qiang, thank you. Really."

The moment he finished, the call cut off, the busy tone going dut-dut-dut four or five times. Shuai Hongqiang still gripped the phone tightly, unable to lower it for a long while. He slowly turned his head and looked back inside — at the table, Lin Wan was gently wiping the sauce from their son's mouth with a tissue. Then she lifted her head, her eyes meeting his, full of question. Shuai Wenyao, meanwhile, held up a half-eaten bun, his big curious eyes fixed unblinking on his father standing on the balcony, taking the call.

Shuai Hongqiang sat back down at the table and turned the phone screen toward his wife. Lin Wan looked down at it for a long moment, her lashes trembling faintly in the morning light.

"Ji Wenshan? The brother you told me about in your startup story, the one you did the building-materials business with?" she asked softly.

"He went to Laos, makes rosewood furniture." Shuai Hongqiang bent his head to drink his congee, the steam warm against his face. "That was him just now, paying me back. Principal and interest."

"That's wonderful." Lin Wan smiled gently and asked nothing more.

Five hundred thousand had once meant nothing to Shuai Hongqiang, but to the Shuai Hongqiang of today, it was no small sum.

He picked up the now-lukewarm fried egg and chewed slowly. Shuai Wenyao clamored for another half bowl of congee, and Lin Wan got up to fetch it. As she passed behind him, her hand came to rest lightly on his shoulder, lingering for two seconds.

"Daddy!" Shuai Wenyao held up his soup spoon, a grain of rice stuck to the corner of his mouth. "Are we having cornmeal congee again tomorrow?"

"We are." Shuai Hongqiang took the congee Lin Wan had ladled and set it in front of his son, then looked over at her. His wife was taking small sips of her own congee, and behind the rising steam there were very faint smile lines at the corners of her eyes.

In front of his wife and son, Shuai Hongqiang held tightly to his outward calm. He slowly finished the last mouthful of cornmeal congee in his bowl and even managed a smile for Lin Wan. "These pickled cucumbers get better every time."

Lin Wan gave him a tender look. "Eat more if you like them — there's plenty."

Shuai Wenyao had already hopped down from his chair and was flying his toy airplane on patrol around the living room. The child's laughter was clear and innocent, but listening to it, Shuai Hongqiang felt as though it reached him through some barrier, not quite real.

Beneath his calm surface, his heart slammed against his ribs like a war drum. Borrowed fortune? Could there really be something so uncanny in this world? That bronze mirror he'd won at auction eight years ago — which he'd only thought of as elegant and antique, maybe enough to win Yao Yuan a smile — did it truly hold some unbelievable power, just as the auctioneer's vague hints had suggested back then?

He thought of that white-haired old appraiser. Eight years ago, at the winter auction at "Yaji Studio," when the old man had displayed the mirror, his fingers had paused for a moment on the cloud-and-dragon pattern on its back, a meaningful look in his eyes, as if there were something he wanted to say but wouldn't. In the end, all he'd said was, "This mirror is said to carry an ancient resonance; its keeper must treat it with a sincere heart." There had been something equivocal and mysterious in his tone.

And more than that — there was Yao Yuan.

This sudden "return on his gift" had thrown open the floodgates of memory. During the years he and Yao Yuan had been together, they'd once gone wandering through the antique market. Yao Yuan, her arm linked through his, had stopped in front of a jade shop. An old man in a gray Daoist robe, with white hair and a white beard, had suddenly stepped out from a side alley, stared at Yao Yuan's face for a full half minute, and then strode up to them.

"Young lady, might I have a word in private?"

Shuai Hongqiang had instinctively pulled Yao Yuan behind him. "Can I help you?"

The old man didn't answer, only kept his eyes on Yao Yuan, a strange light in them. "This young lady's fate is unusual — very like an account in a certain damaged scroll I once read. I'll take not a single coin; I only want to confirm it."

Yao Yuan found it amusing; Shuai Hongqiang thought the man a street charlatan. But the old man persisted, trailing them for half a block, until Yao Yuan, worn down, finally gave him her birth date and hour.

The old man worked the count on his fingers for a long time, his expression growing graver with every calculation.

"You lost a parent young, and your mother remarried — is that so?"

Yao Yuan's smile faded a little. She nodded.

"Your career line is excellent. There's a small setback or two before thirty, but the overall trend is upward." The old man quickened his pace. "After thirty, a great fortune is coming. If this poor Daoist hasn't miscalculated, you are entering the Ninth Purple Fire Cycle. This cycle turns once every twenty years and governs change, innovation, and fire-natured industries. This fortune will push you to heights in your career you've never seen before."

He paused, looking into Yao Yuan's eyes. "You are a person who carries great destiny. One day you may be able to influence — even change — the rules of certain fields. But great fortune comes with great peril. Remember, above all, to hold fast to your true heart."

Before leaving, the old man took from his robe an exquisitely carved fire-phoenix gourd, no bigger than a thumb, strung on a red cord. "This is for you — to forge a good karmic tie."

Yao Yuan didn't let him read her fortune for nothing; smiling, she pressed six hundred yuan into his hand. The episode later became a topic over tea and meals, with friends laughing that the old man had a head for business — trading a gourd worth a few yuan for six hundred. They'd treated it as nothing more than an amusing interlude, and gradually forgot it.

It was only later, after they'd gone their separate ways and his core businesses had been battered by the pandemic — his cash flow on the brink of snapping more than once — while Yao Yuan, just as the prophecy had foretold, rode the wind to soaring heights, her career blazing at its peak, that on some difficult, sleepless night he'd suddenly remembered this old story, and a complicated feeling beyond words had welled up in him.

Three months ago, after yet another fruitless attempt to chase a client for payment, Shuai Hongqiang had been drinking alone late at night when he dug out that bronze mirror from the back of a drawer. Eight years on, the patina seemed a shade deeper, and the lines of the Kui-dragon's eyes had wound like something alive under the desk lamp. He'd thought of the auctioneer's vague "its keeper must treat it with a sincere heart," of the geomancer's "Ninth Purple Fire Cycle," of Yao Yuan's present glory and his own straits.

An absurd notion had quietly taken root that hazy, drunken night.

What if… what if that mirror really did have something special about it? What if the auctioneer's words weren't just mystification?

Once the thought appeared, it grew and twined like a vine. He began looking up all sorts of occult talk online about "borrowing fortune" and "turning one's luck" — most of it preposterous, yet he couldn't stop himself from wondering: what if?

Perhaps… it was precisely in that hard-to-name state of mind — a mix of nostalgia, resentment, and a secret urge to "just try it" — that yesterday he had taken that mirror, sealed away for eight years, and gone to see Yao Yuan.

The mirror had been handed over. And just one night later, Ji Wenshan, gone for ten years, had paid back five hundred thousand. The timing was such a coincidence it made his heart pound with dread.

"I'm going out for a bit." Shuai Hongqiang set down his chopsticks and said to Lin Wan. There was still a little congee left in his bowl. He pulled out a tissue and wiped his mouth, keeping his movements as natural as he could. "Something I need to do — over by the antique market." His own voice came out steadier than he expected.

Lin Wan never pressed him. She only nodded. "Be careful on the road. Coming back for lunch?"

"Hard to say. Eat without me, don't wait." He bent down and kissed his son's forehead, grabbed his coat, and went out the door.
