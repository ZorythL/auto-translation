The endless, all-devouring white pulled back like a receding tide. The sensation of falling stopped abruptly, replaced by a heavy, all-too-real feeling of "landing"—not a physical impact, but the dull thud of consciousness slamming back into its shell.

Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open and she gasped violently, like a drowning woman finally breaking the surface.

Her vision sharpened from a blur, and the first thing she saw was the familiar, finely textured off-white ceiling of her apartment bathroom, and the recessed overhead light spilling its soft glow. Beneath her was the cold, hard tile floor; a dull ache ran through her back and elbows. She was lying flat on the bathroom floor, the hem of her silk robe in disarray, the soles of her bare feet feeling the chill of the tiles seep into her bones.

She was back.

The midnight silence was total, broken only by the faint, low-frequency hum of a city that never sleeps, present like background noise. The water in the tub had long gone cold, its surface flat and still. The vanity mirror was spotless and bright, reflecting her exactly as she was now: face pale as paper, the hair at her forehead soaked with cold sweat and plastered to her skin, her eyes still holding traces of a fright that hadn't fully faded, a daze, and an exhaustion that seemed to cut across time and space. The searing sting in her fingertips was long gone, leaving only smooth skin.

Bracing herself against the cold floor, she slowly sat up; every joint felt rusted. When she gripped the edge of the basin to stand, her legs went weak and she had to lean there a moment. The woman in the mirror slowly pulled her gaze back into focus. The clarity, the composure, and the certain hardness at the core of thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan settled back over her, inch by inch, forcing the shock and emotional aftershock of that bizarre journey through time down to the very deepest part of her eyes.

She reached up and turned off the bathtub tap—it had, astonishingly, been trickling the whole time—then looked around. Everything was exactly as it had been when she "left." Nothing was out of place.

Was it a dream?

No. The details were too vivid—the smell of sweat on a young Jianghai, the dry, dusty heat of the summer air in 2012, the angry tears in the eyes of her twenty-two-year-old self, and finally those bone-chillingly cold words "she" had said to herself... Every single moment was terrifyingly real, branded into her mind, carrying a heavy weight.

She walked into the living room and picked up the phone she'd tossed onto the sofa. The screen lit up, its cold white light making her squint.

The time read: 00:47.

It felt like no more than an hour since she'd stepped into the bathroom. But in that strange space where pure white had interwoven with the past, it had seemed an impossibly long stretch of time.

A vast, cold loneliness, riding the silence of the late night, wrapped around her without a sound. The loneliness didn't come from being alone—it came from the eerie conversation she'd just had with her own past, one she could tell no one, one no one would believe. She was like a traveler who had just tumbled back from a parallel world, road-worn and weathered, with no one to know where she'd been.

She needed a little sound—a living, breathing trace of the "now," of "reality"—to confirm her own existence, to drive away the too-vivid ghosts of the past in her mind. She needed someone... who wouldn't press for details, yet could offer some rational anchor.

Her fingers slid unconsciously across the cold screen, skimming past a string of names both familiar and distant, until at last they stopped on a contact saved as "Yu Hao."

"Boyfriend companion"—that was how she'd positioned Yu Hao in her mind. Thirty years old, a University of Hong Kong graduate, from a family that married media and business. His résumé was the stuff of legend in their circle: he'd made his first bucket of gold in his sophomore year, weathered several setbacks during university—even facing expulsion—yet, on the strength of formidable persuasiveness and resilience, not only graduated smoothly but had already amassed a startling fortune by the time he did. Now he was an independent star financing advisor, specializing in cutting-edge fields like healthcare and tech going global, renowned for his ruthlessly sharp eye, his extreme efficiency, and his knack for linking up top-tier resources. People in the industry described him as "bold and confident, deeply versed in human nature, a man who sets the rules himself."

They'd met a year earlier at a private cocktail reception after a top-tier tech forum. At the time, Yao Yuan had just led her team into exploring AI, seeking strategic resources for the next phase; Yu Hao was one of the youngest invited keynote speakers at that forum, his topic "Efficiency Above All: The Misallocation of Resources in the New Consumer Era." The two of them had slipped away from the noise to the terrace and talked for half an hour over champagne—from choosing the right track to the weaknesses of human nature, from business models to the marginal utility of romantic relationships. No flirtatious probing, only the satisfaction of meeting a worthy opponent and a clear-eyed appraisal of each other's value. Soon they reached an unspoken understanding: for occasions that called for a presentable partner, they were each other's best "face" and conversational extension; in private, they were companions who could sample the city's hottest new restaurants together, talk industry trends and observations on human nature, and occasionally take in a niche art exhibition with a high barrier to entry; and when there was a brief physical or emotional need, they could satisfy each other with the same easy understanding, leaving no loose ends afterward. No talk of profound love, no talk of long-term binding, no intrusion into each other's core social circle or career decisions—keeping a comfortable distance and absolute respect. It was a highly modern "strategic ██ relationship," stripped of romantic illusion, built on powerful rational awareness and the principle of mutual benefit.

Yu Hao met every "practical" requirement she had for a partner at this stage of her life: he was accomplished enough, with a dazzling background, so taking him out was a plus; he was smart enough and on the same wavelength, so the cost of communicating with him was minimal; he was clear-headed enough and clear about his goals, so he wouldn't sink into pointless emotional drain; and most importantly, he was busy enough—and understood the boundaries of this kind of relationship well enough—that he wouldn't over-encroach on her time and attention. As he so often said: "Pick the wrong rules and all your effort is wasted. Only within the right relationship framework can efficiency be maximized."

It was now nearly one in the morning. Making this call was a bit of a breach—it broke their unspoken rule of "no late-night contact unless necessary." But Yao Yuan couldn't be bothered with that now. She needed to hear a calm voice that belonged to the present, a voice that could haul her out of the surreal mire and back onto the solid ground of reality.

The phone rang three times before it was picked up. The background held some kind of very low-volume, slow-paced electronic music, which was promptly switched off, leaving an absolute, focused quiet.

"Yao Yuan?" Yu Hao's voice came through, low, with the slight rasp and clarity unique to someone who had just finished intense mental work—not the faintest trace of irritation at being disturbed, only a precise note of inquiry. His ability to manage his background was first-rate; even taking her call at this hour, his first instinct was to assess priority and potential value—the instinct of a top financing advisor.

"Mm, it's me." Only as she spoke did Yao Yuan realize her voice was a little dry. She cleared her throat and walked to the floor-to-ceiling window, looking out at the sleeping city and its scattered lights. "Did I wake you?"

"Just wrapped up a video call with a Silicon Valley fund partner—jet lag." Yu Hao's tone was steady as ever, carrying a reassuring looseness. "A call at this hour isn't your style. Run into something thorny?"

He didn't ask "what's wrong," he asked "did you run into something thorny," steering the conversation straight to the problem-solving level. That was very Yu Hao.

Yao Yuan was silent for a few seconds. Everything from the world in the mirror churned in her mind, but in the end she chose the safest entry point—the one closest to a part of "reality." She needed to unburden herself, but not about traveling through time; about that cognitive shockwave and sense of emptiness the present had taken on after the past ambushed her without warning.

"I had a very long, very clear dream." Looking at her own blurred reflection in the glass, she spoke slowly, her voice carrying a rare fragility even she hadn't noticed. "I dreamed of years ago, right after I graduated, back when I was in Jingshi—living in a rented room, no air conditioning in summer, treating a router repair like a major event, ready to stamp my feet if my phone froze... The details were terrifyingly real, like... like plugging the hard drive of my memory straight into my brain and playing it back."

The line went quiet for a moment, only Yu Hao's steady breathing. Then he spoke, his voice as calm as if he were analyzing a project due-diligence report. "Retrospective scenario reconstruction. A break in high-intensity work, layered with recent exposure to past-associated information carrying a heavy emotional load—say, Zhao Yiming and Jin Lan, whom you saw this evening, with a conversation touching on emotional AI and memory data. In a relaxed state, the brain's prefrontal cortex is prone to launching a deep memory-sorting program, dramatically recombining fragments of experience that haven't been fully 'archived' or that carry strong emotional markers. It's a psychological defense mechanism, and also a signal of cognitive iteration."

His analysis was calm, rational, proceeding entirely from neuropsychology and real-world associative logic, instantly hauling that bizarre, kaleidoscopic "journey through the mirror" back into the realm of the explicable and the manageable. He'd even pinpointed the likely trigger—the meeting with Zhao Yiming and Jin Lan. This was exactly what Yao Yuan needed right now: a powerful "dimensional reduction strike" grounded in real-world logic and cognitive science, to "normalize" and "technicalize" the surreal experience.

"Maybe." Yao Yuan went along with him, feeling her taut nerves loosen a fraction. "It's just that when I woke up I felt... a little empty. Like I was standing outside of time, watching a film whose ending was already written, but I still had to act it out all over again. It felt a little... absurd. I started to wonder whether the path I've walked these past ten-plus years really had to be walked at all."

"Detachment and existential questioning. A common response after a high-intensity cognitive or emotional shock." Yu Hao's voice came through the receiver, steady as ballast, carrying his particular habit of converting every experience into a cognitive model. "From an evolutionary-psychology standpoint, this ability to 'examine the self from outside of time' is a uniquely human, higher-order cognitive function—the basis for strategic planning and path optimization. Feeling it's 'absurd' means your cognitive system is attempting to reassess and reassign value to the path you've taken. That's a good thing."

He paused, seeming to take a sip of water, and went on: "As for whether it 'had to be walked'... my view is that path dependence is real, but the 'optimal solution' usually only reveals itself in hindsight. The key isn't agonizing over sunk costs already incurred—it's extracting reusable 'algorithms' and a 'guide to avoiding pitfalls' from your existing experience, to optimize your future decision tree. As I always say: pick the wrong rules and all your effort is wasted. But once the rules are validated, you have to squeeze every bit of efficiency out of them."

His words were like a precise scalpel, cutting open the chaos of what she felt right now to reveal the rational veins inside. She thought of the things she—in the mirror—had said to her younger self about "cutting losses," "shortcuts," "saving yourself." Her twenty-two-year-old self couldn't take them in, because back then her "mental model" and "cognitive processing power" weren't yet enough to run such a complex early-warning program built on future pain feedback. And her thirty-six-year-old self, having lived through all of it, had finally "compiled" that pain into runnable "warning code."

"So," she said in a low voice, more as if sorting out her own thoughts, "some roads maybe really do have to be walked yourself—you have to run the 'error code' out before you can fully understand why it was wrong, and then write a more robust 'program.'"

"Exactly right." Yu Hao replied, his tone carrying a rare, almost appreciative note. "Personal trial and error is one of the most efficient ways to learn—provided the cost of the trial is controllable and you can review and iterate in time. The thing most to be feared is looping inside the same error pattern without upgrading your cognitive framework. That 'dream' of yours just now was actually a deep, high-fidelity 'stress-test replay.' Your brain was running a war-game simulation for you. The process is painful, but the result of the simulation—that 'sense of the absurd' and that 'emptiness'—is itself extremely valuable output data. It's telling you that certain underlying emotional operating logic or dependency paths may need to be patched, or even rebuilt."

War-game simulation... stress-test replay... Yao Yuan repeated the words silently. Yu Hao could always use the language of the field he knew best to deconstruct the most chaotic emotional experience into clear operating instructions. It gave her a strange sense of reassurance—no matter how bizarre the experience, in his hands it could seemingly be brought into some rational framework for analysis, dissolving its uncontrollable terror.

"Do you need me to come over?" Yu Hao changed tack, offering a very practical set of options. "My driver can be there within twenty minutes. Or, if you think digesting this 'output data' on your own is more efficient, we can stay on the line—you can keep talking, or just listen, until your system stabilizes. As it happens, I have to deal with some key points from the minutes of that meeting."

He didn't say "don't overthink it," offered no hollow comfort, but laid out two solutions based on different "efficiency models." Coming over meant physical company and possibly more direct "system debugging" (physical comfort included); staying on the line meant remote technical support and "system-log analysis" on the mental level. He handed the decision back to her, while making clear he had a parallel task on hand, so she wouldn't feel any "guilt over occupying his resources."

Yao Yuan closed her eyes for a moment. The city outside the window seemed to breathe slowly along with her gradually steadying heartbeat. Yu Hao's calm was like a potent, efficient sedative, pulling her fully free of that panic of dislocated time and space.

"Mm—I miss you a lot tonight." she said, her voice recovering its usual strength and clarity, even with a touch of coquetry. "What about your minutes? Will it set back your work?"

"No effect—my pleasure! I can finish them on the way over." Yu Hao seemed to smile, almost imperceptibly, very faint, but through the current it carried a slight, relaxing vibration.

The call didn't end; she could hear, on his end, the sounds of packing up a laptop, grabbing clothes, heading out the door.

Yao Yuan opened a saved track of Alpha-wave background music while walking to the sofa, sinking into the soft cushions and pulling a thin blanket over her knees. An exceedingly faint, soothing electronic sound—like the surge of the deep sea or wind passing through a pine forest—rose gently.

She put the phone on speaker and set it beside her. Soon the receiver carried the sounds of Yu Hao getting into the car and starting the engine.

"Has your system stabilized on your end? Should I read you the few points of consensus from the talk just now with the Silicon Valley side about the 'emotional-computing investment track'? I guarantee the information density is high enough to help take your mind off things."

His rare, faintly teasing offer curved the corner of Yao Yuan's mouth, almost imperceptibly. The tension dissolved completely.

On Yu Hao's end, the evenly paced clatter of a keyboard, the rustle of pages being turned, and the soft, soothing background music wove together into a reassuring white noise of order that belonged to the real world—steady, continuous, full of purpose.

Those scenes of the summer of 2012, of young Jianghai, of the cold conversation in the mirror, seemed, against this steady and "productive" backdrop, to be loaded into a background process—still occupying memory, but no longer frantically seizing the foreground of her attention, no longer holding the power to tear at the boundary of reality.

She looked at the ceiling, listening to that regular sound that belonged to another high-efficiency brain at work, and suddenly spoke, her voice very soft, less a question than an act of cognitive calibration: "Yu Hao, do you believe in 'prophecy'? Or rather, warnings from the future that try to help you avoid pain?"

The keyboard barely paused; Yu Hao's answer came almost instantly, with his characteristic, categorical clarity: "No. I only believe in probabilistic judgments based on sufficient data and logical deduction. So-called 'prophecy' or 'warning' is, at its core, a cognitive gap under information asymmetry. The side with more information—even information from a hypothetical future—is trying to intervene in the behavior of the side that lacks it. But the problem is, first, the truth and completeness of the information can't be verified; second, even if the information is true, the recipient's cognitive level, decision model, and execution environment may differ enormously from the provider's, causing the intervention to fail or even backfire."

He tapped the keyboard a few times and went on: "It's like if I told a nineteen-year-old version of myself—one who firmly believes love conquers all, whose cognitive framework hasn't yet been thoroughly 'stress-tested' by reality: 'Don't start a company, go get a job, it's more stable.' Would he listen? No. Because he doesn't have my current cognitive model; he can't understand the real weight of 'stability' at a particular life stage, nor can he grasp the specific pains and gains of the startup process. He has to go run into it himself, to trial-and-error, to accumulate his own 'training data,' before he can iterate into the cognitive version capable of understanding that sentence of mine. So I'm highly skeptical of 'prophecy.' What's more effective is probably providing methodology and thinking frameworks, not specific path instructions."

Yao Yuan listened in silence. His words once again struck the core with precision. Wasn't her present thirty-six-year-old self doing exactly that—trying to "intervene in the behavior" of her twenty-two-year-old self? And the result was fierce resistance. Because the cognitive versions were incompatible. The twenty-two-year-old Yao Yuan didn't yet have the "system environment" required to run that "pain code."

"So," she summed up softly, "some roads maybe really do have to be walked yourself. You only learn how hard the wall is by hitting it—and how hard your own head is, too, or where to go to find the right tools."

"But when you walk it, you can carry more precise measuring tools and a richer library of maps." Yu Hao replied. The keyboard sounds stopped, as if he'd finished a paragraph. "Even if it turns out to be a load-bearing wall in the end, at least you'll know its material parameters and stress distribution, so next time you can calculate the demolition charge more precisely, or simply take a detour. It's purely emotional, repeated head-on collisions that are the greatest waste of cognitive resources and time capital. Our goal is always to raise the expected value—the EV—of every decision."

Expected value... EV... Yao Yuan thought: did her thirty-six-year-old self, in the mirror, raise the future decision EV of her younger self? Not necessarily. But perhaps it had planted, in her thirty-six-year-old self's heart, a seed about "reassessing the path." And this companion at her side—calm, efficient, supplying steady "system white noise" and "cognitive frameworks"—he himself was a very-high-EV "strategic asset" in this stage of her life's decisions.

"Thank you." she said suddenly, her voice very soft but clear, no longer carrying the earlier dryness and daze.

The line went quiet for a beat, and then Yu Hao's voice came through, steady as ever, but seemingly a touch gentler than before, like a precision instrument completing a successful calibration: "You're welcome. The minutes are done—I'm at your door."

The lock gave a soft electronic click—Yu Hao letting himself in with the temporary code she'd given him before. His footsteps were light, carrying the faint chill of the night air.

Yao Yuan didn't get up; she stayed curled on the sofa, only turning her head toward the entryway. Yu Hao appeared at the edge of the living room's warm amber glow. He wore a well-tailored dark-gray cashmere coat over a simple black turtleneck, a slim laptop bag in hand. At thirty, his figure was tall and straight, his bearing clean; his face showed none of the fatigue of a late-night drive—instead a kind of clear-headed sharpness that came after handling something thorny. Only those always-too-calm eyes showed, in the lamplight, a rare softness now.

"Very efficient." Yao Yuan said, her voice still carrying a little of her curled-up languor.

"Just minutes—practice makes perfect." Yu Hao set the laptop bag on the entryway cabinet, took off his coat and hung it up offhandedly, the movements smooth and natural. He walked over to the sofa, didn't sit down right away, but first bent down and lightly pressed his palm to her forehead—a touch free of desire, confirming her status. "Temperature's normal. CPU not fried?"

His fingertips carried the cool of the outdoors, and it felt good against her skin. Yao Yuan closed her eyes and, almost imperceptibly, nuzzled into his palm. "Not fried. Just ran a few high-power-consumption memory processes—a bit of lag."

"A restart will fix it." Yu Hao withdrew his hand and sat down in the single armchair beside her, legs casually crossed. He glanced at the empty wine glass on the coffee table, his gaze lingering less than half a second, and asked nothing. "The Silicon Valley side's consensus on the 'emotional computing' track has a few key points—want to hear them? Or do you need more direct 'system maintenance'?"

He offered a choice, like presenting different solutions. Yao Yuan opened her eyes and looked at him. Yu Hao's face was especially clear in the soft light—straight-bridged nose, a clean, crisp jawline, the kind of handsomeness charged with modernity and intellectual appeal. He looked at her, his gaze calm, waiting for instructions, like the most reliable operating system.

"The consensus first." Yao Yuan said, pulling the thin blanket up a little. "I need a high-density stream of real-world data to overwrite the garbled code in the background."

"All right." Yu Hao leaned back slightly and began to lay it out, his pace steady, his wording precise, instantly drawing the room into another dimension of thought. "First, pure emotional companionship or chatbots have limited value—an obvious ceiling. Second, the real barrier lies in a 'personalized adaptation engine' and 'cross-scenario continuity,' and the difficulty is data acquisition and privacy compliance. Third, medical-grade applications—like assisted intervention for depression and anxiety—are currently the most favored path to commercialization, but the regulatory bar is extremely high. Fourth, and most important," he paused and looked at Yao Yuan, "almost all top-tier institutions believe this track will ultimately produce a few platform-type companies, and a platform's core competitiveness isn't just technology—it's a 'product philosophy' grounded in a profound understanding of complex human needs and ethical boundaries. Zhao Yiming's 'Genesis Engine' has technical ambition, but lacks the latter. Your 'Pretty Big One' has the embryo of the latter, but needs the former to empower it. A union of the two is logically sound, but the execution difficulty is hell-tier."

In a few sentences he placed her tense, undercurrent-laden negotiation with Zhao Yiming that afternoon into a grander, crueler coordinate system. Yao Yuan listened quietly, and the daze about the world in the mirror, about 2012, ebbed further. The hard, clear rules of the real world's game reasserted their dominance.

"So, what's the EV—the expected value—of cooperating with Zhao Yiming, in your view?" she asked, using the term he'd just raised.

"High risk, potentially super-high return, but a failure probability greater than seventy percent." Yu Hao answered without hesitation. "Zhao Yiming is a tech genius, and also a control freak. His 'Genesis Engine' is his life. For you to build an 'emotional application' on top of it is like operating on his core code. He won't hand over the scalpel easily. Your cooperation is, at its core, a fight for control. Your contact this afternoon only earned you the qualification to stand in and observe the surgery—you're a long way from holding the scalpel yourself."

"But if the surgery succeeds, the payoff is monopolizing the application gateway to a future hundred-billion-scale market." Yao Yuan picked up, her gaze turning sharp.

"Exactly. That's why it's worth the bet." Yu Hao nodded. "But the stakes have to be clear. Your stake is your 'Pretty Big One' data, your user ecosystem, your brand. His stake is partial technical openness and future platform earnings. You need to think clearly about the maximum loss you can accept, and where your stop-loss point is. And," he looked at her with a deep gaze, "whether you're ready to work, even spar, at such close range with a man like Zhao Yiming. He's not like any opponent you've met before."

His words were like ice water, sobering her completely. The mood that had been thrown into disorder that afternoon by Zhao Yiming's technical blueprint and the world in the mirror was now combed perfectly clear by Yu Hao's calm investment logic. Yes—control, stop-loss point, opponent assessment. This was the real-world game.

"I need a more detailed opponent-assessment report." Yao Yuan said, her voice recovering its cold clarity from working hours. "On Zhao Yiming, Western World, and every known and potential risk of the 'Genesis Engine.'"

"In your encrypted inbox before ten tomorrow morning." Yu Hao agreed, as if he'd long anticipated she'd need it. This was their understanding: he always supplied the most precise "ammunition" or "map" the moment she needed it.

With business settled, the room fell quiet for a while, only the Alpha-wave music echoing gently like a deep-sea undertow.

Yu Hao suddenly stood up, walked over to stand before her, then crouched on one knee, bringing his eyes level with hers. The posture broke the safe distance they usually kept, carrying a trace of invasiveness that brooked no refusal, yet his gaze remained calm, even gentle.

"Now," he said, his voice dropping a few degrees, "the high-density data stream has finished loading. According to the system-log analysis, your core anomaly tonight, beyond work stress, also involves a high-load retrospective of emotional memory. Pure rational overwriting may treat the symptom, not the root. Do you need to launch the auxiliary... physical cache-clearing program?"

He put it in extremely technical terms, but Yao Yuan understood. He was asking whether she needed physical contact, whether she needed a more direct way to soothe those awakened emotional ghosts of the past.

He didn't simply embrace or comfort her the way an ordinary boyfriend would; instead he gave a "program launch request" that required her confirmation. It preserved her absolute control, and fit the setup of their relationship—intimate yet boundary-keeping.

Yao Yuan looked into his eyes, right there before her, where her own face was clearly reflected.

"Launch approved." She heard herself say, her voice very soft, languid and coquettish.

The corner of Yu Hao's lips curved into a fine arc. He reached out—not to embrace, but his palm steadily cradling the back of her neck—and drew her gently toward himself, then kissed her.

This kiss was the opposite of his calm, restrained surface: ardent, eager, carrying an undeniable predatory force and skill. The two of them explored and soothed without pause in the narrow space of the sofa. His breath was clean and crisp, carrying a touch of the night's chill, quickly warmed by their shared body heat. Yao Yuan closed her eyes and let herself sink into the kiss, let his familiar, steady scent cover every sense. The Alpha-wave music still flowed, but the noisy clamor of memory, the afterimages of the world in the mirror, the dry heat and tears of the summer of 2012—all of it was finally, in this solid, rational kiss, temporarily driven off, overwritten, suppressed.

This was not love—at least not the kind of love she had understood at twenty-two. This was a more complex, more modern emotional contract: built on mutual appreciation, rational choice, clear boundaries, and efficient comfort. It promised no eternity, wove no fantasy, but in this moment it so truly and effectively fished her out of that lonely dislocation of time and space, anchoring her to the solid ground of the present.

She didn't know how long it lasted before Yu Hao drew back slightly, the tip of his nose brushing hers, his breathing a little ragged, but his eyes still clear. "Cache-clearing progress: thirty percent." he said in a low voice, using their code.

Yao Yuan couldn't help a soft laugh; that knot of gloom dissolved entirely. "Efficiency could use some improvement, Boss Yu."

"Feedback received—optimizing the program." Yu Hao stood and pulled her up from the sofa in the same motion. "I suggest we relocate the field of operations for deep system maintenance. This place," he glanced at the sofa, "isn't conducive to peak performance."

Yao Yuan rose on the strength of his pull, her legs still a little weak, and leaned against him. Yu Hao's arm circled her waist steadily, holding her up.

"I'll follow your lead." she said, burying her face in the cool collar of his sweater and drawing a deep breath—that clean, reassuring scent that belonged to this moment, to reality, to Yu Hao.

He held her and walked toward the bedroom.

In the bedroom only a single bedside reading lamp was on, dimmed to its lowest, blooming a patch of warm amber, blurred glow that just barely outlined the quiet contours of the furniture, leaving most of the space to a perfectly judged dimness. Drifting in the air was the faint, lingering scent of Yao Yuan's post-bath freshness, now mingled with the crisp aftershave and faint night chill Yu Hao had brought in, forming a distinctive, private atmosphere.

Yu Hao walked to the bedside and sat down beside her, the mattress dipping slightly. He didn't rush to touch her; instead, in the dim light, he gazed at her quietly for a few seconds, his eyes like the most precise scanner, carrying the aggression of desire, as if assessing the truest status parameters of her in this moment.

Yao Yuan lay on her side facing him, the sash of her silk robe already loosened, the collar fallen open to reveal a stretch of fair neck and a clear line of collarbone. In the warm dim light her gaze looked a little misted—no longer the alarm before the mirror, and stripped of the sharpness of talking shop—but a kind of slight bewilderment and weariness, all defenses laid down, and a faint, hard-to-detect dependence on his presence in this moment.

He reached out, his fingertips first lightly touching the still-damp ends of her hair spread across the pillow, then sliding slowly up along their curve, finally settling on her temple, where, with exceedingly steady pressure, he began to massage slowly in small circles. Less an easing of taut nerves than a tease.

"Detecting excessively high residual-thread occupancy." he said in a low voice, his voice especially clear in the silent room, yet softened by the dimness. "Attempting to force-quit non-critical processes."

The pads of his fingers were warm, the pressure perfectly judged, carrying a reassuring sense of control. Yao Yuan closed her eyes, her long, dense lashes casting faint shadows beneath them. She let his fingers roam over her temple, her forehead, and even the pressure-gathering points behind her ears, as if he really could, through this contact, clear away the stubborn residual "garbled code" in her mind from another time and space.

The massage went on a while, the silence holding only their light, shallow breaths and the faint friction of fingertips on skin. Then those steadily pressing fingers slid slowly downward, grazing the sensitive curve of her ear, brushing past her jawline, finally coming to rest at the collar of her robe. His fingertips, faint as a whisper, touched the skin at her collarbone, bringing a cool, electric-current-like shiver.

"Running a surface-level system scan." he said softly, more as if explaining the "program's" purpose to her. His kiss came down—first on the temple he'd just massaged, then along her brow bone, her eyelids, the bridge of her nose, drifting gently downward, finally covering her lips. This kiss was deeper than the one in the living room, and more patient, like a slow, rigorous data-synchronization protocol, free of impatient demand, trying instead, through close contact, to transmit a steady "confirmation signal" that belonged to this moment.

Yao Yuan's response was at first a little passive, as if she were still adjusting to this switch of channels from rational analysis to intimate contact. But gradually, led by his rhythm, she began to keep pace with this slow, firm "synchronization." Her arms wound around his neck, her fingertips sinking unconsciously into the taut muscle of his shoulders and back.
