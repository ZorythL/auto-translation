Spring comes late to the north, but when it arrives, it arrives with a roar.

After a few gusts of sharp early-spring wind, the pear trees lining the avenues seemed to ignite overnight. First a shy dot or two of pink, hidden among the grey-brown branches, and then it bloomed in sheets, in whole trees at once. That pink was unusual — not the boldness of peach blossom, not the paleness of cherry, but somewhere in between: pink shot through with white, white flushed with pink, with a crisp, reckless brilliance under the clear sunlight of early spring. The flowers crowded the branches, pressing against one another. From a distance they looked like puffs of pink-and-white cloud settling beside city streets that had been dry all winter; up close, each five-petaled blossom was delicate and open, the tender yellow stamens trembling as they reached out. When the wind passed, down came a soft rain of pink-and-white petals.

Yao Yuan's car drove slowly along the main road, awash in pear blossom. Now and then a petal stuck to the windshield, only to be carried off by the airflow. She watched that gorgeous, fleeting cloud of flowers and remembered someone once saying that spring in the north was a "rebirth" — watching bare branches inch into green, then bloom, as if anything could be torn down and started over. Not like the south, evergreen all year, where spring arrives lukewarm and blurred. These pear blossoms, blooming to their utmost as if they would wither tomorrow, did have a kind of resolute beauty.

Her phone buzzed in her palm, pulling her back from the flowers outside the window. The screen lit up.

It was Shuai Hongqiang: "Old Chen got back to Jinshi the day before yesterday. I've set something for three tomorrow afternoon, at the Guangu Hall. Are you free?"

The short message was like a pebble dropped into a heart that only looked calm, sending out ring after ring of invisible ripples.

The Mirror Pact. Time-travel. Those mystifying threads of cause and effect, those "anomalies" she still couldn't fully explain by logic but had genuinely lived through. Her throat tightened a little. Her fingertip hovered above the screen, paused three seconds, then pressed:

"Okay."

The reply came almost instantly: "Want me to come pick you up?"

"No need, I'll go on my own."

"Then… see you tomorrow."

The conversation ended. Yao Yuan stared at the darkened screen, her expressionless face faintly mirrored in the window. She thought of the day of the car crash at the ski resort after New Year's, when Shuai Hongqiang had brought up that brush with death at the "South Lake Cottage" — the complicated emotion that had flashed through his eyes, a mix of relief, guilt, and unease. At the time she had only vaguely felt something was off. Now that thread seemed to be slowly coming clear.

Some debts are invisible, like roots tangled deep in the early-spring earth — you never know when they'll push up through the seemingly solid ground.

Friday, 11 a.m.

The ring light snapped on, its cold white beam falling with the precision of a surgical lamp.

Yao Yuan sat in front of the camera. Today she wore a wine-red silk blouse, fitted to the body, with a deep V neckline that bared a stretch of collarbone and the faint hint of a curve. Over it was a black leather vest, sharply cut, the waistline pulled in extremely narrow. Her long hair was permed into loose, lazy waves, one side tucked behind her ear to show off an exaggerated geometric metal earring that refracted cold light with every movement. Her makeup was meticulous — brows arched high, eyeliner crisp, lips a rich vintage true-red that hit hard under the cold white light.

She adjusted her headset. The midnight-purple polish on her fingertips tapped the desk twice, a crisp sound that formed an odd duet with the faint rush of the Yellow River outside the window. Behind her was a full wall of floor-to-ceiling glass, the blinds half-closed now.

"Hello, my dears." She smiled and lifted her eyes to the camera. Her voice came through the professional sound card — steady, clear, carrying an unquestionable calm. "I'm Yao Yuan. Feelings aren't mysticism; they're a contest of human nature. Today, we keep dissecting."

"Little assistant, please connect our first call-in of the day."

【Call connected】

Caller (a woman's voice, 36, struggling to stay level, but exhaustion and resentment leaking through underneath): "Teacher Yuanyuan, good evening. I've followed you for a long time, and today I finally worked up the courage to call in. My situation… might be a little complicated. I'm 36 this year, with a five-year-old son. The child's father — I'm used to calling him Old Deng — he's 55."

(She paused, took a deep breath.)

"We met at a business event, when I was 27. He had a lot of energy, an expert in a certain field, very well connected. In the first three years together he really did introduce me to a lot of business, helped me get my footing. I'll admit, back then there was gratitude, and also… a reliance on that mature, steady quality of his, on his ability to pull me up. Later I got pregnant and had my son. I thought… this would make our relationship more solid, that he'd give us — me and the child — a clear future."

Yao Yuan (not interrupting, just leaning her body slightly forward, eyes sharp): "Mm. 'Thought.' And then? What does 'a clear future' mean? Marriage? Or an arrangement of assets?"

Caller (her voice beginning to tremble): "It's… both, I suppose. But he never brought up marriage. When I pressed him, he finally admitted he was indeed divorced, but… divorced without leaving home. His ex-wife and their 28-year-old son live in the U.S. year-round, but the house in China, the main social ties — they're all still bound up together. He said it was for the child, for face, for a lot of things he couldn't help."

"I put up with all of it. I told myself, at least we have a child; he'll have to plan for the child after all. But no! Teacher Yuanyuan, nothing! My son is five, and he's never mentioned putting any property in my son's name, never bought any insurance, never set up any trust. When I raise it, he says, 'You're still young, what's the hurry,' 'Isn't all my future going to be yours,' 'The business needs cash flow right now.' But he pays his American son's tuition, buys him a house, without even blinking! My son and I are like… like a project he keeps on the side, given just enough resources to keep going when needed, but never allowed onto the main road, let alone a share of the estate!"

(Her emotion surged, a sob entering her voice.)

"I really don't know what to do anymore! My youth is sunk into it, I've had the child, my career… though it started out leaning on him, it's hit a ceiling now, and the money I earn independently barely keeps my son and me living with some dignity. Leave him? I can't bring myself to, and I'm afraid of the child growing up without a father. Stay? I can't see any hope at all! I'm like someone clutching a straw that's about to snap, drifting in the water! What do I do, Teacher Yuanyuan?"

Yao Yuan (after listening, she didn't speak right away. She leaned back into her chair, hands folded, that midnight-purple polish like the scales of some cold-blooded animal under the cold light. She laughed softly, a short laugh with no warmth):

"Fine. Done crying? Done resenting? Now, take all that mush in your head about 'can't bring myself to,' 'can't see any hope,' 'what do I do,' and pour it out. We are going to do the accounting."

She bit off each word, every one falling like a bead of ice.

"First, get clear on your 'project positioning' and the true nature of your 'client.'"

"You, and your son, in Old Deng's eyes, are not a 'family project' worthy of long-term investment and future planning — not even an important 'subsidiary.' You're merely a 'short-term strategic cooperation project' struck during a particular phase (his domestic emptiness, your need for resources) — he put up the connections and resources (business intros), you put up your youth, companionship, and reproductive value. The project ran three years; the core objectives (helping him sustain his domestic emotional life, you gaining start-up resources for your career) have already been met. The follow-up 'project maintenance' — raising the child, keeping you and your son in basic dignity — is the 'minimum operating cost' he pays to protect his own reputation and avoid trouble. As for the project's 'future planning' or 'asset injection'? Sorry, that clause was never written into this cooperation contract. His core assets and inheritance plan were signed over long ago to his 'lifelong strategic partner' — the ex-wife and that American son. You were never on his list of heirs. You don't even rate a footnote."

【Danmu: [Damn, business breakdown, right to the bone!] [Short-term strategic cooperation project… brutal but so true!]】

"Second, and the point you most need to see clearly —" Yao Yuan leaned forward, her gaze blazing, "about your son. A child born out of wedlock — what, in law, can he actually 'get,' and what can he 'not get.'"

She paused, her voice turning unusually clear and cool, as if reading out a statute:

"Under Article 1071 of the Civil Code, children born out of wedlock enjoy the same rights as children born in wedlock, and no organization or individual may harm or discriminate against them. That sounds lovely, but when it hits reality, you need to break it down."

"What your son 'can get':"

"1. The right to claim child support. As the biological father, Old Deng has a statutory duty to bear your son's support — living costs, education, medical care, and so on — until he turns 18 (or until he can support himself). This is your son's most basic right, and the only thing you can currently demand outright, by legal force."

"2. The right of inheritance. If Old Deng dies without leaving a valid will, then your son, as a first-order statutory heir (a child), enjoys equal inheritance rights with his child born in wedlock (that son in America). Note — it's 'equal,' not 'split evenly'; the exact share depends on the other heirs."

"3. The right to confirm paternity. If Old Deng denies the parent-child relationship, you can sue to demand a paternity test to confirm it. Once confirmed, the rights above arise automatically."

【Danmu: [So a child out of wedlock really does have inheritance rights!] [But only if there's no will…]】

"Now, here's the key point —" Yao Yuan's gaze turned sharp. "What your son 'very likely can't get':"

"1. Exclusion through testamentary inheritance. Under the Civil Code, testamentary inheritance takes priority over statutory inheritance. That means Old Deng can fully, and very likely will, by drawing up a valid will, designate the vast majority or even all of his property to his child born in wedlock (the American son), to his ex-wife, or to anyone else, thereby legally stripping your son of his share. At that point, 'isn't all my future going to be yours' will be a thoroughly worthless IOU."

"2. The limited protection of the 'reserved share.' The law does provide a 'reserved share' system — a will must reserve a necessary share for an heir who lacks the ability to work and has no source of income. But as long as your son is an adult able to support himself, he doesn't fall into this category. He's five now; by the age Old Deng might die (say seventy or eighty), he'll long since be grown. This 'safety net' is basically useless to your son."

"3. The risk of asset transfer. From 55 to death — twenty or thirty years — is plenty of time for a shrewd man, through gifts, sales, setting up trusts, and all manner of legal means, to transfer his assets clean away. By then, even with no will, the 'estate' left may amount to next to nothing."

"4. Emotional and identity recognition. The law can grant rights, but it can't grant a father's love, can't grant open acknowledgment, can't grant a whole family environment. These are things your son will never 'get' — as long as Old Deng doesn't want to give them."

Yao Yuan looked at the camera, as if she could see through the screen to that woman's bloodless face:

"So you're counting on 'waiting till he's old and getting a share of the estate through your son'?" She let out a cold laugh. "That's like counting on a dealer who can change the rules at any moment — even pick up the whole board and walk off — to pay out dividends according to the rules you imagined. Naive, and stupid."

【Danmu: [So true! One will and it's all over!] [Transferring assets is way too easy!] [The rights the law gives and what you can actually collect are two different things!]】

"Third, stop tallying 'sunk costs' and start taking stock of your 'remaining assets' and 'claimable rights.'"

"What you put in was: the nine golden years of your youth, from 27 to 36; the physical wear and the career-interruption risk of one childbirth; and unrealistic emotional and future expectations of this relationship. These have sunk; you can't fish them back. No matter how unwilling you are, they won't turn into your son's education fund. What you need to do now is seize the rights the law currently, clearly grants your son — rights that are enforceable."

"What's left in your hands now?"

"1. A five-year-old son he is legally required to acknowledge. (This is your most powerful card right now, and also your most fragile.)"

"2. The basic foundation of a career you can barely stand on your own. (It started out leaning on him, but it's yours now.)"

"3. You're only 36. Unmarried, legally a single mother. (Which means your choices on the marriage market — complicated though they are — aren't locked dead by a marriage certificate.)"

"4. Your last, and most important, clear-eyed read on his human nature."

Yao Yuan's gaze blazed, fixed on the camera, as if she could see through the screen to that woman's bloodless face. Her words sped up, each one like a driven nail:

"Based on all that, what I'm giving you is not 'what to do,' but a 'three-step stop-loss and rights-securing plan' you must execute immediately:"

"Step one: completely give up the fantasy of 'angling for the estate.' Lock your target onto 'securing your current statutory rights,' and shift your mindset to that of a 'single mother.'"

"From now on, stop treating him as a 'husband,' a 'father,' a 'support.' Treat him as 'a blood relative who is required to pay child support on a regular basis — a stranger.' Your one and only lawful claim on him is the child's support. Expect nothing else. Pull back all your emotional energy, your time, your effort, and pour it into yourself, into your son. The less you expect, the less he can control you with 'IOUs for the future.' The more independent you are, the more he may have to pay in real terms if he still wants to keep up appearances."

"Step two: use the 'biological son' card to sign an 'irrevocable security contract.'"

"Go talk to him — but don't cry, don't make a scene, don't talk feelings, don't talk about the future. Talk only about the child, about the law. In the coolest, most professional tone, tell him: 'Old Deng, the boy is five. He needs long-term education planning and health protection. I want nothing else from you, but as his father, you must give your son a basic, written guarantee. Under Article 1071 of the Civil Code, you have a duty of support. We need to sign an agreement, and the contents are simple:"

"1. Set up a growth fund/trust dedicated to your son, into which you inject a sum — in one payment or in installments (the amount must be specific, enough to make him wince but not impossible for him to produce) — earmarked for your son's education, medical care, and living up to age 18. This money is jointly supervised by you and me, but any use requires both signatures, to ensure it goes to the son."

"2. Buy your son full coverage of critical-illness insurance and savings-type insurance, with an irrevocable beneficiary."

"3. A legally notarized child-support payment agreement with a clearly stated amount (a specific figure can reference 20–30% of his income), pegged to your income growth.'"

"Stress this above all: this is the most basic statutory duty of a father, not charity. If he refuses or stalls even on this, you can 'gently' remind him that a father who is irresponsible, who has money yet won't pay child support, is not only morally deficient — you can also sue him in court to compel him to perform his duty. Drag it into court, and the damage to his face and social reputation will likely outweigh this bit of money by far. Remember, you are not begging him; you are notifying him to perform a statutory duty, and using an agreement to set that duty in stone. He wants face — so you build the 'responsible father' face-project for him, brick by brick, in cold hard cash."

"Step three: restart the main line of your life. Turn your career into the true 'estate' for you and your son."

"The days of relying on him for business intros are over. From now on, run your career as the one and only, the true 'estate' for you and your son's future. Analyze your existing business: which parts truly hold up on your own ability? Cut the ones that depend entirely on his face and have built nothing. Hone your professional skills to the limit; expand a client base and network that belong wholly to you. Even if you start from scratch, even if it's slower, let every step be solid, every step carry your name."

"At the same time, reassess your own worth on the marriage market. A 36-year-old woman with a child, but economically independent and clear-headed, still has a market in the right circles. But next time, remember: read the legal documents before the sweet talk; discuss the security clauses before the moonlight and roses. Your goal is not to find another 'Old Deng,' but a 'partner' who can fight shoulder to shoulder with you and share the future."

Yao Yuan summed up at the end, her voice categorical:

"So, back to your original question: what do I do?"

"The answer is: stop being the failed supplier waiting on a client to toss you the final payment of a project. Convert, right now, into the defender and CEO of your own and your son's rights. Demote Old Deng to your son's 'biological father and child-support payer' — beyond that, he has nothing to do with your feelings or your future."

"Your task is no longer to beg him for a vague, intangible 'family estate,' but to use every legal means to secure for your son the specific protections he's currently entitled to. And then, without a backward glance, go build the real 'family enterprise' that belongs to you and your son — one no one can take away."

"Wasting your youth on a dog isn't the terrible part. The terrible part is spending the years you have left waiting for that dog to turn around and toss you a meaty bone. Your future and your son's are not in his will — they're in every clear-eyed decision you make, every solid action you take, and in the rights the law sets down for your son in black and white. Now, hang up the phone, take out pen and paper, and start drafting that 'support security agreement.' That is the thing you and your son should truly be 'angling for.'"

Yao Yuan didn't look at the flashing comments. She leaned back in her chair and rubbed her brow. Outside the window, the Yellow River shimmered in the early-spring sun, and the pear blossoms along the main road were at their most brilliant. She thought of Yu Hao, who had likewise used "the future" and "feelings" as bait, trying to get her to accept a relationship with no security.

Luckily, she'd woken early, and learned fast enough.

The law is the bottom line, a weapon — but it can never be the only thing you count on. True security always comes from one's own strength and clarity.

【Call disconnected】

There was no ripple on Yao Yuan's face, as if she had just finished a precise risk-assessment report. She faced the camera and said, lightly:

"Counting on someone else's conscience and someone else's will — a man's, especially — to secure your future, is worse than making yourself an unshakable asset right now. All right, connect the next one."
