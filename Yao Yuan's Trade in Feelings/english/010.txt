A Mother's Thorns, a Daughter's Armor
Yao Yuan's mother, Zhang Fengxia—a mother's thorns, a daughter's armor.
The car pulled away from the bustling restaurant street and headed toward an upscale residential complex in Jinshi's new district. The sweet richness of the lamb and the smooth depth of the sesame paste still lingered in her mouth, and the good food and the touch of alcohol had left her body warm and content. Deep down there was also a faint, hard-to-name fatigue and a kind of satisfaction left over from the fierce tangle of last night and this morning. Her stepfather's words, and that thoroughly pleasant lunch where host and guest alike had enjoyed themselves, were like a soft yet sturdy cushion. They eased some of her usual tension about facing the mother who waited ahead, and that "home" that was always thick with a subtle atmosphere.
Home was just up ahead—the "home" her mother had built after remarrying, the one that always felt a little distant and quietly awkward to her. In it was the mother she was tied to by blood yet bound to by a complicated relationship; a half-brother fourteen years younger, sharing a mother but not a father; a lavishly decorated space that never quite let her relax; old knots in the heart still waiting to be untied; and perhaps, too, some attempt to mend them. Over the years, distance and politeness had become the way they got along.
She was no longer the twenty-two-year-old girl with nothing, who had only the fantasy of love to lean on. Nor was she the lone woman startled by the chaos in time the night before. She was coming back to face all of it carrying her own career, her own calculations, her own armor—and a "strategic partnership" that, while unconventional, was solid enough.
The car glided smoothly through the complex gate. The winter afternoon sun was just right, and the city showed a kind of transparent, faintly cold splendor in the cold season. Through the car window, light and shadow flickered across her calm profile.
Stepping out, she checked her outfit one more time. She had put some thought into what to wear home today. Not so casual that it seemed disrespectful, not so formal that it seemed deliberate or distant—ideally a tasteful, understated signal of "excellence" and "independence."
In the end she had chosen a soft cream cashmere sweater, paired with well-cut charcoal-gray wool straight-leg trousers, over which she wore a long camel cashmere coat, with low-heeled ankle boots on her feet. Her hair was loosely pinned up with a simple ebony hairpin, and her makeup was so light it was almost invisible, accenting only the shape of her brows and the color of her lips to brighten her complexion. At her ears were that pair of pale lavender jade earrings; on her wrist, the square leather-strap watch she wore every day. Overall she looked gentle and tasteful, calm in bearing, with a self-possessed ease and certainty—the kind that comes after weathering storms, and that no one could take lightly.
At thirty-six, Yao Yuan was no longer the one who passively endured. She was a traveler who had crossed through the fog, a player who knew how to calculate EV in tangled relationships. She had her battlefield, her calculations, her armor.
What had to be faced always had to be faced. Right now, she needed to face another concrete, real part of where her own life had begun. And this time, she seemed better prepared for this "going home" than she had ever been before.
The elevator rose smoothly, the numbers ticking upward. Yao Yuan looked at her reflection in the metal doors—that flawless image she had so carefully crafted. But when the elevator stopped with a "ding" at her floor, some corner of her heart still curled up despite her.
Behind that heavy solid-wood door was Zhang Fengxia's world.
When Old Zhou opened the door, she paused for half a second, drew in a deep breath, and slowly let it out. She closed her eyes, then opened them again, her gaze clear and calm.
The apartment was very quiet. The three o'clock afternoon sun slanted in through the balcony's glass door. The spacious entryway floor was polished to a mirror shine, and in the passage leading to the living room, a huge peony embroidery hung dead center, its gold thread glinting under the light. This was Zhang Fengxia's taste—warm, direct, making no effort to hide her longing for "the good life." The light cast diamond-shaped patches across the living room floor. A faint scent of tea drifted in the air, along with the lingering trace of burned sandalwood incense—the incense her mother always used, said to calm the mind.
"Mom?" Yao Yuan called into the apartment.
"In here!" The voice from the balcony came strong and clear. There was a rustling, and Zhang Fengxia pushed open the glass door and stepped out, a small spray bottle still in her hand, misting the few pots of golden pothos. She wore a deep purple house outfit with a charcoal-gray cardigan thrown over it, her dark-brown dyed hair pulled into a neat bun at the back of her head. The thin winter sunlight on her hair made it look almost soft.
Zhang Fengxia straightened up and turned her head. Time had carved deep marks into this woman's face, but those eyes hadn't changed—bright as a fire-tempered blade, able to see right through a person at a glance.
"Yao Yuan's back?" She glanced up at Yao Yuan, then went back to what she was doing. She didn't say "Yuanyuan"; she used the full name, "Yao Yuan." That form of address had run through Yao Yuan's whole childhood—when she slacked off at dance practice, when her exam scores fell short, or simply when Zhang Fengxia was in a bad mood.
Yao Yuan took off her coat and hung it up, then walked toward the balcony. She stopped a few steps short of the balcony door—just the right distance.
"Have you eaten with your Uncle Zhou?" In all the years since Zhang Fengxia had remarried Zhou Lufei, she had never required Yao Yuan to switch to calling him "Dad." She had always called him Uncle Zhou, and Zhou Lufei didn't mind.
"Yeah, I had hotpot at the shop with Yuanyuan and her good friend Yu Hao," Zhou Lufei said with a smile, having changed into comfortable clothes and come forward.
"Listen to you, currying favor." Zhang Fengxia gave Old Zhou a soft snort, set down the spray bottle, wiped her hands on the towel beside her, and walked into the living room.
Yao Yuan didn't answer. She knew there was a subtle way her mother and Uncle Zhou had with each other—the kind unique to couples of their age: dismissive on the surface, leaning on each other underneath.
Yao Yuan set down her bag and changed into slippers. Walking over to the coffee table in the living room, she saw a plate of washed winter jujubes on it, along with a few oranges—all the things she'd loved as a child. Her mother was always like this: never saying anything out loud, yet every detail full of remembering.
Zhang Fengxia's gaze swept over Yao Yuan from head to toe, from her hair to the tips of her shoes. There was a touch of criticism in it, and even more, a hard-to-detect pride.
"Sit. What are you standing around for—I'm not about to lecture you." Zhang Fengxia sat down on the sofa and patted the spot beside her.
Yao Yuan chose the single armchair off to the side, keeping a not-too-far, not-too-close distance from her mother. It was a distance they were both used to—close enough, yet leaving each other room. Zhou Lufei tactfully went off to the study, leaving the space to the two of them, mother and daughter.
The afternoon sun was warm, and the living room was very quiet. Zhang Fengxia picked up an orange and peeled it slowly, the orange-yellow rind splitting open between her rough fingers to reveal the plump flesh.
"Where's Xiaofan?" Yao Yuan asked.
"Still in Shanghai. Says he's working on some project with his advisor—won't be back until almost Little New Year." Zhang Fengxia broke off a segment of orange and handed it to Yao Yuan. "That boy, he's like your Uncle Zhou—can sit still, studying medicine." She paused, then looked up at her daughter. "Not like you. Couldn't sit still from the time you were little, always hopping and skipping around."
There was no telling whether it was praise or a dig. Yao Yuan took the orange, put it in her mouth. It was very sweet.
"Busy lately?" Zhang Fengxia asked, breaking off another segment for herself.
"It's year-end. The company's got a lot going on—a few important things I have to oversee myself," Yao Yuan said.
"You're getting more and more famous online, and more and more people know who you are. Those relatives and friends who watched your livestreams at the start, sneaking a look just for the show—they all come around to me now to fish for information. Some want to land their kid a job at your company, some want you to pull strings or find resources, some just want to meet you. I turned them all away. I don't want you held hostage by all this so-called family obligation. Those people have thick enough skin, too—not stopping to think how they laughed at you behind your back back then." Even now, remembering the faces those people used to make, Zhang Fengxia couldn't help feeling indignant.
She still remembered: five years ago, after Yao Yuan's first video blew up online, she'd started livestreaming all over the internet. Because of her bold, blunt, sharp-edged style, full of street-smart wisdom—but also because she objectified feelings and preached utilitarianism—she'd drawn fierce controversy across the whole internet. At the same time, amid those polarized, praise-and-blame-in-equal-measure arguments, she'd steadily become one of the top streamers in the relationship-and-emotional-advice space. Instead of sinking, she'd only grown more popular, with more and more people liking and agreeing with her views. Some gave her the title "the Godmother of Feelings"; others thought "the McKinsey of Feelings" fit her better.
But while going viral online brought Yao Yuan enormous traffic, it also disrupted the quiet life of her mother and Uncle Zhou, bringing them a certain amount of trouble.
Zhang Fengxia still clearly remembered those strange looks and the whispering from five years ago.
The first to "choke on the water" was Zhang Fengxia's own younger sister, Yao Yuan's aunt. The phone rang right on schedule at dinnertime, carrying a deliberately suppressed excitement: "Sis, that video of Yuanyuan's... aiya, it's really something! My Tingting watched it and admires her to death—says Sister Yuanyuan really sees through everything!" But the way the tone curved, Zhang Fengxia clearly heard another layer of meaning: your daughter's "brilliant theories" about how "marriage is starting a company together, feelings have to come with a clear price tag"—just don't let them lead our Tingting "astray." Zhang Fengxia mumbled vague replies, the pineapple in her mouth tasting like chewed wax.
Next came the subtle silence in the family WeChat group. In years past, during the Spring Festival rounds of relatives showing off their kids, there had always been the obligatory compliment: "Yuanyuan's doing so well at the Cultural Center, real prospects." Now the group was just as lively, sharing wellness articles and Pinduoduo group-buy links, yet no one mentioned Yao Yuan's name anymore. That figure who had swept the short-video platforms in the name of "the Beauty's Worry-Cure Shop"—sharp-tongued, stylishly dressed—had become an unspoken taboo. Now and then a cousin's wife would share one of Yuanyuan's hit videos about "female independence," followed immediately by a face-covering laughing emoji, and the group would fall into a vacuum of several minutes where no one picked up the thread. That vacuum sat heavy and uncomfortable in Zhang Fengxia's chest.
Grocery shopping, she ran into Auntie Zhang in the complex, who chased after her clutching her phone: "I watched that video of your Yao Yuan's, saying girls these days shouldn't always count on a man. There's nothing wrong with that, but does she have to put it so harshly? Like she's picking a fight with somebody."
At the cash register of the lamb hotpot shop, Zhou Lufei ran into a former coworker: "Old Zhou, your daughter's teaching people how to find a partner online? My girl watches her every day, says she 'sees right through it all.' I think the way she talks is too blunt myself—isn't she afraid of offending people?"
During that stretch, scenes like these were many.
The real "raging storm" came at a family gathering one weekend. In the not-so-spacious living room of an uncle in his seventies, the crystal chandelier shone bright over a table laden with dishes. Three cups of wine in, Zhou Lufei's eldest uncle—who had always praised Yuanyuan's schooling to the skies—tapped the edge of a plate with his chopsticks and said, as if just in passing: "Lufei, ah, Yuanyuan's a big internet celebrity now. What she says... has no small influence. Yao Yuan may not be your own flesh and blood, but she's family all the same, and we Zhous have always been decent, proper folk. For a young woman, wouldn't it be better to rein some of that talk in a bit? She's not even married yet, after all." As the words landed, the sound of chewing around the table grew softer. Zhou Lufei felt his face burn, as if he were being lashed in public by everyone's eyes. He opened his mouth, wanting to say "the child has her own ideas," but in the end could only squeeze out a dry: "Eh, eat, eat."
It wasn't that they'd never seen this "daughter's" "battlefield." In those finely edited short videos, Yao Yuan had immaculate makeup and bright eyes, holding forth eloquently against a perfectly lit backdrop. She talked about "the shackles of the family of origin," about "bride price as the objectification of reproductive value," about "refusing emotional blackmail from family." Every line was like a stone tossed into a lake, sending tens of thousands of ripples through the comment section—some fervent support, some fierce abuse. The praise or curses of those strangers, the old couple could force themselves to ignore. But when those words were chewed over, digested, and spat back out by the relatives—turning into pointed probing at the dinner table, into roundabout "concern" over the phone, even into a kind of silent questioning of how they'd raised their child—then that all-pervading pressure truly left them nowhere to hide.
They had become the strangers who knew "internet-celebrity Yao Yuan" best. In her videos their daughter lived in dazzling glory, brazenly unrestrained—a side of her they'd never seen and could barely understand. And on the phone, Yao Yuan's voice was still crisp, carrying the fatigue of stolen moments between busy stretches: "Mom, I'm fine, don't listen to the nonsense online. Did the massage device I bought you two arrive?"
Late at night, the old couple lay in the dark. The streetlight outside slipped through the gap in the not-quite-drawn curtains, cutting a dim yellow patch onto the wall.
"Her aunt asked again today, whether the things Yuanyuan says... mean she's got a grievance against the family." Zhang Fengxia's voice was muffled in her pillow.
Zhou Lufei didn't answer right away. After a long while, he let out a long sigh, the breath of it especially heavy in the silence.
"Those people online, praising her, cursing her—it's all at a remove. But the people around us, they're really watching us, watching how we 'raised' a daughter like this." He paused, as if chewing over some hard-to-name bitterness. "She does live pretty 'big-woman.' But us... it's like overnight we turned into the old backdrop in her persona, the part that most needs to be 'broken down' and 'examined.'"
"Ah, let her be. We're old, we can't keep up with how young people think, can't get into their world anymore. Those things she says online, she figured them out from taking her own falls. If people listen, if people relate, that means people agree, people need advice like that. As long as it helps young people, that's enough." The moonlight outside fell on the nightstand, where the family photo Yuanyuan had taken when she came home last year stood. In it she grinned wide and careless, and the image merged with that sharp figure from the videos—which, oddly, settled the old couple's hearts quite a bit.
The patch of light on the wall trembled faintly, probably from a car passing outside. This world of familiar faces—woven from relatives and neighbors, where they'd lived most of their lives—was, because of the daughter they were proudest of, growing full of scrutiny, the air gone thick and sticky. They felt a complicated flicker of pride at their daughter's achievements and courage, and at the same time a kind of embarrassment and loneliness at being flung into the center of a whirlpool of public opinion, forced to stand stripped bare.
Yao Yuan was breaking her shackles, charging toward a wider world. And some invisible shards came falling down, quietly poking dense, painful little holes into the once-peaceful world that had been theirs.
Thinking of how those people's cold mockery had turned into today's fawning toadying, a hidden, secret satisfaction flickered through Zhang Fengxia's heart.
By the third year after Yao Yuan started going viral, the direction the air flowed had quietly shifted.
At first it was tentative ripples. A few distant relatives who usually only sent greetings at holidays suddenly turned active in the family group, eagerly tagging Zhang Fengxia: "Sister-in-law, that Yuanyuan really brings glory to our Yao family! I watched her video about workplace negotiation—she talks about it so well! My niece here is just looking for work; could you have Yuanyuan give her a pointer or two? Not for free—we'll treat Yuanyuan to a meal afterward!"
Zhang Fengxia stared at the phone screen, fingers hovering over the keyboard, thinking, how do you have the nerve? She thought again of those flat, decisive lines from her daughter's videos—"The essence of connections is value exchange. When you have no value yourself, so-called 'relationships' are the most fragile thing there is." Hadn't these very relatives just proved that line themselves? Their cold-eyed looking-on, even their cold mockery, before Yao Yuan made it big—and now this scramble to flock to her after she'd gotten results and succeeded. Zhang Fengxia curled her lip in disdain and typed a perfunctory reply, saying Yuanyuan was busy with work, it probably wasn't convenient. The other side quickly sent back an "I understand" sticker, and after that the group went quiet again—a silence that seemed to carry an added layer of something hard to name.
Then came a more direct wave. Yao Yuan's second aunt-in-law showed up in person, carrying a basket of expensive imported fruit. After only a few pleasantries she got to the point: "Fengxia, ah, look, Yuanyuan knows so many people now, people of standing. That deputy section-chief position at your brother-in-law's unit has been empty for nearly half a year. I hear the final pick is just one word from the higher-ups... Could you have Yuanyuan ask those capable friends of hers, put in a word? We don't even ask that it works out—just put in a word, and whether it works or not, we'll be grateful to Yuanyuan!" Second aunt-in-law clutched Zhang Fengxia's hand, so eager it made her palms sweat. Before her eyes flashed her daughter's sharp, bright eyes mocking "the unspoken rules of a society run on personal favors," and she only replied perfunctorily: "She just does self-media—how would she know any officials... Children's affairs, there's really nothing we parents can say."
On her husband Zhou Lufei's side, the son of an old coworker wanted to get into a well-known internet tech giant. Having found out, through a roundabout chain, that Yao Yuan seemed to have had some business dealings with an executive at that company, he came begging to Zhou Lufei. The old coworker's tone on the phone was earnest, even carrying a careful, ingratiating note he'd never had before: "Lufei, we go back so many years—just help me ask, see if he can pass along a résumé? If it doesn't work out, I won't blame the kid at all!" Zhou Lufei was a man who cared about face and valued loyalty. Faced with a decades-old friend's request, the word "no" rolled and rolled in his throat but never came out. He looked at Zhang Fengxia, his eyes full of difficulty.
Before, these people had used the names of "personal favors," "face," and "we're all family" to pressure them into making Yao Yuan less of a maverick. Now these same people used the names of "personal favors," "face," and "we're all family" to pressure them into helping—and after being refused, would even sour-grape behind their backs: see, the daughter makes it big and the parents put on airs, won't even do this little favor. People, ah—drawn to profit, fleeing from harm; human nature is always this real, yet can't stand having that real human nature dissected and laid out.
Sometimes Zhang Fengxia wavered too, thinking that now that her daughter was capable, and they were relatives after all, she should help where she could—while also satisfying a bit of her vanity as a mother, letting them see just how excellent her daughter was. But the thought was quickly dismissed.
On this day, Yao Yuan's eldest aunt-by-marriage came around again. This time the reason was that her son-in-law's e-commerce company was opening for business, and she wanted Yao Yuan to come cut the ribbon. "Yuanyuan doesn't have to prepare anything special—just show her face, say a few auspicious words, that's all! It's publicity for Yuanyuan too!" Eldest aunt-by-marriage smiled brightly, as if offering a magnificent opportunity.
Zhang Fengxia was in the kitchen trimming vegetables. The water ran loudly. Looking at the bright green leaves, she suddenly felt a wave of speechlessness, and a strange calm. She dried her hands, walked into the living room, and didn't take the hot tea eldest aunt-by-marriage held out. She just stood there, a gentle but somewhat distant smile on her face, and said, clear and slow:
"Sister-in-law, all of Yuanyuan's time and schedule now are strictly planned by her team. She has to handle all sorts of business collaborations and content creation—she just can't get away. And that child, she's stubborn by nature, strong on principle; she never takes on this kind of pure-favor appearance. As her own relatives, we ought to be the first to respect the child's rules and her work, don't you think? Otherwise, if I agree on my end and she flatly refuses on hers, then as her mother I'm not just putting the child in a bind—I'm making myself out to be wrong on every side, aren't I?"
Her tone was mild, even carrying a touch of homey helplessness, but the meaning was like a nail wrapped in cotton—soft yet firm. For the first time she hadn't reached for excuses like "the child's busy" or "I can't get hold of her." Instead she set her daughter's principle plainly out there, and drew her own stance clearly as well.
The smile on eldest aunt-by-marriage's face stiffened. She muttered a few more "I understand, I understand"s, sat for less than ten minutes, then made an excuse about having something to do and left.
The moment the door closed, the apartment went quiet. Zhou Lufei came out from the inner room, looked at his wife, and teased: "That was... maybe a little too blunt?"
Zhang Fengxia walked back into the kitchen and turned the tap on again. The sound of water striking the basin wall drowned out her slightly quickened heartbeat. She looked out at the familiar street scene through the window and said softly: "Out there, in front of hundreds of thousands, millions of people she doesn't know, Yuanyuan dares to tell the truth and set rules. If we, as her parents, can't even hold off these few familiar faces, how could we have the nerve to say... we support her?"
The water ran on. She knew the phone would ring again, that personal favors would keep poking their heads in through every kind of channel, that the words to turn them away might still need careful weighing. But she would block these unnecessary burdens from her daughter's path forward. Perhaps this was the most clumsy, and the most solid, "support" that they—this pair of ordinary parents—could offer, in this rapidly changing, brand-new world their daughter had split open with sheer traffic.
