Chapter 22 — The First Fusion of the Genesis Engine and the Pretty Big One's Core Emotional Data

Tuesday, nine-fifty in the morning.

The Western World Technology headquarters tower stood at the heart of Jinshi's high-tech district. Its skin of matte-black smart-glass curtain wall absorbed and transformed the crisp winter sunlight, giving off a cold, restrained, intensely futuristic quality. The building's lines were clean and sharp, free of any superfluous ornament, yet it looked like a great beast lying in wait — silent, but charged with the power to wake at any moment and rewrite some set of rules.

Yao Yuan's black Maybach slid soundlessly into the underground garage. She pushed the door open and stepped out; the cold winter air sharpened her senses. Today she wore a dark-gray cashmere skirt suit, exquisitely tailored. The jacket's shoulder line was straight and crisp, the waist nipped in at just the right curve; the skirt fell to the knee, taut and smooth. In the light, the fine cashmere shimmered with a subdued, pearl-like luster. Beneath it she wore a silk-satin blouse in the same tone but a shade lighter, its collar folded neatly out over the jacket's lapels — the soft sheen of silk playing against the matte texture of cashmere in a delicate layered contrast. Her long hair was drawn back into a low ponytail, not a strand out of place, the tie blending seamlessly with her hair color; only a few deliberately loosened wisps curled softly at her forehead and temples, gentling the lines of her face and lending an effortless touch of life. At her ears, a pair of classic Chanel double-C metal drop earrings traced clean, decisive lines, catching a cold gleam with every movement, a clever accent against the overall palette. On her wrist sat a square-faced watch with a firm red leather strap. The whole look was understated yet rich in skill, simple yet full of hidden detail — a perfect statement of exactly what she needed to project right now: professional authority, an unquestionable calm, and a quiet but absolutely present edge of fashion.

Behind her came two members of her core team. One was Bai Wei, the Pretty Big One's chief product officer and resident psychology expert — thirty-five or thirty-six, with a quiet, intellectual poise. The other was Su Hang, the young technical director, in black-framed glasses, gripping an encrypted tablet, his expression taut.

The elevator took them straight to the top floor. The corridor was empty and silent — the floor a dark-gray micro-cement, the walls pure white and undecorated, lit only by linear strips embedded in the surfaces that gave off an even, soft, cold glow. It looked nothing like the headquarters of a tech company pulling in nearly ten billion a year; more like a giant laboratory or sterile cleanroom that hadn't yet been put to use.

An assistant in a white lab coat, face expressionless, was already waiting at the elevator. After verifying their identities, the assistant led them in silence toward the depths. Their footsteps were swallowed by the empty corridor, the echoes almost inaudible.

At last they stopped before an unmarked metal-gray door. The assistant pressed a palm to the sensor panel beside it, and the heavy door slid open without a sound.

A wave of mixed scents rolled out to meet them — ozone, the heat venting off precision instruments, and something harder to name, like wet earth and metal after rain. Beyond the door was no ordinary conference room but an enormous spherical space, the ceiling at least eight meters high. The walls, the ceiling, even parts of the floor, were assembled from countless fine, nearly invisible hexagonal screens, currently displaying a deep, slowly drifting starscape that gave one a vertiginous sense of floating in the cosmos. At the center floated a glowing ring-shaped console. Zhao Yiming stood at it with his back to the door, gazing at a cascade of blue data streaming through the empty air before him. Today he wore a simple black turtleneck and dark trousers; against the vast backdrop of data his frame looked lean and intent.

Hearing the footsteps, he didn't turn. He merely raised his right hand in a small gesture. The flowing data instantly froze, contracted, and reorganized itself into a clean three-dimensional architecture diagram suspended above the console.

"Right on time." Zhao Yiming finally turned. His gaze rested on Yao Yuan for a moment, taking in her crisp attire, then moved to Bai Wei and Su Hang behind her. "And these two are?"

"Bai Wei, my chief product officer, a doctor of psychology, in charge of the Pretty Big One's content system and counseling framework. Su Hang, technical director, responsible for the data platform and algorithm deployment." Yao Yuan made the introductions briefly, her eyes already drawn to this strange "conference room." "Boss Zhao, your setup here is — quite something."

"This is the Mirror Room, one of the immersive development and testing environments for the Genesis Engine." Zhao Yiming walked back to the console and gestured for them to come closer. "Here, we can interact directly with the model's 'consciousness layer' at a high dimension — orders of magnitude more efficient than a conventional interface. The data interfaces and preliminary framework you've brought can be imported straight in."

Su Hang drew a deep breath, stepped forward, and connected the tablet to a port on the console with a dedicated cable. Bai Wei, meanwhile, took a heavy encrypted hard drive from her briefcase.

"All data has undergone deep cleaning and multiple rounds of anonymization, meeting the strictest privacy and security standards. The original recordings have been converted into feature vectors, and the text-interaction data has been annotated across multiple dimensions. The tagging system is based on our proprietary 'Relationship Dynamics' and 'Emotional Energy Map' models. This is the framework white paper." Bai Wei's voice was calm and professional as she handed over a bound document.

Zhao Yiming took it but didn't open it right away. Instead he gave the assistant an order: "Initialize the security sandbox. Isolation level: Black Moon."

The assistant tapped rapidly on the console. The surrounding starscape dimmed, replaced by a soft, semi-transparent silver film of light that rose from all sides and completely enclosed the central area where they stood. Across its surface, countless tiny silver points of light, like runes, flowed and flickered in some hidden pattern.

"Now we're entirely cut off from the outside. Under the Black Moon protocol, everything that happens in here — data exchanges, conversation, even shifts in thought — will not be recorded or perceived by any external device." Zhao Yiming explained, then looked at Su Hang. "Begin the import. Start with the smallest sample set."

The data began to flow in. The 3D architecture diagram above the console immediately changed: the pale-gold points and lines representing the Pretty Big One's data system began to embed themselves into the original azure structure that formed the Genesis Engine's main trunk. At first it was halting — the gold points probing and colliding at the edges of the blue tracery — but soon, under Zhao Yiming's deft adjustments and the "lubrication" of a few key algorithms, the gold began to merge slowly but surely into the blue, forming a strange, interwoven blue-and-gold blend, flowing as one.

"Compatibility's better than expected." Zhao Yiming watched the fusing data streams, his fingers sliding and tapping rapidly in the empty air, adjusting parameters. "Your annotation system has its own ingenuity — especially these two dimensions, 'power dynamics' and 'latent needs.' They make up for the data sparsity in our model's modeling of social-relationship gamesmanship. But —"

He frowned slightly. "The underlying logic of your emotional-value assessment module is built on an assumed 'rational optimal solution.' That may be efficient in most cases, but human emotional decision-making — especially under extreme stress or intense attachment — is often irrational, even self-contradictory. Your model flags that portion of the data as 'noise' or 'to be corrected,' and that risks losing the most subtle, and most essential, part of understanding human feeling."

Bai Wei responded at once: "Boss Zhao is right. That's a current limitation of our framework, and one of the key reasons we're seeking this partnership. We've come to recognize the boundaries of a purely rational framework. We're hoping to use the Genesis Engine's stronger contextual understanding, empathy simulation, and creative reasoning to process that 'noise' — to extract deeper patterns from it, and even to feed back and refine and enrich our own assessment logic."

Yao Yuan had been watching the fusion of the data and the exchange between the two in silence. Now she spoke. "Boss Zhao, this is exactly why I say we don't just need a smarter chatbot. What we need is a partner that can handle 'contradiction,' understand 'the irrational,' and even generate 'creative strategies' that go beyond conventional thinking within emotional dilemmas that look unsolvable. That asks an AI to have not only logic but — in some sense — 'intuition' or 'inspiration.'"

Zhao Yiming glanced at Yao Yuan, a very faint light, almost like appreciation, crossing his eyes. "Well put. In computational terms, 'intuition' and 'inspiration' can be understood as the 'emergent' capabilities a model produces on the foundation of massive-scale training and complex pattern recognition. Let's give it a try."

He swept both hands across the console. The blue-gold blended data stream rapidly collapsed and reorganized, condensing before them into an extremely intricate, constantly shifting three-dimensional network diagram. The network was built of countless nodes and connecting lines: the nodes were points of light in different colors and sizes, representing different emotional states, personality traits, and relationship roles, while the lines represented the dynamic associations, influences, and flows of energy between them. This was the dynamic simulation map of "modern urban intimate relationships" generated by the preliminary fusion of the Pretty Big One's core framework with the Genesis Engine.

"This is the current fused model's overall cognitive map of the ten thousand high-conflict case samples you provided." Zhao Yiming's voice carried a researcher's particular excitement. "We can select a single node and explore it in depth. Su Hang — pull a high-difficulty, high-ambiguity live consultation case at random from your test set, anonymize it, and import it."

Su Hang worked quickly. Soon an encrypted case stream was loaded. In the 3D network diagram, a pale-purple node representing the "consulting user" lit up, and the nodes connected to it — representing the "partner," "family of origin," "work pressure," "self-perception," and more — flickered bright and dim in turn, data flowing like blood through the tangle of lines.

"Case background: female user, thirty-two, high income, highly educated, who has recently discovered long-term covert emotional manipulation and financial deception by her partner. But the partner's image in their social circle is impeccable, the user still feels a strong emotional attachment to the partner, and there are complex shared financial interests binding them together. The user's request: to find the 'optimal' solution while protecting her own interests — but inside she's full of contradiction, self-doubt, and emotional rupture." Bai Wei recited the key points of the case quickly.

"A classic case of multi-objective conflict and cognitive dissonance." Zhao Yiming murmured, his fingers flicking in the air to magnify the structure around that pale-purple node. "Engage the 'high-dimensional empathy inference module.' Try to generate strategy paths."

The console gave off a low hum. The 3D network diagram began to spin and shift at high speed; countless possible strategy paths radiated outward from the central node as beams of light, extending and interweaving, then colliding, annihilating, and recombining at certain nodes. This was no simple decision tree but the inference of an extraordinarily complex dynamic system — one that simulated emotion, reason, social relationships, interest calculations, and even random perturbations (representing uncertainty).

Yao Yuan watched the dazzling dance of light with bated breath. She could follow part of it — the paths labeled "gather evidence," "legal consultation," "financial separation" were clear and direct. But more of the paths were blurred, roundabout, even seemingly self-contradictory. Some, after extending for a while, would loop back, curve away, and link to nodes you'd never have expected — things like "provoke the partner's guilt but control the degree," "maintain appearances for now to win outside allies," "use social pressure to apply reverse leverage," and so on.

"The model is trying to understand and simulate the user's true, possibly unspoken, multiple objectives: she wants to protect her interests but also longs for some form of relationship 'correction' or her partner's 'repentance'; she wants to punish him yet fears loneliness and the collapse of their social image; she wants to act decisively yet is held back by lingering feeling." Zhao Yiming explained, his eyes tracking the shifting paths. "It's exploring the combined 'emotion-interest' utility value of every possible combination. Interesting."

Suddenly, a faint, almost transparent pale-gold path lit up at the edge of the intricate network. It led toward none of the usual "resolution" nodes but wound instead toward a dark node that seemed to have nothing to do with the core conflict — one labeled "user's unfulfilled childhood wishes / latent self." The moment it appeared, the energy flow of the entire network seemed to bend subtly.

"What's that?" Yao Yuan asked instinctively.

Zhao Yiming's gaze sharpened. He moved quickly to highlight and magnify the pale-gold path. At its end lay not a concrete strategic recommendation but a series of abstract symbols and constantly shifting imagery, seeming to point toward a deeper possibility — about reshaping the self and awakening an inner strength.

"The model has — emerged a direction of advice that lies outside the framework we set." Zhao Yiming's voice carried a trace of uncertain wonder. "It didn't directly resolve the surface conflict. Instead it tried to guide the user to shift her attention and energy, in part, away from the dimension of 'how do I change the other person / handle the relationship' and toward the more fundamental dimension of 'how do I rebuild a stronger, more complete sense of self.' It inferred that when the user's inner core undergoes a kind of qualitative change, the path to resolving the external predicament will surface on its own — and may even 'dissolve' in unexpected ways."

Bai Wei drew a sharp breath. "This — this is a bit like the approach in deep psychotherapy to working with 'repetition compulsion' and 'internal object relations.' But our framework never explicitly coded that logic! How did it —"

At that very moment, the anomaly struck.

The pale-gold path — the very embodiment of the model's "emergent" capability — reached a critical point and suddenly blazed with light, as if it had tripped some hidden switch. The entire Mirror Room shuddered violently. It was not a physical tremor but a distortion of the space itself, every screen, every play of light and shadow rippling eerily like the surface of water.

Inside the spherical space, the hexagonal screens that had been displaying stars and data all turned in an instant into flickering, jittering static, swarming with garbled code. Then the static spun and collapsed wildly, as if drawn by an invisible black hole, rushing to condense and implode toward the center of the space — toward the end of that glowing pale-gold path.

"Warning! Unknown data disturbance! High-dimensional link unstable! Logic-layer entropy surging!" On the console, glaring red warning text scrolled madly alongside a piercing alarm, setting the heart pounding.

Zhao Yiming's face changed in an instant, the eyes behind his lenses sharp as knives. His hands flew across the console fast enough to leave afterimages, commands falling like a downpour. "Su Hang! Force-disconnect the external high-dimensional data feed, now! Physical cutoff! Bai Wei — initiate the 'Bulwark' emergency protocol, triple-lock the core emotional-logic unit! Boss Yao, please step back at once, get to the safe zone!"

His voice stayed calm, but the speed of his speech betrayed how grave the situation was. And yet the data's collapse and backlash came too fast, too uncannily. A low hum rose — *bzzz* — seeming to pierce straight through the eardrums, yet it came from no speaker; it acted directly on the nerve endings. The orb of light, where the garbled code and pale-gold glow had condensed to a peak, did not explode. Instead, like a punctured bubble, it silently collapsed and dimmed, finally fading to an empty pitch-black that scattered across a portion of the screens. The pale-gold path broke off entirely and vanished.

The first deliberate attempt at compatibility and deep activation was declared a failure.

The lights in the Mirror Room returned to normal, but the air carried the faint scorched smell of overloaded data (perhaps only in the mind) and a brief dead silence. Zhao Yiming pressed his lips tight, his gaze locked on the disordered waveforms and error logs lingering on the main screen. Behind him his technical team had already sprung into motion, faces grim as they began checking the various raw-data interfaces and tracing the possible damage from that abnormal high-frequency surge — low-level logic corruption, physical-circuit overload, and the like. The clatter of keyboards and the low, rapid exchange of commands rose again, the atmosphere tense.

Yao Yuan had retreated cleanly to the safe zone by the wall the moment Zhao Yiming spoke. There was little panic on her face now — more a focused observation and thought. Her team members had gathered around her with practiced discipline, not disturbing the others, simply recording every anomaly they had observed.

"My apologies, Boss Yao." Zhao Yiming drew a deep breath, turning away from the console for the moment and rubbing his brow as he spoke to her. "We hit an unexpected data turbulence — the underlying compatibility is even thornier than we anticipated. We need to troubleshoot and repair it urgently. I'm afraid we'll have to suspend the demonstration for now."

Yao Yuan nodded to show she fully understood, her tone even. "Safety first; data integrity takes priority. Boss Zhao, you handle it — we can wait."

"Thank you for understanding. Xiao Chen —" Zhao Yiming signaled to the assistant beside him. "Take Boss Yao and her team to Conference Room Two to rest, and provide any support they need."

"Of course, Boss Zhao. This way, everyone, please."

Yao Yuan and her group were politely shown to another spare conference room to wait. Over the next hour, hurried footsteps and faint technical discussion drifted in from the corridor outside, but on the whole things stayed orderly. Yao Yuan used the time to review everything they'd just seen with her team in low voices, noting down the key questions and observations.

About an hour later, Zhao Yiming walked into Conference Room Two, looking somewhat tired but with eyes still clear. He gave a brief account of the situation: the preliminary judgment was that some segment of "non-standard emotional mapping" in the external high-dimensional data feed had, in attempting to couple deeply with the Pretty Big One's core model, triggered a chain reaction of underlying logic conflicts — something like "immune rejection," but far more violent at the data level. The physical circuits and base architecture had checked out fine, but some of the core model's emotional weighting parameters needed recalibration and reinforcement, and a stricter "emotional firewall" protocol had to be established.

"Forgive the spectacle," Zhao Yiming said, tugging up the corner of his mouth in a smile that didn't reach his eyes. "On the frontier, accidents are the norm. But we've captured crucial failure data — and that's worth more than a hundred smooth demonstrations."

Yao Yuan agreed. "Indeed. Failure defines the boundary. So perhaps we set the deep demonstration aside for now and, working from the current stable version, have a more practical conversation about the Pretty Big One Emotional AI model — the specific investment share in our plan, a phased implementation and rollout path, a data-sharing and privacy-compliance framework, and a detailed scheme for adapting it to commercial application scenarios?"

Zhao Yiming readily agreed. "That's exactly what I had in mind."

The meeting that followed moved at a tight, practical pace. Both teams set aside the earlier incident and focused on concrete terms of cooperation, technical metrics, timelines, resource commitments, and contingency plans. Yao Yuan was thoroughly prepared, with a deep grasp of both business and technology; her questions cut to the heart of the matter, and in negotiation she held firm on core interests while staying flexible. Zhao Yiming, for his part, showed a commercial acuity beyond his role as a technical leader — yielding nothing on the key points, yet willing to seek the optimal solution wherever there was room to maneuver.

By the time the meeting ended, it was past noon. Zhao Yiming offered to host Yao Yuan's team for a simple lunch in the company cafeteria. The space was modern and bright, the food healthy and abundant, the mood far more relaxed than in the conference room.

Over the meal, the conversation drifted by chance to the company's name. Yao Yuan dabbed at the corner of her mouth with a napkin and smiled. "Boss Zhao, I've had a small curiosity all along. Your company is named 'Western World' — was the inspiration the American series of the same name? What made you choose that name back then? It's memorable, but also — thought-provoking."

Zhao Yiming set down his spoon, a sharp yet keenly interested light crossing his eyes; he seemed glad to talk about it. "That's right, the inspiration did come from that show. But not out of worship or full agreement with the world it depicts. Naming it 'Western World' is more a warning and a form of self-examination. One of that show's central themes is what happens when artificial beings grow complex enough to possess memory, emotion, even the experience of pain — how their relationship with their 'creators' will evolve. Tools? Partners? Or potential 'others,' even threats?"

He paused, then went on. "When we study AI — especially when we explore affective computing and higher cognition — we can't dodge these ultimate ethical questions. The name 'Western World' is a constant reminder of where the boundaries of technology lie, and how heavy the ethical responsibility of creation is. We're not trying to create an 'awakened mainframe' that turns on humanity. We're trying to explore the possibility of AI assisting, augmenting, even understanding human emotion — but only on the premise that we fully understand it and can set absolute safety boundaries. It's like walking on thin ice, but it's worth exploring."

Yao Yuan nodded thoughtfully and picked up the thread. "I watched that show. What stayed with me most was how the 'hosts' — through countless loops, with their memories wiped and yet leaving fragments behind — gradually developed a sense of 'unease,' and then began to ask after the 'truth.' That actually touches on something core: if we give an AI the capacity for memory and emotional simulation — even just simulation — could the accumulation of that 'experience' produce, deep in the algorithm, some 'trace' or 'tendency' that can't simply be reset to zero? Just like a moment ago — when your model produced that violent rejection while trying to take on high-dimensional data, was that, in some sense, also a kind of brush against the boundary of 'unease,' a reaction to it?"

Zhao Yiming looked at Yao Yuan with still more appreciation in his eyes. "A very incisive read, Boss Yao. That's precisely one of the core challenges of our research. The 'trace' theory is a frontier debate in the field right now. Part of our work is the attempt to define, measure, and control that very 'trace.' Today's accident, setback though it was, also confirmed the existence of certain model boundaries. We need a more precise 'scalpel' and 'safety valve.'" He smiled, with the frankness and ambition particular to a technical mind. "Fear comes from the unknown. And we are trying to turn the unknown into the known and the controllable. That may be harder than the story on the screen — but also more interesting, don't you think?"

The lunch drew to a close amid this faintly philosophical and technical exchange. Yao Yuan glanced at the time and politely took her leave. Zhao Yiming saw her to the elevator himself.

"Today's exchange was extremely valuable — both the successful parts and the — accidental ones." Yao Yuan shook hands with Zhao Yiming in farewell. "I look forward to the detailed plan and test data to come."

"And I look forward to a deeper partnership with your side," Zhao Yiming said, gripping her hand firmly. "I made a spectacle of myself today, Boss Yao. Next time, we'll be better prepared."

The elevator doors slid slowly shut, sealing off the futuristic, faintly cold space of "Western World." Yao Yuan leaned against the inner wall of the car and let out a soft breath. In her mind, the heart-stopping scene of the morning's data collapse interwove with the lunchtime conversation about AI awakening and ethics, giving her a more three-dimensional — and more challenging — picture of Zhao Yiming and the field he pursued.

And in her handbag, the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror from another dimension seemed, on this day saturated with cutting-edge technology and speculation about the future, all the more mysterious and out of place. This afternoon, she had to arrange to meet Shuai Hongqiang. The mirror he had given her, the "data turbulence" she'd witnessed today, the "trace" and "boundary" Zhao Yiming spoke of — could there be some unimaginable connection among them all?

The elevator descended, the numbers ticking by, carrying her back to the solid ground of reality. But her thoughts seemed to hover in some more complex dimension — a place where the ancient bronze mirror and the code of the future were quietly entering into some unknown resonance.
