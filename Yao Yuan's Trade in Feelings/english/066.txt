Flame-Souls
Her phone buzzed once against the desktop, a dull droning sound. She walked back, picked it up: her assistant had sent over today's livestream data summary, along with a few business-partnership invitations flagged for her to review first. She skimmed them quickly and efficiently, tapping out a crisp reply on the cold screen with her sky-blue fingertips: "Read it. Numbers look good. Lay out the pros and cons of each partnership and we'll go through them together at the ten o'clock standup tomorrow."
Only after she'd handled these necessary loose ends did she finally step out of that high-energy, responsibility-laden persona of "Teacher Yuanyuan" and return to being plain Yao Yuan.
She dealt with more company emails and a few urgent matters in the study, and the time slipped quietly away. As evening drew near, she picked up the long cream silk shirt-jacket she'd tossed over the back of the sofa, ready to keep her appointment with Jin Lan.
She stepped into her private elevator. The metal doors slid smoothly shut, and the polished, mirror-bright walls of the car threw her reflection back at her with perfect clarity — still immaculate, though a day's fatigue lingered in her eyes that she couldn't quite shake. Only the clashing peach-pink and sky-blue of her outfit declared her unbowed brilliance and vitality. Looking at herself in that mirror, she suddenly remembered something she'd said during the livestream to a girl named Xiaoya: "The more concrete your goal, the more solid your life."
What was her own next "concrete goal"? Beyond continuing to hunt for that faint, elusive thread leading to the mirror-master's descendants, beyond steadily advancing the deep technical integration and commercial partnership with Zhao Yiming's Genesis Engine, beyond the constant iteration and market expansion of the Pretty Big One Emotional AI model... maybe she also owed herself a little time — time to make good on a promise she'd made long ago and kept shelving for one thing or another: the promise of that clear, starry sky over the Gobi.
The elevator reached the silent underground parking garage. Cool air rushed up to meet her, faintly scented with concrete.
She walked toward her reserved space, her steps steady, her heels striking the floor in crisp echoes that rang through the empty garage. The livestream was over, but the night and the life that belonged to Yao Yuan — far more complicated things — were perhaps only just beginning. The city's neon hadn't fully lit yet, but the dusk light already spilled in through the garage exit, dragging a long, silent, resolute shadow out behind her.
On the drive to the Provincial Grand Theater, the city's lamps were just coming on. In Jinshi's summer evening, the day's dry heat was slowly fading, and a lively, languid mood hung in the air. The lights of the restaurants lining both sides of the street were warm, the crowds thick as woven cloth, full of the vivid, fragrant bustle of ordinary life.
Six in the evening, at the main entrance of the Provincial Grand Theater.
Jin Lan was already there, in a sharply tailored navy wide-leg trouser suit of crisp fabric that set off her upright bearing. Her long hair was pulled back into a clean bun, and she carried the steady, capable air unique to a queen of the restaurant world. The moment she saw Yao Yuan, her eyes lit up. She looked her up and down with frank admiration, then shook her head and teased, "Boss Yao, are you here to watch the show, or to crash it? Afraid the dancers up there won't dazzle enough, so you've come to step on stage and outshine them yourself?" Her eyes held nothing but pure, captivated admiration for her friend's presence.
Yao Yuan smiled, walked over, slipped her arm naturally through Jin Lan's, and said lightly, "Can't go embarrassing our Boss Jin and dragging down the average of your social circle, can I?"
The two of them had dinner at "Lanting," a place they'd booked in advance near the theater. The restaurant was elegant and quiet, the dishes refined. Over the meal, Jin Lan was indeed in high spirits. She pulled out her phone, opened the photos and videos saved in her album, and leaned them toward Yao Yuan.
"Here — this troupe, they've been blowing up lately. Look at this lead, here," she said, her fingertip tapping a frozen stage shot on the screen: a figure caught mid-leap in the air, muscle lines carved sharp as knife-work under the harsh light. "Word is he's the soul of the troupe — does all his own choreography, full of ideas. Just look at that body, that control... unreal."
Yao Yuan set down her chopsticks and followed where Jin Lan pointed. The stage lighting in the photos and short clips was strikingly designed, the dancer's figure moving through the frame as lithe as a leopard, full of raw power and a beauty that was precisely calculated. She nodded objectively and gave her professional verdict: "Mm, the foundation looks very solid, the physical expressiveness is strong. He's good."
"I hear their troupe name's interesting too," Jin Lan said, swiping the screen to a section with text. "It's called 'Yanpo' — the 'yan' from flame, the 'po' from soul. The leader-slash-principal dancer's surname is, I think... Zhuge? A compound surname, pretty rare." She read out the name with idle, conversational curiosity.
Zhuge?
Yao Yuan's hand, just reaching for her water glass, paused almost imperceptibly. Her fingertips hung in the air for half a second before they closed steadily around the cup. A name, distant yet unusually vivid, swept across her mind — carrying the damp of a southern plum-rain season, the smell of sweat in a rehearsal studio, and a certain memory both scalding and painful — and left a clear, cool trail behind it.
Zhuge Jinye.
It couldn't be such a coincidence. She denied it instinctively, in her heart. That man who had once crashed into her life like a wildfire out of control — who had loved her fiercely, madly, holding nothing back — and from whom she had finally, brutally, parted because their visions for the trajectory of a life ran in opposite directions. He had talent — could even be called brilliant — but he was proud and sharp-edged, all hard angles. The year they split, his eyes rimmed red, he had said, every word landing like a stone, that one day he would break free of his own road, that he would make her — make the whole world — see, that he would build a Broadway of China. But in the entertainment world, and on the narrow path of pure dance especially, truly breaking through is no easy thing.
"Yanpo"... she repeated the name silently in her heart. She knew it. Once, scrolling her phone late at night, the big-data feed had even pushed a highlight clip of this very troupe's performance in front of her. The man leading the dance in that video — even without a clear view of his whole face, that familiar build, that way of channeling explosive force, and that reckless, all-or-nothing determination in his dancing, as if he meant to burn everything down, himself included — she had recognized him in an instant: that was Zhuge Jinye. She knew he had struggled and floundered in Hangzhou all these years, and that in the end he really seemed to have made a name for himself through that do-or-die ruthlessness and ceaselessly honed craft, his troupe "Yanpo," built around him, gradually heating up, its tour tickets said to be nearly impossible to get. But she had never gone looking, never followed up on the details — like closing a book filled with intense plot and carefully filing it away into some seldom-touched corner of her memory shelf, leaving it to gather dust. It was only a chapter of the past she had long since coolly archived and sealed.
Jin Lan caught, with her sharp instincts, that almost undetectable pause in Yao Yuan, and the faint ripple that flickered through her eyes. She set down her phone, looked at her friend, and a natural note of probing crept into her voice: "What is it, Yuanyuan? From your tone... you both came up as professional dancers. Don't tell me you know him?" She genuinely had no idea about the history between Yao Yuan and Zhuge Jinye. That was a story from the years when Yao Yuan had left Jinshi to make her career in Hangzhou — and Jin Lan's roots, her business, and the vast majority of her social network were all deeply planted in Jinshi. She knew nothing of the specific romantic entanglements of Yao Yuan's southern years.
Yao Yuan had already recovered her composure. She lifted her water glass, took a small sip, her expression its usual calm — even tinged with the indifference of someone mentioning some trivial old matter. "Many years ago, back in Hangzhou, we crossed paths a few times. He's an... old acquaintance, I suppose." She said it lightly, in the tone of someone speaking of a peer she'd met by chance and never grown close to. "I didn't expect he'd actually stick it out and make something of himself. And I certainly didn't expect that the show you so mysteriously dragged me here to see would be his troupe's."
She tucked that thread of stirred, complicated, unfamiliar emotion neatly back down into her heart, gave Jin Lan an easy smile, and tapped the refined dishes on the table with her chopsticks: "Eat up, the food's getting cold. Once we're done we can go see just how 'unreal' our 'old acquaintance' really dances."
Seven in the evening, at the main entrance of the Provincial Grand Theater.
The two of them chatted and laughed as they had their tickets checked and went in. Their seats were dead center in the fifth row — a superb vantage point, close enough to read the dancers' subtlest expressions and muscle control, yet high enough to take in the whole stage's composition and movement. The theater, with a capacity of nearly two thousand, was almost full. The audience was sleekly dressed, mostly fashionable young people, lovers of the arts, and elegant, poised mature women. The air was thick with the lingering notes of various high-end perfumes, the scent of cosmetics, and a suppressed, shared excitement and anticipation.
The lights dimmed gradually, swallowing the last traces of noise like a rising tide. The whole house fell silent, silent enough to hear a pin drop.
The music came crashing in — a magnificent overture mixing ancient Eastern drumbeats, heavy and deep, with the sharp edge of modern electronic effects — seizing every eardrum and heart in an instant. The great curtain drew slowly open. A dim blue overhead light spilled down like moonlight, and on the stage stood several figures, lithe as leopards, arranged in frozen poses full of sculptural weight and the beauty of power — like ancient totems, or like future war machines, holding the force of an explosion within their stillness.
Almost the instant the music began, Yao Yuan sat up a little straighter without meaning to and adjusted her breathing. As a professional trained at the Beijing Dance Academy — a top dance institution — through years of rigorous formal study, even after so many years away from the spotlight, that extreme sensitivity to the language of the body, that exacting eye for the texture of movement, and that instinctive grasp of staging and spatial arrangement, all of it carved into her bones, had never faded. If anything, the sediment of the years had only deepened it. Her gaze turned, in an instant, focused and sharp — like the most demanding examiner, and like a silent observer reunited after a long parting.
The lead dancer broke from the group, slicing apart the stage's stillness in an instant with a string of floor-glides that fused extreme control, explosiveness, and fluidity, linked into jaw-dropping grand jetés, seizing every gaze in the house — it was Zhuge Jinye.
Even across the distance, even with the stage lighting shifting and blurring for effect, Yao Yuan could still clearly make out those distinctive habits of force etched into his body's memory, the path of his shifting axis, and that pursuit of perfect line that ran in his blood. Compared to the youth in her memory — sweating buckets in a Hangzhou rehearsal studio, his brow always knotted with a mix of talent, gloom, and stubborn refusal to lose — the man on stage now had a build more powerful and robust, every muscle straining out clean, forceful lines beneath his tight costume, full of the textured strength that settles only after years of brutal training, countless injuries and healings, and the thousand temperings of the stage. His technique had visibly advanced by leaps and bounds — a qualitative leap, even. His flexibility was astonishing, his jumps light yet full of hang time; the stability, speed, and number of his turns were no longer those of the raw beginner he'd once been, but had reached the top tier of domestic professional dancers, even carrying the marks of internationally minded training.
But what made Yao Yuan hold her breath even more was the intense emotion and narrative force his dancing held, almost spilling over. Every extension seemed to reach for the edge of the sky; every moment of hang time carried a struggling yearning; every halt under a heavy beat struck like a knock against the soul. He was no longer merely executing movements — he was burning with his body, pouring out endless longing, scorching ambition, ripping pain, and the remaking of rebirth. He had woven together modern dance's deep exploration of the body's limits and emotional expression, the raw rhythm and power-aesthetics of street dance, and even, cleverly, the cadence and shape of certain operatic postures — fused seamlessly into a style intensely his own, full of dramatic tension and visual impact. This was no simple piling-up of technique or showing-off of skill. This was expression with a soul, with bone, with flesh and blood.
The other members of the troupe were equally well trained, solidly grounded, their coordination seamless, showing a very high level of professionalism. But Zhuge Jinye was, without question, the absolute core, the soul, the sun. He was like a walking, violent flame, drawing in all the energy and every gaze on the stage, writing across its blank canvas, with sweat and his own body, a savage, magnificent, pain-soaked poem.
Yao Yuan watched in silence, and in her heart gave her objective, cool verdict: he'd done it. And done it far beyond what she'd predicted back then. Not only had he won commercial success on tour, tickets impossible to get — he had also, artistically, found an expressive language all his own, one of tremendous force and resonance, and planted his feet firmly. Back then she'd thought him too obsessive, too heedless of reality, living in a castle in the air. Now it seemed it was perhaps precisely that near-self-destructive obsession and that all-or-nothing gamble that had carried him through the thorns, truly to here — that had built the castle in the air into a solid foundation and a soaring tower.
The performance entered its climax, a male group dance titled "Tempering." Zhuge Jinye's lead section reached a difficulty that beggared belief. Continuous, high-speed fouetté turns flowing into air turns, the landing controlled with precision into a full split, immediately followed by a fierce front tuck-roll into a high-difficulty "exploring the sea" pose... The transitions from one movement to the next were like flowing water and drifting cloud, the shifts between power and suppleness flawless, his control over the core muscles reaching an inhuman level. The knowing members of the audience couldn't help letting out low, stifled gasps. The fingertips Yao Yuan had resting on the armrest tapped lightly, almost imperceptibly — the involuntary, unconscious response of an insider witnessing something exquisite, technique at its absolute peak. His progress and maturity were genuinely striking, enough even to stir respect.
The final dance was Zhuge Jinye's solo, "Embers." The music turned ethereal, plaintive, and vast, like wind over open wilderness. He stripped off the decorative outer robe and wore only the simplest black dance trousers; his bare upper body gleamed with crystalline sweat, like oil-rubbed bronze under the lights, his muscles heaving and trembling with his heavy breathing and his absolute physical control, full of an almost tragic, primal, sacred masculine beauty. He rolled across the stage, curled in on himself, struggled to rise, then seemed crushed down again by an invisible weight, kneeling and prostrate once more... like a soul wandering in the ashes of fate and time, refusing to die, refusing to admit defeat. At last, in a posture of utter exhaustion, he sank to one knee, lifted his head, gazed up at the single beam of light overhead, his throat rolling violently up and down, his chest heaving like a bellows, and in his eyes there seemed to flash a broken glimmer of water — a gaze blank, fragile, scarred all over, yet in its deepest depths still stubbornly flickering a faint, unwilling-to-die spark of starlight.
The music, at its most extreme, cut off all at once.
The lights snapped dark.
The whole house plunged into an absolute, vacuum-like dead silence. As if even breath had been stolen away.
Then came the deafening applause and cheers, nearly enough to lift the roof off the theater, erupting like a tsunami, going on and on without end. Many in the audience leapt to their feet in their excitement, clapping madly, shouting.
The lights came up again, slowly, much softer now. The whole troupe took the stage for their curtain call. Zhuge Jinye stood at the very front, breathing lightly, a few strands of sweat-damp hair clinging to his sharply angled brow. He led the dancers in deep bows toward the audience in every direction, and as he straightened, his face wore the dazzling, sincere smile of a king of the stage — clearly weary, clearly satisfied. His gaze swept across the tide of warm faces below, and in a voice gone a little hoarse but full of infectious feeling, he spoke his thanks.
And then his gaze, as if yanked suddenly by some invisible thread of fate, fell — without warning, with precision, and held there, locked — on dead center of the fifth row, on the figure who, even in the dim of the audience, was impossible to miss, impossible to ignore, for the dazzling peach-pink and the radiant presence she carried.
Yao Yuan.
In the instant their eyes met, time seemed stretched, warped, frozen without limit.
That professional, brilliant smile on Zhuge Jinye's face froze in an instant, like a perfect mask shattered. His pupils contracted sharply, shrinking to a point, and within them roiled an unbelievable storm of towering waves. His fingers, gripping the bouquet, tightened unconsciously, the knuckles paling white with the force. Every sound on the stage — his companions' words of thanks, the audience's cheers, even his own heartbeat — seemed in that moment to recede at high speed, going faint, dissolving into a blurred background noise. His entire world shrank abruptly to nothing but a single figure in the audience: the woman in the peach-pink dress, a cream silk jacket over her shoulders, her makeup flawless beyond reproach, gazing back at him with a pair of tranquil eyes — calmly, even with a trace of professional appraisal.
How many years? Through those days and nights in Hangzhou with no end in sight, sweat soaking every inch of the floor; in the moment of winning an international competition with no one to share the highest joy; behind the successful tour, the flowers and applause he received, and the sense that some part of him still felt hollow... He had, on countless weary or exhilarated nights, imagined all sorts of reunion scenarios. When he'd achieved fame and stood on the highest podium; when he'd returned home in glory, surrounded by flashing cameras; in any moment when she might be down on her luck, or perhaps ordinary. He'd meant to use his own real, solid success to make her see, to make her regret, to make her know what kind of burning energy — destruction and creation marching side by side — she had underestimated back then.
But when this moment truly arrived, in this utterly unprepared way, across a mere dozen-odd meters and a surging sea of people, looking at those tranquil, distant eyes of hers, carrying their clear note of appraisal and even a trace of pure appreciation (the way one appreciates a fine work of art) — all the satisfaction, the mockery, the show of force and release he had rehearsed countless times burst like colored soap bubbles pricked by the lightest touch of a needle, vanishing without a trace in an instant. In their place surged a more violent, more complex, more inexpressible torrent, brutally scouring over the heart's levee he had thought so solid — was it embers not yet fully extinguished, suddenly fanned up by a wild wind? Was it the sharp pain and unwillingness that even the long years had failed to wear smooth? It was enormous pride, the daze of time and space out of joint, the disbelief of sheer coincidence — and a thread of hidden, intense throbbing that even he found deeply repugnant and alarming in himself.
Yao Yuan, too, clearly received the lock of his gaze — that gaze scalding, complex, as if bearing a thousand pounds, meaning to pierce straight through her. She did not flinch, did not panic; even the calm, unruffled expression on her face barely changed. She simply met that gaze, which seemed to hold ten thousand unspoken words, and — very slightly, but in a way she made sure he could clearly see — nodded.
It was not the signal of an old lover at a reunion, not a friend's greeting after a long parting. It was a professional acknowledgment, from a peer and an elder (at least a senior of the same training lineage). It was an objective verdict and a salute to his entire performance tonight, his artistic achievement, his astonishing transformation. Polite. Clear. Cleanly bounded.
Zhuge Jinye's heart felt, at that cool little nod, fiercely clenched, then abruptly released, leaving a sharp ache and a hollowness behind. She had seen. She had acknowledged his dancing, his art, the heights he'd reached. But that was all. That was it. In her eyes, he no longer had anything to do with romance — he was simply a respectable, excellent dancer.
The next second, a companion beside him touched his arm lightly, without a trace, a low reminder. He jolted awake from that dazed trance, and his formidable professionalism snapped back into place in an instant. He lifted the bouquet in his hand once more, offered the audience an even more dazzling, almost flawless smile, his voice carrying through the microphone to every corner of the theater — only, if you listened carefully, you could catch a faint, hard-to-detect hoarseness, the strain of something forcibly suppressed: "Thank you, Jinshi! You're incredible! I love you all!"
His gaze finally moved away from Yao Yuan completely, turning toward the broader, more fervent reaches of the audience, his smile radiant, as if that brief, illusion-like meeting of eyes and that moment of slipped composure had never happened at all.
The performance let out, the crowd thick as woven cloth surging slowly toward the exits.
Jin Lan and Yao Yuan moved out with the flow, unhurried. Jin Lan glanced sideways, studied her friend's calm, unruffled profile, opened her mouth and then thought better of it; a flicker of understanding and concern crossed her eyes. But in the end she asked nothing. She only reached out and patted Yao Yuan's arm gently, passing along wordless support. She was an extremely intelligent woman. The brief, silent storm of towering waves that had passed between stage and seat — perhaps she didn't fully understand its origins, but she had keenly caught the complex tension and history it held, and she knew that silent companionship at this moment was worth far more than any presumptuous questioning.
They stepped out of the theater, and the summer night's warm breeze, carrying the scent of greenery, rushed up to meet them, quickly dispersing the slight stifling closeness left by the theater's overly generous air conditioning. The plaza was bright with lights, the crowd gradually splitting off and dispersing, the clamor subsiding.
"Well, Boss Yao, what score does the professional judge give?" Jin Lan asked with a smile, her tone easy, steering the topic toward the safe ground of technique. "Want to go for a drink, talk over your professional take? Or just head back and rest?"
"Let's head back. I'm a little tired." Yao Yuan drew the thin cream silk jacket closer around herself. That stroke of peach-pink, lit by the night and the plaza lights, was still dazzling, drawing repeated sidelong glances from the scattered passersby. Her expression was perfectly at ease, even with a faint, contented smile at the corner of her lips, the kind that comes after a fine performance — as if that show, charged with invisible tension and the collision of memory, had truly been, for her, nothing more than a high-quality, savored, purely artistic experience and professional viewing.
"All right." Jin Lan said no more, nodding crisply. Her driver had already pulled the car smoothly up to them.
Yao Yuan got into her own car but did not start the engine right away. She took off the diamond hoop earrings that had swayed at her ears all night, dropped them casually into her handbag, and gently rubbed the earlobes left a little sore from the earrings' weight. Her gaze, though, drifted involuntarily out the window, sweeping over Zhuge Jinye's powerful, story-laden silhouette on the giant poster on the theater's outer wall. The heart-guarding jade at her chest, through the fabric, sent up its constant, unchanging warm tenderness, quietly soothing that faint ripple in her heart that had been stirred up.
The phone screen lit up in the dim interior, its cold blue glow falling across her face. It was a message from Zhao Yiming — about some key data summaries and problem analyses from the latest integration-testing phase between the Pretty Big One Emotional AI model and the Genesis Engine. Rational, clear, going straight to the core, full of the exploratory passion and rigorous attitude toward unknown territory, pointing steadily at a future of challenge and limitless hope.
She looked at the message, then lifted her eyes again to the theater plaza outside, slowly returning to quiet, gently wrapped in the night. The faint ripples that had risen in her heart — stirred by that fierce old figure, the burning stage, and the complicated memories — were swiftly settling, sinking, finally returning to a deep, profound calm.
The ashes and shadows of the past might be lifted now and then by the fickle winds of fate, but they could no longer scald her — not the foundation she had built up with her own hands, growing broader by the day. And the blueprint of the future, that chapter drawn together by reason, wisdom, partnership, and limitless possibility, was waiting for her — composed and cool-headed, yet full of power — to add, stroke by stroke, even grander, even more brilliant chapters.
She started the car, the engine giving a low, steady roar. She flicked on the turn signal smoothly and merged into Jinshi's dazzling, never-ceasing summer-night traffic, bright as a river of stars. That stroke of dazzling peach-pink vanished at last into the city's shifting, kaleidoscopic river of light, leaving behind only a faint, almost imperceptible aftertaste in the air — belonging to the height of a summer night, fervent and utterly clear-eyed at once — scattering on the wind.
