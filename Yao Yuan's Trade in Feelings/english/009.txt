Chapter 9 — Stepfather Zhou Lufei and His "Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot"

Yao Yuan was woken by sunlight that had slipped through the gap in the curtains, far too bright. She opened her eyes with a frown, let them adjust to the light, and only then realized it was already late. The space beside her was empty, but the sheets still held their creases and a lingering warmth, and the air was still thick with the mingled scents of the night before. Lit now by the sun, it all took on a lazy, drowsy intimacy she didn't usually feel.
She grabbed the phone off the nightstand and tapped the screen awake.
10:27.
She'd slept well, at least. As she woke, her body's memory came drifting back—the sounds she'd swallowed and held in, the heat where skin met skin, and the deep sleep she'd dropped into afterward, spent and exhausted... It all washed slowly over her mind like a rising tide. She propped herself up on her aching, languid body and sat up; the silk quilt slid away, bringing a wave of cold.
Just then the bathroom door slid open and Yu Hao came out. He had already washed up, dressed in a simple white cotton T-shirt and gray lounge pants, his hair slightly damp, his expression its usual crisp and tidy self—not a trace of fatigue or indulgence on him, as if he'd done nothing but get a good night's sleep. Seeing she was awake, his steps paused for a beat, then he walked straight to the wardrobe—where the shirt and slacks he'd changed out of the night before hung, waiting to be pressed, along with a few changes of clothes he kept here.
"Morning," he said, his voice carrying that particular morning rasp, but clear. "I made coffee. It's in the kitchen."
"Morning," Yao Yuan answered, her voice still rough with sleep. She threw off the covers, climbed out of bed, and stepped barefoot onto the cool wooden floor, a secret ache spreading from the tops of her thighs. She said nothing more and headed straight for the bathroom.
By the time she finished her shower and came out trailing steam and the fresh scent of body wash, Yu Hao was no longer in the bedroom. She changed into comfortable loungewear and walked to the open kitchen. The coffee machine was giving off a faint warming hum, and the air carried a rich, full aroma. Two glasses of water sat poured on the island, along with Yu Hao's laptop, its screen lit, showing what looked like a chart of data.
Yu Hao stood beside the island, one hand holding his phone as he tapped out a quick reply to something, the other resting idly on the counter, his fingers drumming without thinking. Hearing her footsteps, he looked up.
"You don't have much in the fridge, so I just made a sandwich to tide you over. We'll go out for lunch." He kept it brief, his gaze lingering for a moment on her still-dripping hair. "You're not going to blow-dry it?"
"No need, it'll dry soon." Yao Yuan poured herself a cup of black coffee and leaned against the other side of the island, sipping it in small mouthfuls. The scalding, bitter liquid slid down her throat, bringing a clear, sharp alertness that chased off the last of her drowsiness. With the island between them, the air floated with the smell of coffee and a strange, after-the-fact stillness. No awkwardness, no excessive tenderness—more like two people who had just finished a demanding joint project and were now in a brief phase of rest and resupply.
Yu Hao finished his message, set down his phone, and picked up his own coffee. For a moment neither spoke, each drinking, their eyes occasionally meeting in the air before drifting apart again naturally. Sunlight poured in great sheets into the living room, falling on the polished floor, dust motes dancing quietly in the columns of light.
What broke the stillness was a clear, audible growl from Yao Yuan's stomach.
Yu Hao raised an eyebrow and looked at her.
Yao Yuan didn't bat an eye, and took another sip of coffee. "Where's that sandwich you made?"
Yu Hao set down his cup, walked over to the microwave, picked up a plate, and handed it to her.
"Try it." He sat down across from her and passed her a fork.
The sandwich had a rich sauce, the egg fried tender and smooth, the luncheon meat seared crisp and fragrant—a perfectly decent, home-style taste that could quickly soothe an empty stomach. Yao Yuan ate quietly, her stomach filling with warm food, her mood lifting along with it. With a teasing lilt she praised, "Boss Yu, delicious~" She drew out the word "delicious" with all sorts of winding curves, the meaning anything but innocent.
Yu Hao was caught off guard by the tease. The tips of his ears flushed crimson in an instant. He feigned composure, but his throat bobbed lightly, his gaze instinctively dodging away, even as the corners of his mouth couldn't help curving up.
He came around the island and pulled the giggling, doubled-over Yao Yuan into his arms, biting lightly at the tip of her nose.
The bite tickled the tip of her nose, and she laughed and burrowed closer into his chest, her hair brushing the side of his neck, carrying the steam of a fresh shower. "What are you, part dog?" Her voice was soft and fond, but her fingertips toyed lightly at the third button of his shirt—the one whose thread had come loose, the one she'd yanked the night before.
Yu Hao lowered his head, his breath falling on the crown of her hair, carrying the faint bitterness of coffee and the sweet of her body wash. "And whose mind was running somewhere it shouldn't?" His arm tightened, hemming her in between himself and the island. "Are you still eating the sandwich?"
"No more," Yao Yuan tilted her head up to look at him, a trace of steam still clinging to her lashes. "You've stirred me up till I've lost my appetite."
"Then how about a different thing to eat?" His eyebrow arched, his gaze sliding from her reddened earlobe to her slightly parted lips, his throat moving.
Yao Yuan's heart skipped half a beat. She deliberately turned her head to look out the window, where the sun fell warm on her bare ankle. "My hair's still wet." She grabbed for an excuse, but her voice held little conviction.
Yu Hao said nothing, just turned and went into the bathroom. When he came back, he had a hair towel in his hand. He stood behind her, lifting the loose strands scattered over her shoulders and gathering them together, pressing them gently with the towel. His movements were slow, the pads of his fingers occasionally brushing the back of her neck like a feather's sweep, making her back tense slightly.
His breath was right at the side of her neck. The friction of the towel, the birdsong outside the window, the hum of the coffee machine—all of it blurred into background noise, leaving only the faint tremor where skin touched skin.
He bent lower, his lips almost against her ear. Yao Yuan turned around and crashed into the smile in his eyes. Yu Hao's hand was still resting on her shoulder; the hair towel slipped to the floor, and he took the chance to clasp her wrist, his fingertips stroking the fine, delicate skin over the bone.
Sunlight leaked in through the gap between them, casting overlapping shadows on the floor. Yao Yuan looked at his face, so close to hers, and suddenly rose up on her toes to peck him quickly on the chin.
Like a water droplet falling into a pot of hot oil, it burst instantly into a fine, prickling itch. Yu Hao's eyes darkened, and before she could pull back, he lowered his head and kissed her. The sunlight outside was just right, the scent of coffee mingling with body wash, and in this small kitchen it brewed into something thicker, warmer.
After a good while of fooling around, it was nearly noon. The two of them washed up quickly and headed out for lunch.
Time slid toward midday. They left the apartment, and Yu Hao's driver already had the car waiting downstairs—a black Audi A8, steady and understated. The car drove across nearly half the city before finally stopping on a bustling but well-laid-out restaurant street. There were no shortage of famous establishments lining both sides, but one building stood out in particular: a three-story structure in an unmistakable new-Chinese style, with a wide-open front. A massive plaque hung high, gold characters on black, bearing five forceful, vigorous words—"Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot"—signed by some contemporary master calligrapher. Even at noon on a weekday, quite a few nice cars were already parked out front, and uniformed doormen directed them with practiced ease.
"Right here." Yao Yuan signaled the driver to stop. She was clearly familiar with the place, though her expression stayed flat.
Lifting aside the heavy cotton curtain embroidered with an interlocking-lotus pattern, a rush of warmth and noise instantly shut out the chill of the winter day. The first floor was the open dining hall for walk-in guests, with ceilings a good five or six meters high, spacious and bright, with none of the cramped feel of an old shop. The decor was a thoughtfully done new-Chinese style: dark walnut tables and chairs, partitions of carved lattice windows, and walls decorated with artfully rendered diagrams of mutton cuts and black-and-white photos of old Jinshi life. The tables were spaced just right, keeping the lively dine-in atmosphere without feeling crowded. Because it was a Saturday, every seat was already taken, voices clamoring, copper pots bubbling, white steam curling up. The waitstaff, dressed in matching navy-blue Chinese-style jackets, threaded through the room like flying with huge wooden trays, their full-throated calls weaving together with the chatter, laughter, and clinking glasses of the diners into a symphony brimming with life.
"Miss Yuan is here!" A capable-looking woman of about forty-odd, in a dark silk cheongsam, had sharp eyes; she stepped briskly out from behind the cashier's counter deep inside, her smile warm and pitched to exactly the right degree. "Boss Zhou just called to ask. And this would be... Mr. Yu? Boss Zhou gave special instructions—the private room's been kept for the two of you. This way, please." Her gaze swept over Yu Hao very quickly, carrying a professional deference and a barely perceptible note of appraisal.
"Manager Li, thank you for the trouble." Yao Yuan gave a slight nod and gestured to Yu Hao. "My stepfather's place. He usually works upstairs in the office."
"You're too kind, Miss Yao." Manager Li turned to lead the way, guiding them around the noisy first-floor hall and up a staircase on one side to the second floor. The second floor was the higher-grade private-room area; the corridor was laid with thick carpet, and the surroundings turned instantly quiet and elegant. She led them to the door of a private room named "Listening to Snow" at the end of the hall and pushed it gently open.
The room wasn't large, but it was exquisite. The street-facing wall was a single floor-to-ceiling window, hung with bamboo blinds that softened the light. In the center stood a six-seat rosewood table, with a purple-copper charcoal pot polished to a gleam in the middle, the charcoal glowing red, a clear spring-water broth simmering gently with a pure aroma. Four dishes and eight bowls of classic accompaniments were already arranged around it. The mutton was top-grade, hand-cut and fresh, marbled with fat and lean, sliced thin enough to let light through, laid out neatly on blue-and-white porcelain plates like works of art. The condiments—sugar-pickled garlic, chive-flower paste, fermented bean curd, sesame sauce—were even more delicately presented in small dishes.
"Boss Zhou says, please go ahead and start. He's handling something and will be right down." Manager Li set out their bowls and chopsticks with her own hands, asked about any dietary restrictions, then withdrew without a sound, closing the door behind her.
"Quite a setup." Yu Hao took off his coat, draped it over the rack to the side, and looked around the room, his tone flat, neither praise nor criticism.
"He's got a taste for this kind of pomp—thinks it's the only way to do justice to a time-honored name and the business as it stands now." Yao Yuan sat down at the table, picked up the long chopsticks, lifted a slice of rosy mutton, and swished it casually through the clear broth. "But the meat and the craft really are the heart of this place. The food is good."
The two had barely started eating when there was a knock at the door, and it swung open. A man of about sixty-five or sixty-six walked in, his figure kept in excellent shape, dressed in a finely tailored dark-gray Mao suit—this was Zhou Lufei. His face was lean and clear, his temples slightly gray, but his gaze was sharp and his stride brisk, carrying the shrewdness of a successful businessman and the easy composure of someone long seasoned in the world. Seeing Yao Yuan, a smile broke over his face—one that held concern, and also the satisfaction of seeing an accomplished junior of one's own.
"Yuanyuan! You're finally here! And this must be Mr. Yu Hao? I've heard so much about you!" Zhou Lufei's voice was loud and full of vigor as he reached out his hand to Yu Hao, the handshake firm and brief. "This girl Yao Yuan has high standards. Anyone she'd bring to eat with me as a friend must be a fine young talent! Don't stand on ceremony—once you're here, treat it like your own home. Today's meat, I guarantee, you won't forget once you've tasted it!"
"Boss Zhou, a pleasure. Yao Yuan flatters me. Forgive the intrusion today—I came because of the place's reputation." Yu Hao rose, shook hands, his manner neither obsequious nor aloof, his courtesy complete, carrying a steadiness beyond his years. "A time-honored name in Jinshi—it lives up to the reputation."
"Hahaha, never mind all this 'Boss' business—just call me Uncle Zhou!" Zhou Lufei laughed heartily and settled naturally into the head seat, waving for them all to sit. "Just a small business, putting food on the table—nothing next to the big ventures you young people are building. Come, dig in. This mutton came in this morning from the Huyang pastures, still carrying the chill of the grasslands. You've got to eat it fresh!"
He took up the chopsticks himself, lifted a few of the prettiest slices of mutton shoulder, swished them quickly apart in the rolling clear broth, and fished them out the moment the red faded, placing them into Yao Yuan's and Yu Hao's dishes. "Try it—just a touch of salt, that's how you taste it at its purest."
Yu Hao went along readily and tried it as told. The mutton was exquisitely tender, sweet beyond compare, with a milky note—genuinely first-rate. He nodded and praised it sincerely: "Good meat. The heat and the knife work both show real skill."
"You know your stuff!" Zhou Lufei's eyes lit up, clearly delighted that Yu Hao knew the trade, and the conversation opened right up—from the selection, slaughter, and aging of the mutton, to the blending of the secret-recipe sesame sauce, the choice of charcoal, and even how the shop maintained its quality by building long-term partnerships with the pastures and training its meat-cutters... He had it all at his fingertips, his words carrying both pride in traditional craft and the thinking of modern business management—not the kind of stubbornly old-fashioned businessman who clung to the past.
For most of it, Yao Yuan quietly cooked her meat, picked at dishes, and listened to them talk. Her relationship with Zhou Lufei, ground smooth over these years, had long since settled into a stable pattern: courteous, respectful, keeping a moderate distance, but never lacking in due care and decorum. Materially, Zhou Lufei had never shortchanged her, and he was willing to lend support in connections and resources, but he rarely pried into the details of her love life, giving her a good deal of freedom and respect. For Yao Yuan, this kind of relationship felt far more comfortable than the overly intimate sort of family bond that tried to intervene in all her choices.
Over the meal, Zhou Lufei steered the topic naturally toward Yao Yuan's work.
"I hear you've been talking about a partnership with some high-tech company lately? Something to do with that... emotional-counseling AI?" Zhou Lufei took a sip of the warm yellow wine and looked at Yao Yuan, his eyes full of concern but mostly the posture of a listener. "Uncle Zhou's an outsider to all this—can't give you any advice. But, Yuanyuan, with business matters, it's worth hearing different views, especially from a professional friend like Yu Hao." He turned to Yu Hao and raised his cup. "Yu Hao, you're the expert—you've got to help her keep an eye on things. This girl has a mind of her own, but sometimes she pushes herself too hard. I worry she'll come out the worse for it."
It was a deftly put remark—it expressed concern, while cleverly handing the responsibility for assessment and advice over to Yu Hao, and slipping in a bit of flattery for him besides.
"Don't worry, Uncle Zhou." Yu Hao set down his chopsticks, picked up the damp towel to wipe his hands, then spoke slowly, his tone steady and objective. "Yao Yuan has a clear strategic positioning and risk assessment for the project. Western World's technology really does have its unique strengths, and the logic of the partnership holds. As for the specific terms and risk hedging, we'll work through them carefully. I have a lot of confidence in Yao Yuan's decision-making."
His answer both affirmed Yao Yuan's agency and made clear he would provide professional support, while hinting that the partnership was still under evaluation and the risk was manageable. The balance was struck just right—it reassured Zhou Lufei while fully respecting Yao Yuan's independence.
Hearing this, Zhou Lufei's smile grew wider, and he nodded again and again. "Good, good! You young people talk things through, professional and bold both—better than us old fellows! Come, let's have another round!"
The meal went along unhurried, the mood far more relaxed than Yao Yuan had expected. Zhou Lufei was talkative but not long-winded, knowing when to stop; Yu Hao, though a man of few words, hit the key point every time he spoke, and the two of them found plenty to discuss. As they left, Zhou Lufei saw them all the way to the front door on the first floor, said plenty of polite things to Yu Hao about "coming often" and "treating this as your own canteen," and personally watched as the doorman directed Yu Hao's car out of the lot.
"I've got an online roadshow to listen to at three, and some things to handle after that, so I'll head off first." Standing on the wide steps at the entrance, Yu Hao said to Yao Yuan. The winter afternoon sun fell on his crisp dark coat, tracing a clean silhouette.
"All right." Yao Yuan nodded.
"The preliminary assessment of Zhao Yiming and Western World you asked for last night—I've already sent it to your encrypted inbox. The more detailed follow-up analysis I'll send later." He added it in the tone of a work handoff.
"Very efficient." Yao Yuan said, and she meant it. Dealing with him was easy precisely because of this—clear instructions, timely delivery, never any dragging or mess.
"As it should be. Maximize efficiency within the rules."
With no superfluous goodbye, Yu Hao nodded once more to her and Zhou Lufei, then turned and walked down the steps, getting into the waiting car. It pulled away smoothly.
"That young man—he really is something." Zhou Lufei watched the direction the car had disappeared in, and suddenly said to Yao Yuan, his tone carrying the certainty of a man who had read countless people. "Steady, not flighty, with a plan in his head. Not the kind who's got a little cleverness and looks down on everyone. As for your mom—I'll talk to her later, tell her to stop fretting over nothing."
Yao Yuan didn't answer, only smiled faintly. Coming from Zhou Lufei, this counted as a fairly high assessment of Yu Hao, and an indirect sign of his approval of her current social choices.
"Let's go, head home. Your mom's been going on about it since this morning, just waiting for you." Zhou Lufei patted her shoulder, his tone back to its usual briskness, and gestured toward his own Mercedes S-Class parked alongside.
"All right, home." Yao Yuan answered, opening the car door and getting in.
