Chapter 56 — Time-Travel Back to Thirty-Three, in the Gobi, Where an Unfinished Destiny Hides Its Secrets

How long had passed, she couldn't say. Maybe an instant. Maybe a very long time.
Consciousness rose slowly out of the cold depths of darkness.
The first thing to return was the body's sense of "being there"—she was standing, something solid beneath her feet, and yet all around her there was nothing at all. Yao Yuan opened her eyes slowly.
Before her stretched an endless, flawless expanse of pure white.
There was no light source, yet the light was even and soft, filling every inch of space. There was no direction, yet gravity still held, letting her stand steady. She was back here again—this "transitional space" that sat between her several journeys through time, something she could neither understand nor control.
She steadied herself and wasted no time probing the physical makeup of all this white—experience from the previous times had taught her it was pointless. She simply stood there, quietly, and waited.
By the "rules," soon enough images out of the past would surface from within all this white. Those pictures, some sharp and some blurred, would drag her into a particular time and place, to face a particular moment—one she might have forgotten, or deliberately buried.
This time, what would it be?
The dazzling night skyline of the Bund in Shanghai, carrying the shame of being traded away and the cold death of her heart?
Something earlier—the scalding passion and pain of her tangled past with Shuai Hongqiang?
Or… something else?
She held her breath and fixed her gaze on the depths of that empty white.
As if answering her stare, the white space ahead began to ripple in that familiar way. Like a stone dropped into a still lake, rings of waves spread outward; the scene at the center twisted, then sharpened, and at last the empty white was replaced by an image utterly clear—yet completely unlike any personal, emotional scene Yao Yuan had been bracing for.
A boundless, desolate, vast northwestern Gobi.
The sky was that kind of blue scoured by wind and sand, so high and far it made the heart clench. The land was a parched ochre, jagged black stones scattered across it, and in the distance ran an unbroken line of hard-edged, bare mountains. And there, at the center of this seemingly unchanging wasteland, stood a colossal structure of striking futuristic design.
In the pure-white space, the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan narrowed her eyes slightly.
It was an enormous building of silver-gray, its form pared down to the absolute extreme of clean simplicity. It looked like no traditional data center—no extra ornament, no complicated structure, the whole of it a smooth, flattened oval, like a giant metal egg fallen from beyond the sky, half sunk into the solid earth, half exposed to the scorching air and the fierce ultraviolet light. Its surface was sheathed in some optical material that adapted itself to the environment, and under the harsh Gobi sun it refracted a calm, almost inorganic metallic sheen, striking a forceful contrast with the raw, rough surroundings. There was barely a human figure to be seen anywhere near it—only sparse conduits of special weather-resistant material that dipped faintly underground, and farther off, vast wind turbines and arrays of solar panels turning and glinting in silence under the sun, supplying this "metal egg" with a near-perpetual clean energy.
Yao Yuan's gaze swept over the power arrays deployed at scale in the distance, the pipelines built from special weather-resistant materials, and the building's own form—that "function above all cost" simplicity. This was no laboratory, no rough first draft of a startup. This was hardcore infrastructure already on the ground and ready to carry massive volumes of data and the running of complex models. Every inch of the design reeked of money being burned, and of the builder's unquestionable resolve.
Several black off-road vehicles, specially modified to suit Gobi terrain, were parked before the building's one conspicuous entrance corridor. The entrance was a long, narrow opening set into the side of the "metal egg," like a slit coolly appraising the world outside.
The car doors opened, and a group of people stepped out.
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's breath caught, almost imperceptibly.
The man at the front looked about thirty-five or thirty-six, dressed simply in khaki work trousers and a gray fleece jacket.
It was him—Zhao Yiming.
No glasses. His skin, after years of exposure to all kinds of light out in the field and in the lab, was a healthy wheat color, even carrying a touch of the roughness the Gobi sun leaves behind. His hair was shorter than it would later be, more careless. But those eyes hadn't changed—still, focused. Right now they were narrowed slightly, gazing out at this "fortress" he had built with his own hands, with the pure, absorbed attention of a builder examining his own work, and a trace of certainty in the face of the wilderness he had imposed order upon.
So this early, he had already begun to pour the foundations of his "Genesis Engine" in this wasteland. The realization sent a subtle ripple through Yao Yuan's mind.
Then a figure stepped down from the car behind, and the gaze of the Yao Yuan in the pure-white space froze completely.
It was herself—though she couldn't tell how old. Because the scene before her had never once appeared in anything she had lived through.
She wore a beautifully tailored cream-white trench coat over a light-gray cashmere sweater and matching trousers, with flat ankle boots easy to walk in, her long hair tied into a neat low ponytail. Her makeup was refined, but she looked a touch greener than the Yao Yuan of now. The merchant's sharpness and the bone-deep weariness of long battles had not yet fully settled into her brow; in their place was a clear-eyed purpose, a vivid hunger to know, and a… cautious, appraising look that belonged to an "investor."
She got out of the car, and the dry, hot Gobi wind instantly stirred the hem of her coat and her hair. She raised a hand to press down her hat, her eyes first sweeping quickly over the staggering structure before her—a flash of clear wonder in them—and then her gaze settled on the slightly younger Zhao Yiming up ahead.
"Mr. Zhao," the younger Yao Yuan said, stepping forward, hand outstretched, her voice still clear and steady against the empty Gobi and the wind. "I've heard a great deal about you. I'm Yao Yuan. I've come from Shanghai. The northwestern scenery the whole way here was… truly magnificent."
The young Zhao Yiming turned and shook her hand. His grip was dry and firm, his smile frank and direct, with the plainspokenness common to researchers who aren't much good at pleasantries. "You've had a hard trip, Ms. Yao. The conditions here are crude, but"—he half-turned, gesturing toward the silver-gray building—"the 'Genesis Engine' needs an environment like this. Absolute quiet, physical isolation, energy independence, and enough space and cooling capacity to process and train the massive parameters we're about to face. After you."
The group passed through rigorous identity verification and security checks and entered the building.
Yao Yuan—thirty-seven—watched in silence.
Unlike the desolate, sci-fi feel outside, the interior was the ultimate in "cleanliness" and "order." The vast main space rose to a startling height, the temperature held constant and low, and in her ears was a deep hum, as if from the depths of the earth—the foundational chord of countless server clusters at work. There were no tangled cables in sight; all the infrastructure was sealed into uniform modular units, blinking with faint blue or pale green indicator lights, like the breathing of countless sleeping giant beasts. Technicians in anti-static suits moved through it all without a sound.
Zhao Yiming gave the tour himself, his pace of speech even, no boasting, only statement:
"The Zone A we're standing in now is the core compute array of the current 'Genesis Engine' version 1.0, handling mainly the training of general artificial-intelligence models. Zone B, under construction next to it, is a dedicated cluster reserved for the next-generation 'large emotional-parameter model.' It needs higher parallel-compute efficiency and memory bandwidth. The initial hardware investment"—he paused, and named a figure measured in the tens of millions—"is only the base hardware."
"Our in-house liquid-cooling system improves energy efficiency by forty percent over traditional air cooling. We can get PUE—power usage effectiveness—below 1.1. But the cost of developing and deploying this system up front"—he named another figure—"is the price of that. The Gobi's dry, cold air is our free, natural cooling resource, but 'connecting' that resource into the system, the investment in the groundwork and the pipeline network"—he pointed to the conduits beneath their feet and faintly visible in the distance—"accounts for a considerable part of the total infrastructure cost."
"On data security, physical isolation is the first step. All data in and out passes through multiple layers of encryption and one-way gates. Core training data is generated, processed, and destroyed here on site—it never leaves. Beside us is an independent disaster-backup center, hidden inside the mountain. The cost of excavating and reinforcing that part"—he looked at the slightly younger Yao Yuan—"is no less than the aboveground building."
Yao Yuan—thirty-seven—listened to these figures laid out in that calm narration, running the numbers quickly in her mind. Infrastructure, custom hardware, in-house systems, energy support, security redundancy… every module was burning capital. Zhao Yiming never came out and said "flagship scale: starts at two hundred million," but the itemized numbers he kept tossing out, and the level of completion and singularity this building displayed—far beyond an ordinary data center—silently confirmed that threshold to entry. He wasn't pleading poverty. He was calmly displaying the weight of this project and its capacity to devour money—and he was screening, too. Screening for investors who truly understood its value and had the strength to walk the road alongside it.
In the image, the slightly younger Yao Yuan listened closely, and the questions she occasionally raised all cut to the heart of the technology and the cost. She no longer asked "how does an emotional model understand human nature." Instead she asked about "the expected return-on-investment cycle of the dedicated cluster," "the long-term maintenance cost and risk of the liquid-cooling system," "the response level of the disaster backup and the data-recovery time objective." Her questions showed she was assessing the project's feasibility, risk, and potential return through an investor's eyes.
During a break in the tour, in a relatively open curved corridor linking the different zones. One side of the corridor was a vast floor-to-ceiling window, the boundless Gobi beyond it; the other was a black wall, smooth as a mirror, dimly reflecting the figures of those who passed.
The slightly younger Yao Yuan's phone buzzed. She glanced at the caller ID, made an apologetic gesture to Zhao Yiming, and walked off toward the window to take the call. Her profile by the window—it seemed to be a phone call at a critical moment in some cross-border merger and acquisition. Her tone quickly turned decisive and incisive, her expression calm, nodding now and then, issuing a few crisp instructions. She was a different person entirely from the one who had stood listening quietly to the technical briefing moments before.
A few minutes later she hung up, the sharpness of business negotiation not yet fully faded from her face, and turned to head back to continue the tour.
And in the instant she turned—
Her gaze brushed, without meaning to, across the smooth mirror-black wall of the corridor.
The wall reflected her clearly: cream-white trench coat, neat ponytail, the face of a thirty-three-year-old woman in her prime, with a faint flush left by the Gobi sun.
But then, right beside that clear reflection—almost pressed against it—another figure surfaced and congealed without the slightest warning!
The same height and outline, the same brow and eyes familiar to the bone—but the air about her was different. Stiller, more transparent, as though all the fierceness and struggle had been internalized into bedrock at the foundation. In the depths of her eyes lay a profundity the present self could not yet fully understand, the depth that comes after living through far more stories, and a… faint weariness. She wore a wine-red silk blouse and a black leather vest, her long hair slightly waved—it was the future Yao Yuan! Watching her present self quietly, through the mirror, her gaze complicated.
"!!!"
The younger Yao Yuan jerked to a stop, her body going rigid in an instant, her pupils contracting sharply. A chill mingled with a violent jolt of alarm shot up her spine to the crown of her head; her blood seemed to roar in her ears. She stared, fixed, at that eerie pair of "twin reflections" side by side in the mirror, the knuckles gripping her phone white with force. The fifth time. Even though this was already the fifth time she had run into her "future self" in a mirror this way—abruptly, defying every law of physics—the shock and absurdity of that moment had lost not the slightest edge. Especially now, standing inside a data-center that stood for the very frontier of human reason and technology, this surreal dislocation made her scalp crawl.
In the mirror, that older "she" in the wine-red blouse had eyes calm and unrippled—no astonishment, only a deep, almost compassionate understanding. As if this scene had long been marked on her own timeline; or as if this were exactly the coordinate she had come across the chaos to confirm.
(It's you…)
A clear, soundless recognition slammed into the younger Yao Yuan's mind. Not a question—a confirmation. This was the "voice from the future" that, three years ago, at the most crucial fork in her life, had appeared like a cold prophecy, and with cruel truth and a clear accounting of pros and cons had wrenched her—by force—off the track of a "caged canary to be admired and traded" and onto another road entirely.
The "she" in the mirror stirred slightly, as if drawing a soft breath, and then that familiar voice, only a little hoarser for the years settled into it, seemed to sound directly inside the younger Yao Yuan's mind, and also to come through the mirror:
"We meet again." The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan spoke, her tone strangely steady, carrying the faint feeling of a long-parted reunion, yet restrained and distant. "My past self. How old are you this year… thirty-something?"
The younger Yao Yuan forced herself out of that enormous shock, swiftly pressing the churning emotions down into her heart. She met those stiller eyes in the mirror, her voice surprisingly steady, even carrying a trace of the investor's cool appraisal: "Thirty-three. It's been exactly three years since I last 'heard' your voice."
She paused, as though reporting a major strategic transformation to the highest decision-maker, her words clear and concise:
"Three years ago, your 'warning' was right on time. I saw through the game early and didn't end up someone else's chip. I made use of that last bit of 'appreciation' Ding Shitong had—the part he hadn't yet had time to turn into a bargaining chip—and seized the resources and connections he could offer earlier, and harder. Duan Yanqing's 'I will have it no matter what' became, in my hands, a chip for equal negotiation—I took on some of the high-profit but borderline projects he held, and followed their lead to make a few short-term plays in the financial markets. No high-mindedness, no illusions. I only calculated input against return."
Her gaze was sharp, reflecting the still face of that other self in the mirror:
"In three years, I completed the primitive accumulation. Now I have capital, and I have choices. I didn't go become the 'Godmother of Feelings' you mentioned—the one who has to step into the spotlight and dissect human hearts. That's too conspicuous, too easy to make a target. I chose to be the capital beneath the surface. Coming here today, to assess Zhao Yiming's 'Genesis Engine' and his emotional model—this is what I consider the next track worth a heavy bet, based on the 'AI direction' you brought up back then. What I want isn't just financial return. It's a ticket into the future."
She finished and watched the "future" in the mirror in silence. No showing off—only the statement of a fact, a starkly different "fact" that had been born of "her" intervention.
In the mirror, the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan listened quietly, no surprise on her face, only a far-reaching stillness, as if seeing through time itself. Not until the thirty-three-year-old Yao Yuan finished did she give a slow, almost imperceptible nod.
"What you've done," the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan said, a very faint, almost admiring sigh in her voice, "is far better, and far faster, than what the 'me' on the original timeline did."
She looked at the sharpness and vivid ambition in the eyes of this self three years younger, edges not yet worn down by too much of the world, and went on:
"It wasn't until last year, at thirty-six, that I began to follow the AI track systematically, with the intent to combine it with my emotional-case data. That was when I first formally got in contact with Zhao Yiming, to work together on developing the 'Pretty Big One' Emotional AI model. You've laid your groundwork three full years before me, and met him three years earlier too." She paused, then added, "In this field, three years is enough to decide whether you lead the wave or chase it."
The thirty-three-year-old Yao Yuan caught that point in time keenly: "So this is… the fifth time you've appeared before me. You're a year older than when you last told me all this." She suddenly remembered a question she had never had the chance to ask: "I don't think I've ever asked—future 'me,' how old, exactly, are you now?"
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror seemed to pause, then the corner of her mouth curved into a very faint, almost self-mocking arc: "Thirty-seven. The previous four… 'meetings,' I was thirty-six every time. They happened in dense succession, almost as if compressed into a very short span." She didn't go deeper into what that "density" of time-travel meant, but beneath that calm tone there seemed to hide an enormous weariness.
The thirty-three-year-old Yao Yuan nodded, filing the information away. She didn't dwell on those unspoken hardships—time was short. She asked the most crucial question: "Then, with this appearance, do you have any advice for me? About this place"—her gaze swept over the cold walls around her, walls that stood for the very frontier of technology—"about Zhao Yiming, about AI, or… something else?"
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan was silent for a moment, her eyes growing deeper still, as if rapidly weighing what could be revealed against the ripples it might send through time and space. She spoke slowly, her voice lower now, with a note of caution:
"Keep believing in AI—that direction is right. It's one of the core variables of the next ten years and beyond. The depth and breadth of its penetration will go beyond what most people now can imagine. As for how far it can develop…" She shook her head. "The 'Pretty Big One' Emotional AI model I'm building with Zhao Yiming is still in a difficult stage of 'fusion.' The clash between human nature and algorithm is more complicated than we expected. This is a blue ocean full of opportunity but riddled with hidden reefs."
She seemed about to say something more, her gaze flickering slightly: "Also, in the venture-capital circle, if you ever meet someone called Yu—"
The words went unfinished.
The mirror surface suddenly heaved violently! Like water struck by a boulder, the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's figure twisted, blurred, and shattered in the spreading ripples!
"Wait—" The thirty-three-year-old Yao Yuan instinctively took half a step forward, reaching out, but touched only the cold, smooth glass.
The ripples settled.
In the mirror, only her own face remained—eyes slightly widened, faintly pale, carrying an astonishment and a searching that had not yet faded. The "she" in the wine-red blouse, come from three years on, had vanished without a trace, as if she had never appeared.
"…"
She stood frozen in place for a few seconds, then drew a deep, hard breath of the cold air of the data-center—constant in temperature and humidity, carrying that special clean smell—and forced her eyes shut. Her lashes cast fine shadows on her pale skin. When she opened them again, all the violent emotion in their depths had been forced down to the deepest place, leaving only a tempered, cold clarity and sharpness.
Something had already changed. Not shock, but a deeper comprehension and resolve. She turned slowly, her back to the mirror that had just played out that surreal scene, and looked toward the far end of the corridor—Zhao Yiming was finishing his conversation with the technical lead, and seemed about to turn and come find her.
She watched his profile, focused, carrying the particular stubbornness of a scientist, and her gaze became immensely complicated. Within that complexity was an assessment grounded in business logic, an appreciation for top-tier intellect, a hunger for unknown technology—and now, quietly mixed in, a trace of something only she knew: a heavy confirmation and a sense of destiny, come from the far shore of time.
The eternal wind of the Gobi seemed to pass through the solid walls and leave a soundless sob in her ear.
(The view cuts back to the real timeline—the little garden below Yao Yuan's office.)
Yao Yuan's body gave a faint, almost imperceptible shiver. Her long, dense lashes trembled a few times, then slowly lifted.
The spring-afternoon sun still poured warm onto her shoulders, the cloying-sweet scent of mountain-pear blossoms wreathing the tip of her nose, the blurred sound of traffic drifting from the distant street. She was still in the same posture, leaning back against the long bench, an arm draped over its back, as if she'd only dozed off for a moment in a break from work.
Only she knew that in that just-past "moment," her consciousness had been dragged roughly across time and space, meeting—soundlessly, heart-shakingly—with her self of three years ago, in the Gobi wind and the cold mirror of a data-center.
The violent drain that time-travel brought, rising from the depths of consciousness, ebbed slowly away like a tide, leaving behind a familiar weariness and a kind of exhaustion, as if she'd just done several rounds of high-intensity mental sparring back to back. She frowned faintly and raised a hand to press gently at her temple.
She picked up her phone; the screen lit.
16:22.
From the time she'd sat down here, felt the glass curtain wall stir strangely, and slipped into vertigo, only about… half an hour had passed. The flow of real time seemed to have been twisted and compressed in the moment the time-travel occurred.
She leaned back against the bench and didn't move at once, letting the sun smooth over her skin while she digested all she had just "witnessed."
She had seen the "fruit" of the change. Her thirty-three-year-old self—capable, sharp, clear in her aims—standing before Zhao Yiming as an investor, talking about infrastructure investment on the order of hundreds of millions and the technological waves of the future. That was no pawn pushed along by fate; that was a player moving her own pieces. Fate had indeed been pried loose at that tiny node at thirty, shifted off course by a single "warning" from the future. Career path, the way wealth was accumulated, the context and identity in which she met a key figure (Zhao Yiming)—all of it was different.
But she had also seen new "causes" being quietly sown in that altered soil. The thirty-three-year-old "she" was still searching for a powerful collaborator (Zhao Yiming), still laying out a future full of risk (AI), still standing in the complex, precise arena of human nature and the calculation of interests. The abrupt cutting-off of the conversation in the mirror, that unfinished syllable "Yu"… seemed to declare, without a sound: escaping one known trap may be only stepping into another maze laid out differently; changing the starting point of a path does not mean the scenery at its end will be wholly different. The price of growth, the contest of human nature, the eternal pursuit of power and safety—they had only changed their rules, changed their face, and still followed her like a shadow.
The desolate wind of the Gobi mirage, and the cloying-sweet floral scent of this spring day before her, at this moment formed a strange, profound intertextuality in the depths of her consciousness.
She knew that the elusive power of the bronze mirror, the riddle of the "Mirror Pact" that hung over her head, was far from solved. The next "reversal"—to where she didn't know, and for reasons she didn't know—might soon catch her again and drag her into another sealed-away entanglement.
But in this moment, sitting in the spring light, she was only the observer who had just locked eyes—silently—with the "achievement of the past." The stone she had cast with her own hand onto the timeline had stirred up unforeseen ripples, and what those ripples reflected was both comfort and a deeper contemplation of "freedom" and "the foreordained."
The change was real. She had proven that a person is not a wholly passive vessel.
But the road ahead remained thick with fog—and, because of this very "change," had gained new, unknown variables.
The spring mountain-pear blossoms, blooming to their utmost, would in the end wither and fall, scattering into mud. Just like those passages of life that had been rewritten, and those that had not.
And the wind of the Gobi never ceases, passing through all the timelines rewritten and unrewritten, coldly telling of some ancient truth.
The game of chess between a person and fate—the moves, perhaps, can be played differently, but the game itself seems never to truly end. Every step a new position; every step, the heart in its mouth.
