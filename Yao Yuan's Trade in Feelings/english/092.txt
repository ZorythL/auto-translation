The Eighth Time-Travel: Witnessing the Human Game of Self-Interest
Coming back to consciousness felt like floating slowly up from the bottom of deep water.
Her vision sharpened before her thoughts did. All around her stretched the same boundless, silent, pure white. Yao Yuan blinked quietly, then calmly braced herself and rose to her feet. There was none of the panic of the first times, no urge to investigate—just a quiet standing, like a spectator waiting for the next scene to begin, her gaze steady on some point in the void.
And sure enough, somewhere in the blankness, a ripple spread. Like a drop of water falling into a still lake, the rings widened outward, and at the center an image grew clear and solid, becoming a vivid, living scene with sound—
A small meeting room. Clean, modern decor, sunlight slicing through the blinds into bands of light and shadow across the floor. But the mood was at odds with all that brightness. The air held an invisible string pulled taut, as if the lightest touch would snap it.
On one side of the conference table sat a younger Yao Yuan, in a sharply tailored dark-gray suit, her long hair pulled back not a strand out of place, her makeup immaculate, her face betraying nothing. Only the "rust red" polish at her fingertips tapped, once, lightly and unconsciously, against the document on the table.
Across from her sat Zhuge Jinye. Thirty-three or thirty-four, the rawness gone, replaced by the shrewdness and composure that years of riding the business tides had ground into him—yet a dancer's particular command of his own body still lay hidden in his seemingly relaxed posture.
"Yao Yuan, that's the situation." Zhuge Jinye slid a document across to her, his tone a carefully measured sincerity. "'Flame Soul' has grown big now. Capital has come in, the team has to be fed, suppliers and partners up and down the chain have to be managed. Keeping to the old split just isn't realistic anymore—it isn't good for the company's long-term growth either. We've already had professional legal and financial people evaluate this new equity and dividend plan. The return on your early investment has been given full and proper consideration, I promise you."
The younger Yao Yuan didn't reach for the document right away. Her eyes rested on Zhuge Jinye's face for two seconds, then lowered to the cover. Her gaze was as calm as if she were looking at an ordinary project report.
"Skip the preamble, Zhuge Jinye." She spoke, her voice low but level and clear. "Tell me straight—under the new plan, how much is my equity diluted, and the projected dividends over the next three years, calculated on this new model, how many percent lower than the original agreement."
Zhuge Jinye paused, as if he hadn't expected her to cut so directly to the bone. "The exact figures are all detailed in the document—"
"I can read figures." The younger Yao Yuan cut him off, finally reaching out to pick up the document, though she didn't open it—she only weighed it in her fingertips. "But I need to hear you state the core terms out loud, yourself. That's the basic good faith of a negotiation."
There was no anger in her tone, only a brisk, by-the-book coolness that left no room to maneuver.
Zhuge Jinye pressed his lips together and leaned forward slightly. "Fine. Under the new plan, your equity goes from the original 40% to 10% of founding shares plus a stake in an option pool. The profit dividends over the next three years will be adjusted proportionally as well—but we'll guarantee that the absolute dividend you take home each year won't fall below the average of the past three years. This already fully accounts for your historical contribution—"
"Historical contribution." Yao Yuan repeated the words softly, the corner of her mouth seeming to curve the faintest fraction, a smile that never reached her eyes. "So my investment seven years ago, plus all these years of connecting resources and giving advice—in your current valuation system, it's worth the same as a... wealth-management product with a guaranteed annual yield? And what's more, a product whose yield can be 'adjusted' whenever you like?"
"That's not what I mean—" Zhuge Jinye frowned.
"Then what do you mean?" Yao Yuan lifted her eyes, her gaze like two searchlights turned down low, calmly fixed on him. "Do you mean that back then I bet you'd make it, and I bet right. Now you've made it, and you think the stake I put down when I bet on you takes too big a share of your net worth, and you want to renegotiate the split on the bet?"
Her words were a scalpel, cutting cleanly through all the gentle, sentimental packaging. The air in the meeting room seemed to congeal.
"Yao Yuan, do we really have to put it so... ugly?" Zhuge Jinye's voice dropped, taking on an edge of awkward, stiff embarrassment at having his real thoughts laid bare. "Business has its own rules, and a company goes through different stages. You can't take the 'shared hardship' bond of the startup days and demand a 'shared prosperity' split in the mature stage. It isn't reasonable, and it isn't professional."
"Reasonable. Professional." Yao Yuan nodded, as if she fully endorsed the two words. She finally opened the document in her hands, her gaze sweeping quickly over the dense terms, and began to lay things out at an even pace, as if conducting a project post-mortem:
"First, about the 'bond.' From the moment three years ago when you brought in your first round of institutional funding and signed a valuation-adjustment agreement behind my back, there's been no 'bond' between us anymore—only black-and-white contractual obligation. You and I both know that perfectly well."
"Second, about the 'rules.' The rule is that my forty percent of equity corresponds to my bearing the entire risk of taking the project from zero to one. That risk included losing every cent of my capital, and it included the chance that you, personally, might fail. Now the risk period is over and the return period has arrived, and you're using 'the company's growth needs' to rewrite the rules. In essence, that's a unilateral revision of the correspondence between risk and return. That isn't a business rule. That's a breach of contract."
"Third—" She snapped the document shut with a light crack and locked her gaze back onto Zhuge Jinye. "About this new plan of yours, drawn up after 'full consideration.' I've read it. My answer is: I do not accept any form of equity dilution. The dividends for the next three years must be carried out strictly according to the original agreement. As for three years from now—if the company really can reach the scale you've laid out in your PowerPoint, we can sit down, and based on the fair valuation at that time, work out an exit or adjustment plan we can both accept."
With that she leaned back slightly, her gaze settling into stillness as she waited. It was not sulking, but a clear, unshakable declaration of where the line lay.
Zhuge Jinye's face went fully cold, the earlier sincerity gone, leaving only the hard, businessman's calculus. "And if I told you this is the board's final resolution—no room for discussion?"
Even that faint trace of a curve vanished from Yao Yuan's face. She looked at him for a few seconds, then gave a nod so slight it was almost imperceptible, like the confirmation of an answer she'd already expected finally landing.
"I understand what you mean." She stood, her movements crisp, and began gathering the notebook and pen she'd spread out in front of her, sliding the document gently back to the center of the table. "My position and its basis rest on our original investment agreement, the various capital-increase agreements over the years, and the supplementary memorandum to that valuation-adjustment clause you signed three years ago and never disclosed to me. My lawyer will compile all of it into a formal letter and send it to you and the 'Flame Soul' board."
She picked up her clutch, her gaze passing one last calm time over Zhuge Jinye's face, now full of shock and fury.
"Oh, and—" At the door, as if suddenly remembering something, she half-turned. "A reminder: that valuation-adjustment bet you signed three years ago has a personal joint-liability clause. If, before the next funding round, 'Flame Soul' becomes embroiled in litigation involving a core founder in an equity dispute, the clause is triggered, and the amount you'd have to make up personally is, as I recall... a rather considerable figure. Good luck, President Zhuge."
The door was pulled softly shut. In the corridor, the younger Yao Yuan walked on her heels at a steady pace, her rhythm clear, heading toward the restroom. Her back was held very straight, and there was no anger on her face—only the calm that comes after exhaustion, and deep in her eyes, an ice-cold understanding.
In the empty meeting room, Zhuge Jinye stood where he was, facing the bright sunlight outside the window, saying nothing for a long while, his shoulders sagging slightly. There was guilt in that figure, and struggle—but more than that, a hard resolve, the kind a businessman settles on after weighing the costs and gains.
The younger Yao Yuan walked into the empty restroom and locked the door behind her. Leaning back against the cold panel of the door, she finally let out the breath she'd been holding. A crack appeared in the calm mask on her face, and beneath her eyes churned the anger she'd forced down. She went to the sink, turned on the tap, cupped the cold water, and splashed it hard against her face.
Droplets dripped from her jaw. She lifted her head and looked at herself in the mirror. The makeup was still flawless, but her eyes were rimmed faintly red—not the red of having cried, but the mark left by blood rushing under fierce emotion. The woman in the mirror had a sharp gaze, yet it hid a trace of something almost undetectable: bewilderment—the bewilderment of confirming, once again, the bottom line of human nature.
And in that moment, the smooth surface of the mirror rippled silently into a familiar pattern.
Another version of her own face slowly surfaced at the center of the ripples. Only the eyes were stiller—still as a deep pool, as if every raging wave had long since sunk to the very bottom. It was her own future self, who had appeared so many times before.
Two "Yao Yuans," separated by a single pane of mirror, gazed quietly at each other.
No gasp, no fear. This was the eighth time. Even a surreal meeting like this carried an absurd sense of the everyday. The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror even thought, with a touch of self-mockery: look at that—humanity's capacity to adapt really is terrifyingly strong. Even meeting your "future self" can become something you simply get used to.
This time it was the Yao Yuan before the mirror who spoke first. Her voice had already calmed, carrying a weariness and a clarity that went down to the bone. "You saw all of it, didn't you?"
She used a statement, not a question.
"Seven years ago, after I left Hangzhou and broke up with Zhuge Jinye, I listened to your 'advice.' I took most of my savings and invested in the dance troupe he'd just put together." She twitched the corner of her mouth into a bitter smile. "Look how ironic it is. Seven years, and his 'Flame Soul' really did make it. A national sensation, tickets impossible to get. And the first thing he did after his success was try to kick me out—me, the earliest investor, the one who staked everything."
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror nodded gently. There was a complicated emotion in her gaze, like understanding, like pity, and yet also like a pure, distant watching.
"The human heart," the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan said, twitching her mouth into a smile uglier than tears, "in the face of large enough interests, is it always this fragile? Seven years ago I bet on him as a person, on his talent and his character. I won—the troupe took off. And what my winning won me was sitting here, counting out every fraction of equity with him, tearing off all pretense and haggling over money." Her voice sank, heavy with self-mockery and disappointment. "All those beautiful things we went through together, the grand words we said... in the face of self-interest, they weigh as little as a joke."
In the mirror, the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan listened quietly. Only when the other had finished did she speak, slowly, her voice coming through the mirror, steady, yet carrying a power that smoothed down the rough edges: "It isn't that the human heart is fragile. It's that human nature has always been this way. To chase gain and dodge harm, to calculate loss and profit—it's written into the genes. Emotion, morality, promises—those are the coat civilized society drapes over it. When the seas are calm, the coat looks fine; when the storm comes, the coat is usually the first thing to be torn off."
She paused, her gaze clear as she looked at the version of herself outside the mirror, eyes rimmed red with anger.
"What you need to do now isn't to grieve that the coat has torn. It's to accept that it's already torn, and then use something sturdier to protect what's rightfully yours. You've been in the investment world all these years and seen no small number of cases—you shouldn't be fully grasping this only now."
The words were like a basin of ice water, dousing the last flicker of false heat in the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan's heart. She drew a deep breath, then let it out slowly, and the bewilderment and pain in her eyes were replaced by a burn-the-boats kind of clarity. "I understand. The original agreement may not have had a formal contract, but there's a trail to follow. What's mine, not a cent less. I'll get back what I'm owed in the most professional way there is."
She looked at the "future self" in the mirror, and suddenly asked, "Are you... all right now? How far have things gone with Zhao Yiming?"
She paused, her tone taking on a small, "past" sort of pride and the urge to share: "The all-purpose foundational AI model I developed together with my Zhao Yiming here has already gone live, and the market response is even better than we expected. The next step is deep optimization and iteration of the core features—we'll be rolling out paid services soon. This path, we chose right."
In the mirror, a faint, genuine smile surfaced on the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's face, a smile that held admiration, and relief, and a distant resonance. "You've done very well," she affirmed, her voice softening. "This path is the right one. As for Zhao Yiming and me, our relationship is very stable—————"
She didn't get to finish.
Just as before, with no warning, the image in the mirror flickered and warped violently, like a faulty electric signal, and then, with a crack, it dispersed into the air, taking the unfinished answer with it. The restroom fell back into dead silence. Only the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan was left, facing alone that version of herself in the mirror—eyes rimmed faintly red, gaze ice-cold.
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan opened her eyes very, very slowly.
Her vision went from blurred to clear, and the first thing to meet her eyes was the ornate diamond-patterned ceiling of the VIP room's restroom, warm yellow light spilling softly down. She was still slumped in the soft chair before the mirror, her posture no different from when she'd "left," as if she'd only closed her eyes to rest for a moment.
She lifted her wrist and glanced at the hands of her watch. From leaving the table to come to the restroom, to coming back to consciousness now, only about ten minutes had passed. Time, in the gap where she was dragged forcibly into the "past" and then sent back, seemed to have been compressed, stolen, and then returned with precision. She had a vague sense that each "stay" in the past was growing shorter—whether because the rising number of crossings had bred some tolerance between her body and that uncanny mechanism, or because the "heart-guarding" jade she wore against her skin was truly, quietly working some effect she couldn't comprehend.
She didn't get up right away, holding instead that slightly reclined posture, sitting before the mirror. The clamor of the banquet outside the restroom was shut out by the heavy door, leaving only a faint, muffled swell of sound, like the background hum of another world.
In her mind, everything she'd just "witnessed" replayed clearly. Zhuge Jinye's calm, ruthless face; her own thirty-six-year-old self with her angry, wounded eyes; all of it about investment, returns, human nature, and betrayal...
Self-interest.
The word fell like a cold stone into the lake of her heart—stirring not a raging wave, but ring after ring of a heavy, spreading ripple, a ripple about the word "destined."
She had thought that by crossing through time and warning her past self to steer clear of the "Godmother of Feelings" path—glamorous on the surface but standing forever in the crosshairs of public opinion—she could give "Yao Yuan" a life with fewer hardships and more smooth sailing. But what was the result?
At thirty-six, she had not dissected human hearts in a livestream studio for traffic and controversy—instead she had walked into an even more brutal, more real arena of business rivalry. She had run into the abandonment of a business partner and the naked scramble over interests.
Every wall life had to be hit, she'd hit—not one less. It was only that the wall she struck was not the same wall.
But the roads led to the same end.
It was as if an invisible hand were plucking the strings of fate from behind. No matter which fork she chose, which slope she climbed, the chill she was meant to feel, the lesson she was meant to swallow, the dark side of human nature she was meant to see clearly—it would always wait, quietly, in another form, with another face, at some turning of her life.
It only confirmed further how small an individual is before the vast laws of fate; how unavoidable the price of "growth" is; how "clear-sightedness" itself is a credit of blood and tears, earned through hitting one wall after another and "seeing." You cannot go around the pain—you can only choose the posture in which you bear it, and, having borne it, what shape of awareness and armor you forge it into.
Just as these thoughts surged and ebbed like a tide, closing in around her, there came a soft knock at the restroom door.
"Yao Yuan?" It was Zhao Yiming's voice, coming through the panel with a barely perceptible concern. "Are you all right? You've been in there a long time."
Yao Yuan came back to herself with a start. She closed her eyes, pressing down all the dread surging beneath them—about fate, about unreality—and when she opened them again she had recovered her usual lucidity. She stood, smoothed the slightly creased hem of her dress, went to the sink, dipped her fingertips in a little cold water, and patted it gently around her eyes once more. Then, facing the mirror, she put on a flawless, calm smile.
"I'm fine. Coming out now." She answered aloud, her voice steady.
She turned the lock, and there was Zhao Yiming, standing in the warm yellow glow of the corridor outside. He'd shifted into a more casual stance, but his eyes lingered on her face for an instant—a scrutiny that wasn't suspicion, but a sharp observation born of familiarity.
"Really all right?" he asked, his tone mild, even as he reached out and took, perfectly naturally, the small clutch holding her lipstick and compact from her hand. "You were gone so long, Wang Qian left ahead of us. I was afraid you'd had too much to drink and weren't feeling well."
"I'm not unwell," Yao Yuan shook her head and, just as naturally, slipped her arm through the one he offered, drawing on the steady, real warmth and strength coming from his arm to pull herself fully back to solid ground from that ethereal sense of fate. "Just a little tired. The drink hit me, so I sat and let it pass. It was too stuffy in there."
Side by side, they walked along the thickly carpeted corridor toward the faint swell of sound from the banquet hall. Yao Yuan felt the line of the muscle in Zhao Yiming's arm, caught the faint, clean scent of aftershave on him, heard his steady breathing and the regular fall of his steps.
All of it was so real, so concrete, so consistent with the logic of the physical world.
And yet only minutes ago, her consciousness had still been talking with her past self, witnessing a quarrel about equity and betrayal. Her life held, on one side, the rigorous, verifiable, deducible numerical science and business logic that Zhao Yiming represented, and on the other, the bronze mirror's wholly inexplicable, metaphor-and-pain-laden journeys back through time and space that no existing science could account for.
The mystical and the scientific, the absurd and the everyday, false memory and real touch... so contradictory, so split, and yet so strangely, so seamlessly interwoven in her life.
For one instant, an utterly absurd thought struck her: this world she lived in—was it a true and solid existence, or, as depicted in the American sci-fi series "Westworld," merely a vast, peerless, painstakingly designed virtual theater? And she, and everyone she met, only programmed characters (Hosts) set up by some higher existence—every love and hate, every struggle to grow, every business contest, even the uncanny time-travel, only a long, lifelike "situational experience" being observed?
The thought sent a chill down her spine, but soon a deeper rationality welled up.
Whatever the truth, whether the world was real or illusory, the warmth coming through his arm in this moment was real; the unfinished Mirror Pact was real; the countdown to the ninth time-travel was real; the human scramble over interests she'd just witnessed in the "past" was real; and this man beside her, willing to leave a noisy banquet to come looking for her—his concern, his presence, in the feeling of this moment, was real too.
Perhaps, before the ultimate answer was revealed (if such an answer even existed), the only thing that mattered was to keep walking clear-eyed through this "situation," at once so absurd and so utterly real—to shoulder her choices, to feel the warmth, to solve the problems—whether by using the weapon of the law to fight for her equity, or using what courage remained to wait for and face the final pact by the Yellow River, or trying to believe in and accept a warmth that, even after a rational assessment, another real soul was still willing to hold out to her.
She tightened the hand looped through Zhao Yiming's arm, pressing those nihilistic tremors about the nature of existence deep down into her heart. Ahead, the light and the voices of the banquet hall grew clearer—the world of the "now," the one that still needed her to face it clear-eyed, was waiting there.
