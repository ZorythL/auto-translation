Chapter 47 — Shuai Hongqiang's "Good Luck" Dodges a Deadly Disaster at the Ski Resort

Meanwhile, on the second day of the New Year holiday.

Early that morning, Shuai Wenyao was bouncing around in excitement, pestering Shuai Hongqiang and Lin Wan to take him to the newly opened ski resort. "Dad! Mom! All my classmates have been there. They say it's super fun! Tons of kids from my class went! Let's go too — I want to go today!" The boy's eyes brimmed with anticipation; his holiday craving for fun overrode everything else.

Worn down by his son, and genuinely wanting to take the family out for a change of scenery himself, Shuai Hongqiang agreed. Smiling, Lin Wan began packing the snacks, hot water, and heavy clothes they'd need. The family's destination was a large new ski resort in the outskirts of Jinshi — the facilities were said to be brand new and suited to skiers of every level.

The resort had been built on a sheltered, gentle slope in the rolling mountains at the edge of the city. The heavy snowfall just before New Year's had blanketed the near and far peaks generously. In the city the snow had already melted, but up in the mountains the temperature stayed low, so the white expanse held — perfect timing for skiing. For that very reason, the road to the resort was clogged with traffic, mostly families like theirs out enjoying the holiday.

The road wasn't wide — a two-lane track winding up the mountain. On many shaded stretches, the snow that had melted over the past few days had frozen again, forming patches of slick "ice slides" across the road, glinting dangerously in the sunlight. Traffic crawled along, forcing frequent braking. Shuai Hongqiang drove with extra care, both hands gripping the wheel, fully focused. Beside him, Lin Wan kept saying "slow down." Shuai Wenyao, who'd started out pressed eagerly to the window taking in the silver-wrapped forest, gradually wilted under the long, grinding congestion.

The accident came without the slightest warning.

Right where a long downhill fed into a curve, the cars ahead all flared their brake lights at once, glaring and harsh — followed by a teeth-grating screech of skidding tires and the dull crunch of metal crumpling together! Shuai Hongqiang's heart clenched and he slammed on the brakes, but the car simply couldn't stop on the road's thin coat of clear ice. The tires locked, yet the car slid on like a runaway sled, sliding straight down the slope toward the tangled pileup of bumpers ahead!

"Ahh—!" Lin Wan let out a sharp little scream and instinctively threw an arm across Shuai Wenyao beside her. Shuai Wenyao went white with fear.

Cold sweat sprang out on Shuai Hongqiang's forehead. Less than twenty meters ahead, he could see clearly — the fronts and backs of several cars already twisted and crumpled, debris flying. His own car was sliding unstoppably toward the very heart of that "crash vortex"! In that instant, time seemed to stretch out; countless thoughts flashed through his mind, and he could grasp not a single one.

In that razor's-edge moment, the corner of his eye suddenly caught something to the right — behind the snow-covered brush at the roadside, there seemed to be an extremely narrow side turning! It didn't look like a proper road, more like a dirt-and-gravel track worn in by local villagers heading up the mountain to work — barely wide enough for one car, almost buried in snow, with a few dead branches bowed under the weight of it at the mouth.

Without a moment's hesitation, the survival instinct and years of driving experience drove Shuai Hongqiang into a reaction that was almost pure muscle memory! He yanked the wheel hard to the right, feathering the brake to match, and the capable SUV let out a sharp screech of rubber grinding against ice. The front end shaved past the snowbank heaped at the roadside by a hair's breadth and lurched onto that hidden little track! The car jolted violently, and the sound of the underbody scraping over gravel and snow came up from below.

Almost the very instant his rear bumper cleared the main road — "BANG—!!!" — an even heavier crash boomed out behind them. Back in the lane they'd just left, a seven-seater that couldn't stop slammed solidly into the tail of the car ahead, shoving it forward into the next. The chain of collisions kept spreading wider.

Shuai Hongqiang steadied the car on the narrow track and stopped. The whole family was still shaken, the cabin dead silent — nothing but their heavy breathing and pounding, drum-like heartbeats. He looked back at the main road through the rearview mirror and side window — a scene of carnage. At least a dozen cars were piled into one another nose to tail, some with crumpled fronts, some flipped on their sides at the roadside, shattered glass and parts scattered everywhere. Crying, cursing, and blaring horns all blended together. And the spot they had just left sat right in the middle of that "steel centipede."

If… if his reaction had been a tenth of a second slower, if he hadn't seen that track, if he'd turned the wheel a fraction too late… they would already be part of that heap of scrap metal right now.

"Hong… we…" Lin Wan's voice trembled as she clutched the stunned Shuai Wenyao tightly.

"It's okay, it's okay, we got out of it." Shuai Hongqiang drew a few deep breaths, forcing himself calm, and turned to comfort his wife and son — but his own hands on the wheel were still faintly shaking. That was the body's reaction to surviving a brush with death.

He inched the car carefully forward along the narrow road for a stretch, getting well clear of the accident scene, until he found a slightly wider spot where he could turn around. With some difficulty he swung the car about and drove slowly back the way they'd come, planning to take a detour or just head straight home. The mood for skiing had long since been frightened clean away.

On the drive back, the cabin was silent. Shuai Wenyao sat quietly, no longer making a fuss. Lin Wan's face was still pale. Shuai Hongqiang replayed those harrowing dozen-odd seconds over and over in his mind.

That little track… it had appeared too conveniently. As if it had been prepared just for him. It was so hidden, half-buried under snow and dead branches, yet in that tense moment he had spotted it at a single glance. And the timing had been perfect — five or six cars had crashed up ahead, creating a brief "impact gap" and a stall in speed, while the cars close behind hadn't fully reacted yet. In that lightning-fast window, he'd had both the ability and the room to make the turn. A car earlier, and he'd have been dragged straight into it; a car later, and the space would have been completely sealed off.

This could no longer be explained away as simple good luck. The coincidence was so precise it sent a chill crawling up his spine.

A single thought, like a dark current beneath the ice, rose irrepressibly into Shuai Hongqiang's mind — could this… could this be the work of Yao Yuan's "time-travel" again?

Ever since the day he had "returned" the bronze mirror to Yao Yuan, and then — after bearing his "good luck" a second time — told her she had formed a "Mirror Pact" with the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror and was tied to its time-travel, his heart had been uneasy, and harbored a secret kind of expectation too. He couldn't be sure what exactly had happened to Yao Yuan after the mirror carried her through time, but according to the arcane logic he knew (or thought he knew), the person who bore the great fortune of the "Mirror Pact" would, through the fluctuations of their fate and luck, channel the good fortune and blessings transmuted from the Primordial Purple Energy back — in some indescribable way — to the bearer who had given the mirror.

Was this narrow escape from death a manifestation of that "blessing"? Because Yao Yuan had once again triggered the bronze mirror's "regression" or "resonance," had the formless "good luck" spread out like ripples, granting him, the "bearer," a thread of survival at the critical moment?

The thought sent a chill across his back — yet mixed in with it was a faint, hard-to-name relief and… secret delight. If it really was so, then this mirror, this so-called "Mirror Pact," was even more mysterious than he had imagined, and also… more dangerous. It could bring "good luck," but the price of that "good luck"…? And what state was Yao Yuan in right now?

He glanced at his still-shaken wife and son beside him, his feelings a tangled mix. They had dodged it this time. But what about the next? Was this "good luck" sustainable, or a one-time expenditure? And what, because of it, was Yao Yuan bearing on her end?

The ski trip clearly couldn't go on. Shuai Hongqiang drove his family silently back into the city. Beyond the windows, the desolate winter mountainscape slipped past behind them. On the main road, the sirens and the wail of rescue vehicles could still be heard far off in the direction of the accident. The disaster that had brushed past them became an utterly abrupt and heavy footnote to this New Year holiday.

And the turmoil in Shuai Hongqiang's heart surged far more fiercely and tangled than the scenery outside. His attention to Yao Yuan's situation had grown into something he couldn't quite name, along with a deeper layer of worry. The price of this "good luck," he feared, would sooner or later show itself.

The car slowly left the mountains, the chaos and sirens outside gradually falling away behind them, while the cabin sank into the silence of having survived. Shuai Wenyao must have been frightened, or perhaps sensed his parents' heavy mood; he leaned obediently against Lin Wan and soon, surprisingly, fell asleep, though his brow was still faintly furrowed. Lin Wan patted her son's back gently, her gaze somewhat glazed as she stared at the bare trees flashing past the window, her face still pale.

Shuai Hongqiang drove with concentration, trying to steady his heartbeat and breathing, but that harrowing scene and that "conveniently" appearing track kept flashing back, impossible to shake. Unlike the previous two bouts of "good luck," this time the feeling was so intense that he could no longer convince himself with words like "coincidence" or "a turn in fortune."

The first two times — Ji Wenshan repaying his debt with interest, the city government's overdue payment unexpectedly clearing — had been delightful, even a little uncanny, but deep down Shuai Hongqiang could still find a "reasonable" anchor: that money had been his to begin with. Ji Wenshan's debt — the man's conscience had stirred, or he'd finally come into funds. The municipal project payment — maybe it had simply coincided with some special allocation or a push from above. Their "return" had been sudden, but it hadn't fully departed from how the real world worked — the creditor-debtor relationship was clear, the money was right there; only the timing of its return had been full of unexpected "luck." That kind of good fortune felt more like a justice arriving late, or fate's compensation for his earlier streak of bad luck — even if the timing was suspiciously convenient.

But today was completely different.

This was not the settling of a debt, not wealth lost and found again. This was, on an ice-covered downhill, at the edge of a death-vortex of more than a dozen cars piling into one another, a snow-buried farmer's track that should never have entered his awareness or his line of sight — appearing like a precisely deployed escape exit, at the very instant he most needed it and was the only instant he could possibly use it, "seen" by him and driven into successfully. This was not "taking back what was his." This was, at the brink of life and death, being forcibly "yanked" back by some invisible force from the very claws the reaper had already reached out.

The nature of it was worlds apart.

If the earlier "good luck" still carried a measure of the solid feeling of "things returning to their rightful owner," then this time it was pure, undeniable, beyond-all-reason "intervention" and "gift." And the gift was not money — it was the safety of him and Lin Wan and Shuai Wenyao, all three of them. It was the wholeness of the entire family.

And that gripped Shuai Hongqiang's heart with an enormous unease.

The appraiser Master Chen's aged, grave voice sounded in his ears once more, clearer than ever before: "The giver of the mirror bears its fortune; the time-traveler loses their spirit. As one waxes, the other wanes — this is called 'borrowing fortune.'" "What she loses is her spirit and her lifespan; what you gain is luck and opportunity. This debt… can never be repaid."

"Loses their spirit"… "spirit and lifespan"…

Before, his understanding of those words had been vague — perhaps just mental exhaustion, insomnia, restless dreams, that sort of thing. But today, when this "luck and opportunity" had manifested in such a violent, such a crucial way — directly averting a disaster that could have wrecked the car and killed them all — Shuai Hongqiang suddenly realized just how heavy this "fortune" weighed. And so the "price" Yao Yuan had to pay was surely far beyond anything that could be measured by mere "fatigue"!

This time it had saved their lives. So what was Yao Yuan going through right now? A splitting headache? A sudden fainting spell? Or some deeper, more terrifying drain? The two words Old Chen had spoken — "lifespan" — were like two cold needles driven into his heart. He didn't dare think too hard about it, yet he couldn't stop himself from imagining the very worst.

Cold sweat seeped out again — this time not from fear of what might have happened, but from a complex tangle of relief, guilt, dread, and intense unease. He was relieved his family was safe; that was the most genuine feeling. But hard on its heels came the realization that this safety might have been built on another person's — Yao Yuan's — unknown sacrifice, and that left him sitting as if on pins and needles. He had used (or rather, passively accepted) the "good luck" she might have brought, possibly even at the cost of harming her health, and that awareness bred a powerful moral guilt. The dread, for its part, came from his failure to understand the unknown mechanism and his worry over Yao Yuan's condition — and from the fear of whether he would, in the future, come to depend on, even "crave," this kind of luck.

Human nature is greedy, especially after a taste of something sweet. He was afraid he would gradually grow used to it, even start to look forward to this "good luck" — and that would undoubtedly push Yao Yuan into ever greater danger.

He glanced at his son sleeping soundly beside him and his still-shaken wife. Protecting them was his responsibility; he could not bring himself to regret the narrow escape just now. But the "price" of this safety pressed down, heavy, on his conscience.

The car pulled into the city, and the familiar streets and traffic brought back some sense of reality — yet the shadow over Shuai Hongqiang's heart only grew heavier. He took out his phone, his finger hovering for a long while over the name "Yao Yuan" in his contacts. He wanted to ask how she was, whether anything felt wrong. But on what pretext? Was he going to say, "I nearly had a car crash today, saved only by the good luck you maybe paid for with your own spirit — are you all right?" It was too absurd, and too cruel. He couldn't explain why he was linking two seemingly unrelated events, and he didn't dare say it either.

In the end, he set the phone down, dejected. A sense of helplessness and a deeper worry spread through him. The riddle of the bronze mirror, the truth of "borrowing fortune," Yao Yuan's present state — they tangled in his heart like a knot of thread. He realized this could no longer go on in such murky uncertainty. He couldn't keep enjoying the "good luck" that (very likely) Yao Yuan was trading some price for, while pretending he knew nothing of her plight, even secretly counting himself fortunate.

He needed to find Old Chen and get clear on what this "Mirror Pact" really was — how it worked, what exactly the price was, and most important of all, how to end it, or at least how to stop her from continuing to be harmed.

The ski trip had ended in a fright, but Shuai Hongqiang knew that another ordeal — one bound up with conscience and unknown risk — was only just beginning. He drove the car into the underground garage of his own residential complex and parked. Watching his wife and son get out, he told himself silently: he had to do something. For his family's safety, and for… that ever-heavier unease in his heart.

Shuai Hongqiang went straight to the Guangu Hall to find Old Chen, only to be told by the shop assistant that Old Chen had indeed gone south for the winter. He'd be back when spring came. He hadn't expected it himself — that the lie he'd casually made up at the time, about not wanting Yao Yuan to come into contact with Old Chen so soon, would actually come true.
