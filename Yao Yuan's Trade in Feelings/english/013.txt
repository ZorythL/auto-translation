Chapter 13 — The Secret of the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror: Reversing Time, Turning Fortune
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640260.html

Cold wind hit him in the face as he stepped out of the building. Shuai Hongqiang started the car and pulled into the thin Saturday-morning traffic of a winter weekend. He switched on the car radio, then switched it off again. It was too quiet—quiet enough that he could hear his own heartbeat.
He had bought that bronze mirror eight years ago, at the winter auction held by "Yaji Studio." Back then the auction house's white-haired old appraiser had presented the lot himself, but when it came to introducing it, he had been oddly vague.
"This is a Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror. The patterning is clear, the patina natural, though the edge is damaged." The old appraiser adjusted his glasses and paused for a moment. "This piece has years on it. Its collector must be sincere of heart."
Someone had laughed and asked, "Old Chen, is there some story behind this mirror?"
The old appraiser had given the man a long, deep look. "Old things have souls of their own. They only ask to be treated well."
At the time, Shuai Hongqiang had taken it for the auction house's usual sales patter. Now, thinking back, it seemed there had been something hidden in that look on the old man's face—words he had started to say and swallowed.
If… if the mirror really worked, then that old man surely knew something.
The antiques district was in the western part of the city, and on a weekend morning it was still quiet. Shuai Hongqiang parked and walked straight to "Guangu Hall." Beneath a wooden plaque, warm yellow light glowed behind the glass door. He pushed it open, and a wind chime sounded softly.
The shop was very still, only a young assistant wiping down a curio shelf. Seeing a customer, the assistant looked up. "Is there something you'd like to see, sir?"
"I'm looking for Old Chen—is Master Chen in?"
The assistant sized him up. "Master Chen is in the back tidying the storeroom. One moment, please."
Shuai Hongqiang stood at the counter, his gaze drifting over the porcelain vases and jade pieces on the shelves. Everything here carried the weight of accumulated time; even the air was thick with the smell of old wood and paper.
About ten minutes later, the curtain at the back parted, and a white-haired old man came out. It was the old appraiser, Master Chen. He wore a dark grey Chinese-style jacket and a pair of reading glasses, and when he saw Shuai Hongqiang, he gave a slight start.
"It's you." Master Chen's memory was excellent. "The gentleman who bought that Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern Mirror eight years ago."
"You have a good memory, Master Chen." Shuai Hongqiang stepped forward. "I've come today to ask about that mirror."
Master Chen gave him a long, deep look, said nothing, and turned to gesture him inside. Beyond the curtain was a small tea room, with a purple-clay tea set arranged on a rosewood table. Master Chen sat down behind the table and began heating water and rinsing the cups.
"Sit."
Shuai Hongqiang sat down across from him. The water came to a boil, and Master Chen warmed the cups, took out the leaves, and brewed the tea, his movements unhurried. The fragrance of the tea slowly filled the room—the rich, mellow scent particular to aged pu'er.
"That mirror," Master Chen said at last, pushing a cup in front of Shuai Hongqiang. "You gave it away?"
Shuai Hongqiang's hand jerked, and the tea nearly spilled. "How did you know?"
"A guess." Master Chen lifted his own cup and breathed in its scent. "That mirror is no ordinary thing. When you won it that year, I wanted to warn you, but every trade has its rules—some things can't be spelled out. Over the years, I've wondered now and then where that mirror had ended up."
Shuai Hongqiang gripped his cup. "Master Chen, that mirror… what exactly is special about it?"
Master Chen raised his eyes to him, the gaze behind his lenses sharp as a hawk's. "After you gave it away—did something happen?"
Silence spread through the tea room. The din from outside the window drifted in faintly, only making the room feel quieter still.
"This morning," Shuai Hongqiang heard his own voice come out dry, "I received a debt that had gone eleven years unpaid—five hundred thousand." He went on to mention the mirror's original owner—Yao Yuan—and the prophecy a feng shui master had made years ago.
"And then you thought of this mirror." Master Chen nodded slowly when he had finished. "Eight years ago, when you won it, I saw that your eyes were clear—you only wanted to win a beautiful woman's smile—so I said nothing more. Now that you've come to me, you must have a suspicion."
With that he let out a long sigh, his fingers tapping idly on the rosewood tabletop. "So it's true… that mirror has, after all, passed into the hands of the 'one destined to bear it.'"
Shuai Hongqiang's throat bobbed. "Master Chen, please tell me plainly. What exactly is strange about this mirror? This talk of borrowed fortune—can it really be true?"
Master Chen didn't answer directly. He rose and went to the depths of a locked bookcase behind him, taking out a plain, antique wooden box. He opened it; inside was a parchment booklet carefully bound with silk thread, the pages scorched yellow and brittle, the corners badly worn.
"This is a damaged scroll handed down through generations of my school," Master Chen said, opening the booklet carefully. "It records certain… extraordinary old objects. The bronze mirror you won is among them." He turned the pages gently and pointed to one of them.
Shuai Hongqiang leaned in to look. On the yellowed page, a bronze mirror had been rendered in fine, meticulous brushwork—almost identical to the one he had bought. Beside the image ran dense columns of small regular script, the ink faded but still faintly legible.
"In the Tianbao years of the Tang dynasty, there lived a magus named Lingxuzi, deeply versed in astrology and the principles of yin and yang, and master of the art of reading the heavens. On the Kunlun Ruins he once glimpsed the breath of the cosmos at its first opening—that wisp of primordial chaos-energy, condensed yet undispersed, hidden within the marrow of stone, which he gathered and stored by secret art. Reading the omens of disorder in the stars, he knew the golden age was about to fall. Having also chanced upon a strange bronze fallen from beyond the heavens, he took that mystic bronze for its frame and, expending the strength of his whole life, smelted that wisp of primordial energy into it, casting three 'Time-Reversal Mirrors,' each concealing the art of 'turning time and changing fortune.'" Master Chen's aged fingers traced over the characters, his voice low. "The mystic bronze locks the energy in; the talismanic array channels the fortune. The roots of this mirror were sunk, from the very start, into the primordial vein of energy from before heaven and earth divided. The power of this mirror lies not in reflecting form, but in… reflecting fortune, reflecting time."
Master Chen went on, his gaze sweeping over the faint ring of cloud patterns on the mirror's back in the diagram. "Look at this outermost band of patterning. It seems like ordinary cloud ornament, but in truth it is the 'Primal-Drawing Array,' designed solely to lock in the primordial energy. That energy is by nature without fixed form or fixed course—the very root of chaos—yet it can transform, following the human heart and the turning of worldly affairs, into ten thousand possibilities. This is the foundation of 'turning time and changing fortune.'"
"Reflecting time?" Shuai Hongqiang's heart lurched.
"Just so." Master Chen nodded, pointing to several deeply hidden nodes within the patterning on the mirror's back. "Look here, and here… The lines of the pattern secretly accord with the constellations of the celestial round, and within them are embedded two ancient talismanic arrays that generate and draw upon each other. One is the 'Lure of Time's Reversal,' the other the 'Pivot of Fortune's Shift.' Both the 'Lure of Time's Reversal' and the 'Pivot of Fortune's Shift' draw their source from the primordial energy. When the 'Lure of Time's Reversal' meets the intense, regret-laden longing of one born under the 'Nine-Purple Departing-Fire' fate, it can stir the property of 'time-reversal' contained within the primordial energy, drawing that person's spirit briefly back to some important moment in their life. It might be the moment before a major choice, or the moment a regret took place."
"Briefly drawn back? Sent back?" Shuai Hongqiang licked his dry lips.
"It is not the whole body crossing over, nor a lasting stay," Master Chen explained. "According to the scroll, the mirror-bearer's primary consciousness—or 'spirit-mind'—temporarily returns into their past self. Not for long: perhaps only a few hours, or a day or two. During that time, the returned 'spirit-mind' knows the future, and can guide and warn the past self, telling them the 'correct path.'"
Shuai Hongqiang felt his throat go dry. "Then… does the past self listen? Can fate be changed?"
"Hard to say." Master Chen shook his head. "The scroll is vague on this. The 'spirit-mind' returns like an onlooker entering a dream to guide their own self, but whether the past self grasps it, whether it obeys, and how the track of fate truly shifts afterward… these are all variables. Perhaps small pits and bumps can be avoided; perhaps the larger tide cannot, in the end, be turned. But whatever the case, once this 'reversal and guidance' takes place…"
He raised his eyes and looked at Shuai Hongqiang, his gaze deep and dark. "According to the record—" Master Chen turned to the next page, which held a more intricate analysis of the talismanic script, the channels of energy traced in cinnabar like a coiling dragon. He slowly spoke the most crucial part: "The instant the 'Lure of Time's Reversal' is triggered, the 'Pivot of Fortune's Shift' is set off as well. Every attempt the mirror-bearer makes, in the past, to intervene in their own fate—whether it succeeds in the end or not—is regarded by this array as a 'disturbance and refinement of an already-fixed fortune.' And this purer 'fortune-energy,' produced by the disturbance, is drawn by the 'Pivot of Fortune's Shift' into the mirror, to fuse and be refined together with the primordial energy—like melting scattered scraps of silver foil into a single solid ingot…"
"The primordial energy can take in all things, and can transform all things. The fortune-energy it refines becomes purer still. And you—you were the one who gave the mirror, and at the moment you gave it, your heart held the thought of borrowing its fortune to change your own straits—even if that thought was weak at first, only a fleeting 'let's see if it works.' The moment that thought arose, it became a 'pact' linking you to the primordial energy within the mirror, making you the receiving end of this chain of 'fortune-shifting.' Each time a person of that particular fate reverses time and guides their past self once more, the refined fortune-energy peels away from the primordial energy along this pact and flows instead to you—and your fortune in the present improves by a measure. Wealth, gain, opportunity—all become possible."
"Then what about Yao Yuan? What happens to her? If she keeps 'going back' like this, does it harm her?" Shuai Hongqiang asked urgently, guilt surging up in him like a tide. When he first gave the mirror, his feelings had been complicated, but he had never imagined placing her in so strange and dangerous a position.
"The scroll gives a warning: 'Let the spirit cross too often, and its root is easily damaged; fortune's shift is fickle—bear its fruit with care.'" Master Chen's expression was grave. "It means that crossing the barrier of time and space too frequently wears down the very mind and soul of the mirror-bearer; do it too many times, and the consequences are unforeseeable. And the one who borrows the fortune seems to reap without sowing, but the fortune they receive comes from disturbing and refining another's fate—it is unstable by nature, and entangled in cause and effect. Take the benefit, and you must bear the matching fruit. When that retribution will come, and in what form, no one can say."
At last Shuai Hongqiang understood where that five hundred thousand had come from—this money was the return on Ji Wenshan's ten years of struggle, and it was the "fortune" the bronze mirror had drawn off and converted when Yao Yuan "reversed time" in an attempt to change her own course. Without the fortune the mirror had "borrowed," there was no telling when Ji Wenshan would ever have repaid the money.
This was the first "fruit" he had received.
"Master Chen, what should I do now? Take the mirror back? Destroy it?" Shuai Hongqiang's voice was tight.
"The thought of borrowing fortune has already taken root in you, and the mirror-pact is formed. Even if you take it back, the borrowing of fortune will go on—only perhaps more slowly." Master Chen closed the booklet. "The pact is made; forcing it back or destroying it may bring disaster. Especially for the mirror-bearer: if her 'spirit-mind' happens to be in the midst of a reversal, moving the mirror rashly could keep her consciousness from returning to its place properly—and then there would be real trouble."
He looked at Shuai Hongqiang, a trace of pity in his eyes. "For now, you might first watch and wait, but remember two things. First: every stroke of good fortune you gain comes from disturbing the track of her fate. It is not rightfully yours; to use it you must feel shame, and you cannot enjoy it in peace. As the mirror keeps turning and you gain more, the greater the backlash if you cannot keep the energy in balance—everything you've borrowed will be lost in ways you cannot foresee. You can only pray that heaven and earth keep their balance, and the backlash is not too severe. Second: watch her condition closely. If she mentions 'reversals' more and more often, or shows signs of mental disturbance, you must find her at once and bring her to me. Perhaps… there is some other remedy, but it will take careful thought."
"Master Chen, since you knew how dangerous this mirror was, why didn't you stop me back then?"
"I dropped a hint." The old man smiled bitterly. "That line—'its collector must be sincere of heart'—was already breaking the rules. The auction house's code forbids us from openly stating whether a piece is good or ill; it all rests on the buyer's eye and fate. And besides…" He paused. "Back then I read your face, and you were no greedy or wicked sort. I thought you might only be collecting it, so I didn't press to stop you. Who knew that, eight years on, this thought would stir in you."
The clamor of the antiques district drifted in through the window—all those people haggling, appraising, turning pieces over in their hands. In Shuai Hongqiang's ears now, it sounded so distant, so unreal.
As he left "Yaji Studio," Shuai Hongqiang's steps felt unsteady. The winter sun fell on him, but carried not the slightest warmth.
He climbed into the car and didn't start it right away, just staring blankly ahead. His phone screen was lit, showing his WeChat conversation with Yao Yuan. His fingers hovered over the keyboard, typing words out and deleting them again.
In the end, he sent only a single line: "Yao Yuan, about the mirror I gave you yesterday—if you feel anything is off, or… if you have any very strange dreams, tell me anytime."
He waited five minutes, but no reply came from her.
Shuai Hongqiang looked at the WeChat conversation, his heart a tangle of mixed feelings. She still had no idea that she had become the heart of a strange "fortune-shifting." And he, the one who had first given the mirror, was torn between conscience and greed within, even as he watched, helpless, the bronze mirror raise a dangerous, alluring bridge between his fate and hers.
At the far end of the bridge lay her past, and a track that might be rewritten.
At this end of the bridge lay his present, and this endless, scalding "good fortune."
And this, perhaps, was only the beginning.
Just then his phone buzzed—a WeChat message from Lin Wan: "Wenyao says he misses you and wants to know when you're coming home. I've kept lunch for you."
He typed back: "Soon."
Shuai Hongqiang gathered himself, and drove home.
