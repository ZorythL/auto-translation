The sea is only a bigger fish tank.

Let me share a little fable with you all—Pada in the fish tank.

The cook lifted out a live, thrashing fish, stunned it with a single blow, split it open along the spine, stripped away the plump flesh, and scooped out the guts to make a delicacy. Even after the meat had been plated and set on the table, the discarded fish head kept opening and closing its mouth, a last flicker of life clinging to it. One of the diners, struck by a passing whim, stuffed a cigarette into the gasping mouth and laughed as he watched.

The fish left in the tank witnessed the bloody slaughter with their own eyes. The terror sank into their bones, and over time they worked out a rule for survival: whenever a customer came to pick a fish, the whole school would flip belly-up and play dead. Stale, dead fish don't get chosen. By feigning death, they managed to scrape out a wretched existence in the cramped little tank.

One day, a fisherman hauled up a swallowfish from the deep sea, named Pada. The instant it was dropped into the sealed tank, it began hurling itself wildly against the glass walls. Its tank-mates had all been farm-raised from birth, long accustomed to a life in captivity, and they shot mocking looks at this fish flinging itself at the walls. A kindhearted little yellow fish swam to Pada's side and gently tried to talk sense into it: from ancient times to now, no captive fish had ever left the tank alive, and pointless struggle only invited a death sentence.

Pada refused to accept its fate, and went on battering the glass day after day. As it threw itself against the wall, a diner by the window spotted the lively, leaping Pada and immediately told the boss he wanted it made into sashimi. The net went into the water—but the boss changed his mind at the last second and scooped up a fish that had flipped over and was playing dead. In the boss's eyes, the dying, unsellable fish should be dealt with first, to keep his losses down.

Pada narrowly escaped, and watched helplessly as its companion was butchered and carved up, the leftover flesh and blood tossed into the scrap bucket. The bloody scene shattered Pada's last defenses, and it threw itself at the glass more frantically than ever. The violent commotion roused the Flatfish, which lay hidden in the dark at the bottom of the tank. The Flatfish was the ruler of the whole tank—lurking in the shadows by day, patrolling its territory by night. It was the Flatfish that had taught the school to play dead to survive, and by that rule it held together the cold, cruel order of the tank.

Pada had broken the school's shared code of survival and provoked the Flatfish. With a sudden flick of its tail, it whipped Pada into unconsciousness. By the time Pada came to, a strange carp had been thrown into the tank. The carp fled in panic, crying out to the surrounding fish for help, but its former companions sneered and worked together to shove it toward the dead corner where the Flatfish lay hidden. Once the Flatfish had finished its meal, the carp—now nothing but a blind, mangled husk—was spat back out, and the rest of the school swarmed in to share the leftover flesh.

Huddled in its corner, Pada turned utterly cold inside. Only then did it understand: the deadly danger never came from the humans outside the tank alone. The companions it lived with day and night hid murder in their hearts too. It noticed that the carp had been dropped in from above the tank, and seizing a moment when no one was watching, it flung itself up with all its strength and leapt out of the tank. The fish inside stared at it in shock. Pada writhed across the floor, dragging itself toward the sea, freedom seemingly within reach—but the exhaustion of being out of water overwhelmed it, and it fainted on the ground. In the end the boss found it and casually tossed it back into the tank.

These repeated escape attempts thoroughly enraged the Flatfish. By the rules of the tank, anyone who broke the rules had to face an interrogation, and a wrong answer meant having their tail bitten off. The question was unexpected: how many legs does a starfish have? Pada, born in the deep sea, knew the answer well—but in a flash it understood that whether the answer was right or wrong didn't matter at all. The boss had never wanted the correct answer; what it wanted was authority and face that no one was allowed to insult.

As the words fell, the school swarmed in to beat Pada. At the critical moment the little yellow fish stepped forward and held them all back. Little Yellow had been bullied for a long time and had grown used to swallowing every blow, but the boundless, unfettered sea that Pada spoke of had planted a seed of longing for freedom deep in its heart. From the shadows, the Flatfish watched the scene in silence, full of a desolate sorrow—no one knew its past.

Many years before, when the tank's business was slow, the Flatfish had depended on a mate that had come from the deep sea. The mate often described the vastness of the sea to it, and taught it to play dead to survive, and the two of them swore they would seize their chance to escape and make for the open water. But disaster struck suddenly: the mate was chosen by a customer and met its end, gutted and turned into sashimi. After witnessing its beloved die so cruelly, the Flatfish's nature twisted completely. It no longer hoped for freedom, convinced that a safe, wretched survival was the only way to go on living, and from then on it ruled over the whole tank, stamping out any wild dream of escape.

After lying low for a long time, Pada seized the gap while the boss was changing the water and leapt out of the tank together with Little Yellow. The two strained with everything they had toward the shore, freedom right before their eyes. A passing woman accidentally stepped on Little Yellow's tail, and Pada stopped, unwilling to abandon its companion—and in the end both fish were thrown back into the cage.

Having failed to escape, Pada did not give up. In the dead of night it slipped into the king crab's tank, hoping to use the crab's hard pincers to smash the glass and flee together. But the king crab, kept in captivity for years, had long since had its edges ground smooth and resigned itself to its fate. Not only would it not help—it slashed Pada savagely with its claws instead. Covered in wounds, Pada was scooped up again by a passing brat of a child and dumped into an ornamental aquarium, its road ahead growing dim once more.

And just then, a mischievous child spotted the wounded Pada, fished it out, turned, and threw it into the ornamental tank.

It turned out there was a pecking order here too. Looking at this battered creature before him, the clownfish wore a sour expression. Holding himself to be a noble sort, he launched straight into a tirade against Pada. By now Pada was starving, and faced with the endless taunting, it furiously swallowed the clownfish whole in one bite, then unleashed a frenzied massacre—until it was wounded by a toy sword in the tank, and only then did it slowly come back to its senses. Afterward the boss returned Pada to the original fish tank, and the sight before its eyes drove it out of its mind entirely. There was Little Yellow, pinned beneath the Flatfish, a mangled mess of flesh and blood, only half a body left. As it turned out, just a few minutes earlier, Little Yellow had seen that Pada still hadn't come back, and worried for its safety, had ignored everyone's objections and slipped into the king crab's tank to look for Pada. But it wasn't so lucky—before long Little Yellow had been stabbed until its flesh was torn open, and by the time the boss brought it back, it was already a corpse. The friends who had once shared its hardships immediately crowded in, eager for a feast—after all, alive it had been a friend, but dead it was just a piece of meat. Yet the Flatfish, normally so cold and merciless, did the opposite: it shielded Little Yellow beneath its body. But Pada, not knowing the truth, saw this scene and assumed the Flatfish had killed Little Yellow, and rushed in to fight it to the death. The Flatfish only dodged and did not strike back. Just then a customer came in from outside, and this time the customer asked specifically to eat the Flatfish. Having watched countless companions be butchered, the Flatfish never imagined that one day it would be its turn. It lay on the cutting board, surrounded by the corpses of its own kind—steamed ones, braised ones, ones ground into paste—and broke into a cold sweat of terror. But just as the boss was about to bring down the knife, the customer changed his mind at the last second and ordered the smoked fish instead. And so the Flatfish was returned to the tank—but Pada met with disaster. Pada, who would not bow to fate, was in the end set upon the dining table and turned into a tasty plate of sashimi. The customer stuffed a cigarette into its mouth and laughed at it without restraint—and at the longing for freedom buried deep in its heart. Then, on the second day, while the boss was cleaning the tank, the Flatfish flung itself up with reckless abandon and, copying the way Pada once had, dragged itself toward the sea one inch at a time. The Flatfish reached the shore with the very last of its strength, freedom seemingly within reach—but the boss suddenly stepped on its tail and grabbed it tight in his hand. And just then, the Flatfish's mouth spat out a little sword—the very one that had wounded Pada before. The boss recoiled in pain and flung the Flatfish away with both hands, and it landed right in the water. And so the Flatfish, carrying Pada's unfinished last wish, swam with all its might toward the depths of the sea, and at last won its freedom. But no one noticed that the boss was looking out at the sea ahead with a grin—because the sea the Flatfish had leapt into was, in fact, a fish pond he had fenced off himself. And even if it really were the open sea, what difference would it make? It seems that escaping the tank means winning freedom, but only after leaping into the sea do you discover that the sea is only a bigger fish tank. There, it is still the strong preying on the weak, the survival of the fittest—nothing has changed at all. Only the boss is different.
