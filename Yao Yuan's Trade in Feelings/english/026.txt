Suddenly she let out a very faint, very cold laugh.
"Do you understand?" Lin Wan's gaze turned abruptly sharp. "What a 'perfect' division of labor. The wife is the 'feeling,' the 'home to return to,' the cornerstone of 'social judgment.' And those girls are the 'emotion,' the 'dark side,' the 'relationship on the phone.' A complete, internally consistent ethical system, serving certain elite men. The comments were drowned in fury and grief:

"Why he'd learned a more 'mature' way to apologize and show tenderness — because it made the container called 'feeling' more solid and comfortable, made the 'home to return to' more worth maintaining and investing in. The comments finally began to scroll, dense and packed, full of the cries of women suddenly seeing it clearly:

Lin Wan's tears slid down without a sound, but she didn't wipe them away, letting them run across her calm face.
"In their secretly running elite-male ethical system, we wives are no longer simply their partners. We've become the professional managers of their 'feeling' division, responsible for maintaining its stable operation and growing its assets. And those girls have become disposable, single-use 'consumables' on their 'emotion' supply chain, replaceable at any moment. We and those girls seem to be on opposite sides, but in truth we're both trapped in the same cruel order that upholds the appearance of a 'perfect marriage' — fighting on different battlefields, in the same war that can never be won by either side."

"Back then I thought it was possessiveness, insecurity. But now I understand completely — that was him doing everything he could to protect the exclusivity and purity of his 'feeling' division. His 'feeling' container has to look and feel absolutely loyal and clean, because that is his 'moral certificate' in society's eyes and in his own! The cleaner and more unimpeachable that certificate, the more guilt-free and righteous he can feel when he 'outsources' his 'emotion' elsewhere!"
The comments scrolled wildly:

"Because this order is too powerful, its force is everywhere. It wraps you in comfortable material security, soothes you with the social approval of a 'complete family,' persuades you with 'at least he takes responsibility for the family,' 'compared to others he's already pretty good.' It can even manufacture an exquisite illusion — making you feel that you're special, that you're the one finally chosen, that you're the precious 'feeling' in his world, not the cheap 'emotion.'"
"Do you know?" Lin Wan leaned forward, her eyes still red, but her gaze like a cold star. "The most terrifying, most helpless part is that, after years of steady soaking, a piece of this order has quietly become internalized in my own thinking. I'll instinctively protect the dignity of the 'rightful wife' and the posture of the 'winner,' I'll unconsciously weigh the cost and benefit of 'blowing it open,' I'll shrewdly calculate the total loss after 'tearing off the mask.' And on countless nights, I've even used this system's own talking points to comfort myself — 'at least he knows what matters, he only plays with 'emotion' and doesn't touch 'feeling'; at least this family is still whole.'"
She raised a hand, lightly covered her face, and her shoulders trembled almost imperceptibly — the enormous swell of emotion she was forcing down.
After a long while she lowered her hand and lifted her head. The tear-tracks were still there, but her eyes were now a sheet of cold, clear clarity.
"After that, Hongqiang and I had another long talk that lasted all night," Lin Wan said, her voice rough with exhaustion yet utterly resolute. "I didn't cry or make a scene, didn't threaten, didn't use any emotional approach. I just calmly asked him: In this order you've built, what exactly am I? The manager of your 'feeling' division, or a flesh-and-blood partner who deserves to be treated as an equal?"
"He was silent for a long time, long enough that I thought there'd be no answer. Then he said: You're my choice. Not out of pure morality, not just out of duty — it's because… I can only imagine growing old with you. With anyone else, it wouldn't work."
"I looked into his eyes and said: Good. Then from today, I'm going to redefine this 'choice' you speak of."
"First, those friends of yours who practice and flaunt this 'emotion-feeling dichotomy' — I don't want to see them at our private gatherings anymore. My social circle needs a level of cleanliness. Second, from today our family finances must be completely transparent, and I want real joint management and decision-making power, not just the right to be informed. Third, and most important," Lin Wan said, word by deliberate word, perfectly clear, "if in the future I discover you've got any 'relationship on the phone,' I won't cry, I won't make a scene, I won't give you any trouble at all. I'll simply make you pay the price in the way you care about most — you'll lose a sizable portion of the wealth you treasure, and the social reputation you've worked so hard to build. Not because I'm more noble than you, but because I want you to understand completely: I haven't just seen through the rules of your game — I'm also capable of playing this game by your own rules."
Facing the camera, Lin Wan suddenly smiled. This time it was a real smile, glistening with tears, yet full of the strength of something breaking out of its cocoon.
"He said: Good."
"So now, after that reshaping a few years ago, our marriage has entered a new, even stranger phase of 'stability.'" Lin Wan's tone carried a complicated sarcasm. "I'm no longer just the clueless head of the 'feeling' division. I've become Hongqiang's partner — a partner who knows clearly all his rules and dark sides and still chooses to cooperate. Hongqiang knows I've seen through everything; I know Hongqiang knows I've seen through it. On that basis, we've rebuilt a dangerous, fragile balance."
"It sounds pretty pathetic, doesn't it? But this is the most real reality I'm facing right now." Lin Wan's voice was soft, yet it landed heavily on every heart. "When a marriage reaches a certain point, maybe it stops being a fairy tale and becomes two adults who, after seeing through every one of each other's calculations, selfishness, darkness, and limitations, still decide — within this order that isn't beautiful, that's even cruel, to use the chips each of them holds and renegotiate a set of 'terms of cooperation' most favorable to themselves."
"Sisters, if you too feel suffocated and confused in a marriage like this, I want to tell you: seeing the order is the first step to breaking it — or at least to negotiating with it. If, after weighing it, you're willing to keep serving as the manager of the 'feeling' division, then you must know that you have the right to demand corresponding 'equity' and 'dividends,' not just a fixed 'salary.' If you're not willing, you always have the right to 'cash out and walk away.' But whatever you do, don't be the player who walks in clueless, not even knowing the rules of the game, and ends up losing everything."
The comments fell into deep reflection and debate:

"I also want to break down this 'dualism of emotion and feeling' for every girl who meets a so-called 'gentle older man' or 'mature entrepreneur' on a dating app," she said, her tone turning serious and earnest. "Girl, you need to know — the tenderness, patience, and emotional skill at reading a woman's heart that he practices on you will very likely come back doubled, poured into his wife, into building up his family. Your brief doubt about your own worth, your own objectification, may invisibly be reinforcing the seemingly solid marriage another woman — his wife — is living inside. Without knowing it, you've very possibly become the designed, expendable 'emotion supply' link in this oppressive system."
"And once again, I want to say this solemnly to every wife," Lin Wan said, her gaze blazing. "When you feel grateful that 'at least he still knows to come home,' that 'at least he brings the money back,' please stop and think: in this logic, what have you quietly been shaped into? You are the 'gatekeeper' of his social reputation, the 'custodian' of his moral certificate, the 'professional manager' of his 'feeling' division. The 'stability' you've gained may have come at the cost of surrendering part of your dignity and tacitly accepting a certain unequal order."
After the phrase "professional manager" fell, Lin Wan's voice left a long blank. In the livestream there was only the faint crackle of the scented candle's flame, and the tsunami of scrolling comments and gift effects on the screen. Those virtual "rockets" and "castles" kept bursting open, their gorgeous light playing across her profile, calm to the point of being cold. She wasn't looking at the costly tips; her gaze seemed to pierce through the lens and the screen, landing somewhere farther, more empty.
"So, this stream, 'Gentle Awakening' — who exactly is it awakening?" Lin Wan asked and answered herself, slow and heavy. "It's for the girls who think that, by sheer simplicity, kindness, and a naive fantasy about love, they can trade their way to equal sincerity in return — to make them see clearly the real 'price' of 'emotion supply' in a certain game. And it's for myself too — when I sit here and calmly dissect, precisely name, and ruthlessly deconstruct the pain, humiliation, calculation, and gamesmanship I've lived through, I'm no longer just the 'wife' or 'manager' defined by the marriage system. I've taken back a part of the power to define. I've turned my experiences, my marriage, even my husband's 'dark side,' into my material, my content, the cornerstone of my economic and spiritual independence."
"He uses money, social resources, and that secret elite-male ethical order to build his kingdom; I use words, emotional resonance, and a clear-eyed deconstruction of this order — an order that has always been disordered yet has endured for hundreds, thousands of years — to build my own fortress. What we've reached between us is a new balance, an unspoken one, built on mutual insight and mutual check. That's the truest undertone of how we live together, at this stage."
She leaned back slightly against her chair, her posture giving off the weariness of someone who has been through a hard-fought war — and, beneath that weariness, an unmistakable sharpness. "Dear friends, whether the long river of marriage, like the Yellow River, also has nine thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine bends, I don't know. But I do know that every time it flows past a treacherous shoal, every time it passes through a hidden current, the two people in the marriage have to sit down again, review the voyage so far, and negotiate which way to turn the rudder next. So modern marriage may look more and more like a joint venture. There's a clear evaluation of inputs and outputs, a strict risk-control mechanism, shared profit goals — and there may also… be each party's quietly understood 'side circulation.' The surface warmth may be the pleasing decorative figures on a financial report, while what really keeps things running underneath is, more often than not, cold logic and precise calculation."
"Let's stop here for tonight." Lin Wan abruptly ended her outpouring, her tone returning to the gentle steadiness of the stream's early minutes, even carrying a trace of relieved calm. "I'm saying all this not to make you fear marriage or fall into despair. Quite the opposite — I hope you can enter or stay in any relationship clear-eyed, prepared, and with strength. See the rules and you can use them; master the rules and you might even… take part in making new ones. Goodnight. See you next time."
The black "stream ended" notice popped up. In the study, that bright ring of fill light went dark, leaving only the retro desk lamp spreading a small warm-yellow glow. Lin Wan sat quietly in her chair, not moving right away. She watched the last few comments drift across the screen — "Stay strong, Sister Wan!" "Thank you for telling us the truth," "Striving while staying clear-eyed" — her face showing no expression at all. She raised her hand and, slowly and carefully, removed the delicate headset, the motion as gentle as ending a solemn ritual, or like a warrior taking off her armor.
Outside the study door, in the shadows of the living room, Shuai Hongqiang sat on the sofa, having watched the entire stream from start to finish in silence.
He picked up the tea at his side, already gone cold, and took a sip. It was very bitter.
Shuai Hongqiang's feelings about Lin Wan's streaming had always been complicated and conflicted. From thinking at first that it was "fooling around" and "a waste of time," to later feeling it was "embarrassing," "airing the family's business in public," to finally witnessing — and being forced to admit — that Lin Wan's streaming income genuinely supported the family's ever-growing expenses, their son's steep tuition, even part of the capital his business urgently needed for cash flow. When Lin Wan calmly transferred a sum of money to him and said "use this for now," he breathed a sigh of relief, but something inside him twisted awkwardly, a feeling that something wasn't quite right. The gratitude was real, and there was guilt too, but more than that was a hard-to-voice awkwardness at being helped by a woman he'd once looked down on, and a panic at the loss of his sense of control.
Back when Shuai Hongqiang married Lin Wan, it was like bringing a Guanyin into the house. Not only was she pleasant and bright to look at, it felt as if even the Shuai family "bloodline" would be improved by her, lifted up a notch. He was glad to keep her, to give her the best material life, to watch her tend flowers and read the classics with hands that were meant to hold a pen and turn pages — and his heart filled with a strange steadiness and satisfaction, feeling that the money he'd earned, the assets he'd built, the life he led had finally landed somewhere solid, had gained "taste" and "class."
This rule of "the man works outside, the woman runs the home; the man provides, the woman looks up to him" was one Shuai Hongqiang ran very smoothly, and very much enjoyed. He put up the money; she put up "that kind of atmosphere," along with her dependence on him and her admiration. He considered this a perfectly natural balance. Lin Wan loved him — wasn't that how it should be? Maybe he hadn't poured all of a young man's burning love into her the way she had into him, but he'd given her the best material life within his means. Wasn't that enough?
Until tonight, when he heard with his own ears how she took the boasting, vanity-laced "small talk" and "bragging" the men in their circle shared in their idle hours, and with a cold, sharp scalpel dissected it into "a complete, internally consistent ethical system serving certain men." How she pried apart and crushed the thinking he took for granted, the unspeakable "methods" of his business and social dealings, the self-righteous "authority" and "provision" he prided himself on as a husband — and added those terms he found awkward on the tongue yet horrifyingly precise, "the dualism of emotion and feeling," "professional manager," "side circulation," "moral certificate" — turning it all into "material for analysis" and "teaching content," feeding it to tens of thousands of strangers on the other side of the screen.
When he first heard it, Shuai Hongqiang's mind went "buzz" and blanked out. Then came a sharp shame, the feeling of being skinned in public, burning across his whole body. After the shame passed, a nameless rage shot up out of nowhere. He suddenly understood, as if struck by enlightenment, that what he could least stand was perhaps not her complaining about him, accusing him, not even her making those "ugly things" public. It was the detached, specimen-studying calm with which she narrated it all. That clear-eyed, increasingly removed clarity with which she saw straight through him, through the life, the feelings, the gamesmanship they'd built together over so many years.
Shuai Hongqiang could admit to himself that when he first married Lin Wan, "suitability" and "value for money" had weighed more than the burning "love" of a young man (although over the long years of living together, a great deal of warmth and habit he couldn't bear to give up had grown too). But he couldn't accept that Lin Wan seemed to no longer "love" him either, at least not in the familiar way, the love laced with dependence and admiration. And that she could, so calmly, even with a cool scrutiny and a sense of putting it to use, take this shift in feeling, take the power structure and cold calculation inside the marriage, and lay it all out, item by item, in public, as a lecture. It made him feel like a complete fool, seen through entirely and still acting out his own little play. The "good life" he'd once given, the "provision" and "control" he'd believed in without question, had long been clearly converted, in her hands, into "capital" — into the chips that gave her a voice in this "marriage joint venture," into the nerve that let her openly dissect his "dark side" under the spotlight. She no longer needed the light he "gave"; she could generate her own power now, and use that power to light him up, inside and out.
Shuai Hongqiang knew that a new round of negotiation had begun. And the chips in this negotiation were the now-opened eyes of a woman.
Shuai Hongqiang stood up and walked to the closed study door. He paused for a moment before raising his hand to knock, then pushed it open. Lin Wan was tidying up her equipment, her profile serene in the glow of the desk lamp. He walked over and handed her a cup of warm honey water, his face nonchalant, his tone feigning ease: "Tonight's 'tactical analysis' on the stream was pretty deep. How's the 'battle result' assessment? What score do you give our side's performance?"
Lin Wan took the cup, her fingertips brushing the warm back of his hand. She lowered her head and took a sip — the temperature just right, the sweetness just so. Then she lifted her head and looked at him, the same flawless, breezy smile on her face: "The enemy was too strong, the system too complete, the foundation too deep. To have fought to this point, to have won a seat at an equal dialogue, is already no small feat. Tonight, I'd grudgingly call it a draw."
Shuai Hongqiang looked at her and managed a smile: "Admitting it's a draw — that's the most masterful tactic of all."
Lin Wan answered with a faint smile, saying not a word about the heart-stopping dissections from the stream, the cold terminology, the content that had exposed the order inside him to the world. By unspoken agreement, they both stepped around that real "battlefield" tens of thousands of people had just watched live, as if it were nothing more than an ordinary work report from her. The woman who had wept and laughed coldly before the camera, who had torn open the marriage's gorgeous robe with her own hands to reveal the precise, cold gears inside, and this wife now gently tidying her things, were like two completely different people.
Shuai Hongqiang suddenly realized with absolute clarity: she no longer pressed him, no longer argued, no longer even showed anger or sorrow — not because of forgiveness, tolerance, or numbness, but because… she had stepped off the chessboard of their old games. She had become an observer and analyst standing on higher ground — and the architect using all of this to build her own new world.
