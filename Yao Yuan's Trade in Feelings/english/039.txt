Chapter 39 — Do the Wives of Big Shots Always Come to a Bad End?

Friday, one o'clock in the afternoon. Yao Yuan's livestream began right on schedule.
The studio lights were tuned to a cold white, throwing her brows and eyes into sharper, keener relief. The crimson polish on her fingertips trailed a faint afterimage across the screen, like some kind of warning. Outside the window, the rushing of the Yellow River was the only background sound — deep, brutal, impossible to ignore.
Yao Yuan (speaking first, after the faint crackle of the connecting line, her voice carrying a weary certainty): "Hello? Go ahead. Your kid is about three, right? And you've found out your husband either paid for sex, or was sexting, or was tipping some female streamer. You can't decide whether to leave, and you feel like all those years of effort are about to go down the drain, and you can't stomach it. Am I close?"
(A brief silence. The caller's breathing suddenly grew heavier.)
Caller (a woman's voice, fighting to stay composed, but the tremor leaking out at the ends of her words): "…You are. Teacher Yuanyuan. My husband and I, we dated in college, started a company together right after graduation. In the worst times the two of us split one cup of instant noodles. The company's on solid ground now, getting ready to go public. In a good year, the profit is this much (she names a substantial figure). We're both run off our feet, flying all over. After I got pregnant, my health was bad and the baby was fussy, so we started sleeping in separate rooms. In three years, I can count the number of times we've shared a bed on one hand. That one rare time… he asked me how it felt, and I… maybe I was tired, maybe I was just being difficult, and I said 'not great.' I never thought those words would hurt him… Later I took my parents and the baby to the Maldives, and looking at his phone bills, I found that in half a year he'd tipped over four hundred thousand to one entertainment streamer… plus two charges for in-home SPA sessions, the very expensive kind. I pressed him and he admitted it… he'd booked a massage, and with one of them, it happened once."
(Her voice began to break, the sobs she was forcing down bleeding through): "He knelt and begged me. Said it was just the stress, a moment of stupidity, said it would never happen again, said in his heart there's only me and this family… Teacher Yuanyuan, should I believe him? Leave? The company shares, the child, everything we've fought for all these years — how do you tear all that apart? Stay? It disgusts me! Just thinking about it makes my whole body shake! Everyone says men go bad when they get money. Do the wives of big shots… really always come to a bad end? In this world, is there just no such thing as a love that lasts from beginning to end? I don't understand… we came up together out of the hard days, we did…"
(The comments surged: [Holy crap, four hundred grand in tips!] [Once? Who believes "only once"] [The tragedy of a big shot's wife] [What happens to the company, what happens to the kid])
Yao Yuan (did not answer right away. She leaned slowly back, her neck tilting into a fragile arc, her gaze fixed on some empty point on the ceiling. The "tap, tap" of her fingertips started up again, forming a cold duet with the water outside the window. After a few seconds, she sat bolt upright, her eyes spearing toward the camera like an icicle.)
"Good. Done crying? Now, those silly questions of yours — 'is there no love that lasts,' 'do we always come to a bad end' — take them out of your head and throw them away."
"Those questions are a swamp. The more you think about them, the deeper you sink, the faster you die."
"We're going to take this apart one. Piece. At. A. Time."
She raised a single finger, the crimson nail flashing a cold light.
"First. Stop asking 'will he do it again,' and start thinking 'what cards do I still hold.'"
"Your account is full of a 'victim's' grievance and resentment — 'we suffered together,' 'I bore his child,' 'he betrayed me.' Those emotions are one hundred percent valid. But apart from making you suffer and making him feel guilty — and that guilt will burn out fast — what good are they?"
"Your problem right now isn't an emotional problem. It's a major operating crisis caused by a partner's defection. The two of you co-founded a limited liability company called 'marriage.' You're the CEO and majority shareholder, and you produced an important 'new product' (the child). Now your CTO (your husband) has embezzled a huge chunk of the company's cash flow (the tips, the prostitution fees) and tried to use company resources — his body, and possibly your family time — to subsidize external competitors (the streamer, the masseuse). He's damaged the company's core asset, trust, and left you, the CEO, with your management authority and emotional investment wiped out."
"So stop thinking like some heartbroken woman in the inner chambers abandoned by a faithless man. Use the brain you'd use to negotiate a merger, read a financial report, assess investment risk — and use it to assess your marriage!"
(The comments: [The company metaphor is killer!] [Instantly clear-headed!] [Business thinking is the GOAT])
She raised a second finger.
"Second. Settle your 'balance sheet' and your 'negotiating leverage.'"
"1. The asset side: your shared company shares, real estate, investments, cash. Do you know the percentage and source of every single fen? Marital property? Pre-marital contributions? Gifts from parents? Bring out your auditing skills and figure it out, immediately, right now. This is your economic foundation.
2. The liability/risk side: the facts of his cheating (the tip records, the transfer receipts, the possible chat logs, the recording of his admission — if you have one). This is the sword hanging over his head. Then there's your own emotions and health (the cost of continuing to drain yourself), and the child's custody and growing-up environment. This is your core risk.
3. The negotiating leverage: your actual control over the company and how indispensable you are (without you, does it collapse? does the business break down?). The cheating evidence in your hands (is it enough to give you an absolute edge in a divorce suit, or to extract excess gains in a negotiation?). Your ability and willingness to live alone and raise the child without him. This is the source of your power."
Yao Yuan leaned forward, her face enlarged and frigid on the screen.
"Based on all that, I'll give you three plans — upper, middle, lower. Not so you can choose, but so you can see the situation clearly, and then choose only the upper one."
"The lower plan: cry, beg, forgive, and keep limping along."
"You believe his tears and act like nothing happened. The result? Your trust gets a price tag stamped on it — four hundred grand in tips plus one round of paid sex is enough to buy it out. Your bottom line is trampled flat. From now on you'll live in endless suspicion; he'll live walking on eggshells, or grow even bolder. Your marriage becomes a gorgeous coffin, with your dignity and your feelings lying inside it. The company suffers (a feuding boss-couple is a commercial disaster), and the child grows up in a cold, eerie atmosphere. This is slow suicide. The stupidest choice. Rule it out at once."
"The middle plan: driven by emotion, mutual destruction."
"You can't take the disgust, so you divorce, no question. But in the divorce you only think about venting your feelings — the property division is a muddle, the fight over company control wounds you both, and you turn it into a citywide spectacle, letting employees, clients, and the child watch the whole farce. In the end you walk off the battlefield with a sum that looks fair but is actually shrunken, and a self that's exhausted in body and mind. You punished him, but you crippled yourself too. Kill a thousand of the enemy, lose eight hundred of your own. Not smart enough."
"The upper plan: interests first, reshape the whole structure."
"Step one: emotional isolation. Stop asking whether he loves you, whether he regrets it. Treat him as a partner who has developed a serious professional-ethics problem. Your pain, your disgust — digest it behind closed doors on your own, or take it to the best therapist you can find, but do not show him another ounce of it. Your composure is your strongest armor."
"Step two: lock down the evidence and display your strength. Quietly collect and back up everything. At the same time, return to the company in a more engaged, sharper state, making sure the key clients, the core team, and the financial processes are all under your control or clearly understood by you. Let him — and let everyone — see that this family might not work without him, but this company definitely doesn't work without you."
"Step three: lay out your 'terms of reconciliation' (or call them the 'atonement clauses'). Pick a moment when both of you are relatively calm. State it flat and plain, no emotion:
'XX, I know what you did. My feelings about it, I don't want to dwell on. Right now, we're only going to talk about how to solve it. Divorce — for the child, for the company, for our parents, for everything we've built over the years — is a nuclear-level blow. I don't want to go there, unless you force me to.'
'The condition for not divorcing is that our marital relationship, our property relationship, and our family power structure must be completely rebuilt.'
'1. Property restructuring: the money you lost on tips and paid sex gets deducted from your personal share and compensated to me. On top of that, we sign a new marital property agreement that registers XX% of the company shares / XX properties / XX in funds clearly under my name alone — as the penalty for any future wrongdoing on your part, and as security for me and the child. At the same time, all of your financial movements become completely transparent to me.'
'2. Behavioral constraints: sign a fidelity agreement that spells out a punitive payout for cheating again (an amount big enough to hurt). All non-essential socializing gets reported in advance. Family time takes priority — at least X nights a week, you're home before X o'clock. We can try to find a way both of us accept to improve our intimate life, but it has to be built on respect and honesty, not going through the motions.'
'3. Reset of family responsibility: I hold the main decision-making power over the child's education and the family's important decisions. You have to put in real, concrete time on parenting and housework, not just write checks.'
'Accept it, and we go to a lawyer and a notary, then try to repair things. Refuse, or pay lip service while doing otherwise, and we start divorce proceedings immediately — and I will use every piece of evidence I hold to make you pay the biggest price you can imagine, in property and in reputation.'"
Yao Yuan's gaze was sharp as a blade, her voice low but every word weighed a ton:
"Remember, this is not a request. It's a notification. You're not giving him a chance to turn back from the brink. You're giving him a choice: pay an enormous price for the possibility of keeping the marriage alive. If he signs, it means he'll live forever in the shadow of 'cheating costs are sky-high,' while you hold the economic lifeline and the household sovereignty. If he won't sign, you launch the divorce immediately, and on the strength of your evidence and your control, you take an absolute advantage in the property division."
"Finally, to answer those two childish questions of yours."
"Do the wives of big shots come to a bad end? No — people with no independent will, only a dependent mindset, man or woman, all come to a bad end. The wife of a big shot has to first be the 'big shot' of her own life. Your end doesn't depend on whether he goes bad. It depends on whether, when he does go bad, you have the ability to make him pay a price he can't bear, and the nerve to turn and walk away and live better."
"Is there such a thing in this world as a love that lasts from beginning to end? Yes. But it's not a fairy tale. It's a rational choice that two constantly growing, mutually awed adults — who both know full well the devastating cost of losing each other — make together and sustain for a lifetime. Its foundation isn't fragile love; it's a deep entanglement of interests, shared growth in value, and a high cost of breach that neither dares to cross lightly."
"So stop fantasizing about going back to the past. You can't go back."
"What you need to do is take all the composure, nerve, and tactics you had when you built your company, and turn this crisis into a thorough 'marital restructuring' that works in your favor."
"Either you end up with a new 'company' where the shares are reorganized, you hold the lead, and he doesn't dare step out of line."
"Or you walk away with enough 'severance compensation' and 'non-compete damages' to leave with dignity, and use that capital to open the second track of your life."
"Tears and accusations are the most useless things in this world. Only real power, real shares, real agreements, and the ability to turn and leave at any moment — those are your eternal amulet."
After delivering this cold, interest-based analysis, Yao Yuan paused for a moment. The crimson polish on her fingertip rapped lightly against the desktop, giving a crisp sound. Her gaze toward the camera shifted from pure sharpness, taking on a more complex layer — an insight close to compassion.
"Finally, to answer that childish question of yours about a love that 'lasts from beginning to end.'" Her voice slowed a little, yet seemed sharper for it, like a thin, cold scalpel cutting through to deeper tissue.
"Whether such a love truly exists in this world, I can't say for certain. But I do know that marriages which last from beginning to end really do exist. Do you really believe that two living, breathing people — with flesh and blood, flaws and desires — can, over decades of long years, resist every temptation, friction, weariness, and swelling of ego, on nothing but an extremely unstable emotion called 'love'?"
"What fairy tales and idol dramas don't dare tell you is that every marriage that manages to keep going has had problems along the way — has even had affairs (of the mind, or the ⬛⬛). Even the marriage of Yang Jiang and Qian Zhongshu, universally praised as a match made in heaven, that lucid and restrained, that perfectly matched in soul — even they had their bumps and turns, their friction and wavering, let alone us. The difference is that in some cases the problem festers into a sore and finally rots away; while in others, both parties cut it out with enormous wisdom, patience, weighing of interests, even luck — leaving a scar, but the person survives, and the relationship survives too, in a different form."
(She leaned forward slightly, her gaze blazing, as if to burn through every unrealistic illusion.)
"And you — and a lot of women in this stream who are suffering just like you — what's your biggest problem? It's being 'rich, but with no real eyes.'"
"Your 'no real eyes' isn't that you don't understand business. It's that you don't understand real human nature, you don't understand actual marriage. You imagine love as a tower of flawless glass untouched by dust, and treat marriage as a one-time perfect contract. What you love is that symbol of a 'husband who lasts from beginning to end' in your fantasy, the concept of 'fidelity' beautified and purified in novels and films. But you don't actually love — can't love — the concrete, living man who gets tired, gets fragile, runs away, has low base urges, makes mistakes, and may even feel ignored and lonely while you're pregnant."
"You think your 'building a company together, suffering together' is romance. But over the long years, that 'suffering' can curdle into resentment, that 'busyness' can curdle into estrangement, and those nights with no communication can curdle into ice. And you — with 'busy,' with 'tired,' with stubborn words — shut the door on communication and repair. You think you're guarding some kind of 'purity'; in fact you're avoiding the messy, unflattering work of running a real relationship."
"Love the concrete person, not the abstract feeling. The concrete person eats, drinks, and shits, gets bad breath, goes bald, puts on a middle-aged paunch, and under pressure goes looking for crude ways to vent. The concrete person needs to be seen, needed, soothed — not enshrined on the altar of 'meritorious contributor' or 'the kid's dad.' Your husband going to streamers and masseuses is of course his own baseness, his breach, his one hundred percent of the blame. But your 'no real eyes' is that you never truly saw his needs and frailties as a 'concrete man.' You only enjoyed the halo and convenience that came from him as the abstract symbol of a 'successful partner.'"
"So stop dumping all the blame on a shallow excuse like 'men go bad when they get money.' Tragedy is often a conspiracy. His part in the conspiracy was cheating, basely. Your part was being absent, naively. You were absent from the small, concrete work of building feeling between husband and wife; absent from recognizing and guarding against the weaknesses of human nature — until he gave you this bloody lesson in the ugliest way possible."
"Now, what you have to do isn't only what I said earlier — fight for shares, sign agreements, seize the initiative. That's the 'technique' you must master. You also have to learn the lesson of the 'way': let go of your obsession with 'perfect love' and 'absolute fidelity,' and learn how to build, with a concrete and complicated person, a real relationship — one with both solid entanglement of interests and real flow of feeling, one that can share the glory and also have compassion for the frailty."
"If, after all this, all you've learned is how to leash a man with an agreement and how to punish a single mistake with money, then you've only jumped from one kind of 'no real eyes' to another, shrewder kind. True sight is this: after seeing through all the gray and uncertainty of human nature, still having the ability to build a little warmth and certainty; after mastering every weapon to punish the other person, still knowing when not to pull the trigger."
"Whether you choose to use the 'technique' I gave you to balance and negotiate, or choose to leave — that's your road. But whichever you choose, please remember: from now on, love concrete people, handle concrete matters, bear concrete consequences. Stop living in that tower of glass you built out of money and fantasy, the one that shatters at a touch."
"You should grow up — after a price this painful."
(Having said this, Yao Yuan stopped looking at the comments scrolling madly across the screen. She turned her head, gazing out the window at that never-resting river. Her profile, in the cold light of the screen, looked utterly clear and utterly weary. It was the clarity of someone who has seen through all the rules of the game and yet finds no joy in it — left only with a deep fatigue.)
(After delivering her dissection of the "concrete person," Yao Yuan did not stop. She set down the pen in her hand, and the "click" of it rang out strikingly in the silence. Her gaze toward the camera was no longer pure scrutiny now; it was more like a mentor about to lift the final veil on the answer, wearing a complex expression that mixed pity and edge.)
"…You say you love him, that you can't bear to leave behind so many years of effort. But do you know? Your biggest problem, besides the 'rich with no real eyes' I just mentioned, is one even more fatal: you've seen too few men."
(She paused deliberately, letting the line sink into the caller and into every listener.)
"Your life script sounds very inspiring: campus sweethearts, building a company together, going from dirt-poor to financially free. But have you ever thought that this also means your emotional sample size is pitifully thin? In your world, apart from your father, he may be the only 'man' who has ever occupied all of your attention and feeling. You took the love template you carried out of school — untested by the world — and forced it onto a grown man who's been through the cut-and-thrust of business and has seen every kind of desire and temptation. You used the purity of your student days to demand things of a middle-aged man's complexity. That, in itself, is a kind of cognitive mismatch and naivety."
"You don't understand the nature of men." Yao Yuan's voice was very calm, but it was as if she were stating a law of physics. "Or rather, you're unwilling to understand that broader, more real male world beyond your love fairy tale. The nature of men isn't only responsibility, duty, and devotion to family. Written into the nature of men, just as much, are hunting, conquest, possession, vanity, laziness, a craving for fresh stimulation, a need to be worshipped, and the use of the most direct methods (including sex) to relieve pressure and confirm their own existence. These aren't 'evil.' This is a dark part of human nature, code written into the male animal's genes. The only difference is that men with cultivation, with reverence, with higher pursuits, use reason, responsibility, and love to constrain and elevate them; while others let them run wild, and even get devoured by them."
"Your husband is clearly the latter. But that doesn't mean the first man you met represents all of them. You've been trapped inside this 'company' with him too long — so long that you think the world is only this big and men come in only this one kind. You think every problem is 'he doesn't love me anymore' or 'he's gone bad.' You can't see that behind it lies the inevitable collapse of a universal human predicament inside a relationship that lacked effective constraint and nourishment."
"So, back to your question about 'lasting from beginning to end.' What matters isn't whether it exists. It's whether you have the ability — after seeing a person's nature in full, his light and his darkness, his responsibility and his weakness — to still choose to stand at his side, and to build together a set of rules that constrain the darkness and draw out the light. That set of rules includes economic interests, includes emotional communication, and includes a clear-eyed recognition and prevention of human weakness. Before this, you two only had a community of interests; you never built this 'flood-control levee' against human nature."
"When I tell you to love the concrete person, I mean: first go and 'see' this concrete person. See the mediocrity beneath his halo, the anxiety behind his success, the fragility inside his strength, and… the desires and needs he has as an ordinary man — the ones that can't be brought to the table but truly exist. This isn't an excuse for his cheating. It's so you understand what you're facing. If you don't even know the full shape of your 'enemy' (the weaknesses of human nature), how are you going to fight?"
"You feel the sky has fallen right now, because your world has only this one pillar — him. Let me tell you, the world is very big, and there are very many men. Some are worse and more rotten than him; some are more responsible and more restrained than him. Part of your pain comes from his betrayal; the other part comes from your poverty of imagination and the narrowness of your choices. You can't imagine what life could be like without him, because you've never truly broadened your own emotional map and life possibilities."
"So my advice, on top of all the 'technique' (the shares, the agreements), adds one most fundamental 'way': go out, see a broader world, meet a wider variety of people. Not to make you go on dates immediately — to make you first move your eyes off of him. Read books, read history, read psychology, see how human nature repeats its light and its darkness across the long ages. Make friends, befriend men and women of different ages, different classes, different values, and listen to their stories, their desires, their predicaments. Go observe your husband again — not as a 'lover,' but as a 'human specimen' — and analyze the logic of his behavior coldly."
"When you've seen enough samples, you'll know that your husband's cheating is just an ordinary one among countless similar stories. It isn't unique, it isn't forgivable, but it can be analyzed and it can be dealt with. You'll walk out of the 'why me' victim's emotion and into the 'what do I do now' problem-solving mode. You'll understand that running a long-term relationship isn't guarding a fragile dream; it's managing a complex cooperative project in human nature. What you need is wisdom, strength, and maneuvering — not just love and trust."
"Whether you stay and rebuild, or leave and restart, the choice is yours. But whichever you choose, please first free yourself from the narrow world of 'only one man.' Your sight should not be limited by the wealth you possess, and still less defined by one man's betrayal. Only after you've seen the vast ocean will you stop grieving too much over a pond drying up; only after you understand human nature will you stop being caught off guard by a concrete betrayal."
"Now, hang up the phone. Beside the computer where you audit the accounts and draft the agreement, set down a copy of Sapiens or a copy of Intimate Relationships. Treat the symptom, but treat the root too. Your illness is half in him, and half in that field of your own heart that has never truly been opened up. Also, I'd suggest you enroll in our 'Heng-Ha' course. Sex matters a great deal in married life — it's the catalyst of a couple's feelings."
Having finished, Yao Yuan did not wait for any response. She cleanly cut the line. She leaned back against the chair and let out a long, soundless breath, as if those words had cost her enormous strength too. On the screen, her clear and weary face was reflected, along with the Yellow River outside the window — the one that swallows everything, and also gives birth to everything.
