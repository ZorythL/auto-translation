Chapter 38 — Human Nature Is a Balance Sheet That Never Stops Fluctuating

The car headed toward the antiques market. Yao Yuan kept her hands on the wheel, but her thoughts stayed fixed on that Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror—the secret only she and Shuai Hongqiang knew, the three dislocations in space and time that no ordinary logic could explain. This was something she could tell no one. In the world of adults, the value of certain secrets lies precisely in the fact that they are kept airtight.
Her phone rang. It was Shuai Hongqiang. His voice carried its usual note of concern, but Yao Yuan's ear worked like the most precise voiceprint analyzer, and in an instant it caught the faint, unnatural frequency beneath that concern—he was probing.
"Old Chen?" Her eyes stayed on the traffic ahead, her tone as calm as if she were discussing the weather. "Have you heard from him lately?"
On the other end there was a pause, almost too slight to notice. "Old Chen, huh... I think I heard someone mention it—he went south for the winter, didn't he? Right, it's warmer down south. Old folks all like to go."
Yao Yuan said nothing. A lie. She knew the rhythm of a man lying all too well—that deliberately manufactured smoothness only exposed the haste and hesitation underneath. She just hadn't expected Shuai Hongqiang to lie to her, at least not about this.
"Is that so," she said, her tone unchanged. "What a shame, then."
She hung up and set the phone on the passenger seat. The car was quiet, only the faint sound of tires rolling over the road. She didn't immediately dwell on the lie itself; instead, out of habit, she began dismantling the chain of logic behind it:
Behavior: providing false information at a key node concerning Old Chen's whereabouts.
Surface motive: to delay or block her contact with Old Chen.
Underlying assumption: Shuai Hongqiang takes for granted that her contact with Old Chen would be bad for him, or at least would interfere with his plans.
Risk level: provisionally medium. This falls under information isolation and defensive maneuvering. It hasn't yet touched a core conflict of interest, but it marks the point where the trust threshold begins to wobble.
She quickly filed and tagged this assessment in the temporary case folder in her mind, the same way she would handle any instance of concealment between partners in a consulting case. The difference was that this time, she was the party involved.
The GuanguTang storefront was small, heavy with the musty smell of old things. The owner was a middle-aged man, head bent, polishing a jade thumb ring. When Yao Yuan asked after Master Chen Yanqing, he didn't even look up. "Master Chen? His daughter took him south for the New Year. He won't be back until spring."
Yao Yuan stood there without moving.
A sense of absurdity dripped into the deep pool of her consciousness like a single drop of cold water, sending out one faint ring of ripples. To stop her, Shuai Hongqiang had thrown out a casual lie—"gone south for the winter." And that lie had, by sheer coincidence, collided with the truth.
Sometimes life is just like that: the truths you carefully prepare go unbelieved, while the fig leaf you snatch up in a hurry happens to cover the sharp edges of the truth perfectly. An ironic, almost theatrical kind of "correctness."
She turned and left. There was no anger, only a colder clarity—when a coincidence is precise enough to raise suspicion, it usually stops being a coincidence and becomes the surface symptom of some deeper linkage.
Back at the office, her assistant had already left the organized files on her desk. Shuai Hongqiang's credit reports from the past five years, his company's cash flow, his debt breakdown. Yao Yuan put on the "human-nature audit glasses" she used for analyzing complex cases and turned the pages one by one.
The numbers were honest—more honest than people.
Three years ago, the pandemic became the turning point in many stories. Misjudged investments, large sums of withheld project payments—the lifelines of the several projects under his name had lit up red one after another. His credit record was clean, but the company's performance had nearly stalled. Eight million in bank debt, with litigation risk hanging over his head. Scattered unpaid bills to material suppliers and construction crews lay like dust along the edges of the balance sheet.
And then, just a few days ago—at a timing precise enough to make her eyelid twitch—the crucial municipal project payment that had been withheld for years was settled in full, in two installments.
Yao Yuan leaned back into her chair, her gaze drifting out to the jungle of steel and concrete. The setting sun plated the glass curtain walls with an illusory gold rim.
This money had arrived too "punctually." Punctual enough to look like a carefully staged rescue—and the moment that rescue signal was sent overlapped, subtly, with the point at which she had returned for the second time from the turbulence of the bronze mirror's space-time current.
She had no proof, only intuition. But in her framework of understanding, intuition was never mysticism. It was the high-speed computation the brain performed beneath the surface of awareness, drawing on a vast pool of latent information—a result not yet describable in precise words. Her intuition was sounding the alarm.
She needed to see Old Chen, had to see him. But Old Chen wouldn't be back until spring.
She had to wait.
In the days when waiting became the only option, Yao Yuan's thoughts would inevitably drift back to the man, Shuai Hongqiang. To think of him was to be unable to skirt around "human nature"—the most complex and unstable variable of all.
He was not a "good man" in the conventional sense, nor was he simply a "bad man." In fact, across the thousands of cases she had handled, she had long discarded that naive black-and-white binary. The purely good probably exist only in the lives of saints, while the purely evil can usually construct, even for themselves, a logically self-consistent "narrative of hardship" to justify their wrongdoing.
She had long judged people by a different set of standards: their base color, their demands, their behavioral patterns, and the choices they tended to make under specific pressures.
Shuai Hongqiang's base color was the earthy, raw "loyalty" and "substance" forged out on the streets. That was his fundamental ground. The good in him was real: the overbearing tenderness of draping his whole coat over her head in the rain while he himself got soaked through; the sense of responsibility that made him settle his ex-wife properly even at his own expense during the divorce (the twenty-six-year-old "her" inside the bronze mirror had even walked away with half his assets); the bottom-up survival wisdom of never being stingy about sharing resources with "his own people." These were the solid pieces of his personality puzzle, the ones that couldn't be sanded away.
But his limits were just as conspicuous. The ceiling shaped by his educational background and early experiences meant that on certain boards requiring higher-dimensional cognition, his processing power fell short. He might have the nerve for big moves, yet on small matters he could betray a petty, small-trader's calculation. And his concealment and lying in this bronze mirror affair clearly marked out his behavioral pattern under the pressure of a dead end: defensive concealment, a tendency to monopolize resources, and an instinct to keep even his closest allies walled off from information.
Why?
Yao Yuan's gaze fell back on the financial report. Eight million in debt, a cash flow on the brink of collapse, a life-saving sum that suddenly landed.
The answer all but spoke itself.
A person at the edge of a cliff grabs at anything as if it's the straw that will save him. To him, the bronze mirror was no longer a strange encounter or a love token—it was the one and only uncontrollable "variable" that could turn his desperate situation around. He didn't dare let her know everything. He feared she would fight him for the initiative, feared that after a rational weighing she would choose to stop it, feared that this last sliver of hope would slip through his fingers too.
This wasn't evil. This was the biological instinct of a creature in a dead end. Selfish, but real to the point of cruelty.
Yao Yuan understood that kind of reality. She could even calmly take apart the chain of logic within it: survival anxiety > perception of resource scarcity > intense desire to control the only variable > defensive strategy against potential competitors.
She only felt a deep, icy familiarity. For him, and for some past version of herself.
She hadn't been without resentment over their separation when she was young. She had resented that his calculations were never frank, resented that he wouldn't give a clear promise. Looking back now, how much of that resentment had come from a naive misreading of the complexity of human nature? And how much from a stubborn attachment to the fairy-tale premise that "love must be sterile"?
She didn't think that way anymore.
In the world of adults, everything is a mixture. Emotion inevitably carries calculations of interest; calculation may wrap a few strands of loyalty and feeling inside it; truth needs the lubricant of lies to move forward; and deep within certain lies may hide a truth no one dares to name. Just like Shuai Hongqiang—he was deceiving her right now, yet he was also genuinely struggling at the edge of the abyss. He chose concealment, but the real, tangible sacrifices he had made in the past couldn't be erased from the ledger of his life either.
Human nature is just this kind of chaotic asset, woven through with both longs and shorts. You can't strip them apart cleanly; you can only keep assessing their weights and risk coefficients across different situations.
That was why she could keep from clinging when they parted, and still sit at the same negotiating table when they met again. It wasn't that she had let go—it was a cognitive upgrade. She had seen the rules of the game clearly and set aside unrealistic illusions. Love and hate, grudges and passion, were too extravagant, the cost too high. Better to focus on the executable zone of cooperation where consensus could be reached in the present.
For now, her goal was to unlock the physical and space-time secrets of the bronze mirror. His goal was to use the mirror's mystery to get through his financial crisis. Different purposes, but converging paths in the short term.
So she would keep walking that converging path. Maintain cooperation, maintain observation, keep up the quiet retrieval of key information. Until the intersection appeared naturally, or until one party decided to unilaterally change course.
The assistant knocked and came in. "President Yao, we've confirmed Old Chen's daughter's exact address and contact information in Hainan. Should I start laying the groundwork to reach out now?"
Yao Yuan drew her gaze back from the window. "No need. Spring is still a long way off. Going on the offensive now would only expose our need and raise the other side's psychological price. We wait."
"Then on Boss Shuai's end..."
"Everything as usual." Yao Yuan closed the folder, her voice without a ripple. "The cooperation that should move forward keeps moving forward; the information that should be exchanged keeps being exchanged. Some cards, played too early, cost you the initiative at the table. Some truths can't be rushed."
The assistant left. The office sank back into silence, only the city's light dimming inch by inch beyond the window, like a tide pulling back.
Yao Yuan sat alone. Night seeped in through the glass, staining the outline of her form.
Life, perhaps, was a dynamic system built of a series of lies—some careful, some hasty—and countless coincidences, some inevitable, some accidental, all nested within and catalyzing one another.
Shuai Hongqiang had set up a roadblock with a lie, and the roadblock had unexpectedly become a signpost. The mist of time she had crossed through with the bronze mirror might itself have become his floating bridge out of a dead end. True and false, cause and effect, tangled in this moment into a chaos she couldn't immediately sort out.
The one thing she was certain of was that she needed the truth. The physical truth about the bronze mirror, the logical truth about the time-travel, and—most subtle and most crucial of all—the truth about the unfathomable thing called "the human heart" under extreme circumstances.
Until then, waiting was the only strategy. Wait for spring, wait for the ice and snow to melt and all things to revive, wait until the people who should be seen could be seen, the questions that should be asked could be asked, and the cards that should be shown had to be shown.
And until then, she would guard this secret as tightly as he did. Because some transcendent experiences become hysteria the moment they're spoken aloud; some key leverage depreciates the instant it's exposed.
She opened the drawer and took out the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror.
The surface was cold and hard, reflecting her face in a blur. The same brows and eyes, the same exquisite makeup. But something, beneath the mirror's surface, deep in her pupils, had undergone an irreversible chemical change.
She gazed at it for a moment, then returned it to the depths of the drawer. With a soft "click," the bolt slid home.
Wait for spring, then.
When spring comes, the frozen earth loosens; what lies dormant will wake, what is hidden will surface. Many unresolved questions might then find an answer—not a perfect one, but one real enough.
And before that answer was revealed, life still had to go on. With the secret, with the wariness, with the cool calculation, taking step after step through the net woven of lies and coincidences, toward the future she had chosen—or had no choice but to accept.
The afternoon's work proceeded in order. She handled several business partnerships that needed urgent decisions, turned down afternoon-tea invitations from two girlfriends, and signed off on the next phase of the content marketing plan the assistant had drawn up. Her efficiency was high, but her attention was as if behind a pane of frosted glass—the cold touch of the bronze mirror and that faint pause in Shuai Hongqiang's phone call kept flickering past at unguarded moments.
Just after four, she closed her laptop. Today was no day for tasks that demanded sharp rational focus. She picked up her car keys and decided to go to her mother, Zhang Fengxia.
When she reached the building, Zhang Fengxia was already waiting at the entrance, and handed her a thermos. "Just stewed some pear soup. You use your throat so much—soothe it a little." She pulled open the car door and got in, brisk in her movements, keeping between herself and Yao Yuan just the right measure of quiet distance, the kind that belongs to grown mother and daughter.
The sign for "Zhou's Lucky-Year Hotpot" glowed warm yellow against the deepening dusk. Inside, steam rose and voices clamored—another kind of reality. When Zhou Lufei saw them, his smile crinkled the corners of his eyes; he came hurrying out of the back kitchen rubbing his hands, the smell of beef tallow and spices on him.
"Yuanyuan, you came! Quick, help me take a look at this livestreaming—it's worrying me sick. Look here," Zhou Lufei rubbed his hands a little sheepishly. "I've gone live a few times. People come and go, but they just won't stay, and not many place orders. This mouth of mine—chatting with customers in the shop is fine, but the second I face this camera, I don't know what to say. Have a look, will you? Where's the problem?"
He didn't bother with small talk and led her straight to the "livestream corner" set up temporarily in the corner of the shop. Phone, fill light, microphone—the equipment was all there, but it gave off an air of clumsy effort.
Yao Yuan glanced over his livestream backend data: average viewing time under a minute, conversion rate pitifully low. She opened a replay clip. On screen, Zhou Lufei was a bit tense, reciting the set menu like he was reading off a recipe, his movements stiff. When he introduced the "hand-cut fresh lamb," it was like reciting a product manual—the merits laid out clearly, but missing the "soul" that hooks people.
"Uncle Zhou," she dragged over a stool and sat down, her tone the calm, assured one she used at work. "We're not 'selling' meat. We're 'treating guests.'"
She began breaking it down, speaking unhurriedly, every point landing solid. "Don't recite a script. Just imagine the person on the other side of the camera is an old neighbor you haven't seen in years. Talk like a human. Take this lamb—'stands up on the plate without falling' is trade jargon; the audience doesn't get it. You say: 'Look, this meat sticks to the plate—stand it up on its edge and it still won't slide down. Why? It's fresh, no added water, the fibers are tight. This is hind-leg meat from a Inner Mongolian lamb, from the parts that move the most, so it comes out tender with a good chew when you cook it.' Say it and do it at the same time—stand the plate up and show them. That's 'demonstrating value.' It works better than 'describing value.'"
"Camera presence is simple. Don't keep staring at yourself in the frame. Look at the lens like you're looking into someone's eyes. When you're cutting meat, look at the meat. When the soup boils, look at the soup. Be natural, like you're chatting with a regular in the shop. If you get nervous, just think of it as teaching an old friend the best way to cook hotpot."
She adjusted the angle of the fill light so the warm glow fell more directly on the vivid grain of the lamb and the milky white rolling of the pot. "Sight comes before taste. Make the light a little warmer, and the food looks more appetizing, more like the taste of 'home'—not 'merchandise.'"
Zhou Lufei took notes in a little book, nodding nonstop, his brow gradually smoothing out. Zhang Fengxia silently passed over two cups of warm water, one for her daughter, one for her husband.
With the coaching at a pause, the steaming copper pot was brought out. A clear broth base, with goji berries and red dates bobbing up and down. The hand-cut lamb was a tidy arrangement of red and white; the vegetables were fresh and crisp. The sesame dipping sauce was mixed by Zhou Lufei himself, rich and fragrant.
Cooking meat, picking up vegetables, idle chatter. In the warmth of the food's steam, the conversation grew loose. Somehow, the talk turned to Yao Yuan's childhood.
"You were stubborn from the start, always had your own mind." Zhang Fengxia picked up a slice of cabbage and let it swirl slowly in the pot, her tone flat, as if she were talking about someone else.
Yao Yuan gave a small "mm" and waited for her to go on.
"Remember that time in third grade? You were class monitor, collecting homework." Zhang Fengxia raised her eyes, her gaze passing through the curling steam to land on Yao Yuan's face.
The floodgate of memory was pushed open by one flat sentence. The hand holding Yao Yuan's chopsticks paused, almost imperceptibly. Of course she remembered.
It was third grade of primary school, and she was class monitor. The teacher had told her to collect the whole class's exercise books. Everyone else had turned theirs in—only one tall, sturdy boy, Wang Hu, sat tilting his chair lazily and said he hadn't done it and wouldn't hand it in.
A near-obsessive sense of duty had gripped little Yao Yuan. She felt it was a task that had to be completed. She stood in front of Wang Hu's desk, her voice quiet but clear. "You have to hand it in, or I can't account for it to the teacher."
"Not handing it in. None of your business," Wang Hu said impatiently, feeling humiliated at being pressed by a girl.
After a few rounds of back-and-forth, Wang Hu suddenly stood up and shoved her hard. The force was great. Caught off guard, she fell back onto the floor, her elbow and tailbone striking the cold, hard cement with a dull ache. But before the pain came the embarrassment and humiliation of being the focus of the whole classroom's stares. Tears rushed into her eyes, hot and stinging, and she bit down hard on her lip.
At the very instant when that mixture of pain, grievance, and the instinct to cry for help reached its peak, she instinctively turned her head and looked at the back door of the classroom.
Zhang Fengxia was standing there. No telling how long she'd been there—backlit, her figure a little blurred. But Yao Yuan knew her mother had seen the whole thing.
She looked at her mother, tears welling, a silent plea and accusation. She thought her mom would rush in, help her up, scold Wang Hu, maybe even go argue it out with the teacher.
But Zhang Fengxia didn't move. She just stood there, watched for a few seconds, then spoke, her voice without the slightest ripple, even a little cold and hard:
"What are you still lying there for? Aren't you getting up to go home?"
Yao Yuan was stunned, forgetting to cry. She pushed herself up off the floor, stood on her own, patted the dust off her pants, lowered her head, silently picked up the scattered books (including Wang Hu's blank one), and walked to the door.
The walk home wasn't long, but the silence pressed down hard. As they neared home, Yao Yuan finally couldn't hold it back and asked, heavy with a stuffy nose, "Mom, why didn't you help me up just now? Why didn't you scold him?"
Zhang Fengxia stopped walking and turned around. The halo of the streetlamp traced the expressionless side of her face.
"I help you up, and then what?" She looked at her daughter, her gaze direct. "I scold him, and then what? Next time? The time after that?"
"Yao Yuan, you remember this," her voice wasn't loud, but every word was like a little hammer striking the heart that was still innocent back then. "The road of your life ahead is so long you can't see the end of it, and most of the time, you'll have to walk it alone. No one can be by your side every second, ready to lift you up the moment you fall, to stand up for you the moment you're wronged."
"If just now you felt wronged, felt he was in the wrong, then find your own way to take the round back. Use your own methods. Tell the teacher, tell the whole class he's a bully, or wait until you're bigger and stronger than him and hit him back—any of those is fine. But that's your business."
"If you don't have the ability to claim it back on the spot yourself, then don't count on someone else being sure to help you. Someone can help you once; they can't help you a whole lifetime. If you don't have the means today, then you can only endure it first, hold it in, remember it. When one day you have the ability, the means, then you can think about taking it back."
"When you fall, you climb up yourself—that's the first thing you have to learn. Always counting on someone else to lift you, and you'll never learn to watch the road, never learn to use your own strength."
That night's dinner, Yao Yuan ate in heavy silence. Her elbow still hurt, and her heart was even more clogged. But Zhang Fengxia's words, like a rough but hard file, carved themselves stroke by stroke into the understanding she still only half-grasped back then.
Now, sitting in the warm hotpot shop, listening to the gurgle of the boiling broth, smelling the familiar fragrance of sesame sauce, Yao Yuan slowly chewed that slice of sauce-dipped lamb. The meat was fresh and tender, the flavor rich.
"I remember." She nodded, her tone calm and unruffled. "Back then I resented you in my heart, thought you were cruel."
Zhang Fengxia gave a light snort through her nose and put a piece of frozen tofu in her bowl. "Resentment is right. If you hadn't resented it, would you have remembered it so firmly?"
Zhou Lufei, listening from the side, kept shaking his head. "Fengxia, the child was so small, and you—"
"Small?" Zhang Fengxia cut him off with a glance. "Small is exactly why you teach. Wait until the bones harden and the temperament is set, and you couldn't bend it back even if you tried." Her gaze fell again on Yao Yuan's face, no tenderness in it, but a strange certainty. "Look at her now. Does she look like someone who falls down at one shove and lies there waiting for others to lift her up?"
Yao Yuan didn't answer. Slowly, mouthful by mouthful, she ate the food in her bowl.
Maybe it really was so. Zhang Fengxia's near-cold "bystander" style of education, that way of carving the three words "rely on yourself" into her bones in the most painful manner, had invisibly shaped the hardest core of her character.
Not to show weakness easily, not to depend on outside help, accustomed to analyzing the situation, assessing the cost, and searching for solutions on her own after a fall. Grievance and pain could be felt, but she couldn't let herself be drowned by them. If she was powerless to resist for the moment, then endure calmly, store up strength, wait for the right time—rather than useless weeping or impulsive resistance. Lower her expectations of the outside world to the minimum, raise her demands on herself to the maximum.
This had forged her later independence, and also forged the clear sense of boundaries and near-instinctive rational weighing she brought to dealing with people. There were advantages and disadvantages. But in a complex world, this "survival logic," born of a single fall onto childhood cement, had indeed tempered her clarity and independence—and quietly sanctioned a certain degree of loneliness.
"The meat's about to overcook—eat up." Zhang Fengxia's voice broke into her thoughts, and another chopstickful of fresh, tender lamb shoulder dropped into her bubbling little pot.
"Mm." Yao Yuan lowered her eyes and focused on the food in her bowl.
The broth in the copper pot kept boiling, and the hazy white vapor blurred her mother's face across from her. Those distant, sharply painful lessons and the warm, tangible care before her merged in a strange way, forming the bond between them—both distant and tenacious.
Perhaps everyone's base color of character is a pattern, set at last after the imprint of one or several decisive moments in childhood has been painted over, tested, and revised again and again by the years. It was just that her base color had been laid down by Zhang Fengxia with an especially coarse brushstroke.
Yao Yuan thought: if one day she needed to sum up Zhang Fengxia's whole philosophy of education into a single "Godmother of Feelings" aphorism, it would probably be this:
"To recognize as early as possible the reality that 'in the end you have to rely on yourself' is the most hardcore, and the most precious, gift parents can give a child. It doesn't guarantee you'll never fall, but it guarantees that every time you do, you'll know how to climb back up—and which way to go."
Come to think of it, this bitter dose of clarity suited her livestream room rather well. One line, cutting straight to the essence.
She quietly finished the meat her mother had placed in her bowl. A solid texture, warm and comforting.
Outside the window, night had settled, and the road blazed with lights. Inside, voices, the clink of bowls and dishes, the bubbling of the soup wove together into a steady, homely market din. In this moment, there was no space-time riddle to crack, no human-nature leverage to weigh, no emotional tangle to dissect. Only one solid meal, a memory worn smooth of its sharp edges by time, and an unspoken, blood-deep accord of understanding.
For her, this was already a precious enough breathing space that life had granted.
Dinner ended close to eight. Zhou Lufei insisted on packing up an order of the shop's homemade sesame flatbread for her to take. On the drive back, Zhang Fengxia sat in the passenger seat, watching the streetscape flow past the window, and suddenly spoke, her voice clear in the quiet of the car.
"That Shuai Hongqiang—he's been looking for you again lately?"
The fingers on Yao Yuan's steering wheel tightened for an almost imperceptible instant, then relaxed. Her mother's information network was, on occasion, sharp enough to surprise her.
"Mm, there's some cooperation under discussion." Her answer was brief, free of emotion.
Zhang Fengxia was silent for a moment before she said, "That man—his heart isn't bad. But at the root, he's not the same kind of person as us. You keep that straight in your mind."
"I know." Yao Yuan watched the red light ahead turn green and slowly pressed the accelerator.
She knew what her mother meant. Zhang Fengxia read people with a near-instinctive sharpness honed by life at the bottom—she paid no heed to the halos or words floating on the surface, going straight to the bone. She hadn't said Shuai Hongqiang was bad; she'd only said "not the same kind of person." Within that lay an insight into his origins, his way of thinking, his logic of handling matters, even his ordering of values.
"As long as you know." Zhang Fengxia said no more, and changed the subject. "Remember to drink the pear soup."
After dropping her mother home, Yao Yuan drove alone back to her own apartment. The city's neon flowed past the car windows into blurred bands of light. Inside the car it was quiet, and the calm on her face slowly faded, revealing the deep deliberation beneath.
Zhang Fengxia's words were like a pebble dropped into the lake of her heart. She knew what her mother was reminding her of. Not a simple "be careful of men," but a reminder of the fundamental reason they had once parted.
She was used to rational calculation, risk control, clear boundaries. He, at his core, carried the gambler's streak of a street rover, an instinctive division between "his own people" and "outsiders," and a capacity, in a dead end, for action that broke all ordinary rules.
Cooperation could be based on short-term interest, but trust... Yao Yuan thought of that lie about Old Chen. The foundation of trust had already begun to show fine cracks.
Back at the apartment, she didn't switch on the main light, only twisted on a reading lamp by the sofa. She took out the brocade pouch holding the Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern bronze mirror—the one she'd locked in the office drawer, then taken out again and tucked into her everyday bag—but she didn't open it. She just held it in her palm, feeling its heavy weight.
The truth. She needed the truth. The truth about this mirror's secret beyond the laws of physics, the truth about what Shuai Hongqiang had actually done with it, or tried to do, and the truth behind all of it—whether this was a contest of human hearts, or whether some unknowable force was truly pulling the strings.
But the truth often has to be bought at a price, sometimes a steep one.
She gripped the pouch tight. The cold metal contour pressed into her palm.
Spring. She drew that as her final deadline. When the ice and snow melted and Old Chen returned, no matter what, she had to get some answers.
Until then, she needed to position her pieces more carefully, wait more patiently—just as Zhang Fengxia had taught her years ago: when strength isn't on your side or the information is unclear, steady yourself first, see clearly, store up your power.
Beyond the window the city still blazed with light, countless stories breeding, entwining, and dying out within it. Yao Yuan sat in that small pool of warm yellow glow, her form still and quiet, like a chess piece sunk into deep water, silently calculating where to place her next move amid currents no one knew of.
Human nature is a balance sheet that never stops fluctuating, and in this moment, she was checking and rechecking the chips in her own hand, assessing those unseen liabilities and risks, waiting for the moment that could tip the scales in her favor.
