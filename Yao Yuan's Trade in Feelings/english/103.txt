Chapter 103 — Outside the ICU, Shuai Hongqiang Is Consumed by Guilt and Regret
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640350.html

The car braked hard and stopped at the entrance of the emergency department of the Provincial People's Hospital. Shuai Hongqiang shoved the door open and rushed out with his son in his arms. At the entrance, the head of the emergency department was waiting in person, leading several doctors and nurses with a crash cart at the ready—his phone calls had paid off.
"Give me the boy!" The director's tone was calm and decisive. With practiced efficiency, the medical staff helped Shuai Hongqiang transfer Shuai Wenyao onto the crash cart with the utmost care. In that instant, as the boy was moved, the down jacket wrapped around him slipped open at one corner, and his son's horribly mangled right cheek and swollen, tightly shut eye came back into view. Shuai Hongqiang's vision went black and he nearly toppled over, caught at the last second by Boss Li, who was right behind him.
"Preliminary assessment: severe open traumatic brain injury, with deep burns to the head and face and a ruptured eyeball! Take him straight to the hybrid operating room! Notify the heads of neurosurgery, burns and plastics, ophthalmology, and anesthesiology for an emergency consult! Prep for surgery!" The director gave orders even as he examined the boy, and the crash cart raced down the green-light corridor, vanishing in an instant behind the glow of the "Surgery in Progress" sign.
Shuai Hongqiang tried to follow inside but was stopped by a nurse. "Family members, please wait outside the operating room!"
He felt as if every bone had been pulled from his body; only by leaning against the cold wall did he keep from sliding to the floor. Lin Wan parked the car and ran in. The moment she saw him, then turned toward that shut door with its glaring red light, her steps stopped dead and the last trace of color drained from her face. She didn't cry again—she just stared straight at that door, her body trembling faintly like a leaf in the wind.
Boss Li helped her to a bench at the side and sat her down, then handed Shuai Hongqiang a cigarette. Shuai Hongqiang's hands were shaking so badly it took several tries to light it. He took a fierce drag, and the acrid smoke filling his lungs set off a violent fit of coughing—coughing so hard it brought tears to his eyes.
The minutes and seconds crawled by, each one stretching as long as a century. The corridor outside the operating room was terrifyingly silent, broken only by the uneven, heavy breathing of the three of them and the faint distant clamor of the hospital. The red light above that door was like a cold, merciless eye, fixed on them, passing judgment.
Before long, Old Xu hurried over and reported the situation, his expression grave. "The father and son who caused this are already in police custody, and statements are being taken. The man works at the emergency management bureau—the negligence is confirmed, and he's clearly willing to take full responsibility and cooperate with compensation."
The outcome was clean and clear-cut, but at this moment Shuai Hongqiang and Lin Wan had no mind to spare for blame or compensation. Money and justice meant nothing, shrank to nothing, in the face of their child's life or death.
At last, after a wait that felt like a whole lifetime, the operating room door opened. A doctor in blue scrubs, mask, and cap stepped out, his eyes full of exhaustion and a kind of gravity that comes from having seen life and death too many times.
Shuai Hongqiang and Lin Wan sprang from their chairs like coiled springs and rushed up to him.
"Doctor, how is my son?" Shuai Hongqiang's voice was so hoarse it was barely audible.
The doctor pulled down his mask, revealing the serious face of a middle-aged man. He didn't answer right away. His gaze swept over the ashen, despairing faces of Shuai Hongqiang and Lin Wan, and he hesitated a few seconds before speaking in a voice as steady as he could manage, though every word landed with crushing weight. "Parents, you need to prepare yourselves. The boy's condition… is not optimistic."
Lin Wan's body lurched violently. Shuai Hongqiang held her up with all his strength, his own heart seized as if by an icy hand.
"A high-velocity firework fragment entered the skull from behind the right eye socket, causing combined fractures of the orbital and temporal bones. The fragment is lodged right against a branch of the middle cerebral artery and the cavernous sinus—extremely likely to trigger fatal intracranial hemorrhage. At the same time, the blast wave caused acute diffuse brain swelling. Intracranial pressure is off the charts, and early-stage brain herniation has already set in, compressing the brainstem. He could lose his life at any moment."
"The right eyeball has ruptured completely, with massive loss of intraocular contents—the chance of saving any vision is nearly zero. The right ear has third-degree burns and a perforated eardrum, which will most likely result in permanent sensorineural deafness. There are extensive third-degree burns to the head and temporal region, with gunpowder, mud, and fabric fragments embedded deep into the tissue, and severe contamination of the wounds."
"We've already performed an emergency craniotomy to evacuate the hematoma and a decompressive craniectomy, which has temporarily stabilized the intracranial pressure and slowed the worsening of the herniation. But the deep, high-risk fragment is in a treacherous position—removing it rashly would mean certain death, so all we can do is observe conservatively. For the eye and the face, we've only done preliminary debridement and suturing."
The doctor paused, looking at the two parents before him who seemed to have aged ten years in an instant, then went on. "Right now the boy is not out of danger. He's being transferred to the ICU on ventilator support. After this he'll have to make it through three life-or-death barriers in succession: delayed hemorrhage, severe infection, and multiple organ failure. Even if he survives by some miracle, he'll most likely be left with lifelong aftereffects—coma, cognitive damage, paralysis of the limbs, secondary epilepsy, blindness, deafness, all are possible. And repairing the burn damage to his face will be a long, grueling ordeal stretching over years."
Every word weighed a ton; every sentence cut to the bone.
When Lin Wan had heard it all, her body swayed, and finally she reached her limit. Her vision went black and she slid slowly down the wall to sit on the floor. From the moment of the accident until now, the reason, calm, and resolve she had forced herself to hold onto all the way through snapped completely at last when she heard that her son's life was in danger. She sat on the floor, not crying out loud, only her shoulders shaking violently as the tears poured out in silence. It was as if every word the doctor said had turned into a red-hot knife, stabbing deep into her soul. After a long while, she very slowly, bit by bit, turned her head toward that shut operating room door, then slowly turned back to look at the doctor.
Then she spoke, her voice drifting as if it came from another world. "Doctor… use the best medicine… the best equipment… bring in the best specialists… money is no object… we'll spend everything we have, it doesn't matter… I'm begging you… please, save my son… as long as he can live… as long as he can live…" By the end her voice had sunk almost beyond hearing, yet it carried a heartbreaking, animal-like plea and despair, the kind only a mother knows.
Shuai Hongqiang wanted to go help her up, but his legs felt like they'd been filled with lead and wouldn't move. Only by leaning against the cold wall did he keep from falling. His ears were ringing, and the doctor's words seemed to come from somewhere far away, every one of them like a heavy hammer striking his temple, leaving him dizzy and disoriented, his ears buzzing. Delayed hemorrhage… severe infection… multiple organ failure… ruptured eyeball… blindness… deafness… secondary epilepsy… paralysis of the limbs…
These cold medical terms, linked to the son who had once been warm and alive in his arms, the boy who laughed and made a fuss, formed a picture of horror he could not bear and dared not imagine.
Why? Why had it come to this?
On New Year's Eve, a single firework, a stranger child's ignorant prank—how had it turned his healthy, lively son into this thing lying in the operating room, hovering between life and death, with maybe nothing left ahead but a broken shell of a body?
It wasn't fair! It was so goddamn unfair!
Rage, despair, and a heart-rending agony crashed against the inside of his chest, almost tearing him apart.
Old Xu and Boss Li, eyes red, helped Lin Wan up and supported her over to the bench at the side, sitting her down and murmuring words of comfort—though language was pale and powerless at a moment like this.
A long time passed, and a nurse came out to say that the boy had been transferred to intensive care for observation, and the parents could look at him for a moment through the glass. The two of them rushed to the observation window of the ICU. They saw their son's tiny body lying on a bed surrounded by instruments, his head wrapped in thick bandages, half his face grotesquely swollen, the exposed skin covered in bruises and the marks of medicine, tubes inserted into his nose and arm. So still, so fragile—like a completely different person from the bouncing, lively boy of just a few hours before.
Lin Wan's tears burst the dam again. She clamped her hand hard over her mouth to keep from crying out and disturbing the son inside.
Through the glass, Shuai Hongqiang looked at his son, and from the place where his heart sat came wave after wave of a suffocating, wrenching pain. He spun around abruptly, turning his back to the glass, and pressed his forehead against the cold wall.
And it was at that moment that the deeper, darker, more spine-chilling thought—the one held down by sheer terror and chaos—rose silently like a demon from deep water and seized his heart in a tight grip—
The Mirror Pact.
The karmic backlash.
When his daughter had broken her leg skiing, this thought had drifted across his mind like a dark cloud. And now, with his son blown to a bloody pulp before his eyes, hovering between life and death… that dark cloud had turned into a downpour of blood, drenching him through and freezing him solid.
That was it… that was it! It had to be it! Old Master Wang Baoguo had said that the backlash doesn't necessarily fall on you yourself—it can strike down those of your own flesh and blood! Wenrui's leg, Wenyao's disfigurement and grave injuries… this was the price! It was because he, Shuai Hongqiang, in a moment of greed, had made a pact with that demonic bronze mirror and stolen a smooth career and fortune that were never his to begin with. Now the wheel of heaven had turned, the karma had come due, and the entire price was to be paid by his two children! He even thought: was it because, back then, he had never clearly promised Yao Yuan marriage, leaving her so heartbroken that she had aborted their child—and the "good fortune" he had taken on had been transformed out of Yao Yuan's journey through time, so that as karmic backlash, he had made Yao Yuan lose her child, and now heaven was punishing him by making him lose his children too?
A towering guilt and a boundless terror engulfed him in an instant, even greater than when he'd first learned of his son's injuries. It was a shudder and a self-loathing that came from the depths of his soul. Cold sweat soaked through the clothes beneath, clinging icily to his skin. He couldn't stop himself from trembling—from his fingertips to his teeth, everything was chattering. He didn't even dare turn to look at his son behind the glass, didn't dare look at his wife beside him, broken with grief.
He was no lucky beneficiary. He was the executioner who had pushed his own children into the abyss with his own hands, the harbinger of disaster who had dragged down the whole family.
"Hongqiang? Hongqiang, what's wrong? Are you cold?" Old Xu, sensing something off about him, came over to steady him.
Shuai Hongqiang violently flung his hand away, the movement so sharp it left Old Xu stunned. He staggered back two steps, and only by leaning against the wall did he steady himself. He lifted his eyes toward Lin Wan, who was held up by Boss Li and still gazing at the ICU window, weeping silently.
Her profile, in the dim corridor light, looked so frail, so despairing, and yet it carried a stubborn, hard-clenched strength that belonged to a mother. Only moments ago, in front of the doctor, she had been able to force down her collapse and say, "Use the best medicine, we'll cooperate with everything." A mother grows iron-strong for her child's sake.
But what about him? He was the one who had buried such a catastrophe under this family.
Tomorrow was the day the pact was to be dissolved.
But the bitter fruit had already appeared; the backlash had already come.
He couldn't hide it any longer.
His son was still lying in there, his fate uncertain. If… if tomorrow's dissolution didn't go smoothly, if the backlash was still continuing… he couldn't let Lin Wan bear the blows that might come one after another while she knew nothing. Still less could he, after his son had paid such a brutal price, keep guarding this filthy secret alone, pretending it had all been just an accident.
He owed her the truth. He owed this family an explanation.
Even though that confession might destroy the last of her trust in him, might pile snow on the frost of a family already battered by the storm. But this was a consequence he had to bear. He had planted the cause; it was for him to pay it back.
With all the strength in his body, Shuai Hongqiang stopped his trembling. He stood up straight, his face wearing a calm that was almost the gray of ashes. He walked over to Lin Wan and said in a low voice to Boss Li, who was holding her up, "Boss Li, Old Xu, today… thank you. Go home and rest now—the boy and the household both need someone. Here… the two of us are enough."
His voice was terribly hoarse, but his tone carried a strange finality. Old Xu and Boss Li exchanged a glance, knowing in their hearts that staying any longer truly wouldn't help. They comforted the couple a few more times, urged them again and again to call at any moment if anything came up, and only then left together.
In the long, cold ICU corridor, in the end there were only the two of them, and the young child in the ward whose life still hung in the balance.
Shuai Hongqiang walked up to Lin Wan, slowly bent his knees, and half-knelt, taking her cold, trembling hands in his.
Lin Wan, dazed, looked down at him through tear-blurred eyes.
The dim light fell over the two of them, casting a floor full of brooding despair.
"Lin Wan," Shuai Hongqiang said, gazing at his haggard, broken wife. His throat worked, and every word came out hoarse, as if ground through flesh and blood—heavy and sincere.
"There's something I've kept from you… for a long time. I know now isn't the time, but I have to tell you now."
