Chapter 62 — When the Roseleaf Raspberry Blooms, Spring Is Done

Spring in the north arrives in a vast, magnificent surge, but slips away leaving no trace at all. All it leaves behind is a city layered in greenery and shade, the warm breeze soaking in a deepening, mellow gentleness that spreads across the whole human world.

It seems that in the space of a single sweet dream, the pink-and-white clouds of pear blossoms woven across the city quietly finish falling. Then come the winter jasmine spreading its gold, the pink cherry blossoms drooping from the branches, the white apricot bursting into bud, the red peach blazing open, the plum blossoms gathered like snow — every kind of beauty rushing to one grand spring banquet. They take the stage one after another, blooming in turns, lush and clamorous, refusing to pause even for a moment.

When the peony at last arrives to close the show, carrying the bearing of a nation-toppling beauty, regal and unrivaled among all the season's flowers; and then the roseleaf raspberry, its plain white scattered petals unfurling at their leisure deep in the dense shade — this spectacle of spring at its very height has already come to its end.

When the roseleaf raspberry blooms, spring is done.

A grand yet hurried cycle of flowering, no more than two or three months in all, pours out an entire year's brilliance and lingering tenderness. The splendor falls suddenly from the stage, surrendering the whole human stage to high summer, which gathers its momentum and surges forth, its life force sprawling and magnificent.

Since the day she came back from the Hall of Antiquities, more than a month has now passed.

The matter of the Mirror Pact was like a stone dropped into a deep pool — at first it stirred heart-stopping ripples, then it sank to the bottom, and the surface seemed to return to calm. Yao Yuan called on every connection she could think of, asking openly and in secret after any trace of ancient-method occultists, of bronze-mirror craftsmanship, and above all of anything tied to the "Kui-Dragon Cloud-Pattern." But the harvest was meager. She had wrapped that mirror carefully in a length of black velvet and locked it in the deepest corner of a bank safe-deposit box, as if that alone could seal away the uncanny tie along with it.

And the strangest thing of all was that the frequent, uncontrollable bouts of "time-travel" had truly stopped recurring.

Was it the "heart-guarding" old jade that Old Chen had given her, finally doing its work? Or had something shifted on Shuai Hongqiang's side — remorse in his heart, his fortunes turning — temporarily cutting off the "pull of the time-reversal"? Or was it merely some brief calm before the storm? Yao Yuan had no way to know. She only wore that old jade against her skin every day, feeling its unbroken, gentle warmth press soothingly against her spirit, letting her hold an outwardly flawless calm and efficiency through the layered pressures of livestreaming, company affairs, and her secret search for the mirror-maker's descendants. Even her bodily fatigue seemed to ease a little — though deep down, that taut string never truly slackened.

Today was another livestream day.

Early in the morning her phone buzzed on the desk. It was Jin Lan.

"Yuanyuan, what time do you wrap up today?" Jin Lan's voice came through the receiver, steady and capable, carrying the dining queen's particular composure — the ease of someone who controls every affair in hand — but for once there was a thread of relaxed interest in her tone.

"It ends at three this afternoon, Jin Lan-lan." Yao Yuan picked up and leaned back against her chair, at ease. Between her and Jin Lan there were years of close friendship and an evenly matched business partnership; in private they had long since dropped the courtesies.

"Free tonight? A friend forced two tickets on me — there's an all-male dance troupe that's been pretty hot lately, on their national tour, the Jinshi stop, at the Provincial Grand Theatre. I asked around; the standard's not bad, pure artistic appreciation, a good way to clear the head too. Together?" Jin Lan was brief and to the point, but in those words "pure artistic appreciation" there was a knowing, mutual teasing that belongs to mature women. At their age and standing, they had long been able to admire all beautiful things openly and aboveboard — including excellent, strikingly handsome performers — and there was nothing carnal in it, only a refined aesthetic pleasure and a confidence in their own taste.

Yao Yuan was indeed a little worn out in spirit, but Jin Lan rarely took the initiative to suggest something leisurely, and it was a dance performance — an art she had steeped herself in for years and knew inside out. To weigh it with a professional eye would be both a relaxation and a kind of looking back on a passion from her past. She was still mulling it over for a moment when Jin Lan's smiling voice came again, with a touch of "don't miss the chance" urgency:

"Hey, sis, these tickets are hard to come by — my friend's a sponsor, so they're insider tickets." Jin Lan caught her hesitation and laughed as she added another weight to the scale, "In or out? Just treat it as putting your Beijing Dance Academy eye to the test — check the real grade of this male troupe, and give your eyes a holiday while you're at it. I've booked a table at 'Lanting' near the theatre; the dishes are light, just right for you. Six o'clock, meet at the theatre entrance?"

"All right." She no longer hesitated, and agreed. "See you at six."

After hanging up, Yao Yuan walked into the dressing room. The first thing to choose was her livestream battle-dress; what she wore on stream had always been part of her style manifesto. Her eyes swept across the dazzling wardrobe and finally settled on an extremely formal, extremely saturated peach-pink silk taffeta dress. The color was so intense it was almost blinding, but the fabric's own heavy, lustrous sheen reined in just enough of what might otherwise have spilled into frivolous gaudiness, drawing out instead a restrained splendor. The dress was a simple bateau-neck, short-sleeve design, but the cut was punishingly perfect, pinching out a slender waist with precision; knee-length, its lines clean and flowing. This was a color that judged its wearer mercilessly — worn badly, it was vulgar — but on Yao Yuan, set against her snow-white skin, thick black hair, and bright, commanding features, it carried a forceful, who-else-but-me magnificence and brilliance, not the least bit gaudy, leaving only the impression that color this intense had been born to belong to her.

She blew her long hair full and loose, gathered it low into a soft bun at the back, with a few stray strands trailing lazily by her neck, carrying the easy casualness of an early-summer morning. Today's makeup was a touch lighter than her usual livestream look, but it played up a clean eyeliner and a full peach-pink lip color, striking a high-impact echo with the dress. At her ears were a pair of minimally styled but generously sized platinum diamond hoop earrings, refracting cool, fine flecks of light with every movement. On her wrist was a Patek Philippe Nautilus in rose gold, lending this utterly feminine look a barely perceptible note of hardness and rarity.

The heart-guarding old jade was still worn against her skin, tucked neatly out of sight by the dress, leaving only its gentle warmth to press soothingly — the steadying anchor amid her turbulent moods. Over it all she threw a cream-white long silk crinkle-crepe shirt jacket, its fabric extremely light and flowing, glowing with a faint pearl sheen. It could ward off the slight chill of the studio's early-summer air conditioning and also cleverly temper the high-impact richness of the peach-pink dress beneath, adding a touch of ease, layering, and room to breathe, looking all the more relaxed and chic. She stepped into a pair of nude fine-strap high heels and picked up a small silver clutch.

The woman in the mirror was dazzling in peach-pink, her figure slender, half-hidden and half-revealed in the thin mist the cream-white jacket created, radiating all over a beauty and presence that years had settled into something more mellow, more self-aware, and brimming with life. She curved the corner of her lips, satisfied. Whether she was about to face the millions of viewers behind the lens, or the performance that night with its perhaps-stirring undercurrents, she, Yao Yuan, would always appear in her most dazzling, most commanding, and most unrestrained form.
