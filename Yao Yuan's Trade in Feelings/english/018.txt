Yao Yuan Coaches a Cheated-On Housewife to Reclaim Her Bargaining Power in Three Steps
The livestream rolled on. A brass-colored light slanted in through the window, casting one half of Yao Yuan's face in the glow of a deity while the other half stayed hidden in the cold white of the ring light. She glanced at the live viewer count, and the corner of her mouth twitched almost imperceptibly — the curve of a hunter watching prey step into the trap.
Yao Yuan (to the camera, her voice smooth as silk): "Feelings are the most extravagant garbage in the world. Your ability to deal with garbage decides how pure your life is. Next."
[Call connected]
Caller Two (a middle-aged woman, her voice dry, her words slow): "Teacher Yuanyuan... I'm forty-five, married twenty years. I found out he's been cheating, with a woman who works under him, for three years now. I... I don't have a job, my kid just started university. I checked — he's quietly transferred two properties out of reach, and I don't know about the cash. I can't sleep at all anymore. I look at the man beside me and he feels like a stranger. It makes me want to throw up."
(The comments scrolled: [Poor sister][The tragedy of the housewife][How do you even survive a divorce])
Yao Yuan (she didn't answer right away; instead she leaned slowly back into her chair, those long red nails tapping lightly on the desk — tap, tap, tap, in a steady rhythm. Outside the window, the muddy river churned over an unseen whirlpool.)
"First," she said at last, her voice holding no sympathy, only a calm that bordered on cruelty, "stop the 'want to throw up' reflex. It's useless. Your stomach won't win the houses back for you."
The woman (choking up): "I just... I can't take being deceived. Twenty years..."
Yao Yuan (cutting in, leaning forward, her pupils locking onto the camera): "Your 'can't take it' — how much is it worth? One property? Two? Let me tell you: emotion is the cheapest currency in the world, especially at the negotiating table. What you need to do right now isn't to process your feelings. It's to freeze them — turn them into fuel for later."
(She picked up a gold pen and tapped it once on the blank iPad, as if tapping it straight into the heart of every viewer.)
"I'll give you three steps. Listen carefully. I'll only say this once."
"Step one: 'illness.' Starting tomorrow, go to the best hospital. Register for neurology and traditional Chinese medicine. Get a full workup, and walk out with a stack of diagnoses — 'severe anxiety, insomnia, depressive tendencies.' Keep the receipts. This isn't faking it. This is converting your psychological trauma into a 'fact of harm' that he can understand and that the law may recognize."
"Step two: 'study.' Sign up for the most expensive three-month course in town — 'high-end home-organization specialist' or 'private nutritionist.' Pay with the supplementary card on his account. On your social feed, post only study notes and sunny profile shots, captioned: 'Life reset to zero. Starting fresh.' Remember, the posture has to look good. The goal is to leave behind proof of 'active self-rescue' while you keep spending his money."
"Step three, and this is the crucial one—" Yao Yuan paused. The light reflecting off the river outside happened to graze her eyes, startlingly bright. "'Talk.' But not you doing the talking. Find the uncle or brother on your side of the family who carries the most weight, who shows the least mercy, but who truly has your back. Have them 'pay a visit' to your husband, on the pretext of 'we heard our sister's unwell and came to check in.' Let them do the talking — about the family's face, about the children's future, about 'this family is not one to be pushed around.' Your job is to lie in the bedroom looking 'frail,' shedding tears, but not saying a single word."
The woman (seeming stunned by the clarity of the steps, hesitating): "This... won't it look too ugly? Burning the bridge..."
Yao Yuan (with a short, cold laugh): "Look good? The lining of the marriage rotted away long ago, and you still want to paste a pretty face over it? Right now he's a gambler who cheats, and you're a player with one last chip in hand. Your chips are your twenty years of giving, the bond of the children, and that little leftover fear he has of 'trouble.' What you have to do is drag him back to the card table and tell him: either we redivide the chips, or we flip the whole table together — and nobody walks away with their dignity intact."
(The comments had gone wild: [Holy crap this is the hardcore practical school][Terrifyingly calm][Sister woke up sharp!])
The woman (breathing harder, as if drawing courage): "And... after that? What if he still..."
Yao Yuan (flat and final): "There's no 'what if.' Once you finish these three steps — especially the third — he'll come to you to 'negotiate.' That's when you take out the records you found on those two properties and ask just one question: 'Is your future with her worth this price?' Remember: don't bring up feelings, don't bring up betrayal. Just talk about this. It's business. It's an asset restructuring."
[15 seconds left on the call]
Yao Yuan (speeding up, every word a driven nail): "One last line for you, and for every sister struggling in the swamp of a marriage in this room: 'Mercy needs teeth. Kindness needs an edge. You offered yourself up as a sacrifice for twenty years. Now it's time to taste what it's like to be the hunter.' Your battlefield isn't in the bedroom. It's in the property deeds and the bank statements. Now — go."
(The call cut off. A brief silence fell over the room, with only the low, ceaseless flow of the Yellow River as background sound.)
Yao Yuan (leaning slowly back into her chair, her gaze sweeping over the frantically scrolling gift effects and comments, a very faint, almost weary smile surfacing — but it vanished in an instant, replaced once more by a professional sharpness). She reached out and gently adjusted the angle of the ring light beside her, focusing the beam more tightly on her face. Under the strong light those bright red nails looked like congealed blood, and also like a flag of victory.
"See," she said to the camera, her voice suddenly turning soft, yet carrying an even deeper power to sink in, "the Yellow River out there never asks the silt whether it wants to be carried off. It simply flows, and then deposits new land. A woman's rebirth often begins with 'settling the account' — owning every loss, and then, entry by entry, tallying it all up clean."
"Next." She sipped from her thermos, her eyes refocused, cold and intent, waiting for the next soul swept up in the silt of feeling to sink willingly into these waters of hers, so full of temptation and danger.
And outside the window, the Yellow River ran muddy as ever, surging silently eastward, mirroring the countless small and endless wars of the human world — over love and desire, over giving up and getting back.
...
The livestream ran until three in the afternoon.
Yao Yuan rubbed her stiff neck and shoulders, gave her assistant a few quick instructions about what came next, then shuffled back to her office, kneading her aching lower back. The door clicked shut behind her. She unloaded herself into the sofa, too tired to keep up even a shred of composure. In the darkness behind her closed eyes, what flickered was the light and shadow of the livestream, the comments, and all the subtext she'd left unspoken. A single thought rose up, clear and cold as iron: when, exactly, would women finally learn to think like the winners of history? In the face of absolute stakes, to strip away that nagging softness and fantasy, and lock their eyes only on the bullseye of the target.
She thought of the similar shadows in history.
At Changban Slope, to flee fast and light, Liu Bei abandoned his wife and children and galloped off with only a few dozen riders. In the rout at Pengcheng, to shed weight and gain speed, Liu Bang kicked his own children off the carriage three times. Three times Xiahou Ying scooped them back up — and more than ten times the urge to kill rose in Liu Bang's heart.
You see, in a man's dream of conquering rivers and mountains, love and attachment are an ornament in good times and a burden in bad. Later generations are happy to drape them in the cloak of "seeing the bigger picture," but the so-called bigger picture is just another name for their own ambitions. The common people are the foundation, the people's hearts are the capital, and wife and children — once the great enterprise is nearly won, why worry about finding new ones?
They are permitted to be utterly self-serving, and crowned with the title of "heroic sovereign."
And women? Always wrapped in the sacred robes of "maternal love" and "the virtuous wife," expected to become the silent cornerstone, the sacrificial offering. History writes wisdom of trade-offs for men, and for women annotates only the virtue of devotion.
Yao Yuan buried her face in a throw pillow and laughed without a sound. The smile was thin and cool, like a sliver of sharp porcelain.
So what has never changed across the ages was never the human heart, but the ruler by which the human heart is measured.
This may be cruel, but it is the rule written by countless stories.
True clear-sightedness begins with disenchantment. Peel away the dreamy powder the world dusts over relationships, and the essence beneath is mostly the same: value exchange. If you are in full bloom, the breeze and the butterflies come on their own; if you are dim, even tenderness scatters easily into drifting clouds.
History bears witness: which of the women who climbed to the clouds of power didn't understand this deeply? Wu Zetian, Mi Yue, Xiaozhuang — wasn't the creed of their lives, all of them, "everything to be used by me, everything to empower me"? They saw through it early: useless lofty pride buys not an inch of progress; ambiguity can be a passage, connections can be a staircase. The key is this — to be within feeling yet unmoved by it, to own nothing yet to use everything.
This isn't cynicism. It's a profound realism. It doesn't teach you coldness; it teaches you discernment. It doesn't make you a plunderer; it makes you a magnet. When you yourself become an irreplaceable source of value, all that "networking upward" finally transforms into truly "walking side by side."
In her bright office, Yao Yuan let out a long breath. Light filtered through the glass and fell on her clasped fingertips, warm and quiet. She suddenly felt like someone lighting a single lamp in a deep river.
What it could illuminate, in the end, was only the few willing to open their eyes.
She could dissect the rules, could puncture the illusions, could spread that script — the one that has run silently for thousands of years — open under the light. But in the end, whether to pick it up, whether to dare to live by it: that was each viewer's own war. However clear-sighted she was, she could not wake a soul that chose to sleep; however sharp she was, she could not cut through the cocoon it had spent thousands of years winding around itself.
But the lamp stays lit, and someone will follow the light. At that thought, the thread of helplessness deep in her heart was suddenly replaced by a calm clarity.
One person cannot ferry everyone across. But a road of awakening begins the moment the first person opens their eyes.
At least, someone has woken up.
