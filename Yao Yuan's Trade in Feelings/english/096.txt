Chapter 96 — Once You've Seen Top-Tier Devotion, Your Standards Outrun Your Means
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640343.html

"The first operation on the table is complete." She faced the camera, her voice neither glad nor mournful, drawing a calm rest-stop at the end of a violent emotional dissection. "The year-end special of the 'Clear-Eyed Operating Table' continues. Little assistant, put the next caller through."
[Call connected]
The caller—a woman's voice, twenty-six, anxious and tangled, full of contradiction, reluctance, and self-confusion: "Teacher Yuanyuan, I'm honestly so lost. I want to ask you how on earth I'm supposed to choose a partner. My own circumstances are very ordinary—family, job, looks—nothing about me stands out. But my fatal problem is exactly that ordinariness, because I've experienced what 'not ordinary' is like."
She drew a deep breath, as if gathering the courage to touch something forbidden.
"A few years ago, by sheer chance, I dated a man worth billions, a real heavyweight in his circle. He was genuinely, incredibly generous with me—so lavish that I still can't believe it when I look back. While we were together he bought me a car, a house, made huge transfers, covered my every need—food, clothing, housing, travel, all of it. He doted on me beyond measure, tolerated everything, and he was serious—he openly said he wanted to marry me, that he was in it to build a life together. In that relationship I fully tasted what top-tier giving, top-tier favoritism, top-tier sincerity from a man feels like."
Her voice began to tremble, whether from longing or from pain:
"Later, because of certain practical and family reasons, we split. But for the past few years I've kept going on blind dates, trying to settle down. And now I'm completely warped—I can't take to anyone."
"The men my relatives and friends introduce me to honestly aren't bad: steady jobs, dependable temperaments, sincerely willing to marry and build a life. Everyone, my parents included, says I should be content, that I should treasure it."
"But I can't. The moment one of them quibbles over the price of a meal, I'm turned off. The moment they fail to read my moods precisely, I find them dull. The moment they mention mortgage pressure while planning the future, I feel suffocated. Every reaction I have, the measuring stick in my head, is unconsciously, wildly benchmarking against that heavyweight—against how cleanly he solved problems by just throwing money at them, against his forever-steady, forgiving calm, against that 'if the sky falls, I'll hold it up' sense of total cover, even against that unhesitating sincerity of 'I simply want to marry you.'"
"I know perfectly well what I'm worth. I know I can't reach that tier, that I have not the slightest ability to match that kind of life. But my eyes, my instincts, my threshold—they've been raised too spoiled to ever go back. Now I earn a few thousand a month myself, yet I think men on a million-a-year salary are shabby. Teacher Yuanyuan, I know my standards outrun my means, I know this is wrong, but I can't control that enormous psychological gap and disappointment. What on earth do I do? Have I ruined my whole life like this?"
[After a brief stunned silence, the scrolling comments surged like a burst dam:]
[The exact same illness! Once you've seen the top spec, ordinary people just don't register.]
[So real! Once you've eaten a royal banquet, how do you go back to chaff and pickles?]
[The cruelest thing isn't never having had it—it's having had the best, then losing it.]
[Her own rank didn't rise, but a heavyweight dragged her taste and threshold sky-high.]
[A heavyweight gives "no worries about survival" love; ordinary people give "above-survival" love. Different dimensions.]
[Wake up! The treatment a heavyweight gives is the heavyweight's ability, not her standard package!]
[One jackpot win, and she mistakes herself for someone born to live a billionaire's life.]
Yao Yuan listened to the whole thing coolly, with no sympathy, then gave a light laugh, the smile cold, clear, and see-through:
"Let me first puncture all your self-delusions—plenty of women crash at this exact step in the emotional market. Your biggest problem right now is that you've confused 'the other person's ability' with 'your own standard-package value.'"
"Let me run a brutal cognitive disinfection on you first." Her fingertips met out of habit, bracing into a steady triangle, her gaze like a surgical shadowless lamp lighting up every corner of self-deceit.
"That heavyweight you describe—the 'generosity, wealth, total cover, firm intent to marry' he displayed—is his baseline cultivation, a show of his ability, his way of operating. It is not a deserved, sustainable reward that you earned by reaching some peak of personal value."
"It's like this: an ordinary person wanders by chance into a private museum and is allowed to handle a priceless antique with their own hands. That experience is real; the touch is stunning. But the mistake is this—after leaving the museum, you start to find your family's heirloom porcelain bowl crude and unbearable, and you demand to know why every piece of porcelain on the market is unworthy of your touch."
"You've confused 'experience' with 'entitlement,' 'a chance encounter' with 'the norm.' Everything he gave is the basic operating rule of his world. And you were only a guest, invited in by chance for a brief tour. An admission ticket is not a deed of ownership."
[Dense waves of "got it" and "right in the heart" drifted across the comments.]
[An admission ticket is not a deed of ownership! That line is worth a million!]
[His world's baseline rules, my world's ceiling experience.]
[So the pain comes from mistaking someone else's starting line for your own passing grade.]
"Everything you 'can't take to,' everything that 'turns you off,' all your 'pickiness,'" Yao Yuan went on at an even pace, yet bearing down word by word, like a hammer driving nails into wood, "stems from this: you are using an extreme set of standards about 'love and giving'—standards drawn from a top-tier survival system—to harshly judge one healthy, normal sample after another, each running inside an ordinary survival system."
"The love of ordinary people is, at its core, crossing over together. It's two limited pools of resources added together to fend off risk; it's two imperfect temperaments grinding into each other to find a comfort zone; it's the slow-flowing companionship and the carefully budgeted future. Its keyword is 'we, together.'"
"The love of top-tier people is, at its core, giving. It's using his surplus resources to cover your shortfalls; it's using his powerful system to absorb your fluctuations; it's one-sided lifting and bestowing. Its keyword is 'I give you.'"
"You've experienced the latter's 'giving mode,' and now you can no longer adapt to the former's 'crossing-over mode.' But here's the fatal cognitive trap: you think your inability to adapt is because 'crossing-over mode' isn't good enough. In reality, it's because you yourself do not hold the long-term entry pass or the playing ability for the 'giving mode' game. That one time, the system issued you a trial card. The card expired, and the game was supposed to end. Yet you stubbornly stand in the arcade doorway, convinced that every other amusement in the world is unbearably boring."
[The clever metaphor detonated the comments again.]
[Crossing-over mode vs giving mode! Instantly crystal clear!]
[A trial-card player mistaking herself for a lifetime VIP.]
[So it's not that ordinary people give too little—it's that what she wants is no longer the kind ordinary people can give.]
"The essence of your 'standards outrunning your means' is that your own value coordinate is still parked on the ordinary plain, while the threshold of your emotional experience was hauled, in one irreversible stroke, up to the clouds." Yao Yuan's analysis cut deeper, cold and precise. "The reality you won't face is this: that top-spec relationship was an extremely fluky moment in your life—a brief compatibility, a downward projection from a high-dimensional system onto a low-dimensional individual. It's like the sun occasionally focusing its light on a single grain of sand. The grain blazes in an instant, but that brilliance belongs to the sun's power, not the grain's essence. The sun moves away, and the grain is a grain again—yet it can no longer tolerate the brightness of an ordinary candle flame."
"Your contradiction is this: retreat to the plain, and you can no longer stand the ordinary scenery; fly to the clouds, and you have no wings. You're stuck in between, using the standard of the clouds to despise everything on the plain, while forgetting that your own two feet have never truly left the ground."
[The comments filled with painful resonance and awakening.]
[A downward projection from a high-dimensional system onto a low-dimensional individual... so precise, it hurts to the bone.]
[The sun and grain of sand metaphor—incredible, I am that dazed grain.]
[So the problem isn't high standards; it's that her own "dimension" didn't rise, yet she wants "high-dimensional" treatment.]
"So, how do we treat this?" Yao Yuan's tone shifted into a clear, cold, hard solution mode, as if writing a prescription.
"Plan A: total transformation, match upward. Stop all self-pity and fantasy. Recognize that the one truly useful thing that encounter left you is not consumer habits, not aesthetic pickiness, but a fleeting glimpse of 'how the top tier of the world operates,' and a powerful inner drive of 'I will at least move toward that direction.' Convert all your unwillingness into terrifying self-discipline: work yourself to the bone improving your professional skills until you're a leader in your field; ferociously accumulate social capital and cognitive depth; hone your character and your scope. Make your own value truly worthy of the spoiled eye you've been given. This road is brutally hard, but it's the only cure that gets to the root—if you still want to touch that world."
"Plan B: system reset, put down roots. If you assess yourself and conclude you can't endure Plan A's suffering, that you lack the fate and the grit for it, then you must perform a complete formatting reset on your 'spoiled' emotional system. Actively, consciously, set out to understand, learn, even 'downgrade-adapt' to ordinary people's 'crossing-over mode.' Learn to appreciate the solidity of two people saving up to buy a house together; learn to feel the intimacy of solving one small headache as a pair; learn to discover the tiny romances in ordinary days. This requires you to fully set down the obsession with 'being given' and learn the joy of 'creating together.' This road is a compromise, but for you it may be the more pragmatic choice, the one more likely to bring peace."
"The stupidest, most painful path, doomed to end with nothing, is to stay in your current state: guarding the body of the plain while dreaming the dreams of the clouds, using the standard of the clouds to curse everything in front of you. That is self-imposed punishment."
Yao Yuan's voice settled at the end, carrying an almost cruel calm—the calm of one who has lifted the veil on the truth:
"Stop using that heavyweight's cultivation and ability as a ruler to measure every ordinary man who sincerely wants to build a life with you. It isn't fair, and it's utterly meaningless."
"You've seen the light. That's good. That beam of light should become the fuel that drives you to run forward, to try to shine on your own—not the reason you can never again appreciate, never again belong to, ordinary lamplight."
"Remember: top-tier experiences are meant to broaden your vision, not raise your threshold; meant to make you reflect on yourself, not despise the masses. When you yourself become a steady, glowing light source, you'll naturally attract—and deserve—another beam of light, one that is real and lasting in its favor."
[Call ended]
Cold white light filled the studio. Yao Yuan sat ramrod straight, her face calm and clear, her voice steady and sharp: "The biggest mistake many women make in this life isn't never meeting a good man. It's seeing the top spec once, never raising their own rank, yet letting their eye be spoiled. That's exactly how so many people end up with standards that outrun their means."
The broadcast went on. Outside, the northern winter dusk had long since been swallowed by thick night; the bitter wind was sealed off by heavy glass, leaving only a muffled moan. The city's neon ran riot with color, blurring into a cold, flowing brilliance on the window behind her, like an unreal backdrop cut off from the rational world inside. She sat upright before the camera. Hours of continuous output across the livestream had left not a trace of fatigue on her exquisitely made-up face—instead, it had refined her into a stillness so complete it became detachment.
