Chapter 81 — The Child She Lost Eight Years Ago: A Truth Arrived Late, a Wound Still Open
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640328.html

The visit to Old Chen came on the first weekend after that podcast interview wrapped. Autumn was settling in. The sky was a high, clear, pale blue, and the wind had shed its sticky summer heat for something crisp and cool. Yao Yuan and Shuai Hongqiang agreed to meet near the Hall of Antiquities and go on together. Even though the Mirror Pact had forcibly bound them back together inside some strange, unreadable knot of fate eight years after their breakup, traveling side by side like this still carried a deliberately maintained calm — an undercurrent moving beneath a still surface. These days they were more like partners thrown together by an unsolved case: a shared goal, but each with their own wounds and their own calculations.

Yao Yuan had prepared carefully for the visit. Besides the pre-Qingming Longjing tea she'd noticed Old Chen favored last time, she had gone out of her way to trade a collector for a fine Qing-dynasty Hetian jade "river-stone" carving — "Abundance Year After Year." The jade was warm and smooth as fat, the lotus leaves and carp carved in fluid, lively lines, the meaning auspicious, well suited for an elder to turn over in his hands. She wrapped the tea and the jade carefully and set them inside a plain rosewood box. The gift was partly a thank-you for the protection the "heart-guarding jade" had given her, and partly in keeping with the old courtesy — "You gave me a peach; I repay you with fine jade." More than that, it was simply how she conducted herself: thorough, complete.

Shuai Hongqiang had brought some tonics too, but next to hers they looked ordinary. His eyes caught for a moment on the box in Yao Yuan's hands, but he said nothing. The Yao Yuan of today was long past being the young woman who once needed him to weigh out his every gesture with care — afraid that giving too much would look desperate, that giving too little would seem insincere. Now her manner of giving was composed and certain, the natural confidence of someone whose money and standing were firmly in place.

Inside the Hall of Antiquities, that air of settled time still hung over everything. Old Chen was hale and sharp-eyed, and was glad to see them — especially glad to see the jade piece Yao Yuan had brought. He turned it over in his hands for a long while, praising it again and again, his eyes holding the pure appreciation a man feels for fine jade, with no trace of greed. That detachment only deepened Yao Yuan's respect for him.

The three of them sat at the tea table, the delicate fragrance of tea curling up. Yao Yuan spoke first, describing the changes of the four months since she'd put on the "heart-guarding jade." Her tone was even, as if reporting on a peculiar medical case. "...there's only been one time-travel, on the road back from the Gobi. It was very short — maybe less than a third of the earlier episodes. And after I came back, that feeling of having my soul scooped out, of being completely drained, was much lighter. There was still some discomfort, but I recovered quickly. Old Chen, this jade really does have a remarkable effect on settling the soul and steadying the mind." Without thinking she pressed a hand to her chest. Even through the fabric she could feel that soothing warmth — a rare, constant comfort in the middle of a turbulent fate.

Old Chen stroked his beard and nodded slowly. "Old jade has a spirit to it, and this kind — a 'heart-guarding' jade that's been worn close and nurtured over years — is best of all at steadying the soul and shutting out outside ills. Its weakening the Mirror Pact's direct pull on you is a good thing. But it's like building a dyke against a flood: the root cause isn't gone, so the danger remains. You can't let your guard down."

The conversation turned naturally to the lead on the descendants of the mirror-makers. Old Chen had called on a few of his most discreet old connections; Shuai Hongqiang had made inquiries through his own channels from many angles; and adding in the scraps of information Yao Yuan had gathered through the "Pretty Big One" network, all three lines, pulled together, pointed faintly toward a local Jinshi family with the surname "Wang." More specifically, the target had narrowed to a man in his seventies named "Wang Baoguo." From the fragments pieced together, the family scholarship seemed somewhat unusual — an ancestor may have been a sort of diviner or occultist — but it was all so long ago that the lineage had vanished into obscurity. Wang Baoguo himself lived in seclusion, scarcely dealing with the outside world. For now there was only a hazy name and an approximate age; his exact address and contact information were still being painstakingly traced, as if some invisible barrier stood in the way.

"Jinshi isn't large, but it isn't small either. To find an old man who's deliberately gone nameless and lives hidden away — it's like fishing a needle out of the sea." Old Chen looked at Yao Yuan, his gaze meant to soothe. "Girl of the Yao family, don't be anxious. Narrowing it down to Jinshi is already major progress. As long as the heart-guarding jade keeps working, you've bought yourself some time. We'll keep searching separately. The truth will come out in the end."

Shuai Hongqiang spoke up too, his tone unusually earnest. "Old Chen is right. Yuan— Yao Yuan, don't fret too much. Everything on my end is going smoothly. A few of the company's projects are moving along even better than I expected, and things at home are peaceful." He paused, his voice dropping, carrying a complicated kind of awe. "Because of the Mirror Pact... you wear yourself out time-traveling, and I... I've been on the receiving end of the good fortune it converts. I've kept Old Chen's instructions in mind the whole time, never daring to slack off, always reminding myself to do more good, to hold a sense of reverence." As he said it, his eyes met Yao Yuan's for an instant. The feeling in them was heavy — there was gratitude, there seemed to be guilt too, and most of all a solemnity, the gravity of a man seized by an unseen force and forced to look his fate full in the face.

Yao Yuan gave a slight nod and said nothing. She couldn't feel any genuine happiness over Shuai Hongqiang's "smooth sailing" — it had been bought with her own mental wear and risk, and the taste of it was complicated. But rationally she understood that there was no use dwelling on it.

A few rounds of tea, and once everything that needed saying had been said, the two of them rose to take their leave. Old Chen saw them to the door of the Hall of Antiquities. The autumn sun was just right, slanting across the bluestone path.

For the short walk to where the cars were parked, the usual silence sat between them. There was only the soft sound of footsteps. Yao Yuan's car was a little ahead, Shuai Hongqiang's behind it. And just as Yao Yuan was about to open her car door — she didn't know which nerve had been touched, didn't know how long the emotion had been building before it found one fine crack — she suddenly turned and looked at Shuai Hongqiang, a few steps away.

The autumn wind passed over her, lifting a stray wisp of hair at her cheek. The expression on her face was faint, faint as that thread of thin cloud in the sky just then, and her voice was eerily calm, as if she were mentioning some old news that had nothing to do with her.

"By the way. There's something I never told you." She paused, her gaze brushing over Shuai Hongqiang's face — momentarily blank — and fixing on the empty distance. "Eight years ago, before I left Jinshi, I was carrying your child."

Time seemed in that instant to freeze, to stretch out, to warp.

Every expression on Shuai Hongqiang's face — that lingering trace of thought about the Mirror Pact lead, the complicated attention he paid Yao Yuan, even the practiced shrewdness and composure of a businessman — all of it shattered and broke apart in a heartbeat. He looked as if he hadn't heard clearly, and also as if a sledgehammer had slammed into his chest. His pupils contracted sharply, his lips moved, but no sound came out. He simply stood frozen, staring blankly at Yao Yuan, as if seeing her for the first time — or rather, seeing for the first time the deeply buried, blood-soaked truth of that relationship eight years ago.

Yao Yuan's tone still held no ripple, carried even a thread of detachment, as if none of it concerned her, but every word fell like an ice-pick driven into Shuai Hongqiang's abruptly stilled heart.

"Eight years ago, the day I made that last call to you, standing in the hospital corridor... You never once gave a clear answer about marrying me, about registering, about getting married. You'd only put me off, go silent, change the subject — say things like 'no rush,' 'let's think it over.' I understood."

She tugged at the corner of her mouth. It wasn't a smile, only an extremely faint, self-mocking pull of muscle.

"So, that first snow of that winter eight years ago, I had the procedure alone. And then I went to Hangzhou."

Having said it, she didn't look again at Shuai Hongqiang's face — the color drained from it in an instant, pale as paper — nor at those eyes flooding all at once with vast, stunned pain, bewilderment, and something close to collapse. She crisply pulled open the car door, slid into the driver's seat, started the engine, and the car glided smoothly out of the space and merged into the street's flow of traffic, without the slightest pause.

It left Shuai Hongqiang standing there like a clay statue suddenly drained of its soul, frozen under the bright but heatless early-autumn sun. The noise of passing cars, the faint sound of distant voices — all of it seemed to come through a thick layer of frosted glass, blurred, warped, unreal. Only Yao Yuan's words, calm to the point of cruelty, detonated in his mind with perfect clarity, word by word, echoing over and over —

"...I was carrying your child..."

"...you never once gave a clear answer about marrying me..."

"...I had the procedure alone..."

...

Eight years ago. He'd made a success of himself, but in his bones that timidity, born of childhood poverty, clung to the bottom of his heart like damp lichen that no sun could dry. Yao Yuan's beauty was breathtaking, and so bright it left him nowhere to hide. She was the fiercest flower blooming in his barren world, dew and thorns and all — he loved to look at her, and was afraid of being pricked. He wavered, unable to decide to marry her, unwilling to let her go. The suppressed catch in her voice on that last call — he'd heard it, but had chosen to read it only as a beautiful, tearful coercion, pushing him to make a decision.

He'd thought the greatest harm could be no more than that: making her sad, watching her turn and walk away wrapped in that dazzling light of hers, like a band of burning sunset clouds at the edge of the sky he could never reach again — once they burned out, the sky went dark. On certain late nights after social obligations, he'd think of those burning clouds, and feel a hollow, scorched-after ache rise in his chest, and then tell himself: this is reality.

He had never once imagined — had never dared to imagine — that behind those silences and that catching breath was hidden a small life just conceived and just as quickly extinguished. Hidden there was her despair as she lay alone on a cold operating table. Hidden there was her resolve, after that double agony of body and heart, to leave for a distant city for good.

If he had known she was pregnant...

The supposition was like a branding iron heated red, searing hard against his nerves.

If he had known... he would certainly have married her!

He would have held this thorned flower, this flower carrying his own blood, close against his chest — firmly, even clumsily! He might still have lost his nerve, but he could never have been so cold-blooded as to let her face alone that abyss of flesh torn from flesh. He would have used the fragile contract of marriage, that single sheet of paper, to build for her at least the most basic social fence; whether the days inside that fence were sweet or bitter would be a matter for later, another flavor a long life had to chew on — anything better than leaving her alone on an operating table, chewing on cold steel and desperate solitude.

But there was no "if."

She had called, and he had not given her the firm answer she needed. So she had used her own way — cruelly, completely — to cut off every possibility, and to bury that unformed child, and the last thread of tenderness between them.

Eight years. For eight whole years she had kept this secret, watching him marry and have a child, watching his "happy" family, watching him rise and fall in business — even, after the matter of the Mirror Pact, watching him live "smoothly" thanks to her possible "sacrifice"...

No wonder. No wonder there was always that inescapable cold and distance in her eyes when she looked at him — not merely the estrangement after a breakup, but something steeped in blood and pain she had borne alone. No wonder she could never truly let it go where he was concerned, holding to a clear line even while they worked together to find the mirror-makers' descendants.

Enormous guilt rose like a surging black tide and drowned him in an instant. Mixed into it was the agony eight years late, the loathing for his own cowardly, muddled younger self, the helpless heartache that Yao Yuan had borne it all alone, and a deep, absurd grief at how fate toys with people. He thought of how he'd just been saying "everything's going smoothly," "do more good," "hold a sense of reverence"... Now those words were the sharpest mockery, cutting him until nothing was left whole. His "smooth sailing" might truly be built, in part, on her pain and on the life of a child he had never met!

He staggered, catching the cold body of the car beside him to barely steady himself. The sun was glaring, yet he felt cold all over, cold enough that the very marrow of his bones trembled. His stomach churned, nausea rising to his throat. He wanted to chase after her, to grab Yao Yuan and ask her clearly, to roar, to apologize, to do something... but what could he do? Eight years. Seas turned to fields. That child had long since turned to dust; Yao Yuan was no longer the Yao Yuan of back then; and he had a family responsibility he could not shrug off. Everything was beyond recall.

So it turned out: some debts, once owed, truly cannot be repaid. Some wrongs, once committed, truly cannot be made right. The mysterious tie of the Mirror Pact might be severed by some mystical means; but this blood-debt, this debt of the heart from eight years ago, had long since been branded deep into the rings of both their lives — a hidden scar that could never truly heal.

Yao Yuan's car had long since vanished around the corner. Shuai Hongqiang still stood rooted in place. The autumn wind blew across a back that seemed in that moment to have stooped a little, lifting a few strands of hair at his brow, baring beneath them a pair of eyes gone vacant and bloodshot, brimming with pain, remorse, and a boundless emptiness. This man, always so shrewd and capable in business, now looked as if every support had been pulled out from under him — nothing left but a shell shattered by the truth.

He knew that, from this moment on, some things were utterly different. Toward Yao Yuan, toward himself, toward this karmic, cursed bond the Mirror Pact had forcibly wound around the two of them. And that key which had unlocked all of it — that blood-colored truth, eight years late — would coil around the rest of his life like the heaviest of shackles, weighed down with enormous guilt and helplessness.

Eight years of silence, like a deep well, dark and lightless, that she'd thought was long since sealed shut. And yet on this ordinary afternoon — autumn sun bright, the tea fragrance about to fade, in the instant she turned to open the car door — that sentence had slipped from her lips, light as air, almost without any emotion at all. So quickly that even she had been startled for a moment.

Having said it, she didn't look at Shuai Hongqiang's face. She got straight into the car. The engine murmured low, the body of the car slid smoothly out, and that frozen figure was quickly thrown into the rearview mirror, smaller and smaller, until at last it vanished around the corner.

Not until she merged into the traffic on the main road did that late, fine tremor finally crawl slowly up her spine. The fingers gripping the steering wheel had gone a little cold. Why say it? Why today?

The cityscape outside the window slid backward at an even pace, like a reel of film that had nothing to do with her. But across her mind, in disordered flashes, came many images.

Yes — perhaps it was because, just now in the Hall of Antiquities, hearing Shuai Hongqiang talk about how "smoothly" things were going, how he "held a sense of reverence," that look on his face — that mix of relief and caution born of being granted fate's extra favor — had stung her. That "smoothness" was like a polished mirror, reflecting the nights of these past four months when, even with the old jade pressed to her chest, she hadn't dared sleep deeply; reflecting the vertigo and palpitations that could drag her into some unknown time and space at any moment. Why should her torment be the background music to his fair winds and gentle rains? That unwillingness pent up in her chest was like a fine, poisonous vine that, without warning, suddenly put out a thorn.

Or perhaps it was that earlier scene, the chance encounter at the mall. Shuai Hongqiang feeding his young son, his gentle wife at his side, the warm picture of an ordinary family of three — like an extremely fine needle that, catching her off guard, pierced an old wound that had long since scabbed but never truly healed. The child's round, lovable, smiling face had stirred awake the darkest corner of her memory — where there was no smiling face, only cold instruments, ghastly white light, and, after a part of her body had been hollowed out, an endless dull pain and emptiness. What she had lost was not merely an embryo, but, in that moment, part of her naive trust in the very thing of "forging a bond of life with another person."

And then there was Yu Hao. A few months back, he had so matter-of-factly proposed that he wanted an "heir" carrying both their bloodlines — yet could offer no promise of a marriage certificate. That was not only a denial of her present worth; it was like a key, flinging open the floodgate of her memory with a clatter. So after all the turns and turns, in some men's eyes, she — Yao Yuan, this person herself — was still something that could be stripped apart, weighed in isolation, "used" selectively. The value of a womb, it seemed, always overrode the value of her soul. Thirty-seven years, and she'd been like a collector's piece picked up, set down, examined, appraised, over and over. Even though she'd worked desperately to set herself in gold and inlay herself with jade, to raise her own valuation, that humiliation of "being chosen" was like a tumor fused to the bone, never truly gone. All her "upward socializing," all her "fighting to grow stronger," that almost ferocious drive to seize control of her own life — what was it if not a wild rebellion against this passive fate? In her livestream room, she dissected human nature and relationships down to the raw flesh, teaching girls to be clear-eyed, independent, even "calculating." Those words, sharp as scalpels, were every one of them survival-fragments she had picked up and polished with her own hands from a road of bloody, fumbling trial and error. How she wished those girls could live forever inside a rose-colored bubble, and never have to understand any of this. But she knew the bubble always bursts. The girls who at first were frightened by her "naked truths," who denounced her for "twisted values," often became her most devoted followers after they'd run head-on into a wall and split their own heads open. Having never tasted another's suffering, don't preach virtue at them; and even having tasted suffering, you may not match their grace.

Life's lessons are always carved deepest into the bone when you receive them firsthand.

The car climbed onto the Yellow River Bridge, and the view opened up all at once. The setting sun was molten gold, plating a tender illusion over the city's sharp-edged buildings.

There was one more thought, fainter still, one she herself didn't want to examine too closely, that perhaps had also driven those words from her mouth just now. Since getting together with Zhao Yiming, a certain biological pendulum, the kind tied to age, seemed to be ticking more clearly inside her. Rational as she was, even she would — on some exhausted late night, before the bathroom mirror — run her fingertip across her flat stomach, across the lines just beginning to show with the years that even expensive skincare couldn't fully smooth, across the warm, smooth edge of the "heart-guarding jade," and feel an extremely complicated wistfulness. In it was a hidden anxiety about aging, a vague imagining of "the possibility of another life," even a fleeting, momentary glimpse toward a "normal" life's path. This murky tangle of thought, the glaring picture of Shuai Hongqiang's complete family she'd faced today, the cold memory of deciding alone eight years ago, the contempt of Yu Hao reducing her to an "excellent gene-carrier" — all of it churned together and fermented into an impulse beyond naming.

Look — you have a virtuous wife and a filial son now, complete and proper. But do you know what your present completeness is built upon?

She hadn't said that sentence out loud, but the revelation of the secret was itself like dropping a depth charge. The force of the blast wasn't in the moment; it was in the silently spreading, ceaselessly eroding shockwave. She wanted him to remember it — wanted him, in every "smooth" moment, to feel faintly that beneath it lay a hole that could never be filled. Maybe that wasn't rational, wasn't "refined," was even a little childishly cruel; but that was her truest reaction in that instant, not coldly calculated, springing purely from the complicated depths of human nature.

What a complicated thing human nature is. She could calmly dissect the emotional labyrinths of millions of people, could plan the most advantageous career path for herself, could refuse to yield an inch in a business negotiation — and yet still couldn't fully untangle all the dark whirlpools in the depths of her own heart. In those whirlpools were unhealed old wounds, the embers of unwillingness, hidden longings, and a malice and grief, sudden and inexplicable, that even she couldn't understand.

She pressed the brake and stopped at a red light. She raised a hand and looked at her own neatly trimmed nails in their exquisite polish. These hands had signed contracts worth millions, had clasped the hands of top-tier collaborators, and on countless late nights had gripped tight the cold edge of a blanket. They looked strong, steady, in control of everything.

But only she knew that, in the instant she spoke that secret, the faint, icy tremor at her fingertips was real.

The light turned green. She stepped on the gas again, and pressed all of it — the surging, complicated weakness and sharpness that belonged to Yao Yuan — back down, steadily, into that fathomless sea at the bottom of her heart. The car accelerated, driving without a backward glance toward the deepening dusk, toward the unknown tomorrow where she had to go on, clear-eyed, moving forward.
