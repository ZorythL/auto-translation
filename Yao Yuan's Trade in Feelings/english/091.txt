A Romantic Wedding That Happened to Fall on Christmas
Amid the fullness of busy work and the suspense of unresolved waiting, time slid toward the end of the year. Winter in Jinshi was bitterly cold, but the Christmas mood had long since seeped through shop windows, street lights, and the faint anticipation on people's faces, driving off some of the chill and draping the city in a warm, tender filter.
December twenty-fourth, Christmas Eve, was also the day Liu Qingzhuo and Chen Ye were to be married.
Months earlier, Liu Qingzhuo had come to Yao Yuan's company specifically to spend hours in detailed consultation over a prenuptial agreement. Yao Yuan had assigned the sharpest team from the company's legal department. From the real estate, equity, and investment funds in Liu Qingzhuo's name, to the complex asset structures that the Chen family business might involve, all the way down to the final ownership and form of conversion of the bride-price and dowry — every detail, however small, was weighed line by line. The agreement's core goal was clear and cool-headed: within the bounds of the legal framework and social convention, to maximize the security of Liu Qingzhuo's personal property and her future economic autonomy within the marriage. Chen Ye proved unusually generous and at ease about all of it, accepting almost the entire thing wholesale. Behind that readiness lay the confidence his family's strength afforded him, and a clear-eyed recognition of the true nature of this marriage of two powerful houses. In the end, driven jointly by both families, the wedding date was set for this festive Christmas Eve.
The wedding was held in the banquet hall of Jinshi's most exclusive hotel. Crystal chandeliers poured down like a river of stars, and tens of thousands of air-freighted Ecuadorian roses interwove with white-and-green Christmas decorations to create a setting both lavish and dreamily romantic. Guests gathered in a glittering crowd of silk and perfume; more than half of the prominent figures from Jinshi's political and business circles had come, proof enough of the roots and connections of the Liu and Chen families. It was a celebration blessed by all sides — and one that tacitly acknowledged its essence as an alliance of equals.
Yao Yuan attended together with Zhao Yiming. She had chosen a pearl-white velvet strapless gown, minimally cut yet perfectly tracing the lines of her figure, over which she wore a heavy black cashmere coat. Her long hair was loosely pinned up, and at her throat she wore only a slender diamond necklace, with star-blue sapphire studs to match. She was understated, refined wealth from head to toe — complementing the bride's dazzling radiance, yet carrying a presence impossible to ignore. Zhao Yiming wore a classic black tuxedo. He stood tall, the cold technical edge about him softened by the close-fitting cut, lending him a rare touch of formality and reserve. When the two of them appeared hand in hand, they made a strikingly matched pair and drew many eyes.
Her old friend Jin Lan had come too, with that gym-owner boyfriend, Lu Ang, at her side. Lu Ang was young and handsome, his physique impeccably maintained, and standing beside the brilliant, fully commanding Jin Lan, he looked like a pretty vase carefully kept on display. Jin Lan wore a wine-red velvet gown with a deep V, her hair in curls and her lips red, exuding charm in every gesture, completely unguarded in her intimacy with Lu Ang. Yao Yuan also spotted Gao Jieyuan; as Chen Ye's childhood friend, he was naturally on the guest list, accompanied by a girl who looked no more than twenty-one or twenty-two — exquisitely made up and clinging sweetly to him — his new girlfriend. When Gao Jieyuan caught sight of Yao Yuan, his gaze flickered, and then, even more deliberately, he wrapped an arm around his little girlfriend's waist and bent to whisper in her ear, his posture intimate, as if silently announcing something. Yao Yuan simply pretended not to notice and nodded at him with a smile.
Wang Qian, too, was among the invited. As a banker, he was smooth and well-versed in every social grace; his bearing was impeccable, and his smile when he greeted Yao Yuan was the same as ever — the familiar courtesy between old friends. At this point, Yao Yuan still knew him only as a banker friend who had given her no small measure of support and convenience in both work and life. She had no idea at all that he was also the son of old Mr. Wang Baoguo — the very man who had revealed to her the truth of the Mirror Pact and set the appointment by the Yellow River. Wang Qian kept that secret sealed tight. And so, beneath his customary friendliness, the look he gave Yao Yuan hid a complicated scrutiny and quiet worry known only to himself — he knew what had happened to her, yet he said nothing, only exchanging pleasantries as usual.
The wedding ceremony was warm and grand. As Liu Qingzhuo, wearing a crown set with the family's heirloom jadeite and diamonds and a haute-couture gown worth a fortune, walked toward Chen Ye on her father's arm, Yao Yuan watched quietly. She saw the longing for the life ahead in Liu Qingzhuo's eyes, and she saw the practiced tenderness and certainty on Chen Ye's face. This was a union blessed by all, thoroughly calculated, well-matched in standing — love perhaps present, but by no means the only or whole foundation. There was no ripple in Yao Yuan's heart, only the calm of witnessing a "project successfully delivered."
When it came time to throw the bouquet, a crowd of unmarried women stepped forward, laughing. Yao Yuan had been standing somewhat to the back, but Jin Lan laughingly pushed her into the fray. Whether by coincidence or by the bride's design, that bouquet of lily of the valley — symbol of "happiness passed on" — traced a graceful arc through the air and fell, neither too far nor too near, straight into Yao Yuan's arms.
The soft lily petals carried a crisp, cool fragrance. Almost caught off guard, with a flicker of dazed surprise, Yao Yuan instinctively clutched the bouquet. Then her gaze cut across the silk, perfume, and glittering lights and found, instantly and precisely, Zhao Yiming.
He stood there. Unlike the other male guests, he neither cheered nor reached for a camera; he only watched her quietly, his eyes deep behind his lenses, flowing under the banquet hall's brilliant lights with an indescribable, gentle, intent glow. There was no teasing in that gaze, no urging — only a still, patient waiting, and a barely perceptible, restrained anticipation that belonged to him.
Rational as he was, Zhao Yiming naturally understood the social symbolism of the "bouquet." The concept of "marriage" that the symbol pointed to had, because of Yao Yuan's existence, gone from a vague sociological model to a concrete, even worthwhile prospect that could be assessed. Through his mind flashed the precisely running "Light-Traced" tree they had created together in the "Mirror Room," her tightly reasoned yet deeply empathetic speech at the launch, and the undying spark of curiosity about the world and human nature in her eyes after countless late-night discussions. Building a family with her was, to him, the optimal life-partnership plan that reason had deduced — and a clear choice of his own inclination as well.
He needed her, admired her, trusted her, and longed to make this state of standing side by side lasting and formalized. Seeing the bouquet that symbolized "passing on" fall into her hands, what rose in his heart was a certainty mixed with "this is only right," and a trace of anticipation, waiting for his partner's final confirmation. His reaction was not excitement, but a deeper consideration and preparation.
Yao Yuan's heart, in that moment, gave a very light flutter.
It was not pure joy or shyness, but something complicated, a current stirring beneath the ice. Reason told her that marriage was only a piece of paper, a way of binding a social relationship, perhaps even a high-risk investment. She had seen too much scheming, betrayal, and attrition; she herself had once rebuilt a fortress of reason on the ruins of feeling. One of the underlying logics of the "Pretty Big One" AI model she had built for herself and for countless women was precisely to help people assess relationships clearly and avoid blind investment. Marriage, emotionally speaking, had come to seem to her an option she could take or leave.
But in the instant the bouquet landed in her arms, in the split second her eyes met Zhao Yiming's, some corner of her heart that reason had sealed away tight seemed to crack open a faint, fine fissure. A weak but real longing — one that belonged to "feeling" — quietly poked its head out.
That longing was not for the wedding ceremony itself, but a hidden yearning for a kind of "certainty" and of being "firmly chosen." It was knowing full well that the world was chaos, human nature complex, the road ahead unknowable — and still longing for someone who could see, through all the carefully built armor of reason and survival strategy, the soul that might still feel uneasy, still be fragile, still hope to be properly cherished, and who would be willing to give a clear, solemn, calculation-free promise. To be chosen not because she was "useful" or "suitable," but simply because she was "her" — chosen without hesitation, and with a willingness to face all the uncertainty of the future together.
That longing surprised even her, and a touch of self-mockery rose right after. Was she really regressing the longer she lived? She quickly composed herself and, from across the room, openly lifted the bouquet in her hand toward Zhao Yiming, her lips curving into a flawless smile poised somewhere between a joke and an easy candor. Zhao Yiming smiled faintly too and gave her a light nod. Everything understood without a word, and yet as if nothing had been said at all.
The wedding banquet began. Yao Yuan, Zhao Yiming, Jin Lan and Lu Ang, Wang Qian, Gao Jieyuan and his little girlfriend, along with several close friends, were seated at the same table.
The table was lively in the extreme. Lu Ang was young, and a few drinks in, he couldn't help draping an arm over Jin Lan's shoulder and asking, eyes bright: "Sis Lan, look how grand this wedding is! When are we going to do one too? When the time comes I'll throw you the most spectacular one!" A doting smile on her face, Jin Lan tapped his forehead with a finger and spoke as if coaxing a child: "All right, all right — when you're a little more mature and you've grown the gym into a nationwide chain, we'll do it, okay?" The words humored him, but in her eyes lay a clear-eyed indifference. Ten years apart in age, with different experiences and life stages, she enjoyed the passion and companionship of this relationship, but had never truly factored "marriage" into any future plan with Lu Ang. To her, this was an adults' game — each taking what they needed, enjoying the moment. Talk of marriage would only weigh it down.
On the other side, Gao Jieyuan all but wrote the word "deliberate" across his face. He attentively peeled shrimp for his little girlfriend, served her dishes, asked in low tones whether the food was to her taste, and gazed at her tenderly now and then, as if lost in fresh love. But his eyes would always drift, with seeming casualness, toward Yao Yuan, carrying a faint, hard-to-spot edge of rivalry and proof — see, I'm doing just fine without you, loved and loving in return. Yao Yuan only found it amusing, while her own manner stayed perfectly polite and at just the right distance. She talked quietly with Zhao Yiming, occasionally placing a dish he liked into his bowl, her posture naturally intimate — a silent answer to all of Gao Jieyuan's childish theatrics.
Wang Qian was unusually quiet today, simply drinking in silence. Only, the number of times he glanced toward Yao Yuan seemed slightly greater than usual, and his expression more complicated. There was the heaviness of one who knew a secret, worry over a friend's situation, and perhaps a trace of awe at an unknown fate. Several times he raised his glass as if he wanted to say something to Yao Yuan alone, but in the end it dissolved into a deeper silence, and he drained the cup. Yao Yuan sensed that he seemed weighed down tonight, but took it for trouble at work or at home and thought nothing more of it, only returning a caring smile when he looked her way.
The bride and groom came around to toast. By the time they reached this table, the mood grew warmer still. Liu Qingzhuo had changed into her toasting dress, glowing with radiance, with Chen Ye attentively at her side. When she toasted Yao Yuan and Zhao Yiming, Liu Qingzhuo teased with a smile: "Boss Yao, Boss Zhao, you've caught the bouquet — so isn't it about time we drank at your wedding? When are you two holding yours? We're all waiting!"
The rest of the table immediately joined the chorus, Jin Lan most of all, laughing the most brilliantly. Gao Jieyuan's smile turned a little stiff. Lu Ang chimed in noisily too: "Boss Zhao, Boss Yao, get on with it!"
Holding his glass, amid the cheering, Zhao Yiming turned to look at Yao Yuan. His gaze seemed especially serious under the lights, with hardly any teasing in it, and he said, clear and steady: "I'm ready anytime. As long as Yao Yuan agrees, we could go register the marriage tomorrow."
The moment the words were out, the cheering swelled louder. Yao Yuan hadn't expected him to put it so directly. However much of a joke it might be, the earnestness in his tone was unmistakable. Her heart skipped a beat, but a perfectly judged blush flew up on her face in an instant (perhaps a touch of it real). She gave Zhao Yiming's arm a light, reproachful pat and laughed to the newlyweds: "Don't listen to his nonsense! In his dreams — where do you find a bargain that cheap? He hasn't even proposed yet, and already he wants to trick someone into registering?" With a charming pout and a joke, she deftly defused this subtle, probing moment, giving Zhao Yiming his face while holding to her own pace. Hearing this, Zhao Yiming only smiled indulgently and let it go, tipping his head back to finish his glass. In that smile lay a knowing tacit understanding, and a hidden mood in no hurry to rush things.
Today was a good day — a close friend's wedding, and Christmas Eve besides. The mood was warm, and naturally the drinks flowed more freely than usual. Yao Yuan was in good spirits and, rarely for her, relaxed, downing a few extra glasses of champagne and red wine. By the time she left the table for the private restroom of the VIP suite, her cheeks already felt hot and her steps a little unsteady, though her mind was still fairly clear.
In the corridor she ran into Wang Qian, who had stepped out for some air.
"President Wang, out here for some peace and quiet too?" Yao Yuan teased with a smile.
Wang Qian looked at her, his gaze lingering a moment on her wine-flushed face, on the verge of speaking but holding back. In the end he only said, in the tone of an old friend with a touch of worry: "You've had quite a lot to drink. Lately… is everything all right?"
Something stirred in Yao Yuan's heart; thinking he meant work, she laughed: "I'm fine — thanks for the concern, old friend. It's just busy at year's end. I've drunk a bit too hard, so I'm off to the restroom to clear my head."
As she turned to go, Wang Qian took two steps after her and called out low: "Yao Yuan…" She turned back, looking at him in puzzlement. Wang Qian gazed at her, his eyes flickering bright and dim under the lights, holding worry and hesitation, until it all dissolved into a barely audible sigh. He shook his head and forced a smile: "It's nothing… drink a little less." Yao Yuan nodded: "Thank you for caring, old friend." She turned and walked away, able to feel that gaze behind her not moving for a long time. Before long, Wang Qian, unable to hold his liquor, was collected early by his wife.
Yao Yuan splashed cold water on her face and looked at herself in the mirror — cheeks flushed, eyes bright and flowing. The woman in the mirror was beautiful and successful, with an enviable career and a seemingly perfect partner. Yet only she herself knew the heavy waiting in the depths of her heart over the Mirror Pact, and the complicated state of mind, woven of reason and feeling, about the ultimate destination of her emotions.
She let out a soft breath of wine-warmed air and prepared to touch up her lipstick.
Just as her fingertips were about to reach the lipstick tube —
The surface of the mirror, without the slightest warning, rippled outward in a ring.
The familiar sensation of her soul peeling away washed over her. Her clear reflection in the mirror began to twist and blur.
"Ah…" She let out an instinctive cry, her hand bracing weakly against the cold edge of the washstand. Everything before her — the restroom's gorgeous lights, the marble countertop, the clamor of the wedding in her ears — rapidly drained of color, stretched out, and was dragged into an endless whirlpool. At last her body slumped soft into the cushioned chair before the vanity mirror.
The eighth time.
Before the last shred of clarity vanished, a thought, cold to the point of near cruelty, flashed through her mind:
"This time, is it going to make me see clearly at which step 'I' once came within a hair of damnation? Or is it going to make me witness whether another choice can truly lead to happiness?"
On the night of a Christmas wedding that symbolized union, promise, and a new beginning — at the very moment reason and feeling had just held a brief, profound dialogue — she was once again flung out of the track of time by that ancient mirror entangled with her fate, plunging toward another time and space, forced to watch one scene after another of past "possibilities."
The thought had no answer. The darkness closed in, gentle and inexorable.
