Chapter 48 — The Price of the "Good Luck" Bought with the Mirror Pact
https://www.xbanxia.cc/books/417648/72640295.html

On the first weekend after the New Year's holiday, Shuai Hongqiang asked Yao Yuan to meet at "South Lake Cottage," a place the two of them used to frequent. The restaurant was tucked deep inside a park—quiet amid the bustle, elegantly decorated, wrapped in greenery, its specialty the light, refined local cuisine. This had once been Yao Yuan's favorite spot.

Shuai Hongqiang arrived early on purpose and chose a booth by the window. Outside lay a winter courtyard, a few stalks of withered bamboo swaying gently in the wind, sparse and still. Inside, though, it was another world entirely: a man-made eco-garden lush and green, warm and snug, holding onto a small patch of springtime life in the dry, cold northern season.

He ordered a pot of Tieguanyin tea and watched it pour into the cups, steam curling upward, his thoughts drifting. The terrifying scene of the car crash and the heavy unease that came with "borrowing fortune" had haunted him these past days. He had turned it over and over, and in the end he'd decided to ask Yao Yuan out. For one thing, he needed a reasonable, unremarkable excuse to probe into how she'd been doing lately—her health, above all. For another, there was indeed a piece of real business to discuss.

Yao Yuan arrived right on time. She wore a cream high-necked cashmere sweater under a long light-gray coat, her makeup immaculate, her manner its usual composed and capable self—nothing seemed off. Even so, Shuai Hongqiang instinctively studied her face carefully. She looked slightly paler than before, a faint shadow under her eyes, whether from poor rest or… something else, he couldn't tell.

"Been waiting long?" Yao Yuan sat down across from him and slipped off her coat; a waiter took it at once.

"No, I just got here too." Shuai Hongqiang pulled himself together and poured her tea. "This place is just like it always was."

Yao Yuan glanced around and smiled. "It is. The food hasn't changed either." Her tone was easy, as if they were just two old friends catching up, but deep in her eyes lay a hint of caution—she was clearly trying to gauge the purpose of this meeting.

The dishes came out one after another, all light and fresh. The two exchanged a few words about how they'd been, asked after each other's year-end busyness at their companies. The mood was neither warm nor cold, holding the proper distance of acquaintances.

Choosing his words, Shuai Hongqiang finally steered the conversation toward concern. "You look a little tired. Work been too busy lately? Or… not resting well?" He asked as casually as he could, though the fingers gripping his teacup tightened without his noticing.

Yao Yuan's chopsticks paused, almost imperceptibly, as she picked at the food. She raised her eyes to him, her gaze calm. "It's like this every year-end—all the wrap-ups and planning. What about you? You took the family out over the New Year, didn't you?" She deftly turned the topic back on him while watching his reaction.

"We went out to the suburbs, wanted to ski. Didn't expect a pileup on the road—we nearly got caught in it." Shuai Hongqiang sighed and shook his head, his voice carrying the aftershock of fear. "Lucky for us, there happened to be a small side road right beside us, so we slipped past disaster. But it scared us half to death—even Wenyao was wilted afterward." As he spoke, he watched Yao Yuan's expression out of the corner of his eye. He'd mentioned being "lucky," mentioned "slipping past disaster"—he wanted to see whether she'd connect it to anything, whether she'd react in any particular way.

Sure enough, Yao Yuan stopped, brows drawing together, and looked at him in earnest. "A crash? Is everyone all right? Was it bad?" Her concern was genuine, but as Shuai Hongqiang parsed it carefully, it seemed to be only the ordinary concern one shows for a routine traffic accident—nothing deeper, nothing that hinted at "this has to do with me."

"Everyone's fine, just a few scratches on the car—thank goodness." Shuai Hongqiang relaxed a little, then immediately tensed again. She looked entirely unaware. Did that mean the "drain" was invisible, something even she hadn't sensed? That worried him more, not less.

"That's good. You have to be careful driving from now on, especially in weather like this." Yao Yuan offered the reminder and went back to her meal, seeming to set the topic aside.

Shuai Hongqiang knew he couldn't push any further, or it would seem strange. He cleared his throat and steered the conversation toward the day's second purpose—the one out in the open, the main one.

"Actually, I asked you here today for more than just dinner. There's something I wanted to talk over with you." He set down his chopsticks, his expression turning a touch more formal. "Remember the letter of intent I brought you a few months back—the commercial complex tied to that old-town redevelopment district?"

Yao Yuan nodded. "I do. I read it. The overall margins were thin, and it got tangled up with leftover demolition issues in that district, which gave me some reservations. Why—has something changed?"

"Yes." Shuai Hongqiang admitted it frankly, his tone complicated. "I won't hide it from you. When I first came to you, it really was because a long-overdue government works payment hadn't come through, and the new project had a funding gap. I wanted to lean on your capital and your operating muscle. But"—he paused—"a little while ago, that overdue payment was suddenly settled in full, principal and interest both."

Yao Yuan looked slightly surprised by his candor, but only raised an eyebrow. "Oh? That's good news, then—a big problem solved."

"It is." Shuai Hongqiang gave a wry smile; the reason behind this "good news" left him conflicted. "So that supporting commercial complex—the funding problem's solved, and I can handle it myself. I figure the margins and the difficulty of running it probably wouldn't catch your eye anymore."

Yao Yuan neither agreed nor disagreed, waiting quietly for the rest.

Shuai Hongqiang looked at her and went on. "What I wanted to talk to you about today is a different project. After the New Year, the city will most likely launch a new 'National Fitness Center' project. The site may be in the new district—the scale and specifications will both be high. It counts as a key livelihood project. The baseline investment will be enormous, demanding more of both capital and overall operating capacity. Of course, if we win the bid, the prospects and the prestige are beyond what an ordinary project can offer."

He leaned forward slightly, his tone sincere. "This project would be a strain for me to swallow on my own, and the risk would be concentrated. If you're interested, we could talk seriously about the possibility of working together. You have the edge in resource integration and running high-end projects; I have the foundation in local construction and on-the-ground execution. Strong joined with strong—better odds of winning."

These words were half business proposal, half… an olive branch born of some impulse to make amends. He knew Yao Yuan's company was on a strong upward trajectory and might well have no use for his earlier project. But this National Fitness Center really was a better platform. If she could take part, and the partnership went smoothly, perhaps… he could give her more of a boost in business, which would count as an indirect form of… compensation? Shuai Hongqiang couldn't untangle these complicated feelings himself; he only felt he ought to do something.

Yao Yuan didn't answer right away. She sipped her tea slowly, her eyes settling on the leaves floating in the cup, as though weighing it all carefully. The room was very quiet, only faint background music in the air.

After a long moment she looked up, her gaze clear and sharp, fixed on Shuai Hongqiang. "Boss Shuai, this project does sound good. But I'm a little curious." She tilted her head slightly, a faintly probing smile on her lips. "You just solved your funding problem. You could perfectly well take a crack at a bigger piece of the pie yourself, or find other partners. Why come to me again, specifically?"

Her question was direct, and it struck right at the heart of it. Had Shuai Hongqiang really asked her here only to discuss a possible partnership?

Shuai Hongqiang met her gaze and sighed inwardly. Yao Yuan was as sharp as ever—she could pick up on the slightest thing that was off. He couldn't mention the bronze mirror, couldn't mention "borrowing fortune," and certainly couldn't mention that absurd unease and the urge to make amends.

He smiled, the smile carrying some helplessness, and some sincerity. "There are a few reasons. First, this project demands strong government relations and the ability to land things locally—that's my strength—but high-end planning and later-stage operations are yours. We complement each other. Second"—he paused—"we've known each other so many years, we know each other inside out. Working together, the communication costs are low, and there's a foundation of trust. Third…"

He looked at her and said slowly, "When money was tight, the person I thought of was you—because I felt you could pull me out of the fire. Now the money's sorted, and there's a better chance, and still the person I think of is you. I suppose it's just… I feel that good things should be done with people worth sharing them with. Of course, this is only my proposal. The choice is entirely yours."

Yao Yuan listened quietly, her expression betraying little change. Only after a good while did she set down her teacup, the faint click of porcelain.

"If it's convenient, send me the project materials and a preliminary feasibility analysis." Her tone was level, all business. "I'll need my team to evaluate it. As for working together"—she looked up, meeting Shuai Hongqiang's eyes directly—"once I've seen something concrete, we'll talk it through in detail. How's that?"

"Of course." Shuai Hongqiang nodded, a weight lifting from his chest—at least she hadn't refused outright. But he understood, too, that Yao Yuan's "evaluation" would never be only on the business level.

"All right, I'll wait to hear from you." Yao Yuan smiled faintly and picked up her napkin to dab at the corner of her mouth, seeming ready to bring the meeting to a close.

Shuai Hongqiang took the cue and let the project drop. The two chatted a little more, the mood seeming to return to the polite calm of when they'd first sat down.

Yao Yuan picked up her coat, her movements smooth and natural, as if it were just an offhand courtesy before leaving. But her next words froze the quiet air in the room solid:

"By the way—you said you only got past that crash on New Year's by luck? Nothing else? No warning you saw in advance, say? Or some special… feeling?"

Her tone was light, her eyes not even fully lifting from the coat she was straightening. Yet that seemingly casual question pierced, like a fine needle, straight through the calm Shuai Hongqiang had been working so hard to maintain.

His heart skipped a beat, then began to pound wildly.

(She's sensed it? She's felt something wrong herself?)

The appraiser Old Chen's aged, raspy warning rang out in his ears, suddenly crystal clear: "…The Mirror Pact is already made. Unless she realizes it herself, senses in some deep way that her fate has gone awry and comes seeking a way to break it of her own accord, no outside force can sever it… That is the first step to undoing the pact, and the crucial one. If she remains wholly unaware, then even if you talk yourself blue in the face she may not believe you, and the pact will not budge an inch."

Shuai Hongqiang's eyes swept quickly over Yao Yuan's face. Pale though she was, with a faint blue-green beneath her eyes—a sign of drained vitality, true—her gaze was clear, her bearing unchanged, nothing like the badly eroded, listless image he'd dreaded. That let his racing heart drop back down a little, and a tangle of feelings welled up—relief and an even heavier guilt. Relief that she seemed, for now, in no real danger; guilt that this "for now, no danger" was very likely bought with her present exhaustion and with unknown costs still to come.

He had to resolve this as soon as possible. The moment Old Chen returned from the south, he'd take her to see him! The thought was firmer than ever before.

He pressed down the surging emotions and forced his face into an expression that just barely passed for relaxed, though his voice came out drier than he'd expected. "No warning. It was just… good fortune smiling on me, I guess. At the last possible second, that side road suddenly caught my eye—like Heaven had opened a private lane just for me." He paused, then decided to stop circling the point. Meeting Yao Yuan's calm, searching gaze, he laid out the decision he'd been turning over all night:

"Come spring, the moment Old Chen is back in Jinshi, I'll take you to see him. We'll see if there's some… safe way to undo that 'Mirror Pact.'" His words quickened, as if to persuade her—and himself. "Even though it looks like there's no real harm to you for now, just poor rest at worst, who knows about later? Something like 'time-travel' is bizarre enough on its own, and none of us has gotten to the bottom of that mirror. Better to undo it completely, clean and clear, so there's no strange thing hidden inside that we don't know about, no endless trouble down the line."

Having said it, he held his breath and watched Yao Yuan, waiting for her reaction. In those words, the worry was real, the guilt was real, and the secret, faint reluctance to lose his "good luck" had been pressed down beneath a deeper fear—the fear that he might be dragging her down.

Yao Yuan didn't respond at once. She stood there quietly, coat draped over her arm, her gaze deep, as if reappraising this familiar yet unfamiliar man before her. That gaze seemed to have weight as it passed over his forced composure, his slightly tightened fingers, the urgency and unease in his eyes that he couldn't quite hide.

(Shuai Hongqiang…)

A clear realization surfaced in her mind. At his core, this man was, in the end, decent—he had his lines he wouldn't cross and his sense of honor. This was perhaps the limit of his caliber, a lack of ruthless decisiveness in certain capital games, but in this moment it became a rare and precious virtue. Swap him for one of those who'd forged hearts of iron and stone amid the tides of business—who, on learning that sacrificing a negligible bit of an "old flame's" spirit could trade for their own fortune flowing smoothly and disaster turning to safety, would treat it as a heaven-sent piece of leverage and scheme by every means to cement this "contract." Where would they ever think, of their own accord, of "undoing" it? They might even plot to keep the other party from ever noticing.

It really did prove that old saying: it's the dog-butchers who are most loyal, and the scholars who most betray. To be ruthless enough, sometimes, you really do need those "cultured types" who understand too many principles and calculate too finely.

A faint, almost self-mocking curve crossed her eyes. At last she spoke, her voice low but landing clearly in the quiet room:

"All right."

Just that one word. No further questions, no doubt, no flicker of fear or curiosity about the "Mirror Pact" itself—as if she'd merely agreed to the most ordinary little thing.

Yet that single word made Shuai Hongqiang's heart suddenly ease, only to fill at once with more complicated feelings. She'd agreed, just like that. Was it trust—or had she long harbored doubts of her own?

"Then… I'll be in touch when the time comes." He nodded, suddenly at a loss for anything more to say.

"Mm." Yao Yuan answered and slipped briskly into her coat, wrapping her scarf. "I'll go first. Just send the project materials to my email."

"All right. Take care on the way."

The two parted at the restaurant door, one going left, one going right, merging into the bustling stream of people on the street. The winter wind rushed at their faces, bringing a biting clarity.

Shuai Hongqiang walked toward the parking lot, pulled open the car door and got in, but didn't start the engine right away. He gripped the steering wheel with both hands, staring blankly ahead. Yao Yuan's last, calm "All right," and her fathomless eyes, replayed in his mind again and again.

(Just how much does she know? And how much does she believe?)

But no matter what, the path was clear. Spring, Old Chen, undoing the pact. This was the road he had to walk—the only road he could walk.

He started the car and eased into the traffic. In the rearview mirror, the restaurant's sign slowly receded. What he didn't know was that, at a street corner in another direction, Yao Yuan had also stopped. She looked back once toward where his car had vanished, thoughts churning in her eyes, and only after a long while turned and walked off with steady steps into the scattered lights of the city.

The thread of the Mirror Pact seemed, on the surface, to have loosened with the chance of "undoing" raised by one side. But the cause-and-effect and the tangled feelings wound around it seemed only to have begun to draw tight.
