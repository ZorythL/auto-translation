The Ninth Time-Travel Glimpse: However Different the Roads, the Destination Is the Same
Yao Yuan's consciousness settled once more into that familiar pure-white void.
Before she could fully adjust to the emptiness and quiet, the void beside her rippled gently, layer upon layer. Fine flickers of light pulsed like ink dropped into clear water, spreading from faint to deep, slowly resolving into a concrete picture that finally unfurled before her in full.
Another proposal. Once again, that mirror room that blended the feel of technology with a romantic mood.
Overhead, the domed ceiling turned with its steady starry sky; all around stood the neatly arranged ring-shaped consoles, crowded with beaming friends and family. For a dizzy instant, Yao Yuan felt an overwhelming sense of déjà vu — she even half wondered whether the real proposal she had just lived through, that soul-shaking confirmation, had been nothing more than an illusion spun by her overloaded emotions.
But in the next instant, the subtle differences surfaced clearly and shattered that illusion of overlap.
The guests' clothing, the cut and the overall color scheme in this picture, were utterly unlike the scene she had just lived. What Zhao Yiming cradled in his arms was not a fierce, vivid bouquet of red velvet roses, but a soft, lingering bunch of champagne-colored flowers. Most telling of all, the slightly younger version of herself in the picture had eyes that were clear and unrippled — not a trace of the soul-shaking daze, the weightless disorientation. Calmly, brightly, decisively, she took the ring, and accepted that solemn declaration.
In the pure-white space, Yao Yuan stood quietly, her heart still as water.
After eight crossings through time and space, she had long shed the fear, the shock, the confusion of those early days. By now she had grown used to being the one watcher in this chess game of fate — forced to halt, to stand silent on the sidelines. The confusion had faded; all that remained in her heart was a settled calm. Since none of her struggle or resistance could shift the fixed track, she would steady herself instead and look closely, and see just what answer fate meant to reveal to her this time.
The picture flowed on slowly: the congratulations in the room, the warmth of embraces, the crisp pop of champagne corks — all replaying a similar liveliness.
Yao Yuan studied the crowd around her. Jin Lan, Wang Qian, Liu Qingzhuo, Chen Ye were all present — only Jiang Tiantian was missing. Of the other colleagues, only two or three faces overlapped with the people she knew; the rest were strangers, all-new faces.
She understood at once, and instantly traced every thread of cause and effect.
The root of it all lay in the life-track she had rewritten, crossing after crossing. After her third crossing changed the course of her life, the "Yao Yuan of that past timeline" had not become the Godmother of Feelings caught in the eye of the storm. Instead she had turned, far earlier, to technology and business. She had met Zhao Yiming sooner, bound herself to deep collaboration with him sooner, and developed a more fully featured AI — one not confined to the narrow vertical of feelings.
Her life's center of gravity had shifted; the direction she pushed forward had shifted; and so the people around her, naturally, were reshuffled too. Some who had walked beside her had quietly parted ways at fate's forking roads; some all-new faces had come rushing in along the new track. The branch of her life still stretched toward the sun — but through countless small adjustments, it had already grown wholly different limbs and veins.
The shot turned, switching to the temporary banquet hall converted from the room next door.
Yao Yuan's gaze flickered. The arrangement of the long tables, the neatly stacked champagne tower, the chef team standing by on site, even the exquisite styling of the desserts — all of it overlapped almost exactly with the engagement celebration she had just lived through herself.
A tangle of feelings, absurd and yet clear-eyed, washed through her whole body.
In this moment, she saw through to the most paradoxical truth of fate.
A person can, perhaps, rewrite the rough places of the road ahead, swap out the scenery along the way, choose which companions to keep, fine-tune every detail of life through choice after choice. But the base color rooted deep in the soul, the character, the longing — like a riverbed set deep into the earth — have long decided the general flow of a life. However outside forces stir it, in the end it can hardly be wholly overturned, hardly made to change course.
That "Yao Yuan of the past timeline," because of her intervention, had sidestepped the public backlash, the business traps, and the more tangled emotional snarls that came with becoming the "Godmother of Feelings." She had walked a road that looked more "solid," more focused on business and technology. The change was enormous — enough to make the two "Yao Yuans'" lives look strikingly different on the surface. And yet, when each of their lives reached a certain crucial node, the people they were meant to meet, the things they were meant to live through, always came to pass.
This was not entirely "inescapable." It was more like a deep gravity grounded in "character" and "need." Whichever version of herself it was, the same base color lay buried in the bone. Worn down by the world, tempered by trauma, forced to build a hard armor of reason — she looked clear-headed, independent, unbreakable, yet at the bottom of her heart she always longed for one pure, steadfast, solid favoring, one steadfast companionship.
She and Zhao Yiming were, from the start, the optimal solution to each other's logic, cognition, ambition, and feeling.
So however the road ahead twisted and doubled back, once the two of them — their minds, their experience, their life stages — arrived at the point where they fit, once forging a deep alliance became each one's best choice, then turning toward each other, settling on each other, was the inevitability written into fate.
So, was the change meaningful? Of course it was. It changed the concentration of suffering in the other timeline's Yao Yuan's life. It reduced her self-tearing and inner drain. It dodged the erosion from the public eye and the grinding of the heart, the kind that could wreck a mind. In exchange came a smoother career, a lighter state of mind, a past with fewer regrets. What changed was the experience of the process, the specific shape of the hardships and the harvests along the way. But in the end it could not rewrite the essence of the soul. A person's character, cognition, core longings had long decided the choices made at life's crucial nodes. So two equally mature minds, facing similar life problems, after that double calculation of reason and feeling, would most likely point toward the same "optimal solution."
This was the inner logic of human nature and human need. What people call "fate," perhaps, is nothing but countless such "inner-logic choices" stacked one upon another.
In the picture, the warmth of celebration still went on.
Yao Yuan watched quietly from the pure-white space — watched another self ringed by the congratulations of friends and family, watched Zhao Yiming stay close at her side throughout, his arm draped naturally around her waist, his posture loose and certain, holding a wordless possessiveness and a careful, tender guarding. The familiar scene replayed, so familiar it gave her a sudden illusion — as if she herself had lived through one more grand, gentle proposal.
A moment later, the Yao Yuan in the picture slipped away from the noisy crowd and turned back alone to the quiet mirror room.
The Yao Yuan watching from above felt her heart stir. She knew clearly: the most crucial moment of this whole crossing was about to arrive.
And so it was.
When the slightly younger Yao Yuan in the mirror room raised her eyes toward the slowly turning vortex of star-clouds above the dome, a hexagonal screen on one wall — until now dim and silent — abruptly lit up. Light flowed like spilled mercury, like ink staining plain paper, and the figure of the thirty-eight-year-old Yao Yuan slowly condensed out of the void, her outline growing ever clearer.
Two timelines, two ages of the same self, gazed quietly at each other across the cold screen and the unbreachable barrier of time and space.
There was no shock or dread of a first meeting, no tense scrutiny of a repeated encounter. After nine crossings through time and space, this gaze held only the calm and release of dust settling. They shared the same thread of soul, had walked utterly different lives that mirrored each other, and now met here — like a reunion across the years, gentle and at peace.
The thirty-eight-year-old Yao Yuan on the screen spoke first, her voice clear and bright, carrying a knowing little laugh and, beneath it, a flicker of haste that came and went in an instant — she had long grasped the rules, and knew the time for this gaze across time was extremely limited.
"When I confirmed things with Zhao Yiming, it wasn't long after Yu Hao had proposed to you. I never expected that on my side, Zhao Yiming has only just proposed to me, and over here you've come to the very same ending."
Her gaze was clear, piercing through layer upon layer of time to land on the self across from her, her tone certain and full of feeling. "It seems some things really can't be gotten around. No matter how many turns you take in between, no matter which road you switch to, the meeting-point where you're meant to converge always waits in the same place."
The thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan outside the mirror heard this, and a tangled resonance surged in her heart. Almost without thinking, she turned the brand-new ring on the ring finger of her left hand, the diamond's edges scraping the pad of her finger with a real, solid feel. "Yes. I didn't see it coming myself, either." She spoke as if confiding to an old friend who knew every cause and consequence, far more relaxed now. "After I last saw you, I threw everything into handling the equity dispute with Zhuge Jinye. Zhao Yiming was a huge help — the lawyer he introduced is top-tier in the field, someone an ordinary person could never even reach. Once the storm settled, our paths crossed more and more, naturally."
She tilted her head slightly, looking back over the days of being together, her eyes full of clear-eyed certainty. "The longer we were together, the more fitting it felt. There was no earth-shaking flutter, but there was a full-spectrum sameness of frequency. The rhythm of our thinking, the scale on which we handle things, our ambition for our careers, our hopes for marriage and the future — all of it on the same channel."
"I'm thirty-seven, he's forty. We're both long past the age of chasing illusory romance."
"For us, the best feeling has never been unconditional favor and pampering. It's deep understanding, recognition, and fit. It's a lifelong alliance, standing shoulder to shoulder, holding each other up. For me, he is simply the most suitable, the steadiest and most fitting, partner in life."
She said "suitable" with an easy candor, no romanticized gloss — and yet it caught exactly the ultimate emotional pursuit of women like them: not illusory doting, but a solid alliance grounded in deep understanding and recognition.
The Yao Yuan in the mirror nodded, not at all surprised. Of course she understood. However the fine-tuning had gone, they were the same soul at the source, with the same reason and clarity, the same pride and fragility. Facing life's great choices, their judgment and their trade-offs naturally arrived at the same destination by different roads.
Just as she was about to press for more details — how Zhuge Jinye fared afterward in the other timeline, or whether there had been new progress in business — that familiar feeling of the soul being pulled loose returned without warning.
This time the tug was unusually gentle, and yet unusually swift.
Unlike the crude dragging of before, it was more like consciousness settling briefly into stillness, then being gently carried away from the void. Beneath her wine-red cashmere sweater, the heart-guarding jade worn against her skin suddenly warmed — a clear, mellow heat, like a wordless farewell, and like a final protection.
The thirty-eight-year-old Yao Yuan in the mirror faded and grew transparent at speed, until she dissolved completely into the faint light, as if she had never appeared at all.
Only the thirty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan remained, standing alone in the mirror room of her own timeline, facing a screen that had just returned to ordinary and reflected back her solitary figure, lost for a moment in thought.
...
Yao Yuan's consciousness returned again, like slowly waking from a long, deep meditation.
The first thing she caught clearly was a solid, warm embrace, and beneath the chest the steady, regular thud of a heartbeat — boom, boom — settled and strong. Then came the cool, crisp scent of aftershave lingering at the tip of her nose, mixed with the faint, cold smell of metal and ozone unique to the mirror room — familiar and reassuring.
Her long lashes trembled lightly, and she slowly opened her eyes.
"Yuanyuan?"
A low, taut voice fell from above, pressing down on an anxiety it couldn't quite hide. The arms around her tightened all at once, holding a careful preciousness and a lingering dread.
Her vision focused out of the blur, and Zhao Yiming's features, close at hand, came clear. Behind the gold-rimmed glasses his eyes were heavy with worry, his brow knotted tight, his thin lips pressed into a clean, taut line.
He was holding her, his steps halted, as if he had been about to carry her out of the mirror room. Behind him the sensor door was sliding slowly open, and from the corridor outside soft, bright light spilled in, throwing the panic in his eyes into even sharper relief.
"I..." Yao Yuan managed a single syllable, her voice a little hoarse.
"You're awake!" Zhao Yiming stopped at once, adjusting his hold with the gentlest of motions so she could rest more comfortably in the crook of his arm, then lowered his head to study her face closely. "How do you feel? Where does it hurt? You fainted just now."
Fainted... right, the blackout caused by the crossing. Yao Yuan quickly pieced the situation together. It seemed Zhao Yiming had noticed she was gone, come out to look for her, and found her collapsed on the floor. She took quick stock of herself: apart from a faint weariness and that mild floating sensation that always came after switching consciousness, nothing felt wrong. The lingering warmth of the heart-guarding jade still threaded through her flesh, gently mending the wear on her spirit — perhaps that was why she had come through the crossing in such steady shape.
"I'm fine." Yao Yuan cleared her throat softly and kept her tone as steady as she could, raising her eyes to the man brimming with worry. A gentle, soothing smile spread in her eyes as she lifted a hand to pat his tense arm. "Really, I'm fine, don't be nervous. Put me down — don't worry the family outside. Today is our good day."
Zhao Yiming didn't let go right away. He still held her firmly, his sharp gaze examining her face, her pupils, her expression in fine detail, confirming again and again that her eyes were clear, her breathing steady, her color slowly warming back — only then did the tense line of his shoulders ease a fraction.
He was silent for a moment, then at last did as she asked, carefully and steadily setting her down, his arm staying firmly around hers the whole time, not daring to slacken in the slightest, guiding her at a slow pace to sit on the corridor's rest bench.
"Really no discomfort?" He turned to look at her, his tone carrying the practical, brook-no-argument concern unique to a science man. "If there's even the slightest thing wrong, we go to the hospital right now for a full checkup."
Warmth stirred in Yao Yuan's heart. She leaned in slightly and rested gently against his chest. It was a rare thing for her — a fully reliant, willing closeness, soft and candid.
"Don't make a mountain out of a molehill." She raised her eyes to him, real tenderness pooling there, her tone holding just the right measure of languor and softness. "It's probably that last night's year-end livestream drained me too much, and then today I got hit out of nowhere by your surprise proposal. The swings of emotion were too big — for a moment my blood and breath went unsteady and I went weak and lost myself."
"My fiancé's proposal packs too much firepower — I couldn't take it for a second there." Half-joking, she dissolved his anxiety, attributing every strangeness to being moved and being tired — reason and feeling fused, beyond reproach.
Zhao Yiming looked at the true tenderness and warmth in her eyes, the fully trusting ease of her, confirmed she wasn't merely forcing herself through, and only then did the boulder in his heart slowly come to rest. He knew her: she wasn't someone fragile enough to faint easily, but she also never joked about her own body. Her composure now meant her own judgment was that there was nothing seriously wrong.
In the end he didn't insist on the hospital. He only lifted a hand and lightly touched the back of his fingers to her slightly cool cheek, then firmly wrapped up her slightly cool fingertips, the warmth of his palm flowing into her without pause, steadily soothing every ripple in her heart.
"Rest here a while longer. The moment anything feels off, tell me at once."
"Mm." Yao Yuan answered obediently, leaning quietly into his arms, drawing in the steadiness and warmth that were his alone.
And in her heart, she had already calmly and clearly reviewed the whole sequence from beginning to end.
This crossing — from fainting to waking — had taken, in real time, probably less than ten minutes.
From the first to the ninth, all the crossings through time and space, spanning several months, came in this moment to a complete close.
Nine returns, nine times watching from the sidelines, nine conversations and confirmations with the self of another timeline. At last she had seen, in full, every thread of fate, every gain and loss, everything fixed and everything that could be changed.
By her agreement with the old gentleman Wang Baoguo, after nine crossings would come the time for her to keep the appointment at the Yellow River and dissolve the Mirror Pact.
She raised her eyes to the man at her side — tenderly guarding her, his heart full of concern, the man who had just become her fiancé — and a tangle of feelings welled up in her heart. The bronze mirror, the Mirror Pact, the time-travel, the borrowing of fortune: this greatest secret and variable, the one that had run through her whole past year and hung over her fate, she had never breathed to anyone. For one, there was no way to begin; for another, no one would believe so bizarre an experience, so far beyond the bounds of science; and for a third, she didn't want to drag others into it for no reason.
But now everything had changed.
Zhao Yiming was already her fiancé, the one she had assented to with her own lips and settled on, the lifelong alliance who would walk shoulder to shoulder with her for the rest of her days. They were about to bind the most intimate emotional and legal ties, sharing fortune and misfortune, honor and disgrace alike.
The trip to the Yellow River held an uncertain fate. Whether the shackles of the ancient mirror could be wholly dissolved, whether the Mirror Pact would backlash, how many unknown risks lay hidden on the road ahead — she could foresee none of it.
She could no longer keep him on the outside, could no longer let him face the unknown crisis at her side while knowing nothing at all. Still less could she go on bearing every secret and danger alone, separated from the person closest to her by a barrier that could never be pierced.
It was time to confess everything.
Once today's festivities ended, she would tell Zhao Yiming everything — every hidden thing, every experience, every torment and fear — exactly as it had been.
Sitting quietly to steady her breath for a while, Yao Yuan shed the last of her lingering weariness, her color fully back to normal. She straightened up, smoothed the hem of her dress, and raised her eyes to Zhao Yiming, brows curving, her smile bright and open. "There, I've completely come round. Let's head back — don't keep everyone waiting too long."
Zhao Yiming studied her a while longer, confirmed she was full of life and entirely well, and only then nodded, reassured, reaching out to lace his fingers through hers again.
On their ring fingers, the two diamond rings occasionally brushed together with a small, crisp chime, gentle and solemn. He quietly tucked away all his worry and returned to his usual steady, composed self, walking shoulder to shoulder with her back into the room full of liveliness and sincere congratulations.
The time that followed was warm and peaceful, the hours calm and serene.
Yao Yuan reined in every feeling, shed the turmoil and weight at the bottom of her heart, and met everyone's congratulations with composed gentleness. She traded easy laughter and chat with Jin Lan and Liu Qingzhuo, accepted her friends' heartfelt good wishes, and conversed lightly with the core staff of Zhao Yiming's company — measured in every step, gracious and warm.
The room was sweet with champagne, fragrant with fine food, bright with the laughter of family and friends, warm with a homely glow. She settled serenely into it all, savoring the completeness and tenderness of the moment, yet always keeping one corner of clarity in her heart, quietly planning everything that came next.
At two in the afternoon, the banquet drew toward its end, and the guests began saying their goodbyes and leaving one after another.
Jin Lan stepped up and hugged her tight, her eyes full of genuine joy. "It's finally settled! You and Zhao Yiming are a true perfect match — you'd better be happy. We're all waiting for the wedding wine!"
Lu Ang stood to one side, nodding along with a smile, all sincere congratulations.
Liu Qingzhuo took her hand, her eyes gentle. "I'm truly happy for you today. If there's anything at all I can help with as you prepare for the wedding, just say the word, don't be shy."
Chen Ye stepped over to shake Zhao Yiming's hand. Between men, congratulations needed no words — all of it tucked into a knowing look and the strength of a grip.
Wang Qian was among the last batch of guests to leave.
He walked at an unhurried pace up to the two of them, his smile warm and considerate, decorous and composed as ever. "Yao Yuan, Yiming, congratulations to you both — a true union of pearl and jade, a match made by heaven."
His gaze rested on Yao Yuan's face for the briefest instant, and through his eyes flashed something very faint and hard to fathom — like insight, like understanding — gone in a blink, too quick to catch. Before Yao Yuan could savor it, he had already withdrawn his gaze, clapped Zhao Yiming on the shoulder with a smile, and taken his composed leave.
The clamor finally dispersed; every guest had gone.
The vast banquet hall fell quiet in an instant, leaving only a scattering of staff tidying the remains in soft voices. The liveliness that had boiled through the room moments before receded like a tide, leaving behind only the full, settled stillness of dust come to rest.
"Tired?" Zhao Yiming turned to look at her, lifting a hand to brush aside a stray wisp of hair by her ear, the motion tender to the utmost.
"I'm all right." Yao Yuan shook her head gently and raised her eyes to him. Her gaze was calm yet unusually grave, carrying a seriousness that couldn't be ignored. "Yiming, let's go to my apartment. There's something important I have to tell you."
Zhao Yiming raised an eyebrow slightly.
He knew Yao Yuan too well — steady and unruffled by nature, never letting anything show on her face, rarely speaking of anything in so grave and serious a tone. Something stirred in his heart, but he asked nothing extra, trusting her completely, and nodded gently. "All right."
The car drove smoothly toward Yao Yuan's apartment, threading through the bright, clear light of a New Year's Day afternoon. The streets still carried the rich flavor of the holiday, full of festive life.
Yao Yuan gazed quietly out the window at the streetscape rushing backward, her fingertips unconsciously stroking the diamond ring on her ring finger over and over. Hard, cold, and then, warmed by her body heat, gradually warm. It stood for the rest of her life now fixed, and it weighed on her with the secret she had hidden for a year.
What she was taking him back to was not just an apartment.
It was that ancient bronze mirror — the one through which she had tried, in another timeline, to change the course of her own life, to go back over the torments and insights of the past.
What she was going to confess to him was every time-and-space experience and pact of fate that lay beyond the bounds of science, absurd and bizarre, that no one could understand.
She couldn't foresee his reaction — whether he would be shocked, doubtful, baffled, or wholly accepting, standing by her side.
But she had made up her mind.
Not only to face the unknown by the Yellow River, but more because, from the moment she had nodded yes to his proposal, the rest of her life — bright and clear or treacherous and rough, steady and smooth or perilous and uncertain — was to be lived in honesty with him.
The car came to a steady stop below the apartment building.
Yao Yuan drew a deep breath, pressed down the ripple in her heart, pushed open the door, and stepped out.
Nine returns through fate had now come to a close.
And the true chapter of the future was only about to be drawn slowly open, with her confession.
