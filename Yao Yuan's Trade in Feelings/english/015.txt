Amber light leaked through the gaps of the ring fill-lamp and wrapped softly around half of Lin Wan's face. Beside the cup lay an open leather notebook and an elegant fountain pen, but the page was blank, as if waiting to be filled by the story about to be confided tonight.
The most prominent things on the desktop, besides the camera pointed at her, were a tablet standing upright beside it, fan comments scrolling fast across the screen. The colored danmu flowed like a restless, garish river, carrying lines like "let me call in," "Teacher's right," "what should I do." Her gaze swept across it now and then, sharp as a radar hunting for a specific signal — but the moment she fixed her eyes back on the main camera, that sharpness sank away into pure focus.
There was a special "quiet" in the air. Not utter silence, but the cleanness of all background noise filtered out by technology. Behind the microphone's pop filter, every faint breath she took, every brush of lips and teeth as a word left her mouth, was clearly captured, amplified, and tapped straight onto the listeners' eardrums through countless earphones. That absolute "presence" made this small space, circled out by light, feel peeled away from the real world — a floating, transparent consultation room, an emotional vacuum chamber.

He knew that some things had been thoroughly, calmly laid bare on the negotiating table called "marriage." Now, it was this woman — whom he'd married with money and a "yearning for culture" — who, with terminology he found tongue-twisting and with real, tangible spending, was holding up the daily running of this household and redefining the relationship between them. The veil of tender feeling had been torn off, and beneath it was a naked balance of power and exchange of resources. Calmly, she had measured out the clear line called "cooperation" that ran between them.
He slowly drank the last mouthful of water in the cup. The water was warm; it flowed down his throat, but stirred not the slightest warmth.
Outside the window it was pitch black. He suddenly felt that the life the two of them led was like that river back in his hometown — looking on, it was still the same river, but underneath, the channel had long since shifted, silt and sand churning down toward a place he no longer recognized and could not find the bottom of. And that moon that had once shone on the river's surface, making him feel like quite the big shot — that, too, had sunk, damn it, at some point he couldn't name.
