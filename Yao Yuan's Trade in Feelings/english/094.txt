Chapter 94 — A Marriage in a Blended Family Needs an Upgraded Partnership Agreement

After getting home from the hospital, it still wasn't too late, so Lin Wan went ahead with her original plan and started tonight's livestream.

Eight in the evening. Warm yellow light spread evenly across Lin Wan's study. The pothos plant stretched its thick green leaves in one corner of the frame; beside her hand, a cup of red-date and goji tea sent up a gentle, mellow steam, and the faint scent of cedar incense drifted through the air, calming the nerves thread by thread. She sat in her usual spot, in a soft, light smoke-gray cashmere sweater, her short hair neat — only there was a trace of quiet, almost imperceptible fatigue between her brows tonight, like a piece of fine porcelain veiled in the lightest cool mist you could sense only by touch.

She adjusted the microphone, met the camera with a steady gaze, and her lips curved into that warm, certain arc her countless fans knew so well.

"Good evening, everyone, and welcome to 'The Gentle Sobriety.' I'm your host, Lin Wan."

The scrolling comments came in right on cue:

[Evening, Wanwan!]

[Here on time as always!]

[Tonight's mood feels especially calm?]

"Thank you all for coming on time." Lin Wan's voice was clear and even, like a stream gliding over pebbles. She paused slightly, looking frankly into the camera as if she could see through the screen and meet the eyes of every listener. "Tonight's topic was decided at the last minute. The subject I'd originally prepared, we'll set aside for later."

The comments:

[Huh? A last-minute change?]

[Did something happen, Wanwan?]

"I changed the topic because two things collided today." Her tone was calm, as if recounting an ordinary thing worth pondering. "The first was that earlier today I went to the hospital to visit a young girl who'd been admitted. She's the daughter of Brother Qiang and his ex-wife — the child broke her leg skiing. I stood there, in my role as her stepmother." She paused for a moment here, looking honestly into the camera.

"What I saw in that hospital room was very concrete. The child's birth mother, and that birth mother's current husband — the child's stepfather — kept watch at the bedside, fussing over her meals and care, asking the doctors and nurses their questions. That was real, day-and-night, wrapped-around worry and responsibility. Brother Qiang and I — the child's biological father and her later stepmother — we rushed over, anxious inside, but we knew there was a lot we couldn't reach. That was the birth mother's home turf. When you stand in that position, a lot of things come into sharp focus: when the child hurts, who's the first person she wants; through the night at the bedside, who should be the one keeping watch; the medical bills — who pays as a matter of course, and who pays out of goodwill but is still, in the end, one layer removed; even a word of comfort — who should say it, and how, the weight of it is never the same."

Her tone stayed level, with no complaint, simply stating a complicated "position" and "sense of measure" she had felt firsthand.

"It's these very concrete things that make you suddenly understand — in a newly blended household, the difference between 'blood' and 'step,' between 'kin' and 'family in the eyes of the law'; the weight of that responsibility, that worry; and the subtle measure and distance in how people deal with one another; and each person's position and the hardships they carry in this relationship."

Her words came unhurried, with no dramatic flourish, yet that calm, firsthand account made the people on the other end of the screen unconsciously go still.

The comments fell quiet for a moment, then slowly drifted by:

[Sigh, the honest truth. It makes my heart ache to hear it.]

[It's so hard for Wanwan. That position, that sense of measure, is the hardest to get right.]

[This is the reality of second marriages. She put it so clearly.]

"The second thing was a letter I read in the back-end inbox right before going live, from a fan we'll call Sister S for now. She's in a blended-family marriage, with an eight-year-old son of her own, while her husband has a ten-year-old daughter. The letter was long, filled with how a wife trying to be a good stepmother had, over the course of two full years, been worn down to nothing — by her stepdaughter's invisible hostility, by her husband's helpless avoidance — to the point of an exhausted, heart-cold consideration of divorce."

The comments began to scroll quickly:

[Blended families really are so hard!]

[Is being a stepmother an original sin?]

[My aunt went through exactly this. She nearly fell into depression.]

"What I saw at the hospital was a sharp, focused instant; Sister S's letter is a long, day-after-day erosion. From different scales of time, they both point to the same, more complicated model." Lin Wan's fingertip tapped absently at the desk — a little habit of hers when she was thinking deeply. "So tonight, if you're all willing, I thought we could talk about this: when a marriage upgrades from 'two people in a startup together' to 'a merger and restructuring where each side brings their own core asset — a child' — how exactly should this new, more complicated 'Partnership Agreement' be signed, so as to avoid systemic exhaustion and collapse?"

The comments:

[Partnership Agreement! Great metaphor!]

[So it's not about who's right or wrong — it's that the rules were never set?]

[Wanwan's drawing on her own life tonight. It hits even harder.]

"Let's start with Sister S's predicament." Lin Wan steered the discussion to the concrete case, her voice steady, patient as she teased the threads apart. "She tries hard to be good to her ten-year-old stepdaughter, but the child thinks she 'stole Dad away' and opposes her at every turn — one face to her, another behind her back. She talks to her husband, and he says 'the child's still young,' 'be more tolerant,' or even asks, 'Is it that you can't make room for her?' It sounds like the child is being difficult and the husband is refusing to muddle things over, doesn't it?"

She gave a small shake of her head, the gesture of denial gentle but firm.

"If we stop at moral judgment — is the child an ingrate, is the husband playing favorites — then the situation is dead. We have to see this new family's system bug. The bug is that everyone assumes, or unconsciously treats, the stepmother — the newly joined partner, Sister S — as the only variable in the system that can be debugged, the one expected to tolerate without limit. Meanwhile the states of the original shareholder who actually needs adjusting — the husband — and the core asset — the child — are allowed to stay exactly as they are. Their emotions and problems even become the fixed environment that the new variable has to adapt to and absorb."

The comments:

[System bug! Right to the point!]

[The new partner debugs herself while the old shareholder sits back and enjoys it?]

[So true. I feel like my eyes have been opened.]

"Is that fair? Of course not. But it usually forms unconsciously." Lin Wan picked up her tea and took a sip, then her well-moistened voice went on, clear and full of empathy. "The child's hostility very likely comes from a loss of security after the family upheaval. She uses confrontation to test how firm the new boundaries are — even, subconsciously, hoping to trade 'driving off the stepmother' for the family picture she used to have. And the husband's avoidance, beyond whatever guilt he may feel, is also an escape from responsibility. He can't handle his daughter's complicated emotions, and he can't firmly establish new rules between his daughter and his new wife. So the simplest method is to ask the adult who seems more 'sensible,' more 'in love with him' — namely his wife — to endure, to absorb it all."

"And so Sister S is stuck fast. Forward — she can't break through the child's defenses, can't get the closeness she longs for. To the left — she can't get her husband's allied support, and is questioned instead. Stay put — and it's day after day of erosion. All her 'kindness' turns into shoveling sand into a bottomless emotional black hole."

The comments:

[Tearing up. That's me!]

[Men really do love to avoid conflict!]

[So there's no solution? How hopeless.]

"It's not unsolvable. The rules of the game simply have to change — or rather, this 'Upgraded Partnership Agreement' has to be redrafted." Lin Wan's gaze turned sharp and clear-eyed, the calm strength of someone who has seen through the structure. "This new agreement needs at least three core clauses, and they have to be talked out in black and white by the two core partners — the husband and wife — and firmly carried out in daily life."

"The first is called the 'Original Shareholder Responsibility Wake-Up' clause." Her voice was iron-firm. "Make it explicit: each person is the first party responsible for the emotional management and behavioral upbringing of their own child. When a child develops emotions or behaviors directed at the stepparent, the biological parent must step forward and take primary responsibility for communicating, guiding, and setting boundaries. This isn't 'not making room for the child' — this is putting authority and responsibility where they belong. The spouse can offer support, but isn't a stand-in to charge into battle, much less a dedicated recycling bin for negative emotions. The agreement has to spell it out: when the child gives you dirty looks, sabotages things, shows one face to your face and another behind your back, what your husband needs to do is not turn to you and say 'the child's still young.' He must face his own child and tell him or her clearly: this is our new family member, respect is the bottom line. We can talk about your feelings, but treating family this way is not allowed."

The comments:

[The husband needs to step up! So right!]

[A lot of men lack exactly this kind of backbone.]

[Wanwan said that with such firmness!]

"The second is called the 'Partners United Outward' clause." Lin Wan continued, her tone leaving no room for doubt. "Inside the family, especially in front of the children, the husband and wife must be a solid management alliance. That means the basic family rules — respect, honesty, responsibility — have to be upheld together. When one of them is carrying out their duty as the first responsible party, disciplining their own child, the other needs to give clear, visible support: an affirming look, a word echoing the rule, or, afterward and in private, recognition of what the spouse has put in. Absolutely no undermining, no questioning, no muddling things over in front of the children. That line from the husband — 'Is it that you can't make room for her?' — is blowing up the very foundation of the alliance with his own hands. The agreement has to state clearly: behind closed doors we can argue about anything, but in front of the children, on upholding the family's basic rules and each other's dignity, we must be on the same side."

"The third is called the 'Emotional Expectation Management' clause." At this point her tone softened, taking on more understanding and pragmatism. "Allow that building affection takes time — even accept a degree of 'friendly but slightly distant.' Don't demand that the stepparent instantly love the child as their own, and don't demand that the child instantly feel a child's longing for the stepparent. Lower the fixation on 'instantly close as one family,' and put the focus on building a clear, stable, mutually respectful pattern of behavioral interaction. Be good neighbors first, then talk about being good family. At the same time, the husband and wife have to deliberately tend each other's emotional account — that's the new company's most important cash flow. Communicate regularly, affirm each other's contributions and difficulties, make sure the connection between you is solid. Only when the two of you are steady do you have the strength to steady this more complicated system."

The comments:

[Lower your expectations! Be neighbors first!]

[The marriage itself is the anchor that holds everything steady.]

[So well said. I feel like there's a way forward.]

"Sister S's predicament is, to a large degree, the complete absence of this new agreement. Everyone's still running a multilateral system on the fuzzy expectations of the old two-person world." Lin Wan summed up, her gaze clear and forceful, as if it could drive away the fog. "She's been single-handedly debugging herself this whole time, trying to be a 'perfect stepmother' to fit a system riddled with holes — and the result is the system swallowed her."

"So if there's a sister in a similar situation right now," she leaned forward slightly, her voice full of gentle firmness, "perhaps you could try shifting your gaze away from the self-interrogation of 'Am I not good enough?' and 'Why doesn't the child like me?' — back to the most fundamental question. Have a clear-eyed, and possibly difficult, conversation with your partner:"

"Our new company is founded — have we made clear who's responsible for whose child? When one of us has friction with a child, or a child has friction with a new family member, what's our process for supporting each other? Do we have realistic, aligned expectations about the emotional intensity and interaction pattern of this new family?"

"Marriage has never been easy. So, coming back to that first metaphor of ours," Lin Wan's voice grew ever more quiet and strong as the broadcast neared its end, as if all the analysis had to settle at last into a graspable core, "when a marriage upgrades from 'a two-person partnership' to 'a joint venture after asset restructuring,' the measure of its health is no longer whether it looks 'close as one family' on the surface. It's whether that invisible 'Partnership Agreement' is clear, fair, and jointly upheld."

"That agreement doesn't need to be filled with vague words like 'love' or 'sacrifice.' What it needs to write down are specific, fine-grained clauses like 'allocation of responsibility,' 'allied stance,' and 'emotional expectations.' Its purpose isn't to create estrangement — quite the opposite. It's to build, on the premise of acknowledging differences and histories, an order in which everyone — including ourselves — won't be inexplicably drained, and can exist safely."

She looked into the camera with clear eyes, as if she could see the weary figures behind the screen.

"When we work hard to run a blended family, the ultimate goal may not be to prove that 'we can be just as good as a first-marriage family.' It's to prove that, even carrying the scars and marks of the past, we can still use reason and sincerity to create together a new, resilient way of getting along. That way may not be perfect, but it's real enough; it may not be passionate, but it's stable enough. It respects the natural bond of blood, and it also defends the new covenant forged out of love and commitment."

"It asks us — especially the husband and wife, the core 'partners' — to stand up out of the quagmire of emotion and take our place as builders of rules. To clearly divide responsibility, firmly form an alliance, and pragmatically manage expectations. That's much harder than simply demanding of yourself that you 'do more' and 'love deeper' — but it's also much more effective."

"Finally, I want to say to Sister S, who wrote to me today, and to everyone in a similar predicament:" Lin Wan's tone was full of a gentle, firm strength. "When you feel drained, wronged, isolated with no one to turn to, it's very likely not because you aren't good enough, but because something is wrong with the system's 'default settings.' Your energy shouldn't all go into patching the system's holes. What you need to do, perhaps, is take some of that energy back, light up yourself — and then use it to light up that negotiating table between you and your partner, the one about rules and responsibilities."

"Go talk, divide, build. If the negotiation fails, then at least you'll know where the limits of the system are. And if it succeeds, then together you'll have driven the first — and firmest — stake of rules into this complicated, precious home."

"That's all for tonight. May we all have the courage, in our complicated relationships, to be the clear-eyed builders — not merely the silent ones who endure. Good night."

The soft music marking the end of the broadcast began to play. Lin Wan took off her headphones but didn't leave right away. Only the faint hum of the incense diffuser remained in the study. The screen went dark, and the black surface reflected her face — calm, yet unable to fully hide a trace of fatigue.

She thought of the hospital that afternoon — of the wordless, understanding support in the way Teacher Chen had quietly handed over warm water; of the "mother" aura around Qiu Ying, mixed with anxiety and a refusal to be questioned. That was a "partnership" already fully run in, one whose solid structure showed itself in a crisis.

And her own partnership? She thought of Shuai Hongqiang gripping her hand, of the uncontrollable faint tremor and the cold sweat in his palm. That bottomless fear of his — where on earth did it come from? Their tacit, "limited-liability" partnership agreement — in the face of the coming, perhaps even greater storm, was it solid enough?

She didn't know. But she did know, just as she'd said tonight, that clear rules and shared responsibility are the bedrock for getting through any complicated relationship. She picked up the now-cold tea and took a small sip; the faintly bitter aftertaste melted on the tip of her tongue, and let her tangled thoughts settle.

No matter what, first guard the order and calm within her own heart. That was the most important thing she could do for herself — and for this partnership.

After a while, she packed up her equipment, turned off the main light, and then gently pushed open the study door.

In the living room, only a single floor lamp in the corner was lit, its light dim and yellow, carving the spacious room into great swaths of shadow. Shuai Hongqiang sat on the sofa at the edge of the shadow, staring blankly out at the thick night beyond the window. Hearing the door open, he snapped back as if startled and turned his head.

The look of him made Lin Wan's steps falter slightly.

In the depths of Shuai Hongqiang's eyes was a desolation, like a place swept through by a storm. His lips moved, but he couldn't pull up his usual smile.

"Done broadcasting?" he said, his voice badly hoarse, as if he hadn't spoken in a long time. He tried to stand, but his movements were a little slow.

"Mm. Is Wenyao asleep?" Lin Wan sat down, a seat's width away.

"Just fell asleep." His eyes clung to her face, then seemed to pass through her, gazing at some void. "Today… thank you, it was hard on you."

"I'm fine. Wenrui will be okay. Don't blame yourself too much."

The words "blame yourself" made him give a sharp shudder; he abruptly turned his face away, his jawline pulled taut. The living room sank into a silence that swallowed all sound.

He suddenly sprang up, his movements a little flustered. "Milk — I warmed some milk for you." He left the living room almost as if fleeing, as though one more second there and the heavy secret would spill out of his eyes on its own.

Lin Wan sat where she was, watching his back disappear at the kitchen doorway. The cloud of doubt in her heart grew thicker. His state today was far too abnormal — well beyond the range of worry over a daughter hurt by accident. It was a kind of… fear, as if something invisible were chasing and gnawing at him, about to drag him down. But he wouldn't say, and she couldn't press him. This was the tacit understanding — or rather, the boundary — that had formed between them over the years.

Shuai Hongqiang came back carrying the milk, and when he handed it to her, his fingertips were icy, even with a faint, barely perceptible tremor. The warmth of the milk came through the glass, just the right kind of warm.

"Thank you." Lin Wan took it, cupping it in both hands, the warm sensation spreading out from her palms. She watched Shuai Hongqiang sit back down, still sunk in that heavy silence. An arm's length apart, yet it was like a misty abyss between them. She knew he was lingering alone at the edge of that abyss, but she couldn't see what lay at the bottom, and didn't know how to reach out — or whether he would even let her.

The night deepened, and the city lights outside the window thinned out. They sat there quietly in a room of dim yellow light and silence, one cupping warm milk, the other steeped in an unspoken, mutual chill. All those rational discussions in the livestream — about responsibility in blended families, about system rules, about partnership agreements — were now refracted back, concrete and small, onto themselves: forming a partnership that looked solid, yet in which each carried an utterly different, unspeakable secret.

The Yellow River flowed soundlessly under the night, and the countdown to the ninth time-travel ticked away in someone's heart. And life, in this strange balance of keeping each other company while keeping each other shut out, went on with its unresolved narrative.
