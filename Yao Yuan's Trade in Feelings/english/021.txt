Chapter 21 — The Prophecy in the Mirror

In the pure white space, thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan closed her eyes for a moment. The memory came to an end.

The memory of the body and the embers of feeling, across all those long years, still seemed to carry back a faint, complicated warmth she could hardly put into words. The flutter of the heart, the giving of herself, the vague unease and the enormous longing of that time, all mixed together, had once again brewed a strong liquor called "love" — and she, half resisting and half giving in, had drained it in a single swallow.

Now, looking back in absolute clarity, what she saw was an exchange whose price had long since been marked, a mutual choice that began in passion and calculation. Every detail of that night, every touch, every whispered word, was now stripped of its romantic sugar coating, baring the more essential shape beneath: a contest of desire, a swapping of resources, the comfort of the lonely, and a hazy investment in future gain.

The time-travel had set her here in this pure white space to relive the scene — perhaps not to humiliate her, but to let her see clearly at last: the road that had led her to where she was today was not paved entirely with flowers at its starting point. Some of it was made of the chips she herself had handed over, and the gorgeous, heavy necklace she had been all too willing to put on.

It had all been written down long ago. She had simply, at the time, been unable to read the opening chapter of her own fate.

When Yao Yuan decided to be with Shuai Hongqiang, she knew he was divorced and single, with a daughter living with his ex-wife. But she only truly understood the story behind it all after they had been together for more than half a year.

Shuai Hongqiang's ex-wife was named Qiu Ying, three years his senior, a native of Jinshi born and raised. Their first meeting came in 2003, beside the clattering lathes of a factory in the western district — back then, twenty-four-year-old Shuai Hongqiang became a coworker of Qiu Ying's stepfather. The old man liked the honesty and quick wits of this young man from the mountains, and a year later he brought his stepdaughter to meet him. Another year, and the wedding character was pasted to the window. Shuai Hongqiang followed his father-in-law back to Jinshi and set his foot on the path of starting a business.

In 2006 their daughter Shuai Wenrui was born. The real turning point for this family came in 2010 — Jinshi pushed forward with urban planning, and demolition of the old town began. Qiu Ying's family was inside the zone. Overnight, the bulldozers of urban renewal also bulldozed in fate's generous gift. At the time Shuai Hongqiang's household registration was still in his home village, so to ladle out an extra bowl of soup through the cracks in the policy, they calmly signed a divorce agreement, "folding away" the marriage for the time being — legally, the daughter, the house, and the savings all went to Qiu Ying.

With the three properties he was allotted and the capital he had built up earlier, Shuai Hongqiang caught the wave of urban construction over the following years. He swiftly transformed from a young dropout out of the mountains into the well-known "Boss Shuai" of the business world.

As his enterprise grew larger and his social circle wider, he gradually lost any common language with Qiu Ying, who had stayed home tending to the family ever since the marriage. Even so, Shuai Hongqiang was never stingy with money. The living expenses, the daughter's education, even the care of Qiu Ying's parents — he shouldered all of it generously and without complaint.

It was only when Yao Yuan walked into his life that this "fake divorce" finally turned into a real separation. Shuai Hongqiang gave Qiu Ying a one-time compensation generous enough to guarantee her a comfortable, want-free life from then on. Their daughter Shuai Wenrui stayed with her mother, while all of her education costs were borne by her father.

Later, word had it that not long after Shuai Hongqiang married Lin Wan, Qiu Ying too remarried — to a fellow demolition resettler from the same district, an old classmate of hers from primary school.

Back then, once Yao Yuan knew the full story, the thought did flash through her mind — that the men of this world generally tire of the old and crave the new, even though she herself happened to be that "new." But in private she did not dwell on it too much. For one thing, Shuai Hongqiang cherished her with the utmost care, his doting tenderness plain in every gesture. For another, she did not believe she was the main reason Shuai Hongqiang and Qiu Ying had parted ways.

If thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan looked back now, she would see straight through to the core at a glance: in a relationship, when one person races ahead while the other stays rooted where they were — and this "racing ahead" is not necessarily the ability to make money, but rather a matter of vision, insight, and the territory of the spirit — then parting is all but inevitable.

There was something else in Shuai Hongqiang, an almost primal yearning passed down through a thousand years: he wanted a son. After they got together, Yao Yuan came to truly know how vigorous his appetites were, sometimes to the point of being insatiable, leaving her hardly able to keep up. Each time, he would gather her into his arms and coax her in a breathy, honeyed voice: "Let's have a son, all right? With the two of us for parents, the kid will be unbelievably good-looking, unbelievably smart… I love making money, so our son's nickname will be 'Little Shuai Money.' A jaunty cap worn at an angle, riding a kick scooter — he'll definitely be the cutest little guy in the whole neighborhood." He said this far too many times, half true and half in jest, and bit by bit it seeped into Yao Yuan's heart.

Yao Yuan had lost her father at seven. Although her stepfather, Uncle Zhou, treated her well enough, emotionally she could never quite reach the level of utter closeness and complete reliance. But thirty-seven-year-old Shuai Hongqiang gave her endless doting and indulgence, letting her feel both the warmth of being loved like a daughter and the sweetness between lovers. Steeped for so long in this kind of tenderness, and with Shuai Hongqiang bringing up the idea of a son more and more often, the thought of stepping into marriage took root in Yao Yuan's heart for the second time. She thought: judging from the way Shuai Hongqiang had handled things with his ex-wife, this man's character couldn't be too bad — perhaps he was worth entrusting her whole life to. On top of that, her coworkers of the same age were marrying one after another, her mother Zhang Fengxia often nagged in her ear, and Shuai Hongqiang was successful in business and well-off in every respect. He truly was an ideal candidate for marriage.

And yet, who could ever truly fathom what Shuai Hongqiang really thought in his heart? His love for her was real; his tenderness was real; he was never stingy with material things. But every time the conversation drifted toward the threshold of marriage, he would skirt around it without leaving a trace. In business they could advance hand in hand — Shuai Hongqiang was smooth in handling affairs, Yao Yuan moved deftly through human relationships, and the two worked together so seamlessly they were nearly unstoppable. But to actually marry Yao Yuan home, Shuai Hongqiang lost his nerve. He was afraid he could never truly "catch" a Yao Yuan who shone so brightly.

Yao Yuan kept waiting for Shuai Hongqiang to bring up a proposal on his own, but all she ever got was vague, evasive answers. Two years later, Yao Yuan unexpectedly fell pregnant, but she did not tell Shuai Hongqiang. She only asked him one last time when they would register the marriage, and once again failed to get a straight answer. So the child was gone, and she left far away for Hangcheng.

At this point in her thoughts, thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan unconsciously laid her hand gently over her lower belly again. If that child had been born, it would have been seven this year.

What was even more ironic was that the man who had once clung to her, begging for a "Little Shuai Money," turned around and married Lin Wan, who looked every bit the "virtuous wife and loving mother," a woman fit to keep a home.

Perhaps this was simply human nature: full of self-contradictory desires, finely calculated trade-offs, and a dim complexity that one cannot even face head-on. In human nature there is no absolute good, and no absolute bad.

Thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan stood in the pure white space, helplessly reliving the past on one hand, and on the other watching the twenty-seven-year-old version of herself still strolling with Shuai Hongqiang through the antiques street. How could her twenty-seven-year-old self have resisted that feng shui master's relentless cajoling? In the end she let the man read in her a destiny of the "Great Fortune of Nine-Purple Departing Fire," and even accepted the fire-phoenix gourd the old Daoist gave her. After that she walked into a somewhat shabby antique shop. The store was small, the light a dim yellow, and old wooden cabinets held a scattering of relics whose authenticity was hard to tell. Shuai Hongqiang gave it a single glance, lost interest, and ambled out the door to chat with the neighboring shopkeeper about the quality of phoenix-eye bodhi beads. So the only one left in the shop was twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan, her gaze drifting idly over those dust-covered old things.

Then, in the instant she looked up, she caught sight of a faint, dark glint of bronze amid a tangled heap in the corner of the counter.

She reached out and worked it free of the clutter. It was a small bronze mirror, about seven or eight centimeters across, its surface a dull yellow that reflected a person only faintly. But the back was carved with fine craftsmanship — a cloud-and-thunder pattern as a ground, with a single Kui-dragon coiled within, plain and antique yet carrying a touch of the mysterious. "Quite exquisite," she murmured to herself, and was just about to raise her voice and ask the price —

The air around her abruptly sank, as though frozen by an unseen force. That dull yellow mirror surface rippled like water, and slowly there surfaced a blurred yet utterly familiar face. It was her thirty-six-year-old self, gazing back at her across the void of time.

Two Yao Yuans, in dislocated time and space, met each other's eyes once again.

"You—" they spoke at the same moment, then fell silent at the same moment, each startled by the other's voice.

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan steadied her emotions first and broke the silence, her voice calm but carrying an all-knowing chill: "The twenty-two-year-old you still didn't believe what I said, did you? Otherwise, how would the twenty-seven-year-old you now have crossed paths with Shuai Hongqiang again?"

A flicker of awkwardness crossed the twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's face, and her fingers tightened slightly around the bronze mirror. "At first I really did find it hard to believe. You appeared without any warning, saying all those bizarre things that ran completely against my wonderful life at the time. Anyone in my place wouldn't have believed it so easily. After you vanished, I even thought it had only been a hallucination. Then that Spring Festival, I didn't go back to Jinshi — I went to Jianghai's family with him instead. His family had a lot of relatives, and their words were laced through and through with gossip. I even met that Miss Zhou. After I got back, I thought it over for a long time, analyzed everything carefully, and finally decided to believe what you'd said. Later I got a spot for advanced study at the Amsterdam dance academy in the Netherlands and studied abroad for two years. Not long after I came home, Mom suddenly coughed up blood. Her condition kept flaring up; several times she was at death's door, and in the end she had a lung resection. To make it easier to care for her, I decided to move my work back to Jinshi. After that, in the course of work, I slowly came together with Shuai Hongqiang. I imagine you know all of this already — after all, you're the future me, aren't you?"

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan listened quietly, her face neither sad nor glad, as though all of these ripples were within her expectations, and as though every deviation would, in the end, return to its origin. Was a fixed fate truly impossible to shake? The fine details might be altered — like her twenty-two-year-old self, who got to part from Jianghai more gracefully and to glimpse a wider world, so that the wound ran much shallower. But the crucial junctures, as if drawn by an invisible magnetic pull, stubbornly returned to their original track. And yet — wasn't that different "experience" itself a kind of change?

She drew back the deep musings surging in her eyes and refocused on the twenty-seven-year-old self's eyes, which still held reliance and hope, and spoke word by word, clear and cold:

"You've already proven my prophecy. So listen well to my warning right now: leave Shuai Hongqiang. Now."

The twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's pupils contracted slightly. Having only just entered a formal relationship with Shuai Hongqiang, wrapped in doting and sweetness, she instinctively recoiled. "Why?"

"Because he does love you, and he treats you well — but he won't marry you." The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan's words were like a blade tempered in ice, cleaving precisely through the tender surface. "In his heart, you're clever and dazzling, a partner who can stand shoulder to shoulder with him in business." Thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan went on, her words terse and cruel: "But you're not suited to be married home to keep a husband and raise children — simply because you don't have that so-called virtuous, demure face. If you don't leave, a year from now you'll fall pregnant by accident, you'll break your heart in his silence, and in the end you'll go far away alone. That taste — I don't want you to swallow it a second time."

"How… could it be like this?" the twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan murmured, the bronze mirror in her hand seeming to grow heavy and scalding. "Then what should I do?"

"Apply for unpaid leave and go to Shanghai," the thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan said, picking up speed, with a kind of urgency that raced against time. "That's the financial and internet center of the future — a vast world, full of promise. You absolutely can—"

Before the words were finished, the scene before her suddenly convulsed violently. The twenty-seven-year-old Yao Yuan's astonished face, the dim-yellow antique shop, and the entire pure white space all shook like a reflection in water stirred by an unseen giant hand. They surged, twisted, and shattered into hundreds of millions of flecks of light, then were swallowed by an absolute void.

Right after came the familiar, dizzying, whirling sense of weightlessness.

The thirty-six-year-old Yao Yuan's eyes snapped open, her heart slamming heavily in her chest. A hard, cold sensation pressed into her palm. She looked down — the cloud-and-thunder Kui-dragon bronze mirror was clutched tightly in her hand, its antique patterns pressed deep into her skin, bringing a distinct ache. She slowly loosened her fingers; the outline of the Kui-dragon on the mirror's back stood out clearly.

So it turned out that this bronze mirror, which Shuai Hongqiang had obtained from an auction, had already quietly broken into her life at such an early time.

The office was very quiet, the light bright and clear, the bustle and clamor of the real world outside the window.

This time, she was utterly certain.

It was not a dream, nor a hallucination. She had truly, in some bizarre way that transcended time and space, returned to a certain juncture of the past. She had tried to help her past self take fewer detours, to cut straight to a shortcut — and yet she had still arrived at the predetermined juncture.

Her past self had not gone through that heart-rending pain of parting with Jianghai, but had instead suffered the anxious heartache of a mother gravely ill. Truly, if fate offers a gift, it must secretly draw something away.

And the next time — where would she travel to? Or rather, could she really change the ending behind that bronze mirror, the one that seemed already written?

Yao Yuan steadied herself and glanced at the corner of her computer screen.

16:10.

From the moment she clutched the bronze mirror and fell into that pure white expanse of time and space, to this moment of jolting awake in the silence of the office, only a single hour had passed in the real world.

She rubbed her temple, pressing down for now the exhaustion and daze welling up from the depths of her consciousness. Right now there were matters in the realm of real-world science, far more pressing than any uncanny mysticism, that demanded handling. She pressed the intercom, her voice already restored to its usual crisp decisiveness: "Tell the project team — number one conference room in fifteen minutes. We'll do a final confirmation of every detail for tomorrow's negotiation with Western World Technology on the 'Pretty Big One Emotional AI model.'"

At 17:30, she politely declined a business partner's dinner invitation and brought home a simple takeout meal. The drain of the livestream and the mental shock of the time-travel layered together into a heavy fatigue, and tomorrow's meeting with Zhao Yiming's company could not afford a single misstep. She needed plenty of rest. After a hot bath, drowsiness surged in like a tide, and she sank into deep sleep almost the instant her head touched the pillow. The Kui-dragon bronze mirror lying quietly in her bag, together with all the tangled riddles tied to Shuai Hongqiang behind it, were, in the priorities of this one night, set aside for the time being by reason and weariness.
