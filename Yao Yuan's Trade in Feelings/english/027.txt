Chapter 27 — Nature Is Without Stain; It's the Heart That Sorts Clean from Foul

Nature is without stain; it's the heart that sorts clean from foul. A necessary act for human reproduction, yet treated as "dirty and unmentionable," something not fit for polite company.
Wednesday, two o'clock in the afternoon.
Yao Yuan started her livestream right on time.
Behind the host's chair the great Yellow River flowed on in silence, ancient and unchanging, its muddy yellow waves rolling a thousand years of silt over banks creased by time. The wind drifted past the windowsill, mingling with the low sob of the river. Beyond the noise and the studio lights lay the silence and vastness of mountains and rivers that had stood for ages.
On the slice of wall the camera could catch hung the gold-framed "Energy Pyramid" diagram, its edges hard and cold, its tiers sharply divided, glowing in the warm white light with a restrained yet showy matte gold. The structure narrowed as it climbed, layer by layer, stacking up an order and a desire that someone had defined — on one side the clamor of human voices and traffic inside the screen, on the other the silent hierarchy of rules hung high on the wall.
The moment Yao Yuan sat down, the picture in the frame was wrapped in a fine, shimmering glow.
The silver-wire crystal crown in her hair, its openwork flower-and-leaf design lit by the spotlights, scattered shards of light across her deep-brown waves like a handful of crushed stars. As her hair brushed her ears, the pair of dangling amethyst earrings swayed gently, the glass petals rippling out a hazy violet light that softened her naturally cool features.
She wore a silver-grey sequined cocktail gown — understated luxury — its semi-sheer mesh studded with fine diamonds and silver-thread embroidery, the low neckline cut just low enough to reveal the line of her collarbone, then veiled again in a soft haze of tulle, carrying a wealth that seemed to hold something back. On the long mesh sleeves, the silver-thread patterns brightened and dimmed in the frame as she raised her arm, like flowing moonlight.
When the camera moved in close, the fans could see the tiny diamond flecks on her lashes blend with the pearl sheen of the gauze, making Yao Yuan look like a princess carved from crystal. Just then, lashes lowered, she was adjusting her equipment, the curve of her lightly pursed lips exactly right, the line of her neck drawn taut in a cool arc — and the whole silver-grey ensemble gave off an aloofness that said, look but don't touch.
The comments scrolled by fast. Someone said that tonight, in her blingbling dress, she looked like a princess kept in a crystal box. Only she knew that every time she went live, the lavish — even slightly over-the-top — clothes, jewelry, and makeup were the armor she wrapped around herself. They were her battle robes.
"Good afternoon, my beautiful darlings! Welcome to my studio. It's time again to clear up some confusion for the lovely ladies. All right, let's connect our first call-in friend of the day." After a brief adjustment, Yao Yuan's voice came through with a smile — steady and carrying.
[Call connected]
The caller (female, voice clear, pace even, with a restrained rationality, but a barely perceptible thread of confusion underneath): "Teacher Yuanyuan, good evening. I've followed you a long time — this is my first time calling in. Here's my baseline: I'm thirty-five, I run a small company of my own, my annual income is fairly stable, around two hundred thousand after tax. All these years I've thrown myself into my career and figured relationships could wait. But now my parents are pushing, the social clock is ticking, and I myself feel… maybe it's time to move into the next stage of life."
(Comments: [Sis is amazing!] [Thirty-five with two hundred K, I'm jealous] [The social clock really is a pain])
Yao Yuan (without interrupting, just lifting her chin slightly to signal her to go on): "Mm, and then?"
The caller: "Through an introduction, I met a man. He's thirty-seven, middle management at a large company, his income about the same as mine. Family background, education, values — we match on every front. You could call it a perfect pairing. We've been seeing each other for half a year. It's calm, almost no arguments. He understands my work and respects me. Both sets of parents are very pleased and are pushing us to settle things."
Yao Yuan (catching the key words sharply): "'Almost no arguments,' 'very calm'… it sounds like you're describing an ideal business partner. So — where's the problem?" Her gaze turned keen in front of the camera.
The caller (silent for two seconds, that thread of confusion finally surfacing in her voice): "The problem is… I don't feel any spark for him. His looks are… pretty ordinary, a little below average, to be objective. I've tried, but there's genuinely no physical attraction. In fact… I'm a little put off by intimate contact. I keep telling myself that at this age, meeting someone who matches my conditions so well and is easy to be around is rare, and that I shouldn't chase some vague, illusory feeling. But I'm also afraid — if there isn't even the most basic attraction, can a marriage like that last? Am I overthinking it, being too greedy?"
(The comments exploded: [This is the dilemma of so many older elite women] [Great on paper but no feelings] [Why marry without love] [Can feelings put food on the table?])
Yao Yuan didn't answer right away. She leaned back into her chair, her long crimson nails tapping lightly on the desk — tap, tap, tap — a steady rhythm, as if she were calculating something. The sound of the Yellow River outside the window seemed to swell, becoming the backdrop to her thinking.
A few seconds later, she leaned forward, her pupils — deepened by the colored contacts — as black as a bottomless pool, locked firmly on the camera.
"All right. I heard you clearly." Her voice wasn't loud, but it carried a cold, cutting clarity that sliced straight through the tangle.
"First, stop using 'greedy' to gaslight yourself."
"You're not greedy. You're half clear-eyed and half muddled. The clear-eyed part: you know that 'matching conditions' is an important foundation for a stable marriage. The muddled part: you're trying to use that single answer, 'matching conditions,' to cover the whole complex, multiple-choice question that is marriage. Marriage isn't a consolidated balance sheet — a balanced asset column alone isn't enough. Physical attraction, emotional attachment, shared language, life rhythm… these are all scoring items with different weights. Your situation right now is that you score very high on the other items, but on 'physical attraction' you've handed in a blank sheet — maybe even a negative score."
The caller (her voice a little dry): "But feelings change. Affection can be cultivated…"
Yao Yuan (cutting in directly, her tone almost severe): "Second, don't gamble the back half of your life on 'affection can be cultivated'!"
"Affection might be cultivated, but biological attraction is very hard to 'cultivate.' That's hormones, that's dopamine, that's the most primal code in the body. You're thirty-five, not twenty-five. At twenty-five you might have the patience and the hormonal surplus to 'cultivate' feelings for someone who doesn't really attract you. But at thirty-five, your career has drained huge amounts of your emotion and energy. What you need is a partner who recharges you — not a business partner who requires you to spend extra effort just to 'boot up' the feeling. You're put off even by intimate contact — so tell me, how do you plan to 'cultivate' anything? Through willpower? Through repeatedly auto-suggesting to yourself that the 'conditions match'? That's burning through the psychological resources you barely have left to deal with real life!"
(Comments: [So true…] [When you're exhausted you really don't have the energy to cultivate feelings] [The body is the most honest])
Yao Yuan (speeding up, pressing harder with every step): "Third, you're using the 'social clock' and 'parents are pleased' to dodge being honest about your own desire."
"You say your parents are pushing, the social clock is ringing. Honestly, that's the most high-sounding excuse you've found for yourself! Because you're afraid — afraid that if you give up this 'condition-matched' option, you might never meet a 'better' one (better in your eyes meaning equally good on paper). You're afraid of facing the brutal judgment the dating market hands to women over thirty-five. You're afraid of bearing the risk of 'waiting' or 'choosing wrong.' So you'd rather ignore the clear alarm your body is sounding, try to talk yourself into it with logic, and numb yourself with 'it's time to get married.' That isn't maturity. That's cowardice. It's disrespect for your own true feelings."
The caller (seemingly struck, her breathing growing a little heavier): "Then… Teacher Yuanyuan, what do you suggest?"
Yao Yuan (easing her tone a touch, though the content stayed razor-sharp): "My advice comes in three steps. Carry them out immediately."
"Step one: face it honestly. Run a test. Stop all the self-persuasion. Find a quiet moment and seriously ask yourself: setting aside his income, his background, your parents' approval, society's gaze… purely as a woman, do you have any longing to embrace this man? Any urge to kiss him? When you imagine decades of intimate life ahead, do you look forward to it, or does it feel like a burden? Your body's reaction knows the answer earlier than your brain does."
"Step two: reassess 'matching conditions.' What you call being 'evenly matched' is static. But marriage is dynamic, stretching across decades. Do his 'conditions' include emotional stability, a high tolerance, the ability to give you the emotional value you need (even if not passion)? When you face stress, illness, low points in the future, can this 'calm' turn into solid support? If 'matching conditions' is his only core advantage, while you have a hard flaw at the level of core feeling — intimate attraction — then the long-term risk on this 'deal' is extremely high."
"Step three, and the most important: expand your options, but don't settle. Your biggest misconception is thinking it's 'either him, or someone worse, or growing old alone.' Who decided that? You're thirty-five, earning two hundred thousand a year, with a career, experience, and capital. Your power to choose a partner is far greater than you think. What you need to do isn't to lower your standard on 'feeling' to accommodate the 'conditions.' It's to use your 'conditions' to attract and filter for people who can both make your heart race and stand at your side. You could also choose someone whose 'conditions' don't match yours but for whom you feel genuine physical attraction — someone who'll take good care of you and your family. That takes time, takes initiative, takes breaking out of the passive mindset of 'waiting to be arranged.' But that's what it means to take real responsibility for your own life."
Yao Yuan paused, looking into the camera as if to pierce through the screen and gaze straight at the woman on the line — and at everyone with a similar confusion.
"Last, let me leave you with a line — and leave it with everyone caught struggling between 'suitable' and 'in love': when our own conditions are already good enough, the 'conditions' of the person we marry are the threshold of the marriage. They decide whether the two of you can walk through that door together. But 'feeling' is the light inside the house. It decides whether you'll be willing to stay under the same roof for the long haul. You can choose someone whose threshold isn't so high, but whose house is bright and warm. Just never force yourself to spend a lifetime with someone whose house always feels dim and cold to you — not even if that threshold is rimmed with gold."
"Your worth doesn't need to be proven by a marriage that 'looks perfect' but is cold at the core. You fought your way to where you are today not to win a 'condition-matched' trophy and then go home every day to a roommate you have no desire to be close to."
[Call time is almost up]
Yao Yuan (her tone firm and final): "So the answer is clear. If, after the most honest examination, you still can't feel any attraction, then cut your losses in time. Thank him for showing up — he confirmed what you require in a partner's 'conditions.' Then let go politely and move on. Your life's blueprint should include a partner, but it shouldn't hold only one 'roughly suitable' option. You deserve both the 'threshold' and the 'light.'"
"Time's up. I hope you understood."
[Call disconnected]
The studio was once again drowned in a surge of comments. Yao Yuan watched the countless reactions scroll past the screen and didn't speak right away. She just turned her head and gazed once more out the window at that all-devouring, ceaselessly rushing darkness. The sound of the Yellow River was like an eternal background noise, reminding everyone: the strongest force often springs from the deepest, most primal drive. A choice that betrays your true heart is like swimming upstream against the current — in the end, it drains you until there's nothing left.
She turned back to face the camera and gave a faint, meaningful smile.
"Darlings, the question from the lovely caller just now is also a common dilemma facing many accomplished career women today — our incomes have climbed a step up, but our thinking still lingers in the era of bound feet. That's the lingering poison of several thousand years of patriarchal society, but we ourselves have to wake up to it."
"We talk about independence. Independent in what? Not just economically, not just intellectually, but also in having a say over our own bodies. For body and mind to be content — note that 'body' comes first. If two people marry without any physical attraction, the 'body' is already uncomfortable, so how can the 'heart' be content? You work yourself to the bone, you earn money — surely it isn't so you can find someone who looks like a match on paper but whom you don't even want to touch behind closed doors?"
"'The appetite for food and sex is human nature.' In our cultural tradition, we avoid talking about sex, treating it as something hidden, unmentionable, not fit for the table. So many women lack any real understanding of sex education, are strangers to their own bodily sensations, and end up overlooked in marriage. On this point, I owe special thanks to the sociologist and sexology scholar Teacher Li Yinhe — she pulled 'sex' out of what we considered a 'filthy mire' and restored to it the dignity it deserves."
"Human reproduction depends on sex, yet for thousands of years it could never be discussed openly and honestly. And on top of that, sex is one of humanity's most primal, most direct sources of joy. Suppressing it, ignoring it — both are steps backward."
"I am firmly against any independent, accomplished woman choosing a partner she feels no physical attraction to — whether boyfriend or husband. Make yourself happy first, and only then can you bring happiness to someone else. From there, your life and your career will unfold smoothly in a state of contentment. If you don't know how to make your body happy, you can come to our 'Hum-and-Hah' class and learn the body's code."
"Remember," she looked into the camera, her gaze blazing, "whether you're facing the silt of emotion or the choices of life — being clear-eyed is forever your sexiest quality."
(Call-in segment ends)
The calls were still coming through one after another. Each caller walked away with the path to a solution they wanted, or didn't want. As for whether they later followed what Yao Yuan said, and how they followed it — that remained unknown.
