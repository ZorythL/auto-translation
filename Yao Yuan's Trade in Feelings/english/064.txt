Burn "Make Money" and "Raise Your Value" Into Your Soul!
The livestream rolled on. One caller after another came in carrying their own confusion, and left again with new thoughts and new resolve.
"Put the next one through." She spoke up again, her voice already roughened a little after three straight hours on air, but somehow that rasp only added a worn, real texture and a deeper reach to her words. She rubbed the space between her brows without thinking, and the small gesture let slip just how much that level of mental output had cost her. Outside the window, the early-summer afternoon sun over Jinshi was bright to the point of dazzling, falling through the blinds to lay parallel bars of light and shadow across the floor beside her. And she, at the very center of this swirl of light and sound, stayed steady as bedrock.
[Call connected]
Caller (Xiaoya, female voice, 28, her tone hollowed out with exhaustion, a sob barely held down beneath it, like a string pulled tight to the breaking point): "Teacher Yuanyuan, hello. I really… I can't hold on much longer. It feels like my body and my heart have both been completely scooped out — not one drop of energy left. I'm twenty-eight this year, working in operations at a big internet company. The salary sounds decent, but the price is overtime till late into the night as the norm, and weekends I often have to watch the data and respond at any moment. What's worse, I feel like I've already hit the ceiling in this job — no hope of promotion any time soon, and at any moment I could be replaced by someone younger, someone who can pull more all-nighters, someone cheaper."
She sniffed, her words speeding up on their own, the anxiety almost spilling off the screen: "On top of all that, my parents have been nagging me to get married until I'm nearly losing my mind. Every sentence on the phone is, 'Once a girl's past thirty, nobody wants her,' or, 'What's the point of all your striving? Better to find a good family and settle down safe.' But every day I get home from work with barely enough breath left to shower, and I lie in bed with my mind gone numb. Where would I find the energy to meet new people, to nurture a relationship? I look at myself — barely any savings, no love, my career stuck at a wall — and the whole of me is like an old machine with a battery run bone-dry, caked in dust, grinding away numbly and mechanically, with no future in sight anywhere… I even bought your book, I watch your videos all the time. At the time I felt slapped awake, full of strength. But once that surge passed, faced with the chicken-feathers of real life everywhere, I'm still lost. I have the strength, but no direction. I don't know where to put it…"
[The scrolling comments erupted into a flood of recognition, as if a collective emotion had found its outlet:
[She's literally me! A true portrait of a 28-year-old internet drone! Soul scooped clean!]
[+1. That hollowed-out feeling — even crying feels like a waste of energy.]
[Parents nagging about marriage + career wall + self-doubt: a triple knockout, white-knuckling it every single day.]
[Strength but no direction — too real… You drink your fill of motivational soup and still don't know which way the road goes.]
Yao Yuan (she listened quietly through the whole long, suffocating outpouring, not cutting in. She leaned far back into her chair, her sky-blue fingertips tapping once lightly on the smooth desktop, a crisp, cool little sound. She let out a near-inaudible sigh — a sigh that held no cheap pity, but the almost ruthless calm of someone who has seen straight to the heart of a thing):
"Xiaoya, I hear you." Her voice was even, like a river flowing in the dead of night, carrying a force that smoothed over agitation — but cold, too. "Yet through your voice I can't feel the surging 'life force' a twenty-eight-year-old woman ought to have. All I hear is a deep, passively-borne exhaustion, and the panic and numbness of being driven and whipped forward by every external standard there is — your parents' expectations, society's clock, the workplace's KPIs."
She leaned slightly forward, her gaze blazing, as if to pierce the screen and look straight into the other woman's frightened eyes:
"All your pain right now, the confusion, the drained energy — its root is this: you're trying to crack a systemic problem that is multidimensional and exponentially complex, using the single-threaded logic of 'work hard at a job, accumulate in a straight line.' Career achievement, marriage pressure, self-worth, the first pile of capital, your parents' traditional expectations… these problems all tangle together and feed off each other, and the complexity climbs exponentially. And you're counting on more overtime, on forcing yourself onto the 'matchmaking market' to tick off a task, to find a one-and-done answer. It's like —" she paused, and reached for a precise image — "like you've got an eating spoon in your hand and you're trying to dig a tunnel with it. It's not that the spoon isn't trying hard enough. It's that your tools and your strategy were dead wrong from the very start."
[The comments were struck dead by the image, boiling over in an instant:
[Linear logic against an exponential problem! Digging a tunnel with a spoon! So sharp it's suffocating!]
[Got it in one second! It's not that we don't work hard — it's that the direction and the method are completely wrong!]
[So we've been doing useless work this whole time, and half killing ourselves doing it…]
"You feel tired, with no energy left for love, choked breathless by the marriage nagging, like your life has no direction." Yao Yuan turned the blade. She threw out an extremely sharp, even cruel hypothetical: "Then let me ask you, Xiaoya — if right now, this very instant, five million yuan after tax suddenly appeared in your bank account, entirely yours, with no debt against it. Would you still feel that this job grinding you to exhaustion is a weight you can't bear? Would you still spiral into anxiety and self-doubt over your parents' 'hurry up and marry someone'? Would you still feel like a headless fly, with no direction at all?"
Caller (Xiaoya was visibly stunned; the hypothetical was too abrupt and too concrete. She hesitated for several seconds before answering, not quite sure): "…I suppose, it'd be a lot better? At least… my heart would feel a lot steadier. I wouldn't be so afraid of the future. Maybe… I'd even have the nerve to try some different things?"
"See — you've already given the answer yourself, and very clearly." Yao Yuan's voice was even and firm, carrying the undeniable clarity of someone laying bare a cruel truth. "In the real adult world, money of course isn't all-powerful. But it is the most effective lens for shrinking problems and amplifying choices. When your own economic base is thin and your chips are few, any small disturbance — one unpleasant overtime shift, one sigh from your parents, the number of your age ticking up, even someone else's glossy life on social media — gets magnified without limit, turned into a disaster movie that crushes you. But when you hold a real, thick-enough cushion of wealth in your hands, most of that outside noise and pressure automatically downgrades into distant background hum. You gain the nerve to say no, and room to choose."
She paused, her tone now aimed straight at the core, peeling back the emotional surface to expose the kernel of interest and power beneath:
"You feel marriage is enormous pressure — fundamentally that's because, in your subconscious, you assume you're standing in the 'being chosen,' 'being appraised' position. Your sense of security, the quality of your future life, seem to hang on another person's 'choice' and 'ongoing supply.' But if you've laid your own solid economic base, if you have an independent ability to create wealth, then you go from being a 'player' to, in a sense, the 'house' — and the whole picture changes. You no longer have to anxiously go to the 'matchmaking market' to be looked over and priced. You hold the right to filter and to choose actively. Marriage even downgrades from a 'lifeline you can't survive without' to a 'nice-to-have, take-it-or-leave-it option.' If you meet someone and you fit each other — wonderful. If not, you can live a rich, vivid, secure life on your own. Your anxiety is rooted in 'fear of not having,' and wealth is the most solid medicine there is for curing that 'fear of not having.'"
[The comments argued fiercely, viewpoints colliding:
[Cruel, but it seems to be the truth… The economic base determines the superstructure.]
[Isn't she making money out to be too important? What about feelings, sincerity? Don't those matter?]
[But reality is, love without economic independence rarely has equality or dignity. Yuanyuan's teaching us to see this society's rules of the game clearly.]
[My parents are the proof — my mom isn't financially independent, and she's swallowed her grievances her whole life at home.]
Caller (Xiaoya's voice grew urgent, deeper helplessness in it): "Teacher, I understand money matters — I get that! But making money isn't that easy, is it? Right now I work myself half to death every day, trading my life for money, and I still see no breakthrough, no hope — I'm nearly depressed. I genuinely have no idea where to even start 'making money.' It feels like every road is blocked."
"There — the classic 'student mindset' trap, back again!" Yao Yuan's tone sharpened abruptly, edged with the keen, urgent frustration of someone watching iron refuse to become steel. "You think that, like in your school days, if you follow the steps, listen to the teacher (the boss), work hard to finish the homework (the KPIs), and earn good grades (promotion and a raise), you can just wait for wealth to steadily pile up and for life to feel secure? Xiaoya, I've seen too many women at twenty-eight, at thirty, grinding themselves into the ground at work, burning the midnight oil, depleting themselves, only to reap almost nothing — even to be cast aside. The era changed long ago, and the rules of the game changed with it! Working a job won't make you big money — it only lets you 'survive,' or barely even that. If you want to 'live well,' what you need is means of production, leverage to move resources, a profit model of your own."
Her voice softened, drawing in her own lived experience, which made the argument more convincing and more resonant:
"When I first pivoted to building my own media and personal brand, I went through a stretch that was bleak, lost, full of self-doubt too. Watching others seemingly hit their stride with no effort while I couldn't find the door, I was so anxious I lay awake whole nights, my hair falling out in clumps. How did I claw my way out? I made a decision that, at the time, looked cruel and lonely: I steeled myself and cut away every 'useless social connection' and every bout of 'self-congratulatory emotional drain' that couldn't directly bring me a gain in ability or a flow of cash. Every minute, every drop of energy I saved, I focused like a laser on two things. First: like doing academic research, I dug deep into the underlying logic of how different industries make money, the secrets of how traffic gets distributed, the weaknesses and needs of human nature. Second: I threw away my face and poured out value like crazy, connected with people above me, built my one-of-a-kind professional calling card and personal brand."
She shared a specific story, full of that 'Yao Yuan' style and sense of ritual:
"I remember it very clearly — when I truly earned my first ten million in net profit, the first thing I did was go put down a deposit on a Porsche Panamera I'd been eyeing for a long time but always told myself to 'wait a bit longer' on, that it 'wasn't necessary.' I chose my favorite color, with the configuration I most wanted. I did this not to show off, and certainly not out of vanity. I wanted to use that intensely ritual, tangible, weighty and fast material vessel to confirm my ability hard and concretely, to plant my value, to reward my own effort. When I held the wheel alone and truly drove it for the first time, threading through the dazzling river of city night traffic, that solid sense of achievement — built up inch by inch with my own hands — and that absolute sense of control over my life washed away every bit of my old lack of confidence, my fear of others' eyes, my confusion and uncertainty about the future, scrubbed it all clean. In that moment I finally understood, all the way through: money isn't just numbers jumping on a bank card. It's the weapon you use to win back the right to choose your own life, the bedrock on which you rebuild your inner order and reach an ultimate peace, the nerve that lets you freely say 'yes' or 'no.'"
[Comments: [First ten million and she buys a Panamera! That sense of ritual is unreal!]
[Using material things to confirm and reward your own value — what an honest, powerful angle!]
[Honestly, sometimes you just need a solid, real 'proof' like that, to tell yourself: you did it, you deserve it.]
"So, Xiaoya, take one piece of advice from me — the most solid, down-to-earth advice there is." Yao Yuan's tone turned grave, almost a stern warning. "Until the spendable balance in your bank account has stably broken into seven figures (you can set that number to your own situation), until the 'threshold' deep inside you for material security has been fully satisfied — your single most central, highest-priority task in life is one thing and one thing only: cast off every impractical false pride and every wild, idle thought, block out all the noise that drains your energy, and with your whole mind, by every means you can, make money."
"Don't think these words are too realistic, too harsh, too vulgar. The more concrete and quantifiable your goal, the more solid the point you push from, and the more direction your actions have. You love poetry, you long for far-off places, you crave the pure love of souls meeting? Of course you can — these are all beautiful adornments of life. But please first make sure you've built yourself an economic deck from which you can enjoy all of it with ease and a clear mind. Otherwise, all that romance and refinement is like a gorgeous castle built on quicksand: it looks beautiful, but any small wave of reality could collapse it in an instant and let you fall even harder."
She reached for an even more vivid, more piercing image:
"It's like this: every day, on your way to and from work, you pass that three-Michelin-star restaurant you have to book three months in advance. Through the glass you see the women inside, dressed in the season's haute couture, draped in dazzling jewels, savoring exquisite dishes you maybe can't even name, laughing and talking with ease. You can tell yourself the lamb skewers at the night market next door are delicious too, full of warm, smoky life. And yes, lamb skewers are of course tasty. But can you truly enjoy that skewer with a heart entirely untroubled, with no comparison, no longing — genuinely at peace, purely happy? It's hard. Because that 'wanting,' that 'curiosity' about the 'unknown deliciousness,' about 'another way of living,' has been suppressed by your reason and your circumstances. It's like a wooden float pressed under water: every time you push it down hard (telling yourself not to compare, to be content, to keep calm), it stubbornly bobs back up, because of the real unwillingness and longing inside you. This repeated self-suppression and struggle is itself violently draining your psychological energy."
"Real letting-go doesn't come from suppression and persuasion. It comes from having money, having enough nerve, being genuinely able to walk in anytime and unhurriedly order a full table and slowly taste that Michelin meal. And after you've eaten, you might suddenly realize, even be a little disappointed: 'Oh, so that's the taste — it's just this, nothing as magical as I imagined.' In that moment, you're truly free, truly released. After that, when you sit at the night-market stall again, eating your lamb skewers, that taste is pure and happy — because you know clearly you're not sitting there because you 'can't afford it' or 'don't deserve it,' but because 'I've experienced it, and I choose' to sit here, enjoying this plain, lively warmth. Money, at that point, becomes an utterly pure tool: to satisfy an experience, to dissolve an obsession, to widen the borders of your understanding, letting you see more clearly and more calmly what your heart actually wants, and what truly makes you feel happy and fulfilled."
[In the comments, countless people said the image had struck them hard to the core:
[The wooden-float image — I cried. This is exactly my daily mental drain!]
[Turns out I've been desperately suppressing my own desires and ambition, thinking I didn't deserve them…]
[Real freedom is 'I choose,' not 'I have no choice.' Said it straight into my heart.]
[So making money is to gain the freedom to choose, not to be enslaved by desire.]
"When, through your own wisdom, effort, and persistence, your material base reaches and even comfortably surpasses that inner 'threshold' of security and freedom," Yao Yuan concluded, her voice now charged with a powerful force that lifts a person up and forward, "you'll find the whole world's attitude toward you turns gentler. Your parents may stop nagging about marriage, because they've seen with their own eyes that you can live a rich, vivid life on your own, full of nerve. You'll stop being anxious about age, because time has become a friend that builds your experience, wisdom, and wealth — every wrinkle may hide a story and a kind of nerve. As for love and marriage, they'll no longer be the 'lifeline' that rescues you from disaster, but a pure gift that fate hands you on top of everything else. Have it, and it's icing on the cake, two people lighting each other up. Don't have it, and the scroll of your life lived alone is already vast and brilliant enough, full of endless possibility."
"So, Xiaoya — now, right now, stop all the meaningless inward draining!" Her tone was decisive, carrying the rallying force of a charge bugle. "Pull all your attention, all your heart's strength, hard out of those empty emotional vents — 'I'm so tired,' 'I'm so lost,' 'why does no one love me' — and shift it onto these three concrete questions: 'How can I make myself worth more?' 'What skill or interest do I already have that could be turned into cash flow?' 'Whom should I move closer to — which people or things that have already gotten results and can lead me to make money?' Take action, even if it starts from a tiny side hustle, from paying to connect with one industry role model you admire, from grinding away at one small skill the market needs. Getting moving is the cure for all confusion and inner drain. Only action produces real energy and real hope."
Caller (Xiaoya's voice changed markedly — the exhaustion was still audible, but the heavy numbness had been broken, infused now with a new, ship-burning resolve): "I understand, Teacher Yuanyuan! I really heard it! I can't keep spinning in the old thought-trap and the emotional swamp — I have to jump out myself! Making money, improving myself, building irreplaceable value — that's the only thing worth doing at this stage of my life, and the most worthwhile! I know how to adjust where I put my weight and my force now! Thank you!"
Yao Yuan finally let out the first truly relaxed smile of the whole broadcast, warm with encouragement and firm with trust: "That's right. Remember — making good money is the hardest nerve you have at this stage, and the shortest, most solid path to true independence of character and freedom in life. Now, hang up, take out paper and pen, and immediately list three concrete action plans you'll carry out over the next three months to increase your income and raise your value. Get moving — that's how you gather the energy you've let scatter back onto yourself."

Yao Yuan watched the fighting-spirited comments roll across the screen, picked up the insulated cup beside her, and took a slow sip of perfectly warm aged-tangerine-peel white tea, soothing the dry ache in her throat from all the long, intense talking. Her sky-blue nails left a faint water mark on the pure white wall of the cup, like a patch of clear sky mirrored on snow. She let out a small, easing breath, and to the camera her tone returned to its usual cool calm — yet every word was clear and forceful:
"Be a little more clear-eyed, a little more pragmatic, a little harder on yourself. You'll find that the vast majority of the problems that bring you pain, anxiety, and sleepless nights will gradually shrink — even resolve themselves — as your personal value and the number in your account grow solidly."
Having said that last line of conclusion, Yao Yuan gave the camera a polished, perfectly curved smile. She didn't move right away, her gaze resting calmly on the comments still racing across the screen, unsatisfied, hungry for more. Those words — [Thank you, Yuanyuan!] [So good to be slapped awake!] [Going to list my plan right now!] — reflected in her pupils, still as a deep pool, as flowing, faint, but real points of light.
